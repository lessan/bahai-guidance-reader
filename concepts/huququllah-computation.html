Computing Ḥuqúqu’lláh in Practice

What it is

This page explains how to compute and pay Ḥuqúqu’lláh (“the Right of God”) in practice — the 19% offering on accumulated surplus wealth that Bahá’u’lláh ordained in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas. The focus here is on modern, fluctuating assets: a stock portfolio that rises and falls, a home whose value changes, savings that build up year by year. Those cases are harder than the simpler household economies the early questions in the compilation assumed.

The binding texts are the Research Department’s Codification of the Law of Ḥuqúqu’lláh (codification-law-huququllah, 1987/2007) and the Universal House of Justice compilation Ḥuqúqu’lláh: The Right of God (huququllah-right-god). Throughout this page, bracketed numbers like `[n]` refer to paragraphs in that compilation; “Q&A no. X” refers to the Questions and Answers of the Kitáb-i-Aqdas.

The worked examples, the “high-water mark” model, and the joint-household walkthrough below are editorial synthesis — a principled application of the texts to contemporary cases. They are not legislation. No official formula exists; believers work out their own methods on the basis of the texts ([65]).

The Research Department’s preamble warns that the compiled questions come from friends in “infinitely simpler economic systems and relationships than those which obtain today.” Modern cases therefore apply principles, not a rulebook you can look up line by line. Computation and payment are “exclusively a matter of conscience between the individual and God”; demanding or soliciting Ḥuqúq from another believer is prohibited (Codification of the Law of Ḥuqúqu’lláh, Preamble). For how binding sources are ranked when texts appear to conflict, see Authority and infallibility.

Authority levels

When this page cites a ruling, it draws on sources in the following order:

  • Bahá’u’lláh — Divine Revelation. The binding law itself; infallible.
  • ‘Abdu’l-Bahá and Shoghi Effendi — authoritative interpretation of the Writings. Binding within their respective spheres.
  • Universal House of Justice (UHJ) — legislation and application of the law within its sphere. Binding.
  • Individual conscience — expressly delegated to the believer’s own judgement. Not a binding ruling on anyone else.

The core rule

Everything a believer possesses, with specific exceptions, is subject once and only once to Ḥuqúqu’lláh (Codification III). That “once only” principle is the spine of the whole computation. In practice, four further rules follow:

  1. Threshold. Ḥuqúq becomes payable once assessable possessions reach the value of 19 mithqáls of gold — approximately 2.2 troy ounces or 69.2 grams (Codification III.B.1). The money equivalent floats with the gold price (roughly `2.2 × current spot`; recompute when needed, or use the figure published by the Office of Ḥuqúqu’lláh). Below this threshold, no obligation applies ([62]).
  1. Rate — 19%. The offering is 19% of assessable property (Codification III.B.1.a), reckoned on whole units of 19 mithqáls. Bahá’u’lláh states that “any amount added to nineteen is exempt … until it reacheth a further nineteen” (Q&A no. 90 [40]) — meaning you do not owe on every small increment; the law works in steps.
  1. Base — net surplus, paid once. You pay on income remaining after annual expenses — not on your entire wealth afresh each year (‘Abdu’l-Bahá [44], [47]). A sum once cleared through Ḥuqúq is never assessed again “unless it should pass from one person to another” (Shoghi Effendi [49]; Bahá’u’lláh Q&A no. 8 [33]).
  1. Conditional on ability to pay. “If a person is unable to meet his obligation, God will verily excuse him” (Bahá’u’lláh [34]). Debts take precedence over Ḥuqúq (Codification III.B.6; [70]). No one is required to liquidate assets hastily; the believer chooses the time and method of payment ([68], Codification III.C.6).

Exemptions (binding)

Certain categories of property are exempt from assessment:

  • Residence and needful furnishings (Bahá’u’lláh, Q&A no. 8 [33], no. 42 [35]).
  • Needful tools, equipment, or stock-in-trade used in a trade, business, or agriculture (Bahá’u’lláh, Q&A no. 95 [41]; ‘Abdu’l-Bahá [46]).
  • Capital already cleared whose income merely meets one’s needs (‘Abdu’l-Bahá [46]).
  • What counts as “needful” or “necessary” is left to conscience ([56]), bounded by honesty — you may not engineer “lavish” spending to avoid Ḥuqúq ([55]).
  • Income-earning or invested capital is not automatically exempt. The texts state: “No specific text has been found exempting capital used to earn income” — how to treat it is left to conscience ([57]).

Fluctuating assets — the operative model (editorial synthesis)

Modern wealth rarely sits still. The model that fits the texts best is this:

Track a running “cleared base” — a high-water line of wealth you have already assessed and paid Ḥuqúq on. Each year, pay 19% only on net assessable wealth above that line.

Four principles support that model:

  • Unrealized appreciation is not assessed until realized. The Codification specifies assessment “on the sale of the property” (III.B.4); Bahá’u’lláh directs payment on “the profit realized” (Q&A no. 8). A collection held as a non-income heirloom need not be valued until sold ([66]).
  • Shares and other non-cash assets. The believer “may find it disadvantageous … to pay nineteen percent of the value of the non-cash assets until they are disposed of,” deducting disposal costs first (UHJ [54]). You are not required to liquidate investments merely to pay Ḥuqúq on paper gains.
  • Losses, then recovery — no second payment on the same money. If you lose half your wealth (or all of it) and later build back up to the prior level, “the Ḥuqúq is not payable” / “payment of Ḥuqúq is not required” (Bahá’u’lláh, Q&A no. 44 [36], no. 45 [37]). The Codification adds: “If possessions decrease … Ḥuqúqu’lláh falls due again only after the loss has been made good and the total value … is augmented” (III.B.5).
  • Compute on accumulation, not each year in isolation. The UHJ directs that “computations … should be made on the basis of the accumulation of savings … Only in this way can one take account of losses in one year … or compute the profit or loss on the sale of an investment which was purchased in an earlier year” ([71]).

The “high-water mark” — three clarifications

Think of the cleared base as a permanent, upward-only ceiling: the highest level of wealth you have ever cleared Ḥuqúq on. It does not fall when your holdings drop.

The decisive text is the total-loss case in Q&A no. 45 [37]: even after a sum is “lost in its entirety” and later regained, “payment of Ḥuqúq is not required.” That ruling rules out an alternative model — one that would treat only the “purified capital you still hold” as protected. Under that rival reading, money you lost and then earned again would be taxed a second time. The texts refuse that. What remains is the sticky-ceiling model: once you have cleared a level, rebuilding to that same level does not trigger a new assessment.

Three further points sharpen the picture:

  1. It is a level you’ve cleared, not “every dollar you’ve ever owned.” The ceiling ratchets up when you clear a new high. It does not ratchet down — not even through total loss (Q&A no. 45). The benchmark is the figure Ḥuqúq was assessed on, not what you kept after paying. Example: you clear $100,000 → pay $19,000 → retain $81,000. Your benchmark is $100,000, per ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s deduction method [48], not $81,000.
  1. The ceiling only matters when money actually leaves your assessable base. If you spend already-cleared wealth on consumption or on exempt property — living expenses, travel, a residence, needful goods — your holdings fall, and re-accumulating up to the old ceiling is not taxed again. But if you merely move cleared money from cash into another investment, your assessable position has not dropped (the investment counts at cost, and the money stays purified). There is nothing to “rebuild,” and nothing newly owed.
  1. Deliberate spend-down, then rebuild, sits at the edge of the explicit texts. The rulings on involuntary loss (Q&A 44/45) and on expenses exceeding income (III.B.5) are clear. A purposeful spend-down of a windfall may follow the same logic in principle — but two guardrails apply: the anti-avoidance rule (do not engineer lavish spending to dodge Ḥuqúq, [55]) and the counsel of conscience plus a Representative for structured cases ([65], [72]).

Reconstructing your cleared base from past payments

If you have paid Ḥuqúq over the years but never kept a running record of your cleared base, you can reconstruct it from the payments themselves. Each payment was 19% of an assessable increment; those increments, added together, reach the peak you cleared:

Cleared base (high-water mark) ≈ (total Ḥuqúq ever paid) ÷ 0.19

‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s own worked example [48] confirms the arithmetic: he cleared 1,000 + 1,000 + 500 = 2,500 and paid 190 + 190 + 95 = 475; and 475 ÷ 0.19 = 2,500.

The method also surfaces past under-payment. Compare the recovered base to your current assessable wealth. If you are above the recovered ceiling, a catch-up may genuinely be owed — payable over time, conditional on ability ([34], [68]).

Worked answers to common cases (editorial synthesis)

Savings for a first home deposit

Funds you are saving toward a residence are assessable in principle — they are “not in themselves exempt.” But you have a choice (UHJ [71]): pay Ḥuqúq as you save (and then the home you buy is exempt), or defer inclusion until purchase, when the residence value itself becomes exempt. The discretion is yours. What you may not do is treat liquid savings earmarked for a future home as already exempt while they remain in the bank.

Selling the primary residence at a gain

While the property is your residence, it is exempt (Q&A no. 8/42).

If you roll the sale proceeds straight into a new primary residence, in substance no Ḥuqúq falls due — the exemption carries across ([71]; no haste is required, III.C.6).

If you keep the proceeds liquid, they enter assessable wealth. Deduct selling costs first ([54]). The realized gain is the clearest assessable component (III.B.4). Whether the entire proceeds or only the gain above your original purchase cost is assessable depends on whether the money you used to buy the home had already been cleared — an edge the texts do not fully specify ([57]). A Representative of the Trustee can advise ([72]).

Cumulative savings ($1,000 cleared → spend $400 → save another $1,000 → $1,600 total)

Pay on $600 — the amount above your cleared base.

‘Abdu’l-Bahá instructs that each year you pay on what remains after expenses “and after the amount he paid Ḥuqúq on the previous year is also deducted” ([48]). Here: cleared base = $1,000; current savings = $1,600; assessable increment = $1,600 − $1,000 = $600.

What you spent the $400 on does not change this calculation, because the $400 came from money already cleared. Ḥuqúq is paid once ([33]); spending taxed money is neutral — it triggers neither a refund nor a new charge. The necessity/luxury test applies earlier, when you deduct expenses from income to calculate each year’s surplus ([56]), not when you spend capital that has already been purified.

Invest the surplus, then hold cash next year

Suppose you clear $100,000 (pay $19,000, retain $81,000), invest all $81,000, and hold $10,000 cash the following year. Your benchmark remains $100,000 — the figure you were assessed on, not what you kept.

Investment flat, $10k cash: Assessable position = $81,000 (investment at cost, still purified) + $10,000 cash = $91,000. That is below the $100,000 ceiling, so nothing is due. The $10,000 is simply rebuilding toward the peak; it is not taxed on its own.

Investment doubles to $162,000, only $10k cash: The $81,000 unrealized gain is not assessed until realized (III.B.4; [54]) — that deferral is binding, not optional. Your realized/basis position = $81,000 + $10,000 = $91,000, still below $100,000. Nothing is due, even though your paper wealth is $172,000. On sale for $162,000 you become liquid (roughly $172,000 total) and owe 19% on the excess above the $100,000 ceiling — here, $72,000 — after deducting selling costs ([54]).

Whether to include non-liquid investments at all: You cannot choose to ignore unrealized gains — they simply are not owed until realized (III.B.4). But the timing of paying on the underlying capital is your discretion: no haste to liquidate (III.C.6); you may wait until disposal ([54]).

Couple vs. individual — whose ceiling?

Ḥuqúq attaches to property “indisputably recognised as being one’s own” ([58]). The law is therefore individual by default.

A couple may nonetheless elect to hold and pay jointly. The House of Justice states that “Freedom is left to each husband and wife to decide whether to comply with their obligations … jointly or separately,” and that it “does not wish to interfere with the right of each couple to decide how they wish to deal with their own finances” ([70]; [73]; Codification III.C.7).

The high-water mark follows whichever model you choose:

  • Separate finances → each spouse keeps their own cleared base.
  • Joint finances → one combined pool, one combined ceiling.

The couple is asked to “take counsel together and to define precisely the limits of their personal belongings” ([58]).

Worked example — a joint household, end to end (editorial synthesis)

Illustrative numbers; representative, not prescriptive. This walks a single-earner couple who treat all income and assets as jointly and equally owned. The homemaker spouse’s care of children and household is understood to enable the earner’s income — a common reading of how joint household labour relates to joint ownership.

Setup — the joint election

The couple deliberately adopt genuine joint ownership with right of survivorship: one combined pool, one combined cleared base, documented in writing and matched by how assets are titled and by the will. This is fully within the freedom granted in [70] and [73]; it is a recognised option, not a workaround. Structurally it resembles a joint tenancy: the pool stays intact with the survivor on death, and is divided on divorce.

Step 1 — Recover the cleared base

Total every Ḥuqúq payment the household has ever made and divide by 0.19 (see Reconstructing your cleared base above). Suppose total paid = $19,000. Then:

Joint high-water mark = $19,000 ÷ 0.19 = $100,000.

Step 2 — Pick an annual reckoning date

They choose 30 June, aligned to the Australian financial year so tax and bank records line up. Time and method of reckoning are the believer’s discretion ([54], [64], III.C.6). Any consistent annual date is acceptable.

Step 3 — Tally assessable wealth at 30 June

Include:

  • Cash in bank.
  • Investments at cost basis (unrealized gains are deferred until sold, III.B.4).

Exclude — primary residence.

Fully exempt (Q&A no. 8/42). The deposit and principal paid down over the years have become exempt equity — money that legitimately left the assessable base and entered an exempt asset. This is guarded only by the anti-lavishness rule ([55]).

Exclude — the mortgage on that residence.

Because the home sits outside the calculation, its loan should stay outside too. Deducting a home mortgage against cash and investments while excluding the home itself would let the loan shelter wealth that ought to count. Deduct only non-residence debts — personal loans, business loans — against assessable wealth. (This specific netting is not spelled out in the texts; see the questions list below.)

Exclude — a needful family car bought outright, if conscience deems it “needful” ([56], [57]). A car used for work may qualify as professional equipment ([41]). A luxury or collector vehicle held mainly as a store of value would be included.

Deduct living expenses and taxes already accounted for in the annual reckoning (Codification III.B.2).

Step 4 — Compare and pay

If assessable wealth at 30 June exceeds $100,000, pay 19% on the excess. If at or below $100,000, nothing is due this year. Then raise the cleared base to the new level you have assessed on.

For how appreciating but illiquid investments behave in this step, see the Invest the surplus case above.

Step 5 — On the death of one spouse

Under genuine joint ownership, the survivor continues with the same assets and the same $100,000 cleared base. On this reading, nothing “changes hands,” because both spouses already owned the whole pool.

That reading is reasonable and arguably correct — but it is the single point the texts do not legislate explicitly. Paragraph [59] holds that property “passing from one person to another … through inheritance … becomes again subject to the payment of Ḥuqúqu’lláh.”

Mitigations if you adopt the joint-survivor reading: document joint ownership clearly; align the will (for example, a clause directing all assets to the survivor; sole-title items like a car named explicitly).

If a Representative holds that the deceased’s beneficial half does transfer on death, the consequence is bounded: Ḥuqúq on that half only, payable over time, never by forced liquidation ([68], III.C.6), with unrealized gains still deferred until the survivor actually sells (III.B.4).

Either way, the estate settles the deceased’s own outstanding Ḥuqúq first ([67]; III.B.8), and exempt items (residence, furnishings, tools) pass exempt ([52]).

Step 6 — On divorce (clean 50/50)

Split the assets and the cleared base 50/50. Each party ends with half the pool and half the high-water mark.

A clean even division triggers no fresh Ḥuqúq: neither party acquires property from the other; each simply takes their pre-existing half (each then holds what is “indisputably their own”, [58]).

The only wrinkle: if a settlement gives one party more than their half, the excess could count as property “passing from one person to another” and become re-assessable.

Questions for a Ḥuqúqu’lláh Representative

This page leaves several points genuinely open. A Representative of the Trustee can advise confidentially in light of your specific circumstances ([72]). Worth raising:

  1. Mortgage on an exempt residence — Should both the home and its loan stay outside the calculation, or should the outstanding loan be deducted against other assessable wealth?
  2. Needful vehicle — Is treating a genuinely needful family car as exempt acceptable, and where is the line between “needful” and a vehicle held as a store of value?
  3. Joint ownership with survivorship — On the death of one spouse, does the deceased’s beneficial half “change hands” and become re-assessable, or does the survivor simply continue with the same cleared base ([59] vs. genuine joint ownership)?
  4. If Ḥuqúq re-triggers on death — Is the transferred share assessed at cost basis or current value? Is payment over time without liquidating confirmed?
  5. Sole-name-for-tax assets — An investment held in one spouse’s sole legal name for tax reasons but beneficially jointly owned: how is it treated on death or transfer? Does legal titling affect the answer?
  6. Divorce — Does a clean 50/50 settlement trigger any fresh Ḥuqúq? Is splitting the joint cleared base pro-rata the correct approach?
  7. Reconstruction — Is total-paid ÷ 0.19 an acceptable way to establish a cleared base? How should years of under-payment before you understood the law be handled (catch-up scheduling under ability-to-pay)?
  8. Superannuation / locked retirement savings — Is wealth you own but cannot access until preservation age assessable now, or only when it becomes available?
  9. Unrealized appreciation on long-held investments — Confirm the deferral-until-realization treatment for a fluctuating portfolio, and the handling of selling costs.
  10. Annual reckoning date — Any guidance on fixing a consistent date (e.g., 30 June) for the household’s calculation?

Open / refer to a Ḥuqúqu’lláh Representative

No official calculation formula will be issued. Believers “work out their own methods on the basis of the texts” ([65]).

The law is progressive — it unfolds as new economic forms appear. Novel cases are not directly ruled in the compilation: retirement accounts and superannuation, derivatives, cryptocurrency, business equity versus working capital, the residence-sale cost-basis edge, the death-transfer treatment of a jointly owned pool. For these, use your best judgement, consult a Representative, and remember that responsibility remains personal ([72]). The numbered list in Questions for a Ḥuqúqu’lláh Representative above collects the main open points.
