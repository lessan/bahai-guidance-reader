Computing Ḥuqúqu’lláh in Practice

What it is

How to actually compute and pay Ḥuqúqu’lláh (“the Right of God”) — the 19% offering on accumulated surplus wealth ordained by Bahá’u’lláh in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas — with particular attention to modern, fluctuating assets such as a stock portfolio, a home, and year-to-year savings. Grounded in the Research Department’s Codification of the Law of Ḥuqúqu’lláh (codification-law-huququllah, 1987/2007) and the Universal House of Justice compilation Ḥuqúqu’lláh: The Right of God (huququllah-right-god; bracketed numbers `[n]` below are its paragraphs; “Q&A no. X” are the Questions and Answers of the Kitáb-i-Aqdas). Worked examples, the “high-water mark” model, and the joint-household walkthrough below are editorial synthesis — principled application of the texts, not legislation.

The Research Department’s preamble warns that the compiled questions come from friends in “infinitely simpler economic systems and relationships than those which obtain today”; modern cases apply principles, not a rulebook. Computation and payment are “exclusively a matter of conscience between the individual and God”; demanding or soliciting Ḥuqúq is prohibited (Codification of the Law of Ḥuqúqu’lláh, Preamble). See Authority and infallibility for how binding sources are ranked.

Authority levels

  • Bahá’u’lláh — Divine Revelation, infallible: the binding law itself.
  • ‘Abdu’l-Bahá / Shoghi Effendi — conferred-infallible authoritative interpretation. Binding.
  • Universal House of Justice (UHJ) — infallible legislation/application within its sphere. Binding.
  • Individual conscience — expressly delegated to the believer’s judgement (not a binding ruling).

The core rule

Everything a believer possesses, with specific exceptions, is subject once and only once to Ḥuqúqu’lláh (Codification III). Practically:

  1. Threshold — payable once assessable possessions reach the value of 19 mithqáls of gold ≈ 2.2 troy oz ≈ 69.2 g (Codification III.B.1). Money value floats with the gold price (`≈ 2.2 × current spot`; recompute, or use the Office of Ḥuqúqu’lláh’s published figure). Below this, no obligation ([62]).
  2. Rate — 19% of assessable property (Codification III.B.1.a), reckoned on whole units of 19 mithqáls; “any amount added to nineteen is exempt … until it reacheth a further nineteen” (Bahá’u’lláh, Q&A no. 90 [40]).
  3. Base — net surplus, paid once. Pay on income remaining after annual expenses; not on one’s entire wealth each year (‘Abdu’l-Bahá [44], [47]). A sum once cleared is never re-assessed “unless it should pass from one person to another” (Shoghi Effendi [49]; Bahá’u’lláh Q&A no. 8 [33]).
  4. Conditional on ability to pay — “If a person is unable to meet his obligation, God will verily excuse him” (Bahá’u’lláh [34]). Debts take precedence (Codification III.B.6; [70]). No forced/hasty liquidation; time and method are the individual’s ([68], Codification III.C.6).

Exemptions (binding)

  • Residence and needful furnishings (Bahá’u’lláh, Q&A no. 8 [33], no. 42 [35]).
  • Needful tools/equipment of trade, business, or agriculture (Bahá’u’lláh, Q&A no. 95 [41]; ‘Abdu’l-Bahá [46]).
  • Capital already cleared whose income merely meets one’s needs (‘Abdu’l-Bahá [46]).
  • What counts as “needful”/“necessary” is left to conscience ([56]), bounded by honesty — no “lavish” spending to dodge Ḥuqúq ([55]).
  • Income-earning / invested capital is NOT automatically exempt: “No specific text has been found exempting capital used to earn income” — left to conscience ([57]).

Fluctuating assets — the operative model (editorial synthesis)

Track a running “cleared base” (high-water line) of already-assessed wealth; each year pay 19% only on net assessable wealth above that line.

  • Unrealized appreciation is not assessed until realized — “on the sale of the property” (Codification III.B.4); pay on “the profit realized” (Bahá’u’lláh Q&A no. 8). A non-income heirloom collection need not be valued until sold ([66]).
  • Shares / non-cash assets: one “may find it disadvantageous … to pay nineteen percent of the value of the non-cash assets until they are disposed of,” deducting disposal costs first (UHJ [54]).
  • Losses then recovery = no second payment. Lose half (or all) and trade back up to the prior sum: “the Ḥuqúq is not payable” / “payment of Ḥuqúq is not required” (Bahá’u’lláh, Q&A no. 44 [36], no. 45 [37]). “If possessions decrease … Ḥuqúqu’lláh falls due again only after the loss has been made good and the total value … is augmented” (Codification III.B.5).
  • Compute on accumulation, not year-by-year in isolation: “computations … should be made on the basis of the accumulation of savings … Only in this way can one take account of losses in one year … or compute the profit or loss on the sale of an investment which was purchased in an earlier year” (UHJ [71]).

The “high-water mark” — three clarifications

The cleared base behaves like a permanent, upward-only ceiling on the wealth level you have ever cleared Ḥuqúq on. The clinching text is the total-loss case: even after a sum is “lost in its entirety” and later regained, “payment of Ḥuqúq is not required” (Q&A no. 45 [37]) — which rules out the rival “purified capital you still hold” model (that model would wrongly tax the regained money) and leaves the sticky-ceiling model.

  1. It is a level you’ve cleared, not “dollars ever owned.” It ratchets up when you clear a new high and does not ratchet down — even through total loss (Q&A no. 45). The benchmark is the figure Ḥuqúq was assessed on (e.g., clear $100k → pay $19k, retain $81k → benchmark = $100k, per ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s deduction method [48]).
  2. It only does work if the money actually leaves your assessable base. Spending purified wealth on consumption or exempt property (living, travel, residence, needful goods) genuinely lowers your holdings, so re-accumulation up to the ceiling is untaxed. Merely moving it into another investment doesn’t lower assessable wealth at all (and it stays purified) — there is nothing to rebuild and nothing newly owed.
  3. Deliberate spend-down then rebuild is at the edge of the explicit texts. The literal rulings cover involuntary losses (Q&A 44/45) and expenses exceeding income (III.B.5); a purposeful spend-down of a windfall follows the same principle, but with two guardrails: the anti-avoidance rule (don’t engineer lavish spending to dodge Ḥuqúq, [55]) and conscience + a Representative for structured cases ([65], [72]).

Reconstructing your cleared base from past payments

If you have paid Ḥuqúq over the years without keeping a running record of the cleared base, you can recover it from the payments themselves. Because each payment was 19% of an assessable increment and the increments accumulate to the peak:

Cleared base (high-water mark) ≈ (total Ḥuqúq ever paid) ÷ 0.19

Check against ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s own worked example [48]: he cleared 1000 + 1000 + 500 = 2500 and paid 190 + 190 + 95 = 475; and 475 ÷ 0.19 = 2500. ✓ The method self-corrects for past under-payment: comparing the recovered base to current wealth surfaces any catch-up genuinely owed — which is itself payable over time, conditional on ability ([34], [68]).

Worked answers to common cases (editorial synthesis)

Savings for a first home deposit. Assessable in principle — funds saved for a residence are “not in themselves exempt” — but you may either pay as you save (then the bought home is exempt) or defer inclusion until purchase, when the residence value becomes exempt (UHJ [71]). Discretion is yours; what is not allowed is treating not-yet-a-home money as already exempt while held liquid.

Selling the primary residence at a gain. While it is your residence: exempt (Q&A no. 8/42). Roll the proceeds straight into a new primary residence → in substance no Ḥuqúq falls due (the exemption carries across, [71]; no haste required, III.C.6). Keep the proceeds liquid → they enter assessable wealth (deduct selling costs, [54]); the realized gain is the clearest assessable component (III.B.4). Whether the whole proceeds or only the gain above original cost is assessable depends on whether the original purchase money was itself already cleared — an under-specified edge ([57]); a Representative of the Trustee can advise ([72]).

Cumulative-savings example ($1,000 → spend $400 → +$1,000 = $1,600). Pay on $600. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá: each year pay on what remains after expenses “and after the amount he paid Ḥuqúq on the previous year is also deducted” ([48]). Cleared base $1,000; current savings $1,600; assessable = $1,600 − $1,000 = $600. What you spent the $400 on does not matter here, because it came out of money already cleared — Ḥuqúq is paid once ([33]); spending taxed money is neutral (neither refund nor new charge). The necessity/luxury test matters one step earlier — when deducting expenses from income to derive each year’s surplus ([56]) — not when spending already-cleared capital.

Invest the surplus, then hold cash next year (e.g., clear $100k → retain $81k → invest all of it → $10k cash next year). Benchmark = $100k (the assessed figure).

  • Investment flat: assessable position = $81k asset (at cost, still purified) + $10k cash = $91k, below the $100k ceiling → nothing due; the $10k is just rebuilding toward the peak (not taxed in isolation).
  • Investment doubles to $162k, only $10k cash: the $81k unrealized gain is not assessed until realized (III.B.4; [54]) — binding, not optional. Realized/basis position = $81k + $10k = $91k < $100k → nothing due despite the $172k paper value. On sale for $162k you become liquid (~$172k) and pay 19% on the excess above the $100k ceiling = $72k (deduct selling costs first, [54]).
  • Whether to include non-liquid investments: the unrealized gain is not your choice — you simply don’t owe it until realized (III.B.4); the timing of paying on the underlying capital is your discretion — no haste to liquidate (III.C.6), may wait until disposal ([54]).

Couple vs. individual — whose ceiling?

The law is individual by default (Ḥuqúq attaches to property “indisputably recognised as being one’s own”, [58]), but a couple may elect to hold and pay jointly — “Freedom is left to each husband and wife to decide whether to comply with their obligations … jointly or separately,” because the House of Justice “does not wish to interfere with the right of each couple to decide how they wish to deal with their own finances” ([70]; [73]; Codification III.C.7). So the ceiling follows the ownership model chosen: separate finances → each spouse keeps their own high-water mark; joint → one combined pool, one combined ceiling. The couple is asked to “take counsel together and to define precisely the limits of their personal belongings” ([58]).

Worked example — a joint household, end to end (editorial synthesis)

Illustrative; numbers are representative. This walks a single-earner couple who treat all income and assets as jointly and equally owned (a homemaker spouse’s care of children and household being understood to enable the earner’s income) through a full annual reckoning.

Setup — the joint election. The couple deliberately adopt genuine joint ownership with right of survivorship: one combined pool, one combined cleared base, documented in writing and matched by title and will. This is fully within the freedom of [70]/[73]; it is a recognised option, not a workaround. Structurally it behaves like a joint tenancy — intact with the survivor on death, divided on divorce.

Step 1 — Recover the cleared base. Total every Ḥuqúq payment the household has ever made and divide by 0.19 (see Reconstructing your cleared base above). Suppose total paid = $19,000 → joint high-water mark = $100,000.

Step 2 — Pick an annual reckoning date. They choose 30 June (aligned to the Australian financial year, so records line up). Time and method are the individual’s discretion ([54], [64], III.C.6) — any consistent date is fine.

Step 3 — Tally assessable wealth at 30 June.

  • Include: cash in bank; investments at cost basis (unrealized gains deferred until sold, III.B.4).
  • Exclude — primary residence. Fully exempt (Q&A no. 8/42); the deposit and the principal paid down over the years have become exempt equity — money that legitimately left the assessable base into an exempt asset. Guarded only by the anti-lavishness rule ([55]).
  • Exclude — the mortgage too. Because the home is outside the frame, keep its loan outside as well; deducting a home loan against cash/investments while excluding the home would let the loan shelter other wealth. Deduct only non-residence debts (personal/business loans) against assessable wealth. (This specific netting isn’t spelled out in the texts — see the questions list below.)
  • Exclude — a needful family car bought outright, deemed “needful” by conscience ([56], [57]); a car used for work would be exempt as professional equipment ([41]). A luxury/collector vehicle held as a store of value would be included.
  • Deduct deductible living expenses and taxes (Codification III.B.2).

Step 4 — Compare and pay. If assessable wealth at 30 June exceeds $100k, pay 19% on the excess; if at or below, nothing is due this year. Then raise the cleared base to the new level. (For how appreciating but illiquid investments behave here, see the Invest the surplus case above.)

Step 5 — On the death of one spouse. Under genuine joint ownership the survivor continues with the same assets and the same $100k cleared base — the couple’s reading is that nothing “changes hands,” since both already owned the whole. This is a reasonable and arguably correct reading, but it is the single unlegislated point: [59] holds that property “passing from one person to another … through inheritance … becomes again subject to the payment of Ḥuqúqu’lláh.” Mitigations: document the joint ownership and align the will (e.g., a clause directing all assets to the survivor; sole-title items like a car made explicit). If a Representative holds that the deceased’s beneficial half does transfer, the consequence is bounded — Ḥuqúq on that half only, payable over time, never by forced liquidation ([68], III.C.6), with unrealized gains still deferred until the survivor actually sells (III.B.4). Either way the estate settles the deceased’s own outstanding Ḥuqúq first ([67]; III.B.8), and exempt items (residence/furnishings/tools) pass exempt ([52]).

Step 6 — On divorce (clean 50/50). Split the assets and the cleared base 50/50 — each ends with half the pool and half the high-water mark. A clean even division triggers no fresh Ḥuqúq: neither party acquires property from the other, each simply takes their pre-existing half (each then holds what is “indisputably their own”, [58]). The only wrinkle: if a settlement gives one party more than their half, the excess could count as property “passing from one person to another” and be re-assessable.

Questions for a Ḥuqúqu’lláh Representative

Points this concept leaves genuinely open, worth raising with a Representative of the Trustee (who can advise confidentially in light of specific circumstances, [72]):

  1. Mortgage on an exempt residence — keep both the home and its loan outside the calculation, or deduct the outstanding loan against other assessable wealth?
  2. Needful vehicle — is treating a genuinely needful family car as exempt acceptable, and where is the line between “needful” and a store-of-value vehicle?
  3. Joint ownership with survivorship — on the death of one spouse, does the deceased’s beneficial half “change hands” and become re-assessable, or does the survivor simply continue with the same cleared base ([59] vs. genuine joint ownership)?
  4. If it re-triggers on death — is the transferred share assessed at cost basis or current value, and confirmed payable over time without liquidating?
  5. Sole-name-for-tax assets — an investment held in one spouse’s sole legal name for tax reasons but beneficially jointly owned: how is it treated on death/transfer, and does titling affect the answer?
  6. Divorce — does a clean 50/50 settlement trigger any fresh Ḥuqúq, and is splitting the joint cleared base pro-rata correct?
  7. Reconstruction — is total-paid ÷ 0.19 an acceptable way to establish a cleared base, and how should years of pre-understanding under-payment be handled (catch-up scheduling under ability-to-pay)?
  8. Superannuation / locked retirement savings — is wealth you own but cannot access until preservation age assessable now, or only when it becomes available?
  9. Unrealized appreciation on long-held investments — confirm the deferral-until-realization treatment for a fluctuating portfolio, and the handling of selling costs.
  10. Annual reckoning date — any guidance on fixing a consistent date (e.g., 30 June) for the household’s calculation.

Open / refer to a Ḥuqúqu’lláh Representative

  • No official calculation formula will be issued — believers “work out their own methods on the basis of the texts” ([65]).
  • The law is progressive; novel cases (retirement accounts/superannuation, derivatives, crypto, business equity vs. working capital, the residence-sale cost-basis edge, the death-transfer treatment of a jointly-owned pool) are not directly ruled — use best judgement and consult a Representative, while responsibility remains personal ([72]).
