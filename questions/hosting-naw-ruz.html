How should a Bahá’í host or plan Naw-Rúz — at home or in the community?

Naw-Rúz opens the Bahá’í year as both a Holy Day and a feast under the Covenant — the crown of the months, the day when the breath of life is wafted over creation, and the termination of the Fast. Hosting it well means honouring what the law requires (work suspended where circumstances permit; gathering at a convenient hour) while expressing the day’s character: gratitude after restraint, renewal, hospitality, and praise. The House has not legislated a fixed programme; capacity, consultation, and the spirit of the verse determine whether the observance unfolds at a kitchen table or in a hall filled with neighbours.

What Naw-Rúz Is — and What It Is Not

The Badí‘ calendar places Naw-Rúz on 1 Bahá, the first day of the first month. Bahá’u’lláh ordains it at the close of the Fast:

“We have enjoined upon you fasting during a brief period, and at its close have designated for you Naw-Rúz as a feast.” — Bahá’u’lláh, Kitáb-i-Aqdas, ¶16

The sequence is deliberate: nineteen days of restraint in the month of ‘Alá’, then the feast that marks the year‘s turn. Naw-Rúz is therefore not an isolated holiday dropped into the Gregorian calendar; it is the hinge between abstention and abundance, the day on which the community steps from the Fast into the new year’s rhythm.

Bahá’u’lláh names its stature in another passage:

“Happy the one who entereth upon the first day of the month of Bahá, the day which God hath consecrated to this Great Name. And blessed be he who evidenceth on this day the bounties that God hath bestowed upon him; he, verily, is of those who show forth thanks to God through actions betokening the Lord’s munificence which hath encompassed all the worlds. Say: This day, verily, is the crown of all the months and the source thereof, the day on which the breath of life is wafted over all created things.” — Bahá’u’lláh, Kitáb-i-Aqdas, ¶111

The verbs matter for hosts: evidenceth bounties, show forth thanks through actions. Naw-Rúz observance is outward-turning gratitude expressed in deed, not only in sentiment. A host who provides good cheer after a disciplined Fast has read the calendar correctly.

Naw-Rúz is also among the nine Holy Days on which work is forbidden — a provision of TheFrameworkOfLaw. Shoghi Effendi, in guidance repeated by the Universal House of Justice, affirms that believers should make every effort to have their right to these days recognised by employers, though they are not required to forfeit employment if refused. The binding constant is suspension of work where circumstances permit; gathering may occur at any convenient hour during the Bahá’í day (sunset to sunset).

Since Naw-Rúz 172 (2015), the worldwide implementation of the Badí’ calendar has fixed its astronomical determination: Ṭihrán serves as the reference point for computing the vernal equinox, and Naw-Rúz falls on the day the sun enters Aries — even should that occur one minute before sunset. Communities consult the calendar issued by the Universal House of Justice for the exact Gregorian date each year; it may fall on 20, 21, or 22 March. Planning therefore begins with the official calendar, not with local secular new-year customs.

Naw-Rúz is not Ayyám-i-Há (which precedes the Fast with gift-giving and hospitality but carries no work suspension) and it is not Riḍván (the twelve-day King of Festivals centred on Bahá’u’lláh’s Declaration). Its tone is renewal and thanksgiving after discipline rather than historical commemoration of a single dramatic event — though the day’s sacred character is no less real.

The Obligations — Work, Gathering, and What Remains Open

Three layers govern planning.

Work suspension. On Naw-Rúz, believers with independent businesses should refrain from operating; those in employment should seek leave on religious grounds. Bahá’í-owned enterprises in which non-Bahá’ís are employed remain under the obligation to close. The principle is conscience: institutions entirely managed by Bahá’ís enforce the law; individuals do their utmost without forfeiting livelihood when employers refuse.

Gathering. Unlike the Twin Holy Days, where both work suspension and gathering are explicitly required on each of two consecutive days, Naw-Rúz carries the standard Holy Day pattern: work forbidden, and the friends may meet at any time during the day they find convenient. No fixed hour is prescribed.

Programme. The House has confirmed that reciting Tablets of Visitation is not mandatory at anniversary commemorations. Communities develop devotional content — prayers, readings, music — as interpretive practice. The absence of prescribed liturgy is freedom for hosts who might otherwise defer planning until an institutional template appears.

A letter written on behalf of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá notes that in some cultural settings work might be suspended on a few days beyond Naw-Rúz itself during the new-year period; such extension would be preferable though not binding. Hosts in diaspora communities need not import multi-day closures from other cultures, but they should not be puzzled if some friends remember Naw-Rúz as the opening of a longer festive season. The law’s core remains one Holy Day.

Hosting at Home

A family or household can observe Naw-Rúz fully without a community event. The day’s requirements do not scale with headcount.

Preparation before sunset. Because the Bahá’í day begins at sunset, the night preceding Naw-Rúz is already part of the Holy Day; work during that period is forbidden. Households with school-age children or shift workers should plan leave and schedules around the sunset boundary, not only around breakfast on the Gregorian calendar date.

Breaking the Fast‘s final hours. The last day of the Fast ends at sunset on the eve of Naw-Rúz. Families who have fasted should mark that transition deliberately — a simple meal after sunset, prayers of gratitude, perhaps the passage from the Kitáb-i-Aqdas on the crown of the months. The feast of Naw-Rúz begins in continuity with the Fast’s conclusion, not as a separate secular party the next afternoon.

The home gathering. A modest observance might include: obligatory prayers or devotional readings; a shared meal whose hospitality extends beyond the nuclear family; conversation about the year ahead and service goals; children’s participation in setting the table or selecting readings. Tone is radiant and thankful, not solemn in the manner of the Ascension anniversaries.

Hospitality outward. Naw-Rúz‘s feast character invites opening the home — a neighbour recently isolated, a seeker who has attended children‘s classes, an elder who might otherwise eat alone. The host need not stage an elaborate banquet; presence and welcome fulfil the law’s spirit of evidencing bounty through action.

Mixed-faith households. The Naw-Rúz observance belongs to the Bahá’í calendar; family members of other backgrounds may be invited to share in the meal and atmosphere without the event being reframed as a generic spring celebration stripped of its religious meaning. Consultation within the household prevents either suppression of the Holy Day or unnecessary tension over symbols.

Planning in the Community

Where several believers share a locality, consultation — often through the Local Spiritual Assembly or an ad hoc committee — coordinates observance without prescribing a single template.

Timing. Because gathering may occur at any convenient hour, communities sometimes hold an evening devotional on the Holy Day’s opening night (after sunset), a daytime picnic on the Gregorian date, or a brunch that accommodates families. Multiple modest gatherings are not inferior to one large event; isolated believers who cannot travel should be offered a ride or a home visit rather than being told that only the central programme counts.

Scale and venue. A cluster with capacity might rent a hall, organise a potluck in a park, or combine Naw-Rúz with a brief report on community activities for the new year. A group of five friends in a living room fulfils the same obligation set. Field practice (Tier F) in many regions has found that Naw-Rúz gatherings with music, children’s activities, and simple historical framing about the calendar attract seekers without requiring the event to be rebranded as outreach.

Institutional rhythm. Naw-Rúz opens the Bahá’í year; in many jurisdictions it is followed in the coming weeks by the election of Local Spiritual Assemblies. Some communities use the Naw-Rúz gathering to remind the friends of electoral procedures and the spiritual posture required for Bahá’í elections — not to campaign, but to orient hearts toward the year’s institutional duties. This is practical coordination, not a liturgical requirement.

Calendar education. New believers and children often encounter Naw-Rúz before they grasp the Badí‘ structure. A brief explanation — nineteen months of nineteen days, Intercalary Days, Fast, Naw-Rúz — helps participants see the day as architecture, not accident. The Universal House of Justice‘s 2014 letter on calendar implementation notes that adopting a new calendar in each dispensation reshapes humanity’s perception of time; hosts who explain that reshape participation.

Employer recognition. Communities with experience sometimes collect guidance for believers seeking Holy Day leave, especially when Naw-Rúz falls on a workday. A letter from the Assembly confirming the religious character of the day has helped in some workplaces. The effort is collective solidarity, not a guarantee of employer compliance.

Devotional Content and Atmosphere

No mandatory readings exist. Common choices drawn from Tier A and B sources include:

  • The Kitáb-i-Aqdas passages on Naw-Rúz and the Fast’s termination (¶16, ¶111).
  • Prayers from Bahá’í Prayers expressing gratitude and praise.
  • Passages from Days of Remembrance associated with the new year where available.
  • The Universal House of Justice‘s annual Naw-Rúz message, read aloud or summarised, to connect the household or community with the worldwide Bahá’í family’s orientation for the year ahead.

Music and song are appropriate to a feast day; silence and reflection are equally appropriate for smaller groups. The standard is sincerity, not production value.

The Framework for Action describes Holy Day commemorations — their prayers, stories, and songs — as stirring the heart and filling the soul with awe. Naw-Rúz‘s awe is tied to renewal: the community that has fasted together and now feasts together has lived the calendar’s meaning in the body, not only in the intellect.

Practical Planning Checklist

Tier F orientation; not institutional prescription.

  1. Confirm the date from the official Badí’ calendar for your year.
  2. Arrange work leave early; note sunset-to-sunset boundaries for shift workers.
  3. Decide venue and scale in consultation — home, shared meal, community hall.
  4. Assign logistics — food, childcare, transportation for isolated friends.
  5. Prepare devotional content — short, accessible, joyful.
  6. Extend invitations beyond the core community where appropriate.
  7. Connect to the year ahead — service plans, institute campaigns, without turning the feast into an administrative meeting.
  8. Document for learning — brief reflection after the event on what strengthened fellowship, for the community’s own growth.

Points friends often raise

“Naw-Rúz is a cultural spring festival we may celebrate whenever convenient.” It is fixed by astronomical rule and is a Holy Day with work suspension; postponing observance for weekend convenience contradicts the calendar law unless the community is recognising the correct Bahá’í day boundary.

“We need an Assembly-sponsored programme or the day is not observed.” A family meal with prayers after the Fast satisfies the day’s spirit; institutional events are enrichment, not validity.

“Naw-Rúz is only for Persian culture.” The law is universal; cultural expressions of hospitality vary, but the feast and Holy Day belong to all Bahá’ís.

“Children are too young for Holy Day leave logic.” Children do not suspend employment, but they participate in the feast and learn the calendar; their joy is part of evidencing bounty.

“The Fast ends at breakfast on Naw-Rúz morning.” The Fast ends at Naw-Rúz‘s beginning — sunset on the preceding Bahá’í day.
