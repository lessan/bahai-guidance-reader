What is the difference between the Bahá'í concept of "society-building" and the secular development paradigm?

The two share a vocabulary — development, progress, capacity, empowerment — and a sincere wish for the material betterment of populations. They differ in the layer beneath the vocabulary: in their account of the human being, in the locus of agency they assume, and in the metric by which they judge that progress has actually occurred. The Bahá'í position is that civilisation-building is a religious vocation operating bottom-up through the transformation of relationships and patterns of life, with the population as protagonist of its own development. The secular development paradigm, in its dominant twentieth-century forms, treats development as a sectoral activity performed by experts on recipient populations, measured in quantitative outcomes. The two are not symmetrical alternatives. The secular paradigm has done immense good and the Bahá'í community works alongside it without polemic. But the two operate on different doctrinal assumptions, and conflating them empties the Bahá'í contribution of the very thing that makes it distinctive.

The doctrinal ground: civilisation as religious vocation

Bahá'u'lláh grounds civilisation-building in the purpose of creation itself. He writes that "All men have been created to carry forward an ever-advancing civilization," and that "the betterment of the world can be accomplished through pure and goodly deeds, through commendable and seemly conduct." Civilisation-building is not an addendum to the Faith; it is part of what the Faith is.

'Abdu'l-Bahá's Secret of Divine Civilization (1875) develops the same teaching as a treatise, addressed anonymously to the people of Persia at a moment of confusion between blind imitation of the West and blind adherence to inherited custom. He writes:

"The happiness and greatness, the rank and station, the pleasure and peace, of an individual have never consisted in his personal wealth, but rather in his excellent character, his high resolve, the breadth of his learning, and his ability to solve difficult problems."

The treatise's central move is the integration of intellect and moral virtue: material progress without spiritual foundation collapses, while inherited tradition without science ossifies. Where wealth is to be praised, 'Abdu'l-Bahá says, it is praiseworthy because "expended for the welfare of society" — never as an end in itself. Civilisation, on this account, is the cultivated public expression of the soul's own capacities.

Shoghi Effendi draws out the structural implication. In The World Order of Bahá'u'lláh he names the principle of the oneness of mankind as "the pivot round which all the teachings of Bahá'u'lláh revolve" and writes:

"It is no mere outburst of ignorant emotionalism or an expression of vague and pious hope… Its implications are deeper, its claims greater than any which the Prophets of old were allowed to advance… It implies an organic change in the structure of present-day society, a change such as the world has not yet experienced."

The Guardian's exposition fixes the key claim: oneness is not a sentiment to be promoted alongside other development goals but a structural reality that reorders the relationships through which any society is built.

The contemporary vocabulary: "the society-building power of the Faith"

The phrasing the Universal House of Justice now uses to draw out this doctrinal ground for the present age is "the society-building power of the Faith." It denotes not a programme but an emergent capacity — what arises when the institute process, the cluster framework, and the three dimensions of endeavour (community-building, social action, contribution to the discourses of society) operate together over sustained time in a population.

The phrase enters UHJ vocabulary prominently in the Riḍván 2010 message, which observes that "once human resources in a cluster are in sufficient abundance, and the pattern of growth firmly established, the community's engagement with society can, and indeed must, increase." It is developed in 28 December 2010 and consolidated in the 30 December 2021 message launching the Nine Year Plan, which reframes the architecture doctrinally:

"Within clusters, expansion and consolidation, social action, and contributing to prevalent discourses are dimensions of a single, unified, outward-looking endeavour carried out at the grassroots of society… the release of the society-building power of the Faith."

The verb release is the operative one. The power is understood as latent in Bahá'u'lláh's Revelation and in the community's coherent pattern of life; the work of the Plan is to release what is already there, not to construct a programme from outside. Riḍván 2022 ratifies the formulation, reporting that "the efforts of the friends to build communities, to engage in social action, and to contribute to the prevalent discourses of society have cohered into one global enterprise."

The unity claim that organises the difference

The pivotal axiom is Bahá'u'lláh's. He writes:

"The well-being of mankind, its peace and security, are unattainable unless and until its unity is firmly established." (World Order of Bahá'u'lláh, p. 203, citing the Tablet of Maqsúd)

The conjunction "unless and until" is what distinguishes the Bahá'í case. Unity is not an instrument the development process applies to achieve other goals; it is constitutive of the goal. The Universal House of Justice, in its 2 March 2013 letter to the Bahá'ís of Iran, draws out the structural meaning of this axiom for contemporary social life: the principle of the oneness of humankind "asks not merely for cooperation among people and nations. It calls for a complete reconceptualization of the relationships that sustain society." The Bahá'í International Community's 1995 statement The Prosperity of Humankind — prepared at the Bahá'í World Centre for the UN World Summit for Social Development — is the document that brings these threads together for the international development discourse, opening with Bahá'u'lláh's axiom and arguing that genuine prosperity is impossible without the structural reordering that oneness implies. As a BIC statement (Tier D, institutional non-authoritative) Prosperity applies and translates; the doctrinal weight is borne by the Word and its authoritative interpretation.

The contrast: what the secular development paradigm assumes

The secular development paradigm is not a single thing — it includes modernisation theory, dependency theory, structural adjustment, the human-development index, the capabilities approach, the Sustainable Development Goals — and within it many practitioners hold positions much closer to the Bahá'í view than to the caricature. With that caveat, four assumptions are recurrent across the dominant strands:

  • Top-down deployment of capital, expertise, and projects to solve problems identified by external agencies. The expertise resides in the agencies; the population receives it.
  • Quantitative outcomes as the metric of success: GDP per capita, school enrolment, child mortality, women's labour-force participation. What can be counted is what is measured; what is measured is what is improved.
  • The population as beneficiary, not protagonist. Aid recipients are the objects of intervention, not the agents of their own development; the agency-architecture treats consultation with them as good practice, not as the locus where the work is actually done.
  • Material progress as the proper unit of analysis, with spiritual or moral content treated as a private cultural matter outside the development process.

The paradigm has produced real and durable goods — vaccination programmes that have eliminated diseases, literacy campaigns that have raised hundreds of millions out of functional illiteracy, infrastructure that has saved lives. The Bahá'í community participates in many such efforts and the BIC engages constructively with the SDG framework. The point of contrast is not that secular development fails; the point is that on its own terms it cannot reach what the Bahá'í view names as the deeper task.

The Bahá'í alternative: bottom-up cultivation, the population as protagonist

Where the secular paradigm deploys, the Bahá'í community cultivates. The 20 October 1983 UHJ message establishing the Office of Social and Economic Development is explicit on this point: progress "will largely depend on natural stirrings at the grass roots, and it should receive its driving force from those sources rather than from an imposition of plans and programs from the top." The grassroots remain the source; institutions exist to nurture and accompany, not to generate.

Three structural differences follow.

Locus of agency. The Bahá'í framework names three protagonists — the individual, the institutions, and the community — operating together. The population is not the recipient of development but the agent of it. The institute process exists precisely to raise up the capacity of every member of a population to read its own social reality and act on it.

Metric of success. The Bahá'í community measures progress by the transformation of relationships and patterns of life — the depth of community life, the quality of consultation, the number of children's classes and junior youth groups operating in coherence, the population's capacity to sustain its own progress. Quantitative outcomes (literacy, health, school attendance) typically follow, but they are signs of the work, not its objects. The 26 November 2012 Department of the Secretariat letter, which transmitted the OSED Social Action paper, refuses to treat social action as something separate from community-building and insists on its grounding in Bahá'u'lláh's teachings and on protagonist-led local design.

Anthropology. Behind the methodology sits a different account of the human being. The secular paradigm, in its dominant strand, treats the human being as a bearer of preferences whose welfare is to be improved through the rational allocation of resources. The Bahá'í view treats the human being as a soul in a moral universe, possessing latent capacities the work of development is to release, and oriented toward a "two-fold moral purpose" — to attend to one's own spiritual and intellectual growth and to contribute to the transformation of society. The two anthropologies generate different methodologies the same way different blueprints generate different buildings.

Field expression: FUNDAEC and the alternative paradigm

Tier D scholarship locates the alternative in concrete form. Farzam Arbab, founder of FUNDAEC in Cali, Colombia, in 1974, articulated the conceptual case in The Process of Social Transformation (1987) — neither modernisation theory nor dependency theory will serve, because both presuppose a materialist anthropology. FUNDAEC's Sistema de Aprendizaje Tutorial (SAT), now serving more than 25,000 students across the Americas, organises secondary education around mathematics, science, language, technology, and service to the community — the curriculum is rigorous and secular in its content while Bahá'í in its conception of the learner. Sona Farid-Arbab's Moral Empowerment: A Framework for Education for Development (2016) sharpens the theoretical statement: the question is not "what should be in the curriculum?" but "what is the nature of the learner?" Erin Murphy-Graham's longitudinal ethnography of SAT in Honduras (Opening Minds, Improving Lives, 2012) supplies the empirical case that the programme's distinctive results — measurable shifts in women's domestic decision-making, gender attitudes, civic participation — derive precisely from its moral content, not despite it.

Boundary line: complementary, not adversarial

The Bahá'í community does not present its alternative in adversarial terms. Bahá'í-inspired organisations partner with states, NGOs, universities, and UN bodies; the BIC contributes constructively to the SDG framework; individual Bahá'ís work professionally across the development sector. The 26 November 2012 letter is explicit that social action is not political advocacy and not a substitute for the secular work of governments and agencies. The distinction is doctrinal, not territorial: where the work involves the kind of transformation Bahá'u'lláh names — the unity of humanity as constitutive ground, the population as protagonist, the integration of material and spiritual progress — the Bahá'í framework supplies what the secular paradigm cannot. Where the work is the technical delivery of goods, both frameworks coexist without competition.
