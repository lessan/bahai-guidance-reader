What is a learning site, and why is it called a “site” rather than a “centre”?

A learning site is a cluster (or sub-cluster) selected by the International Teaching Centre, in consultation with the Counsellors and the National Spiritual Assemblies concerned, to serve as a focused locus where particular learning is generated and propagated about a specific aspect of cluster work — most often the junior youth spiritual empowerment programme, increasingly the integration of social action with community-building, and the operation of advanced clusters at and beyond the third milestone. The vocabulary is precise. A centre would suggest a fixed seat of authority that exports learning to dependents; a site names a place where learning occurs, generated within the cluster's own work and made available to sister clusters as one peer to another. The word preserves the cluster-as-protagonist framing that underlies the entire learning mode of the present era.

Where the term is established

The Universal House of Justice's Riḍván 2010 message names the operative reality without yet using the noun: a core activity — the junior youth programme — has, in several clusters scattered across the globe, given rise to “the capacity… to each sustain over one thousand junior youth in the programme,” and the Office of Social and Economic Development was therefore “establishing a network of sites in all continents… that can be used to provide training to coordinators from scores upon scores of clusters.” The same message issues the call: “we are calling for 32 learning sites, each serving some twenty clusters with full-time coordinators, to be in operation by the end of the current Plan.” (Riḍván 2010, paragraphs 153.17–18.) From that point forward the term is settled vocabulary. By the 30 December 2021 message inaugurating the Nine Year Plan, the House writes plainly of “a site for the dissemination of learning about the junior youth spiritual empowerment programme” and refers to “the learning site” as a working designation: collaboration “between the learning site and associated institutes has already proved extremely fruitful, and it should intensify; their pursuit of a common goal and their shared desire to see clusters advance create ideal conditions for the spirit of cooperation and mutual assistance to flourish.”

The International Teaching Centre's Insights from the Frontiers of Learning (2013) elaborates the concept on the field side, drawing out how a learning site functions in practice — the curriculum-stage seminars, the coordinator-tutor accompaniment chain, the propagation outward through sister clusters. Insights sits at the conferred-authority tier as elaboration of the UHJ frame; the Riḍván 2010 and 30 December 2021 messages remain the authoritative source.

Why “site” and not “centre”

The choice of word is doctrinal, not stylistic. Three distinctions are at stake.

A centre is a seat. A site is a place where something happens. A centre in administrative usage names a fixed locus distinguished from its periphery — a national centre, a regional centre, a centre of authority. The word implies an asymmetric relationship in which the centre is the producer and the periphery the recipient. A site by contrast names a location where work is done, ground that has been made fertile by the labour of those upon it. The learning site does not generate learning for other clusters; it generates learning about a question that other clusters are also working on, and shares what it has found. The asymmetry is in subject matter, not in standing.

The protagonist remains the cluster. The framework of the present era treats every cluster as a protagonist — “a rising spirit of universal participation in the work of community building,” in the language of the 30 December 2021 message. A learning site is a cluster doing concentrated work; its cluster status, and the protagonism that goes with it, is unchanged by the designation. To call it a centre would imply that the ITC or the National Assembly had elevated it above its peers; “site” preserves the relation of equality.

Sites can move; centres tend to be fixed. Riḍván 2010's call was for 32 learning sites; the 30 December 2021 message reports that Counsellors have arranged for institutes to be organized into “groupings of varying sizes” — a configuration whose geography is not static. As learning matures or moves into a new question, new sites are designated and earlier ones are reabsorbed into the broader life of their cluster's institute. A centre, by contrast, would acquire institutional permanence — staff, premises, prerogatives — that the work itself does not require. The contrast here is not between administration and its absence; institutes, Counsellors, Auxiliary Board members, and Regional Bahá’í Councils are all present in learning-site work. The contrast is between modes of administration — between an extractive arrangement in which a centre exports learning downward and a consultative one in which a site shares learning peer-to-peer. Learning mode and administrative dimension, in the texts, are presented as complementary, not opposed.

The same logic governs cognate vocabulary across the Plan: gatherings rather than conferences, campaigns rather than missions, cycles rather than quarters. Each pairing rejects the more administrative noun in favour of the more living one. The community is not a hierarchy of centres serving peripheries; it is, in the Riḍván 2026 phrase, an “earnest conversation” extended “to ever-widening circles” by friends who happen, in particular places, to be working on particular questions intensively.

What a learning site does

Operationally, a learning site is doing four things at once. First, the cluster sustains the activity that has occasioned its designation — most commonly a junior youth programme involving hundreds or thousands of young people across many neighbourhoods and villages. Second, the friends in the cluster document what they are learning — what works, what fails, what surprises — through reflection meetings, written notes, and conversations with visiting friends. Third, the cluster receives coordinators and resource persons from associated clusters who come to learn by working alongside, returning home to apply what they have observed. Fourth, resource persons from the learning site travel to associated clusters, accompany them on the ground, and convey back to the learning site what is learned in those settings — so that the learning loop is two-directional, not a monologue.

The 31 December 2025 message names this fourth element directly when it places resource persons “from the learning site” alongside Auxiliary Board members and representatives of the Regional Bahá’í Council or National Growth Committee in the “gatherings for consultation, periodically convened by the institute”. The learning site's people move; its insights move; its institutional weight does not accumulate. That is precisely what distinguishes it from a centre.

Beyond the JY programme: an extending pattern

The first generation of learning sites, designated under the Five Year Plan 2011–2016, focused entirely on the junior youth programme — the area where field experience had outrun received practice fastest. The 30 December 2021 message extends the pattern: alongside JY learning sites, the message envisions analogous concentrations of learning for third-milestone clusters generally, where the question is no longer how to scale a single programme but how an entire population is served from childhood to adulthood through one coherent educational path. The Riḍván 2026 message confirms that “third milestone clusters are proving to be fertile ground for rich experience to be harvested and propagated” — language that extends the learning-site logic to advanced clusters as a class. The same message adds a complementary observation: such clusters are increasingly understood as cultivating “a way of life patterned on the divine teachings,” and the propagation of that — not the propagation of a programme — is what the next phase of the Plan will require learning sites to support.

The taxonomy will likely keep extending. Concentrations of learning around social action, around participation in prevailing discourses of society, or around the integration of work in neighbourhoods of high receptivity in fragmented urban contexts are all candidate areas. The grammar is the same: a cluster doing intensive work on a specific question, designated as a place where others can come and learn, propagating what is learned outward without becoming an institutional centre.

Distinguishing nearby terms

A few words about what a learning site is not.

A learning site is not a training institute. The training institute is a permanent infrastructure of an NSA (or a Regional Bahá’í Council under it) charged with the institute process across an entire territory. A learning site might host an institute's seminars; it does not replace the institute. A learning site is not a cluster in a special class — its cluster status, including its position on the continuum of milestones, is unchanged by designation. A learning site is not a local Mashriqu'l-Adhkár site — though some of the clusters where local Houses of Worship have been designated (e.g. Tanna, Norte del Cauca) are also long-standing learning sites; the two designations recognise different qualities of the same maturity. And a learning site is not a project with a beginning and an end; it is a designation that holds for as long as the cluster is generating learning others can use, and is reabsorbed, without ceremony, when that ceases.

The vocabulary, in the end, is faithful to the work. A community that learns by doing, with no fixed seat of expertise and no permanent dependence of one place on another, requires a noun for those places where the doing has been most intensive. Site is that noun. Centre would have meant something else, and that something else is not what the work is.
