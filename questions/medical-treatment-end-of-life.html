What does Bahá’í teaching say about medical treatment and end-of-life decisions?

Bahá’í teaching affirms both competent medical care and the sovereignty of God over life. Believers are required to seek treatment when ill and to follow physicians' instructions; they are also taught that ultimate healing belongs to God. On end-of-life decisions — euthanasia, assisted suicide, and withdrawal of life support — the Universal House of Justice has not yet legislated; until it does, conscientious judgment by those responsible is the guidance. The general principle stated is that God, the Giver of life, alone disposes of it as He deems best.

The obligation to seek medical treatment

The Kitáb-i-Aqdas directs believers to consult competent physicians in times of sickness. ’Abdu’l-Bahá states plainly:

"It is incumbent upon everyone to seek medical treatment and to follow the doctor's instructions, for this is in compliance with the divine ordinance, but, in reality, He Who giveth healing is God."

— ’Abdu’l-Bahá, Selections from the Writings of ’Abdu’l-Bahá

The tier structure is clear: medical means are obligatory; divine healing is ultimate. The two are not rivals. In Some Answered Questions, ’Abdu’l-Bahá teaches that healing proceeds through material and spiritual means, and that both should be used.

Bahá’u’lláh, in a Tablet to a physician, writes:

"Do not neglect medical treatment when it is necessary, but leave it off when health has been restored. Treat disease through diet, by preference, refraining from the use of drugs; and if you find what is required in a single herb, do not resort to a compound medicament.… Abstain from drugs when the health is good, but administer them when necessary."

— Bahá’u’lláh, Tablets of Bahá’u’lláh (Tablet to a physician)

The passage counsels moderation — prefer diet where sufficient, avoid unnecessary medication — but it does not authorise refusal of care when care is needed. End-of-life medicine falls within the same logic: treatment that relieves suffering and serves the patient's condition is consistent with the Writings; neglect of available care is not.

’Abdu’l-Bahá also instructs that even a physician who is himself ill must consult another competent doctor rather than rely solely on self-treatment. Humility before medical expertise is part of the ordinance.

Bahá’í communities have historically supported medical institutions and public health — from clinics in rural areas to collaboration with physicians in times of epidemic. The attitude toward science in Bahá’u’lláh and the New Era is representative: sciences that benefit mankind are to be esteemed and promoted. End-of-life medicine is not an exception to that esteem. Hospice physicians, palliative nurses, and pain specialists serve the same ordinance as surgeons: competent care in time of sickness.

Diet, drugs, and proportion in treatment

Bahá’u’lláh’s preference for diet over compound drugs when a single remedy suffices counsels proportion — neither refusal of modern medicine nor uncritical acceptance of every intervention. At life's end, this may mean accepting pain relief that carries sedation while declining a futile experimental protocol. The believer asks what each treatment accomplishes for comfort, for consciousness, and for dignity, in consultation with those who know the medicine.

’Abdu’l-Bahá’s teaching that spiritual healing and material healing are compatible invites prayer alongside treatment — for the patient, by the family, and by the community — without substituting prayer for morphine when morphine is what mercy requires.

Life, death, and the soul's journey

Medical decisions at the end of life gain their proper weight from the Bahá’í account of death. The soul does not end with the body; it "will continue to progress until it attaineth the presence of God." Death is a passage, not extinction — developed in soul-and-life-after-death. This doctrine does not make bodily life unimportant; the body is the temple the soul inhabited, and Bahá’í burial law treats it with dignity. It does mean that the believer approaches terminal illness with a horizon beyond the immediate medical prognosis.

Prayers for the dying and for the deceased, acts of service in their name, and the Prayer for the Dead before burial connect medical endings to spiritual continuity. The family and community sustain the person spiritually while physicians address the body.

The Kitáb-i-Aqdas designates the Prayer for the Dead as the one congregational obligatory prayer — recited by one believer while others stand in silence, before interment for those over fifteen. Terminal care therefore opens onto a defined community duty: friends ensure that when death comes, the law of prayer and burial is ready to be observed, not improvised under duress.

Euthanasia and assisted suicide — forbidden in principle, conscience in application

The Universal House of Justice has not enacted legislation on euthanasia or physician-assisted suicide. In a letter of 23 July 1985, written on behalf of the House to a Local Spiritual Assembly, the guidance states:

"In general, our Teachings indicate that God, the Giver of life, can alone dispose of it as He deems best. The Universal House of Justice has found nothing in the Sacred Text about the matter of withholding or removing life support in disabling or terminal illnesses where intervention prolongs life. Therefore, until such time as the House of Justice considers legislation on these matters, it is left to the conscience of the individual concerned whether or not to subscribe to a 'living will.'"

— Universal House of Justice (on behalf), 23 July 1985 — Family Life

Several points follow. Active measures to end life — euthanasia, assisted suicide — stand on the side of human disposal of life that the general principle restricts to God. The Bahá’í condemnation of suicide applies by extension: the believer does not plan or request assistance to end life prematurely. Withholding or withdrawing treatment is not legislated; the Texts do not define which interventions are obligatory and which may be declined. Living wills are a matter of individual conscience until the House of Justice rules otherwise.

National Spiritual Assembly handbooks summarise the same position for euthanasia and for decisions to turn off life-support equipment in particular circumstances: God's prerogative over life is affirmed; detailed application rests with those responsible — patient, family, physicians — in consultation.

Societies that legalise assisted suicide place believers in a distinctive civic position. The believer does not participate in ending life by prescription or provision, and does not counsel others to do so. The same obedience to government that requires tax payment does not require availing oneself of every legal option; spiritual law forbids what civil law permits. Pastoral care for a believer facing pressure to end life early parallels suicide-and-community-response: medical care, accompaniment, and clarity that the Faith forbids the act while embracing the person.

Discerning treatment, comfort care, and prolongation

Where the House of Justice has not drawn lines, believers and families reason from principle and from medical counsel. The following distinctions help, though they are interpretive rather than legislative.

Treatment that aims at cure or meaningful recovery aligns with the obligation to seek medical care. Refusing such treatment without medical reason conflicts with the divine ordinance to consult physicians.

Treatment that prolongs biological function without consciousness or comfort raises the questions the 1985 letter leaves to conscience. Many believers, in consultation with physicians and family, decline aggressive intervention when medicine offers no prospect of recovery — not to end life, but to cease artificially prolonging dying. That choice is not equivalent to euthanasia in Bahá’í terms, though civil law and hospital policy vary on how such decisions are documented.

Palliative care — pain relief, nursing support, hospice services — serves dignity and comfort and is fully consistent with the Writings. Bahá’u’lláh’s counsel to treat disease and relieve suffering does not expire at the terminal stage.

The believer should consult physicians who can explain what a proposed intervention accomplishes, and should consult family members who will bear the consequences of the decision. Consultation among those affected is not optional etiquette; it is the method the Writings prescribe for difficult collective choices.

Hospital ethics committees, chaplaincy services, and second medical opinions are lawful resources. The Bahá’í does not treat institutional medicine as adversary; the Writings direct resort to competent physicians. Disagreement with a physician's recommendation is itself a reason for further consultation — another physician, a family conference — not for isolated decision.

Pain at the end of life deserves explicit attention. Undertreated pain is a failure of the medical ordinance; overtreatment that obliterates consciousness without relieving suffering is equally a matter for physician and family to correct. Comfort is not abandonment of the soul's journey; it is honour for the body that carried the soul.

Advance directives and family conversation

Because living wills are left to conscience, believers who wish to record their preferences may do so, typically in legal forms recognised in their jurisdiction and in letters to family and the Local Spiritual Assembly. Clear communication before crisis reduces the burden on relatives who might otherwise guess at the patient's wishes.

Advance planning should address:

  • Desire for competent medical treatment and pain relief.
  • Preferences regarding resuscitation, ventilation, and other extraordinary measures if prognosis is terminal.
  • Bahá’í burial requirements — no cremation, burial within one hour's journey, Prayer for the Dead for believers over fifteen.
  • Organ donation if desired, as in organ-donation-and-autopsy.

Wills are often read after medical crises; letters of instruction and family conversation carry the practical weight.

The community and the Local Spiritual Assembly

Terminal illness in the community mobilises the same pastoral care as any severe test. Friends visit, assist with practical needs, pray with and for the person and family, and respect the intimacy of the family's medical decisions. The Assembly does not direct clinical choices; it ensures that Bahá’í burial requirements are known and, where relatives may not observe them, tactfully advocated.

When disagreement splits a family — one child urging every intervention, another urging comfort care — the Assembly may facilitate consultation without taking sides in medical judgment. Its concern is unity, truthfulness, and observance of Bahá’í law at death.

Friends may read prayers at the bedside when the family welcomes it. They should not intrude on intimate moments or treat the dying person as an occasion for teaching. Presence, quiet service, and willingness to sit without speaking often serve better than words.

Children, elders, and proxy decisions

When the patient cannot decide — dementia, unconsciousness, legal incapacity — proxy decision-makers carry a heightened burden of consultation. Bahá’í law does not designate a particular family member as medical guardian; civil law and family consultation determine who speaks. The proxy should ask what the patient would have wanted when competent, what medicine can still offer, and what burial law will require afterward. caring-for-aging-parents develops honour toward parents in decline; the same consultation patterns among siblings apply when life-sustaining treatment is in question.

Relation to suicide and mental health

End-of-life decisions must not be confused with suicide. The person in terminal illness who declines a fourth course of chemotherapy is not in the same category as the person in reversible depression who seeks death. suicide-and-community-response develops the community's duty toward mental health crisis: professional care, accompaniment, and absence of stigma. Depression in the seriously ill is common and treatable; palliative psychiatry is part of medical care.

Pastoral caveats

Do not treat "God's prerogative" as a reason to refuse all intervention. The Writings require medical treatment when necessary; leaving a treatable person untreated is not piety.

Do not impose one believer's conscientious choice on another. Until legislation exists, families may legitimately reach different conclusions about the same medical facts. Consultation and respect, not censure, are the community's tools.

Do not delay burial planning. Medical endings are followed by burial law; the community should know the believer's wishes before death.

Do not assume every believer will face the same end-of-life path. Home death, hospital death, sudden death, and prolonged decline each carry different medical and pastoral demands. The principles — seek care, honour God's prerogative, consult, prepare burial — apply throughout; the particulars require local wisdom.
