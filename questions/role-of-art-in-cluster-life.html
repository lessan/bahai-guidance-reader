What does the guidance say about the role of art, music, and creative expression in cluster life?

Art, music, and creative expression are not peripheral to cluster life — they belong to community-building from the start. Bahá'u'lláh ordains music as "a ladder for your souls, a means whereby they may be lifted up unto the realm on high" (Kitáb-i-Aqdas, ¶51). 'Abdu'l-Bahá calls music "the food of the soul and spirit" and describes its power to uplift (Promulgation of Universal Peace, p. 52; importance-art [20]). In mature cluster settings, the Universal House of Justice writes that "the arts as a whole, so integral a part of the development of a community from the start, stand out in such settings as an important means of generating joy, strengthening bonds of unity, disseminating knowledge, and consolidating understanding, as well as of acquainting those in the wider society with the principles of the Cause" (30 December 2021).

Art in Devotional Life

Devotional gatherings are definitional cluster activities. The first milestone is reached when "in whatever combination and however small in number, devotional gatherings, children's classes and junior youth groups are being maintained by those progressing through the sequence of institute courses" (28 December 2010). These gatherings are not purely verbal occasions: music, poetry, chant, and creative performance belong in how communities draw near to God together.

'Abdu'l-Bahá's utterances on music give artistic expression an affective power that rational discourse alone does not carry — "Through the power and charm of music the spirit of man is uplifted" (Promulgation, p. 52). A gathering enriched by music or beautiful verse cultivates a spiritual atmosphere that sustains reflection. As the cluster develops, devotional life ideally grows in richness and variety, incorporating local artistic traditions and individual gifts in ways that make the gathering hospitable to friends, neighbours, and seekers.

Art in the Junior Youth Programme

The JuniorYouthSpiritualEmpowermentProgramme is where artistic expression is most systematically woven into institute process — though this is Tier F Ruhi pedagogy, not Writings-tier doctrine. Ruhi Book 5 (JY animator training) treats drama, poetry, visual art, and music as activities that "can influence an educational process greatly" and that carry cultural values animators must help youth align with moral education. Book 5 warns that unsuitable pursuits can end up "inadvertently imposing values that contradict, in subtle ways, the educational process" in which junior youth are engaged. Ruhi Book 7, Unit 3 (Promoting the Arts at the Grassroots) extends this pattern to children's classes, junior youth groups, and study circles at the neighbourhood level.

The UHJ's 26 November 2012 letter on the junior youth programme names spiritual empowerment, moral perception, and agency — but does not prescribe a particular arts methodology. In practice, when a junior youth group writes and performs a short play or composes verses about service, the Ruhi materials frame this as enriching learning and expressing ideals, not as a substitute for systematic study of the texts.

Art in Community-Building and Public Discourse

Plan-era guidance on the arts is continuous, not novel. The 21 April 1996 Riḍván message already asked the friends to "give greater attention to the use of the arts, not only for proclamation, but also for the work in expansion and consolidation" — a call that prompted the Research Department's importance-art compilation. The 30 December 2021 message describes what this looks like in receptive neighbourhoods and villages where community-building activities engage large numbers: "Uplifting, well-prepared community gatherings—extending in some cases to camps and festivals—occur with increasing frequency, and music and song feature prominently on such occasions."

Art rooted in a transformed inner life can explore spiritual themes across differences of background and belief. A letter written on behalf of Shoghi Effendi conveys the Guardian's view that "Art can better awaken such noble sentiments than cold rationalizing, especially among the mass of the people" (importance-art [68]). Music at public gatherings, murals through neighbourhood collaboration, theatrical pieces developed in junior youth groups and shared with families — these are field patterns consistent with the 2021 description, not a separate agenda from community-building work.

The Quality Criterion: What Art Is Called to Be

Tier A — Writings. 'Abdu'l-Bahá draws the primary distinction: "All things are beneficial if joined with the love of God; and without His love all things are harmful" — a melody "sweet to the ear, bringeth the very spirit of life to a heart in love with God, yet staineth with lust a soul engrossed in sensual desires" (Selections §154.3). Bahá'u'lláh warns against making music "as wings to self and passion" (Aqdas ¶51). Arts, crafts, and sciences that "uplift the world of being, and are conducive to its exaltation" are the standard Ruhi Book 5 cites for junior youth animators.

Tier A (via memoir). 'Abdu'l-Bahá described art as "a gift of the Holy Spirit" whose gifts "are fulfilling their highest purpose, when showing forth the praise of God" (Lady Blomfield, The Chosen Highway; importance-art [22]).

Pastoral draw-out (KB synthesis). Authentic art can engage suffering, injustice, and moral complexity; the Writings' test is orientation of the heart — whether love of God transforms the work — not whether difficulty is excluded. The importance-art compilation gathers these Tier A passages; its vehicle is Tier C, but the criterion within is spiritual purpose, passion, and uplifting effect, not a violence-or-degradation taxonomy.
