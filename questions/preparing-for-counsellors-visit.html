How does a cluster prepare for a Counsellors' visit?

A Counsellors' visit is not an inspection. It is an occasion for the friends of a cluster to learn together with someone whose vantage is continental and whose accompaniment is global. The cluster prepares by readying its experience — the recent stories, the honest statistics, a clear reading of where the work stands; by readying its participants — the friends most active in core activities, the Area Teaching Committee, the institute coordinator, the Auxiliary Board member, the Local Spiritual Assembly; and by readying its questions — the matters on which it most needs to consult.

What a Counsellor is — and what the visit therefore is

The character of the preparation is set by the character of the institution. The Institution of the Counsellors, the Universal House of Justice's 1 January 2001 document on the Continental Boards of Counsellors, names the distinctive feature of the role plainly:

The existence of the institution of the Hands of the Cause, and subsequently of the Counsellors, comprising individuals who play such a vital role in advancing the interests of the Faith, but who have no legislative, executive or judicial authority, and are entirely devoid of priestly functions or the right to make authoritative interpretations, is a feature of Bahá’í administration unparalleled in the religions of the past.

— Universal House of Justice, The Institution of the Counsellors (1 January 2001)

The same document quotes the Constitution of the Universal House of Justice on the legal foundation of the Boards:

The institution of the Boards of Counsellors was brought into being by the Universal House of Justice to extend into the future the specific functions of protection and propagation conferred upon the Hands of the Cause of God. The members of these Boards are appointed by the Universal House of Justice.

— Constitution of the Universal House of Justice, cited in The Institution of the Counsellors

A Counsellor cannot direct an Area Teaching Committee, cannot legislate for a Local Spiritual Assembly, cannot pronounce on the cluster's reading of its own experience. The Institution of the Counsellors states the division plainly at the national level — the same logic of counsel versus executive authority that governs cluster life:

The National Assemblies make all the necessary executive decisions and take responsibility for their implementation. The Counsellors bring to their functions a continental perspective which, when offered to the Assembly in the form of counsel, advice, recommendations, suggestions or commentary, enriches the latter's understanding, acquaints it with a broader experience than its own, and encourages it to maintain a world-embracing vision.

— Universal House of Justice, The Institution of the Counsellors (1 January 2001)

The weight of a Counsellor's presence is the weight of conferred rank — the House of Justice's appointment — and of the accumulated wisdom that comes from accompanying many clusters across many continents. The visit is therefore consultative in form, educative in purpose, and companionate in tone. A cluster that prepares as if to be inspected has misread the institution.

The same document names the substantive content of what the Counsellors do, which sets the agenda for what a visit is most likely to draw out:

Counsellors have the duty to stimulate on each continent the expansion and consolidation of the Faith and to promote the spiritual, intellectual, and social aspects of Bahá’í life. The spiritual health of the community and the vitality of the faith of the individual, the strengthening of the foundations of family life, and the study of the Teachings receive special attention from the Counsellors and those called upon to assist them.

— Universal House of Justice, The Institution of the Counsellors (1 January 2001)

These five clauses — expansion and consolidation, spiritual and vital health of community and individual, family life, study of the Teachings — are the axes along which a Counsellor will read a cluster. Preparation that has those axes in view has prepared well.

The 28 December 2010 message to the Counsellors' Conference frames what this accompaniment looks like in practice. The Counsellors and their auxiliaries are to assist the community in building on its achievements — extending the mode of learning that has come to characterise its teaching endeavours. Of the Auxiliary Board members in particular, the same message writes that they should stand shoulder to shoulder with the friends, supporting them through their struggles and partaking in their joys; that all require "support and encouragement, offered not in the abstract but on the basis of that intimate knowledge which is only acquired by working side by side in the field of service." A Counsellor's visit is one occasion within that ongoing relationship — not a substitute for it.

Readying the experience

The specific counts and story numbers below are field-practice synthesis drawn from R.O.G. accounts and cluster experience, not prescriptions in any UHJ letter. They represent what clusters have found useful when a Counsellor's accompaniment is to land on honest ground.

The cluster's experience is presented in two intertwined registers — narrative and statistical — and the Counsellor's accompaniment lands best when both are ready and honest.

The narrative register is what R.O.G. 36 calls stories from the field: a Bellas Vistas neighbourhood in Madrid, a vegetable-garden project in Matunda Soy, tutor teams in Sydney whose Hyde Dunn and Clara Dunn Initiatives led to four new programmes of growth. These are not anecdotes; they are how a Counsellor reads what is happening. R.O.G. 36 was itself prepared in the wake of the December 2015 Counsellors' Conference precisely because the texture of cluster life is conveyed in stories that a number cannot tell. The cluster preparing for a visit should ready three or four such stories, drawn from the previous one or two cycles, that genuinely express what is moving and what is stuck.

The statistical register is honest counting. A Malaysian national junior youth coordinator told R.O.G. 26 that "if she were to build strong relationships with the animators, she would have to go from being a 'statistician' to being a friend." A coordinator who has become a statistician in that sense has lost the relationship with the friends she or he serves — but a coordinator who avoids statistics has lost the cluster's grip on its own reality. The cluster preparing for a visit should arrive with the institute pipeline counted (Books 1, 5, 6, 7 active; new tutors, animators, children's-class teachers in the past year), the four core activities counted by neighbourhood and sector, and the receptivity patterns plain. R.O.G. 35's Sanniquellie account — "as we consulted, we realized that our goals did not match the available human resources. To devise more realistic plans, we needed to establish a system to collect more reliable statistics" — describes exactly the statistical readiness a cluster should bring.

Readying the participants

The visit is convened with the cluster's own institutional architecture. The Institution of the Counsellors describes a now-established practice at country and regional level — members of several institutions and agencies of a country, or a region thereof, coming together in consultation:

A highly fruitful practice has developed in many parts of the world whereby members of a number of institutions and agencies of a country, or a region thereof, come together in a meeting of consultation to reach a common vision for the growth of their community and discuss strategies for action. These "institutional meetings" help to steer the friends away from thinking merely in terms of the mechanics of projects and to infuse their plans and subsequent action with the spirit of the Faith.

— Universal House of Justice, The Institution of the Counsellors (1 January 2001)

That passage names national and regional consultations among Auxiliary Board members, National Spiritual Assemblies, Regional Councils, and Regional Teaching Committees. The same logic operates, scaled down, when a Counsellor visits a cluster: the cluster's agencies convene in advance to reach a common reading of where the work stands, rather than each presenting a separate account. The specific convening described below is field practice synthesised from R.O.G. accounts — notably R.O.G. 19's Bangui account, in which the cluster institutions met, updated their statistics, and organised for broad participation before a resource person's arrival (an illustrative analog to Counsellor-visit preparation, not a Counsellor protocol).

Preparing the visit means convening the cluster's triad of agencies — the training institute coordinator (and the children's-class, junior-youth, and main-sequence sub-coordinators where they exist), the Area Teaching Committee, and the resident Auxiliary Board member (with assistants) — together with the Local Spiritual Assembly of the cluster's central locality, in advance, to agree what the cluster wants to bring to the consultation. The agenda is jointly drafted; the participants are jointly briefed; the friends most active in core activities — tutors, animators, children's-class teachers, neighbourhood-based families — are invited as participants in their own right, not as exhibits.

A pastoral note. The friends should be present as themselves, in their service. The cluster that briefs participants to perform — to highlight the successes and conceal the struggles — has already weakened the visit's value. R.O.G. 23 records the Harris County (Houston) ATC secretary saying, "We started about a year ago to learn how to learn. We wanted to be students of the Five Year Plan by studying the guidance and constantly acting, reflecting, consulting, and then acting again within the framework of the Plan." That is the posture: students of the guidance, sharing what they have learned and what they have not yet learned.

Readying the questions

The specific number of questions named below is editorial synthesis from field practice, not a textual prescription. What the institution requires is that the cluster take responsibility for the consultation's substance.

A Counsellor's accompaniment lands on the cluster's actual learning edge only if the cluster has named where that edge is. The triad's pre-meeting consultation should produce two or three questions on which the cluster most needs counsel — not generic questions about the Plan, but the specific consultative questions live in the cluster now.

These will look different in different clusters. An emerging cluster's questions might centre on how to widen the institute pipeline beyond the original nucleus of friends, or how to follow through with the families that have hosted children's classes for a cycle. A cluster crossing the second milestone might bring the question of how the reflection meeting now needs to be re-organised — sectors? a unified gathering? — as core activities have multiplied. A cluster well past the third milestone might bring the questions R.O.G. 36 traces in Bellas Vistas and Matunda Soy: how a community-building pattern stretches into social action, conversations about marriage and family, meaningful conversations with civic actors. Naming the questions in advance is the cluster's way of taking responsibility for the consultation rather than waiting to be told what to consult on.

Aftermath

A Counsellor's visit produces its harvest only if the cluster integrates what was learned. The visit is not the resolution of a stuck question; it is a moment in an ongoing accompaniment. The Riḍván 2010 message names a cultural shift in how the friends understand their service together:

This evolution in collective consciousness is discernable in the growing frequency with which the word "accompany" appears in conversations among the friends, a word that is being endowed with new meaning as it is integrated into the common vocabulary of the Bahá’í community.

— Universal House of Justice, Riḍván 2010

The 28 December 2010 letter to the Counsellors' Conference then asks the Counsellors and their auxiliaries to build on the community's achievements by extending to other spheres of operation the mode of learning that has come to characterise its teaching endeavours. Accompaniment continues between visits through the resident Auxiliary Board member and the assistants she or he has appointed — the Auxiliary Boards, as The Institution of the Counsellors states, "do not themselves constitute a decision-making body." The cluster's task after the Counsellor's visit is to translate the consultation into the next reflection meeting's planning — what was offered as encouragement, what was offered as caution, what was offered as a question the cluster itself should now consult through. The Counsellor's words are most fruitful when the cluster reads them as material for its own consultation, not as a pronouncement that closes one.

What to avoid

Three patterns weaken a Counsellor's visit, drawn from the institution's own reading of itself and from the field record.

First, staging the cluster — choreographing the visit so that the Counsellor sees only the strongest activities and meets only the most fluent friends. The Counsellor's vantage depends on seeing what is actually there. A cluster that hides its struggles deprives itself of the counsel it most needs.

Second, surrendering the cluster's agency — treating the Counsellor as the answer to a question the cluster itself must consult through. The Counsellor cannot, and is not constituted to, decide for the cluster. Where the visit becomes a place to off-load the responsibility for a difficult consultation, the cluster has misread the architecture.

Third, closing the consultation when the visit ends. The Counsellor leaves; the work continues. The Auxiliary Board member and assistants are the ongoing channel; the next reflection meeting is the next occasion at which the cluster takes up what was learned.
