What is the role of the Local Spiritual Assembly in the cluster era — and how is that role evolving?

The short answer is that the Local Spiritual Assembly‘s role in the cluster era is not diminished but focused. Cluster agencies — the Area Teaching Committee, the training-institute coordinators, and the Auxiliary Board members and their assistants — have absorbed much of the operational work of expansion and consolidation that the LSA used to carry alone, and that absorption has freed the Assembly to attend more deliberately to its pastoral, spiritual, and community-anchoring functions. The 28 December 2010 message of the Universal House of Justice describes the resulting collaboration as one in which the Assembly’s relationship with the Area Teaching Committee and the training institute “gradually becomes cemented in a common purpose” — the two working alongside each other in shared work rather than one over the other.

Where the LSA stands

The LSA is not a recent administrative invention. Bahá’u’lláh ordains it in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas: “The Lord hath ordained that in every city a House of Justice be established wherein shall gather counselors to the number of Bahá, and should it exceed this number it doth not matter.” (¶30). Nine members, locally elected each Riḍván, the local arm of the Administrative Order — and, in ‘Abdu‘l-Bahá’s Will and Testament, “in all countries a secondary House of Justice must be instituted,” the body that paralleling the National and the Universal completes the elected tier. The LSA‘s warrant is therefore divine, not constitutional; the Bahá‘í community in a locality does not create the Assembly — it elects the body Bahá’u’lláh has already established. The 1972 Constitution of the Universal House of Justice later gave the architecture its formal description: a single Administrative Order “of a series of elected councils, universal, secondary and local,” the LSA being the local instance.

Shoghi Effendi gave that revealed institution its working form. His letter of 12 March 1923 set out the substance of its work: “To it all local matters pertaining to the Cause must be directly and immediately referred for full consultation and decision.” All local matters — teaching, deepening, the Nineteen Day Feast, marriages and funerals, the local fund, relations with civic authorities, “the helping hand to the poor, the sick, the disabled, the orphan, the widow” — were to converge on the LSA. From 1922 to roughly 1996, the Assembly was the central operational body of Bahá’í life in its locality, carrying virtually every dimension of community-building work itself.

What changed in 1996

The 26 December 1995 message calling for a worldwide network of training institutes, and the 9 January 2001 message that introduced the cluster as the unit of planning, re-shaped the field-level architecture. Cluster-level agencies — the Area Teaching Committee, the cluster coordinators of children’s classes, junior youth and the main Ruhi sequence, and the assistants of the Auxiliary Board — became the operational engines of the programme of growth. The work the LSA had once carried single-handedly was now distributed across a wider set of institutions. Two unhealthy reactions appeared, and have to be named: an LSA that tries to absorb all cluster work duplicates the agencies and exhausts itself; an LSA that withdraws from cluster work entirely surrenders its formative function. The 28 December 2010 message addresses both: “Evolving relationships among administrative structures have brought the Local Spiritual Assembly to the threshold of a new stage in the exercise of its responsibilities to diffuse the Word of God, to mobilize the energies of the believers, and to forge an environment that is spiritually edifying.” The threshold language is precise — the Assembly is not displaced; it is being asked to step forward.

The focused role

What the cluster era has done, in practice, is to return the LSA to a more concentrated set of functions. Five are visible in the field record and the UHJ guidance.

Spiritual nurturing of the local community. The Nineteen Day Feast, Holy Day commemorations, devotional life and the deepening of the believers — these remain the LSA‘s, and the cluster agencies do not touch them. The 28 December 2010 message names the test: an Assembly‘s strength “must be measured, to a large extent, by the vitality of the spiritual and social life of the community it serves.” Meeting regularly with neat minutes is no longer the criterion; whether the locality’s spiritual life is alive is.

Marriage, divorce, and pastoral care. Bahá‘í marriage, the registration of divorce, Bahá’í burial, and the counsel of believers in difficulty remain LSA work. A 28 September 1941 letter on behalf of the Guardian frames the pastoral office in terms that have not aged: believers should turn to the Assembly “in the strictest confidence” and “as a child would to its parents.” Cluster agencies do not stand between believer and Assembly here.

Local funds and finance. The local fund, local property, the disbursement of resources and the cultivation of contributions — administrative bedrock that no other body in the cluster carries.

Engagement with civic authorities and external relations. The LSA, where it has acquired civil personality, represents the Bahá‘í community to local government, registers Bahá’í marriages with the state, and maintains the relationships from which a Bahá‘í voice in the locality’s public life will eventually emerge.

Collaboration with the cluster agencies. The 28 December 2010 message describes a rural-cluster Assembly beginning “to consult as a body on one or two specific issues with immediate relevance to the life of the community” — the letter then names four such issues: the devotional character of the village, the spiritual education of the children, the empowerment of junior youth, and the home visits among the friends. “As the Assembly consults on such tangible matters and learns to nurture the process of growth lovingly and patiently,” the letter continues, “its relationship with the Area Teaching Committee and the training institute gradually becomes cemented in a common purpose.” This is the relationship’s hinge sentence. The ATC and the LSA do not occupy the same office: the ATC is an agency of the NSA (or RBC where one exists), reporting upward; the Assembly stands on its own divinely-ordained ground. They work together because they serve the same community — not because either commands the other.

How the role is still evolving

The Riḍván 2015 message names a destination. In an advanced cluster, “people are coming to regard the Local Spiritual Assembly as a radiant source of wisdom to which they too can turn for illumination.” The Assembly that attains this is not the busiest one but the one whose pastoral and consultative voice the believers and the wider community trust.

The 30 December 2021 message opening the Nine Year Plan pushes the role further. As clusters mature toward the third milestone, the message describes settings in which “a significant proportion of the population of a particular area is taking part in community-building activities” — particular neighbourhoods and villages in which, in the same letter‘s words, “a release of the society-building power of the Faith” becomes visible. In such settings, the same message records, “Local Spiritual Assemblies strengthen their relationships with authorities and local leaders, even entering into formal collaborations,” and the Assembly‘s role becomes more visible too: the body that connects this society-building work to the lawful, formal life of the Bahá‘í community — marriages, the Feast, holy days, the fund, the local Bahá’í voice in civic life. In third-milestone clusters, the integration of community life with the prospect of a local Mashriqu‘l-Adhkár brings the LSA’s pastoral and stewardship functions into newly sharp relief.

The pattern is not displacement and is not absorption. It is complementarity. The cluster era has lifted direct campaign-planning from the LSA‘s shoulders; the LSA’s task is to use that lifted weight to attend to what only it can do — the spiritual life of its community, the pastoral care of its believers, and the patient consultation that, over years, makes the locality a place the wider population recognises as a source of light.
