What is the role of the Auxiliary Board member and the Auxiliary Board assistant in advancing the Plan?

Auxiliary Board members are appointed by the Continental Boards of Counsellors for renewable five-year terms to serve, within an assigned region, on one of two Boards — for the Protection or for the Propagation of the Faith. Auxiliary Board assistants, in turn, are appointed personally by Auxiliary Board members at the cluster level, for shorter terms set by each Continental Board. Neither role carries administrative authority. The mode is consistently catalytic — to stimulate, encourage, accompany — and in the cluster era both ranks function as the closest institutional companions of the friends, the institute process, and the Cluster as they advance the Plan.

Two distinct Boards, established under Shoghi Effendi; assistants added in 1973

Shoghi Effendi brought the Auxiliary Boards into being in two stages during the 1950s — first as deputies of the Hands of the Cause, and then in a dual structure so that one Board on each continent would serve the protection of the Faith and a second Board would serve its propagation. The dual structure has continued since 1968 under the Counsellors. The Constitution of the Universal House of Justice (1972) gives it statutory form: in each zone “there shall be two Auxiliary Boards, one for the protection and one for the propagation of the Faith,” whose members “shall serve under the direction of the Continental Boards of Counsellors and shall act as their deputies, assistants and advisers.”

A pivotal extension came in 1973, when the Universal House of Justice authorised Auxiliary Board members themselves to appoint assistants. The 2001 document The Institution of the Counsellors is precise about the designation: “The correct designation is ‘assistants to the Auxiliary Board members‘ and not ’assistants to the Auxiliary Board‘. An assistant is appointed by an Auxiliary Board member to work in a specified area and functions as an assistant only in relation to that area.” The appointment is therefore personal and local, not corporate — and that small grammatical insistence carries a real institutional weight: the assistant’s accountability runs to the Board member who named her, in the cluster she has been asked to accompany, and not to a board sitting elsewhere.

ABM at regional scale; assistant at cluster scale

The two ranks operate at different geographical grains. An Auxiliary Board member is appointed for a defined territory — typically a sub-region of a country, or sometimes an entire small country — and accompanies several clusters within it. An assistant works at the cluster level, often in a single cluster, sometimes in two adjacent ones. Together they are the means by which continental vision becomes regional accompaniment, and regional accompaniment becomes the household-scale presence that cluster work requires. Assistants — unlike Board members themselves, whose role is incompatible with administrative service — may simultaneously serve on Local and National Spiritual Assemblies, Regional Councils, and committees; this is the principal practical difference between the two ranks beyond geography.

The mode is catalytic — not legislative, executive, or judicial

The character of the role is the most important thing to grasp about it. The House of Justice’s Institution of the Counsellors (1 January 2001) names it directly, in a passage that has been treated as definitive by subsequent UHJ correspondence:

The existence of the institution of the Hands of the Cause, and subsequently of the Counsellors, comprising individuals who play such a vital role in advancing the interests of the Faith, but who have no legislative, executive or judicial authority, and are entirely devoid of priestly functions or the right to make authoritative interpretations, is a feature of Bahá’í administration unparalleled in the religions of the past.

That sentence governs the work of the Auxiliary Boards as it governs the Counsellors who direct them. An ABM cannot direct an LSA; an assistant cannot instruct an Area Teaching Committee. The 2001 document is explicit on the point: the Auxiliary Boards “do not themselves constitute a decision-making body.” What ABMs and assistants can do is the work that has no other institutional carrier: to encourage, to read conditions accurately, to stimulate without compelling, to accompany in a manner that releases capacity rather than supplying it from above. The 28 December 2010 message of the House of Justice describes the texture of that work in the cluster era. Speaking of the Auxiliary Board members and their assistants who help the friends visualise the first milestone of growth, it says: “They should stand shoulder to shoulder with the friends, supporting them through their struggles and partaking in their joys.” The same passage names “unqualified love free of paternalism” as indispensable to the work — a posture that leaves the agency of the cluster’s progress with the friends themselves.

Carrying guidance into the cluster

Practically, the ABM and the assistant are the means by which the spirit and content of UHJ guidance reaches the cluster‘s pattern of action. A Counsellor’s reading of a region‘s conditions, the House of Justice’s most recent message, the lessons distilled in the International Teaching Centre‘s Reflections on Growth — these reach the cluster not as documents posted on a wall but as conversations: with the institute coordinator, with the ATC, with the LSA, with the animator who is wondering whether her junior-youth group is drifting. The ABM and her assistants visit reflection meetings; they study guidance with cluster agencies; they counsel LSAs on the developmental questions the agencies are facing; they encourage the friends in the cluster’s own learning. The 2001 document anticipated this integration: “Intimate involvement in institute operations is a part of the evolving functions of Auxiliary Board members.”

In healthy clusters the relationship is companionate. ABMs and assistants are seen, by the friends and by the cluster‘s agencies, as fellow learners further along the path — as accompanists in a shared enterprise — rather than as supervisors. That posture is not a matter of personality; it is what the absence of legislative, executive, and judicial authority structurally produces, when it is honoured. Where it is not honoured — where an assistant begins to direct an ATC, or an ABM begins to second-guess an LSA — the relationship sours and the cluster’s own capacity is set back. The pastoral mode is the work, not its packaging.

Under the Nine Year Plan

The Universal House of Justice‘s 30 December 2021 message launching the Nine Year Plan addressed the Conference of the Continental Boards of Counsellors directly, and named the cascade — Counsellors, Auxiliary Board members, their assistants — as the principal accompanists of the Plan‘s central aim. The goal the message set is “to establish intensive programmes of growth in all the clusters in the world” — what it calls “this formidable objective” — to be advanced “in the course of the Nine Year Plan.” The ABM cascade is the means by which that ProgramOfGrowth objective becomes a workable practice in any one of the tens of thousands of localities involved. Each cluster’s path is its own; each is accompanied by an ABM and the assistants she has appointed; each is learning, through the institute and the cycle, how to carry the work itself.

The role, in short, is catalytic, pastoral, and carrying — neither directive nor passive. It is the rank that makes a Counsellor‘s continental vision visible to a junior-youth animator on a particular street, and that makes an animator’s discovery legible to the Counsellors when they next meet at Haifa.
