What guidance applies to serving on non-Bahá’í boards and committees?

Bahá’ís are not called to withdraw from the institutions of civil society. The Guardian instructs believers to distinguish diplomatic and political posts from purely administrative functions — and to welcome the latter as opportunities to serve the nation’s highest interests in an unselfish, patriotic spirit. That principle extends to voluntary service on school boards, hospital foundations, professional associations, charities, neighbourhood councils, and nonprofit governing bodies. Individual believers working as volunteers, professionals, and experts contribute to social change as a normal part of Bahá’í life — the constructive civic register developed in ReligionInTheLifeOfSociety (§4.4). What the guidance excludes is partisan alignment: using a board seat to advance a political party, organising the Bahá’í community behind a factional campaign, or lending the Faith‘s name to a contest for power. Service on a non-Bahá’í board is permissible and often laudable when the body‘s aims are constructive, its methods lawful, and the believer‘s participation does not compromise non-partisanship or Bahá’í administrative duties.

Civic engagement, not withdrawal

The Bahá’í prohibition on partisan politics is frequently misread as withdrawal from society. The opposite is the case. Shoghi Effendi warns against “the other extreme of never taking part, with other progressive groups, in conferences or committees designed to promote some activity in entire accord with our teachings.” A letter written on his behalf gave, as an example, conferences promoting better race relations — activities Bahá’ís should join, not shun.

The Universal House of Justice, in guidance on social action, affirms that Bahá’ís are committed to efforts toward social transformation and that “service to others and to society is a hallmark of the Bahá’í life.” Obedience to government and non-involvement in partisan politics are described not as obstacles to social change but as aspects of an approach that addresses root causes through unity, consultation, and lawful means.

spiritual-conquest-vs-political-non-involvement develops the positive register: the Faith‘s advance is spiritual and social, not a capture of partisan power. bahai-in-social-justice-movements draws the line between constructive participation and factional alignment. Board service sits comfortably on the constructive side when the body‘s purpose is education, health, poverty relief, environmental stewardship, professional standards, or community welfare — and when the believer serves as an individual citizen or professional, not as an emissary of the Bahá’í institutions. Teaching through example, not partisan advocacy, is the mode the Writings prescribe in such settings.

Administrative service versus political office

The controlling distinction from The World Order of Bahá’u’lláh is between posts that are “diplomatic or political” and those that are “purely administrative in character” — the latter “under no circumstances affected by the changes and chances that political activities and party government, in every land, must necessarily involve.”

National Spiritual Assemblies determine which local posts fall on which side of the line. The general pattern: civil-service employment, professional regulation, academic governance, hospital administration, and charitable trusteeship are administrative. Holding elected office whose occupancy is determined by partisan competition, serving as a party official, or publicly campaigning for a party’s platform is political and excluded (bahai-and-partisan-politics).

School-board service illustrates the nuance. In some jurisdictions school boards are non-partisan bodies concerned with education policy; in others they are overt battlegrounds for party-aligned agendas. The believer asks whether the role is administrative service to children’s welfare or a partisan platform — and consults the National Spiritual Assembly when the answer is unclear.

Voluntary boards, NGOs, and professional bodies

The Riḍván 2006 message celebrates the “hundreds of thousands of local, national and international organizations engaged in some form of social action” emerging across the planet. Bahá’í institutional projects are one stream; individual initiative is another. The 23 December 2008 letter observes that believers work “as volunteers, professionals, and experts” to contribute to social change, avoiding conflict and contest for power while seeking “underlying moral and spiritual principles and… practical measures.”

Serving on the board of a literacy charity, a community health foundation, a professional engineering society, or a neighbourhood development corporation fits that description when the organisation’s aims are constructive and its methods lawful. The believer brings trustworthiness, consultation, and service-orientation to the table — the same character bahai-owned-business-conduct requires in commerce:

“Every business company should be established on divine principles. Its foundations should be trustworthiness, piety and truthfulness in order to protect the rights of the people.” — ‘Abdu’l-Bahá, Trustworthiness

Trusteeship of a charitable endowment is a form of stewardship. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá describes representatives of the people — trustees in the social sense — as bearing responsibility for the weak, the sick, and the poor. A board member who administers funds honestly, refuses self-dealing, and seeks the welfare of beneficiaries serves that vision.

The Prosperity of Humankind statement (BIC, Tier below UHJ legislation) frames economic and social organisation as capacity-building for a global civilisation. Board service that strengthens schools, clinics, cultural institutions, or environmental protection contributes to that horizon without requiring the believer to adopt partisan means.

What institutions may and may not do

Bahá’í institutions — Local and National Spiritual Assemblies, Regional Bahá’í Councils — do not govern the private civic choices of believers. They do not appoint members to non-Bahá’í boards or direct individual trusteeship. The 23 December 2008 letter and subsequent House of Justice guidance distinguish activities institutions may support explicitly from those where individuals have latitude:

“A further distinction can be drawn between those activities that are fully in keeping with the teachings and that can be supported explicitly by Bahá’í institutions and those where the situation is less clear, in which Bahá’í institutions should not participate but in which individuals can be given some personal decision to take part, without in any way implying that they are representing the Faith directly by their choice.” — Universal House of Justice, 23 December 2008 — Messages from the Universal House of Justice, 2001–2022

A devotional meeting or a Bahá’í-initiated tutoring centre may be institutionally sponsored; membership on a hospital foundation board is an individual matter. The believer does not announce the board seat as a Bahá’í institutional project or use Bahá’í community resources to fund a non-Bahá’í organisation’s partisan activity.

The House of Justice has stated that Bahá’ís should not organise to take part in demonstrations against a government — or any government — while leaving individuals free to pursue lawful means of social improvement. The parallel for boards: the Assembly does not organise the community to join a partisan advocacy organisation; an individual may serve on a non-partisan board promoting education or health.

Organising versus serving

Several distinctions keep board service within guidance.

Individual versus collective Bahá’í action. The believer serves as a citizen or professional. The Local Spiritual Assembly does not pass resolutions endorsing a non-Bahá’í NGO, allocate Bahá’í funds to it, or recruit Feast attendees to its board unless the organisation’s work is an explicit, non-partisan institutional collaboration approved through Bahá’í channels.

Non-partisan purpose versus partisan vehicle. A foundation dedicated to clean water serves all people; a foundation that functions as the fundraising arm of a political party does not. Co-option — when a just principle becomes attached to one faction — is the live risk named in the 2008 letter (bahai-in-social-justice-movements).

Lawful methods versus civil disobedience. Board service operates through lawful governance. The believer who serves on a regulatory body reports corruption through proper channels (whistleblowing-workplace-integrity); the believer does not direct the organisation toward lawbreaking as strategy.

Consultation when uncertain. If a believer harbours doubt about a board’s character, guidance should be sought from the National Spiritual Assembly, which evaluates specific circumstances.

Conflicts with Bahá’í life

Board service is voluntary; Bahá’í administrative obligations are binding. A believer elected to a Local Spiritual Assembly or appointed to an Area Teaching Committee has primary duties to the Bahá’í institutions of the Cause. National guidance encourages Auxiliary Board members to remain free for their appointed work; the same principle of prioritisation applies by wisdom to any believer whose board commitments would chronically conflict with Feast, Holy Days, cluster activities, or institutional office.

Financial conflicts require transparency. A trustee who profits from contracts the board awards violates the standard that Bahá’ís in public trust “stain not their fair character through acts of bribery and fraud.” Service on a board that exists primarily to benefit the trustee’s commercial interests is not service.

Teaching on a board is example-first. A trustee who uses meetings to pressure declarations or who makes religious identity the foreground of every deliberation mistakes the mode bahai-conduct-in-workplace identifies. Excellence, honesty, and care for stakeholders are the witness.

Discourses, expertise, and the board room

The third dimension of the community‘s endeavour — contribution to the discourses of society — often flows through the same professional standing that qualifies believers for board service. A physician on a hospital board, an educator on a curriculum committee, an architect on a planning commission, and a lawyer on a legal-aid foundation each carry specialised knowledge the Bahá’í writings encourage believers to place at society‘s disposal. The 23 December 2008 letter affirms that Bahá’ís may contribute to social change “as individual professionals, avoiding conflict and the contest for power while seeking underlying moral and spiritual principles and for practical measures.”

Board deliberation is itself a form of consultation in the broad human sense — airing views in search of a decision — and the believer practises the Bahá’í discipline of consultation — courtesy, truthfulness, and detachment from ego — while participating. The trustee who listens before speaking, who cites evidence rather than factional talking points, and who seeks the welfare of the whole organisation rather than a narrow interest models the consultative spirit without invoking Bahá’í terminology in a secular setting.

Writing for publication, speaking at professional conferences, and serving on standards committees extend the same principle beyond the board table. bahai-engagement-with-society develops the discourses dimension at length. Board service is one venue among many where expertise meets service.

International and interfaith contexts

Bahá’í institutions participate in certain international and interfaith bodies through the Bahá’í International Community and national external-affairs arrangements. Individual believers may also be invited to serve on interfaith councils, UNESCO national commissions, or international NGO boards. The same non-partisan standard applies: the believer serves the stated constructive purpose, does not represent the Bahá’í institutions unless formally appointed to do so, and consults the National Spiritual Assembly when the organisation’s character or a proposed affiliation is unclear.

Interfaith boards dedicated to education, relief, or moral dialogue are generally aligned with Bahá’í aims. Bodies that function as instruments of a single partisan or sectarian agenda require scrutiny. The believer’s presence should unite, not inflame; where the organisation’s methods include partisan pressure or condemnation of other groups, wisdom may counsel declining the invitation.

Pastoral caveats

Do not equate non-Bahá’í with suspect. The vast majority of civil society organisations are precisely the venues through which believers serve humanity for the love of God.

Do not let board service become partisan office. The line is not always visible at the outset; watch whether the body becomes a factional instrument.

Do not represent the Faith on the board unless invited in a professional capacity and wisdom counsels it. The trustee serves the organisation‘s lawful mission; the Bahá’í community does not have a single political voice to project.

Do not neglect Bahá’í institutional duties for civic honour. Feast, Holy Days, and institutional office take precedence when scheduling conflicts are chronic.

Do not confuse board generosity with Huqúqu’lláh or the Fund. Charitable giving through a secular board does not substitute for Bahá’í spiritual obligations (huqququllah-not-a-tax, charitable-giving-beyond-huquq).
