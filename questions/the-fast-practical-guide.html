What is the practical guidance for observing the Fast?

The Bahá’í Fast is a nineteen-day period — the month of ‘Alá’ — during which believers abstain from food and drink from sunrise to sunset. It is binding on men and women from the age of fifteen, with exemptions ordained in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas and elaborated in the Synopsis and Codification. Practical observance is not merely physical abstention; it is participation in a rhythm the Revelation places between the generosity of Ayyám-i-Há and the feast of Naw-Rúz, a season the Pen of the Most High associates with blessing and special virtue. The Fast belongs to the framework of law the Kitáb-i-Aqdas ordains for the community of the Covenant. This guide addresses what the law requires, who is exempt, how to navigate employment and health, and how communities support believers without replacing individual conscience.

The Law in Its Calendar Context

Bahá’u’lláh reveals:

“O people of the world! We have enjoined upon you fasting during a brief period, and at its close have designated for you Naw-Rúz as a feast.” — Bahá’u’lláh, Kitáb-i-Aqdas, ¶16

The same passage ordains Intercalary Days before the Fast — days of giving and good cheer — and commands entry upon the Fast when those days end. A believer who arrives at ‘Alá‘ without having lived the Intercalary Days’ outward generosity may find the Fast spiritually and psychologically abrupt; the calendar intends a transition.

The abstention rule is explicit:

“Abstain from food and drink from sunrise to sundown, and beware lest desire deprive you of this grace that is appointed in the Book.” — Bahá’u’lláh, Kitáb-i-Aqdas, ¶16

Editorial apparatus summarises the binding elements: the Fast commences after Ayyám-i-Há and ends with Naw-Rúz; abstention runs sunrise to sunset; maturity is fixed at fifteen years. The Bahá’í day runs sunset to sunset, but the Fast’s interior hours follow the solar reckoning of sunrise and sunset for the locality.

The Synopsis lists exemptions (discussed below). Two Questions and Answers add practical texture: travelers on foot may break the Fast if their journey exceeds two hours; those engaged in hard labour are excused but advised to eat with frugality and in private out of respect for the law’s station.

Bahá’u’lláh affirms the Fast’s rank in response to a question about the sick:

“In truth, I say that obligatory prayer and fasting occupy an exalted station in the sight of God.” — Bahá’u’lláh, Kitáb-i-Aqdas, Q&A on sickness and prayer

The Universal House of Justice, in its Ridván 1999 message, quoted Bahá’u’lláh’s further assertion that “Fasting and obligatory prayer are as two wings to man’s life,” and confirmed that all elements of the laws dealing with obligatory prayer and fasting are, without exception, now universally applicable. The believer approaching the Fast therefore does so under a law fully operative worldwide — not a cultural optional practice.

Who Must Fast — and Who Is Exempt

Obligation. Men and women who have attained fifteen years of age and are not within an exempt category abstain each day of ‘Alá’ from sunrise to sunset.

Exemptions recognised in the Synopsis and Codification:

CategoryGuidance
TravelersJourney exceeding nine hours exempts; on foot, exceeding two hours. Those breaking journey for less than nineteen days remain exempt while traveling. Those who break journey during the Fast at a place they will stay nineteen days fast only the first three days after arrival. Those reaching home during the Fast commence fasting from arrival day.
IllnessThose who are ill are exempt. Severity is a matter of honest medical and spiritual judgment, not performance of toughness.
AgeThose over seventy are exempt.
PregnancyWomen who are with child.
NursingWomen who are nursing.
MenstruationWomen in their courses — parallel exemption as for obligatory prayer; a specifically revealed verse is repeated ninety-five times daily in place of fasting for that period.
Heavy labourThose engaged in hard labour — exempt, with frugality and private eating commended if they eat.

Holy Day overlap. If the Twin Holy Days or the Declaration of the Báb fall within ‘Alá’, Q&A 36 releases the believer from fasting on that day.

Vowing additional fasts. Q&A 71 permits vows to fast outside ‘Alá‘ when directed to aims that profit mankind; the ordained Fast of ‘Alá’ remains distinct and binding for those not exempt.

The exemptions are not loopholes to be minimised in shame but tokens of God’s grace, as the Text says of travelers, the ailing, and those with child or nursing. The mature believer asks: Am I exempt? If not, how do I observe with joy? If yes, how do I honour the season without pretence?

Daily Mechanics — Sunrise to Sunset

Determining times. Use reliable local sunrise and sunset tables for your location. High latitudes where twilight extremes distort the day may require consultation with national guidance; the House has addressed polar extremes in calendar correspondence — believers in such regions follow Assembly direction.

What abstention includes. Food and drink; smoking and oral medicine that passes as nourishment would contradict the spirit of abstention though the Text names food and drink. Medical necessity is treated under exemptions — a diabetic whose safety requires monitoring or controlled intake is not bound to endanger life for the appearance of fasting.

Morning before sunrise. Eat and hydrate deliberately before Fajr-equivalent dawn; families with children fasting for the first time should plan a sustaining pre-dawn meal.

Evening at sunset. Break the Fast at sunset with prayer and gratitude; many communities share a short devotional at sunset during ‘Alá’. The first day of the Fast and the last evening before Naw-Rúz carry particular resonance.

Work and school. The Fast is not a public holiday in most countries; students and employees fast while continuing responsibilities. Scheduling demanding physical tasks for non-fasting hours where possible is wisdom, not evasion.

Medication. Believers taking prescription medication consult physicians and, where appropriate, the Assembly: timing doses outside fasting hours, or recognising exemption when alteration is unsafe. The law does not require martyrdom by insulin deprivation.

Athletics and manual labour. Youth athletes and construction workers often fall under heavy-labour exemption or require schedule adjustment. Eating frugally and in private respects the law when exemption applies.

Spiritual Posture — Beyond Hunger

The Fast is not a diet. Bahá’u’lláh warns that desire can deprive the believer of the grace appointed in the Book — the deprivation is spiritual, not caloric.

The Universal House of Justice‘s 1999 message linked deepening awareness of devotional laws to the community‘s capacity for service. Individual believers worldwide, the House wrote in a 2009 message to believers under oppression, rely on “the spiritual forces released by the Fast” for prayerful remembrance and reflection. The Fast’s social dimension appears when communities notice who might fast alone, who might need a sunset meal invitation, and who might be ashamed to claim a lawful exemption.

Practices that strengthen observance without adding unauthorised rules:

  • Obligatory prayer maintained through the month (with exemptions for menstruation as applicable).
  • Reading portions of the Writings each day — brief, consistent.
  • Charity — discreet attention to those in need; the Fast pairs naturally with the Intercalary Days’ generosity now interiorised.
  • Avoiding gossip and backbiting — the Text forbids calumny in the same breath as murder and adultery; the Fast month is a fitting season to guard speech.
  • Not broadcasting hardship — neither boasting of hunger nor performing exemption unnecessarily in public.

Vowing to fast outside ‘Alá’ for aims that profit mankind remains permissible; it must not distract from or replace the ordained period.

Employment, Family, and Community Support

Employers. The Fast is not among the nine work-free Holy Days; work continues. Believers may explain dietary needs at sunset meetings or shift meals. Some request schedule accommodation for particularly demanding days; employers are not obligated to grant it.

Families. Mixed observance — one parent exempt nursing, another fasting — requires coordination for evening meals and childcare. Children under fifteen are not bound but may practise with parental guidance; forcing children to fast is not the law.

Community meals. Some communities host simple sunset gatherings during ‘Alá’; these are fellowship, not obligation. They particularly serve isolated believers.

Sensitive pastoral care. Believers with eating-disorder history should consult health professionals and spiritual guidance; the Fast is not a test of worthiness through self-harm. Assemblies offer support without intrusive interrogation.

Pregnancy and nursing. Exemption is granted; some women choose to fast partially or on alternate days from devotion, not obligation — the House has not required this. Honest consultation with medical providers governs.

Elderly believers between sixty and seventy may fast if health permits though exemption begins at seventy; frailty before seventy warrants the illness exemption honestly claimed.

Travel — Worked Examples

Illustrations (Tier F); individual circumstances vary.

Day trip under nine hours. Fast observed if otherwise bound.

Flight exceeding nine hours. Exempt for the travel day(s) per Synopsis rules; resume when conditions change.

Arrival to stay three weeks mid-Fast. Fast three days from arrival, then continue if not otherwise exempt.

Return home mid-Fast. Commence fasting from arrival day.

Commuter crossing time zones. Consult national guidance if confusion arises; honesty about “home” and “journey” prevents self-deception.

Ending the Fast — Naw-Rúz

The Fast terminates with the Naw-Rúz festival. The last fasting day ends at sunset on the eve of Naw-Rúz; the community then enters the Holy Day and feast described in hosting-naw-ruz. The year’s spiritual rhythm turns: restraint, then gratitude expressed in action.

Believers who could not fast due to exemption still join Naw-Rúz observance without secondary status. The Intercalary Days, the Fast, and Naw-Rúz form one architecture; each part has its place.

Points friends often raise

“The Fast is optional for modern believers.” Ridván 1999 confirmed universal applicability.

“Coffee and tea are fine if they are not food.” The Text says food and drink; infusions consumed as beverages break the Fast.

“Exemption is failure.” Exemptions are ordained by God as grace.

“Children fifteen and older must fast regardless of health.” Illness exemption applies at any age.

“Naw-Rúz morning still belongs to the Fast.” Naw-Rúz begins at sunset the prior evening; the feast opens the new year.

“Heavy labour exemption means one may feast publicly at noon.” Q&A 76 commends frugality and privacy.

“Twin Holy Days in ‘Alá’ require fasting anyway.” Q&A 36 releases the obligation on that day.

A Practical Preparation Timeline

Tier F orientation.

Two weeks before Ayyám-i-Há ends: Note ‘Alá’ dates on the official calendar; plan work and travel; discuss health exemptions with physician if needed.

During Ayyám-i-Há: Live the days of giving; avoid arriving at the Fast depleted of joy.

First day of ‘Alá’: Begin with conscious pre-dawn meal; evening devotional optional.

Mid-Fast: Reassess health; adjust if illness emerged; reach out to isolated friends.

Final days: Prepare Naw-Rúz hospitality; arrange Holy Day leave.

Sunset before Naw-Rúz: Close the Fast with gratitude; transition to Holy Day observance.

Fasting Alongside Obligatory Prayer and the Greatest Name

The Fast does not suspend other devotional obligations for those bound to observe it. The believer continues obligatory prayer each day unless menstruation or another exemption applies to prayer specifically. The verse requiring repetition of “Alláh-u-Abhá” ninety-five times daily — emphasised in the Universal House of Justice‘s Ridván 1999 message as timely for every land — remains part of the month’s discipline. Fasting without prayer is an incomplete observance; prayer without the Fast‘s restraint during ‘Alá’, where binding, is equally incomplete for those not exempt.

Missed obligatory prayers are compensated according to the provisions in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas and Questions and Answers; the Fast does not alter compensation rules. Travelers who are exempt from fasting may still need to attend to prayer requirements depending on their circumstances; consultation with national guidance resolves edge cases.

Hydration, Nutrition, and the Body’s Limits

The pre-dawn meal deserves planning as seriously as the abstention hours. Slow-release nourishment — whole grains, protein, fluids — sustains better than sugar spikes followed by mid-afternoon collapse. Evening breaking of the Fast should not become nightly excess; the law’s spirit is restraint and gratitude, not compensatory feasting that harms health.

Headache, dizziness, or fainting signal the body’s limit. Adults and youth honour the Fast by recognising exemption candidly when illness emerges mid-month, not by endangering themselves to avoid embarrassment. Assemblies create cultures where claiming lawful exemption is respected.

Caffeine withdrawal affects some believers in the first days; tapering before ‘Alá’ or accepting temporary headache as adjustment differs from illness requiring exemption. Discernment is individual.

Community Life During ‘Alá’

Clusters in the institute-process era do not pause growth activities for nineteen days, but many moderate intensity during the Fast — fewer evening meetings, gentler expectations of volunteers, deliberate inclusion of sunset times when scheduling. Children‘s classes may continue with parents‘ consent; junior youth service projects might shift toward reflective rather than physically demanding tasks. The point is attentiveness: a community that schedules a major fundraising banquet mid-Fast ignores the month’s tone.

Some Assemblies host a simple online or in-person sunset check-in during the first week, especially for new believers fasting for the first time. No institutional template exists; the gesture is pastoral. Believers under oppression or in crisis, remembered in Universal House of Justice Fast messages, hold a place in the prayers of the worldwide community during ‘Alá’; local friends name them in devotions.

Nineteen Day Feast falling within ‘Alá’ proceeds according to national guidance on Feast and Holy Day scheduling; hosts choose devotional portions appropriate to the Fast‘s restraint while maintaining the Feast’s three-part character.
