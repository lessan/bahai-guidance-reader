What is a Regional Bahá’í Council, and how does it differ from a National Spiritual Assembly?

A NationalSpiritualAssembly is the nine-member elected body that directs the affairs of the Bahá’í community of an entire country — a "Secondary House of Justice" in the language of ’Abdu’l-Bahá’s Will and Testament, ordained in the Sacred Text and developed in constitutional form by Shoghi Effendi. A RegionalBahaiCouncil is a sub-national institution introduced by the Universal House of Justice in its 30 May 1997 letter to handle expansion-and-consolidation work across a region of clusters within one country. The NSA derives its authority from the Writings; the Council derives its authority from the NSA's devolution under UHJ instruction. The two are different kinds of body — one a divinely ordained level of the House of Justice, the other an administrative instrument that, in the House of Justice's careful phrase, "partake[s] of some, but not all, characteristics of Spiritual Assemblies."

The NSA: a Secondary House of Justice

The National Spiritual Assembly's warrant runs to the Kitáb-i-Aqdas and the Will and Testament. ’Abdu’l-Bahá writes that "in all countries a secondary House of Justice must be instituted, and these secondary Houses of Justice must elect the members of the Universal one." Shoghi Effendi confirms the identification: the National Assemblies, "designated by ’Abdu’l-Bahá in His Will as the 'Secondary Houses of Justice,' … constitute the electoral bodies in the formation of the International House of Justice" (God Passes By, pp. 332–333).

The Guardian gave the body its constitutional shape across the 1920s and 1930s. From Bahá’í Administration (3 June 1925): the National Assembly is "the pivot round which revolve the activities of the believers" — "supreme … their position, grave their responsibilities, manifold and arduous their duties." Membership is fixed at nine. Election is annual, by delegates assembled at convention during Riḍván; there are no nominations and no candidates. Authority within the country is broad: the Assembly administers funds, holds property, represents the community to government, oversees the Training institutes, appoints RegionalBahaiCouncils where these are warranted, and guides the pattern of teaching and community-building across its territory. What the Assembly does, it does as the elected expression of the believing community itself; the Guardian's keynote (23 February 1924, Bahá’í Administration, Letter 19, "The Foundation of the House of Justice") is that "the keynote of the Cause of God is not dictatorial authority but humble fellowship, not arbitrary power, but the spirit of frank and loving consultation."

The RBC: a 1997 administrative instrument

The Regional Bahá’í Council is a younger institution, introduced when the cluster-era expansion of the Faith made purely national coordination too distant from the grassroots. The 30 May 1997 letter to all NSAs gives the foundational reasoning:

The expansion of the Bahá’í community and the growing complexity of the issues which are facing National Spiritual Assemblies in certain countries have brought the Cause to a new stage in its development. … From the experience gained … and from detailed examination of the principles set forth by Shoghi Effendi, we have reached the conclusion that the time has arrived for us to formalize a new element of Bahá’í administration, between the local and national levels, comprising institutions of a special kind, to be designated as "Regional Bahá’í Councils."

The same letter is precise about the Council's status: "Regional Bahá’í Councils partake of some, but not all, characteristics of Spiritual Assemblies." A Council is more than a national committee — its decisive distinguishing effect is that "it provides for a level of autonomous decision making on both teaching and administrative matters, as distinct from merely executive action, below the National Assembly and above the Local Assemblies." It is also less than a Spiritual Assembly — its formation depends on UHJ permission and NSA authorisation; it is established only "in countries where conditions make this step necessary."

The 30 May 1997 letter and its accompanying enclosure ("THE ESTABLISHMENT OF REGIONAL BAHÁ'Í COUNCILS IN CERTAIN COUNTRIES, THEIR CHARACTERISTICS AND FUNCTIONS") together remain the constitutive document for the institution. Subsequent UHJ guidance has not altered the foundation but has refined the Council's brief in the cluster era. The 28 December 2010 message to the Counsellors locates continued progress in "the increased capacity of Regional Bahá’í Councils and ultimately National Spiritual Assemblies," noting that "all Regional Councils pay close attention to the operation of the training institute and the functioning of Area Teaching Committees." The 29 December 2015 message — opening the Five Year Plan that ran into Riḍván 2021 — names accompaniment of cluster agencies as "a critical responsibility of Regional Bahá’í Councils and regional training institutes." The arc is one of deepening application within the 1997 framework, not departure from it.

Working relationship

The 30 May 1997 letter is careful to distinguish the Council from a national committee. A committee, in the Guardian's language, is among "bodies that should be regarded in no other light than that of expert advisers and executive assistants" — they assist; they do not decide. A Council decides, within its regional brief. That is what makes it a new kind of institution rather than a re-named teaching committee. The same letter draws the contrast directly: "the relationship between a National or Local Spiritual Assembly and its committees is of a different nature from that between National and Local Spiritual Assemblies." The Council sits in neither relationship cleanly. It is a hyphenated body: more than a committee, less than an Assembly.

In practice the working pattern is straightforward. The NSA forms the Council with UHJ permission, sets its size and terms, and (where the Council is appointed) names its members. The Council then takes operational responsibility for the cluster-development work in its region — pioneer placement, Training institutes coordination at regional scale, regional reflection gatherings, allocation of resources to Cluster developments, accompaniment of the AreaTeachingCommittees. The 30 May 1997 letter underlines that "the greater degree of decentralization involved in the devolution of authority upon Regional Bahá’í Councils requires a corresponding increase in the capacity of the National Spiritual Assembly itself to keep fully informed of what is proceeding in all parts of the territory over which it has ultimate jurisdiction." Devolution does not mean disengagement; the NSA holds the national whole.

The NSA retains what cannot be devolved: external affairs, relations with government, national funds and properties, judicial and pastoral authority over the national community as a whole, communication with the UHJ, participation in the international convention that elects the Universal House of Justice. A believer's appeal of an LSA decision goes to the NSA, not the Council; the Council's remit is cross-cluster coordination, not local governance. The Counsellors maintain "direct consultative relationships" with both the NSA and the Council, the 30 May 1997 letter specifies — the Council is not screened from the appointed arm by the NSA's intermediation.

Where no Council exists — in most smaller national communities — the work a Council would do remains with the NSA itself. The Council is not a universal feature of Bahá’í administration. It is an instrument calibrated to the size and complexity of the community it serves.

Why the distinction matters

Confusing the two flattens a real architectural difference. The NSA's authority is constitutional in the strict sense: it is named in the Sacred Text, traced by the Guardian to the Aqdas, and stands as one of the pillars whose membership composes, in part, the body of the Universal House of Justice. The Council's authority is operational and conditional: it exists where the UHJ has authorised it, with the size and selection method the UHJ has approved, exercising the powers the NSA has devolved to it. Both are real institutions doing real work; they are not the same kind of institution.

The point is the one Shoghi Effendi made about all Bahá’í administration (14 October 1941, on his behalf): "The friends must never mistake the Bahá’í administration for an end in itself. It is merely the instrument of the spirit of the Faith." The NSA is the elected national instrument; the RBC is a sub-national instrument calibrated to the cluster-era reality. Both serve the same Cause; one is older and stands on the Writings, the other is younger and stands on the UHJ's authorisation, transmitted through the NSA.
