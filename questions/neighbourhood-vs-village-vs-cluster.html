How does a neighbourhood differ from a village, and how do both relate to the cluster?

The cluster is the primary planning unit of the worldwide Bahá'í community — a geographically defined area within which a Programme of Growth is conducted by an Area Teaching Committee. A neighbourhood (urban) and a village (rural) are both subdivisions within a cluster: the natural, human-scale communities where actual relationships are built and core activities take root. The two terms describe the same functional role in different geographic contexts.

The 9 January 2001 letter introduced the cluster as the operational geography: "Most of these will consist of a cluster of villages and towns, but, sometimes, a large city and its suburbs may constitute an area of this kind." The 28 December 2010 letter then operationalised the neighbourhood/village distinction: "In an urban cluster, such a centre of activity might best be defined by the boundaries of a neighbourhood; in a cluster that is primarily rural in character, a small village would offer a suitable social space for this purpose."

The Nesting Logic: Cluster Contains Many Villages and Neighbourhoods

A cluster is not simply a larger version of a neighbourhood or village — it is a different kind of unit. The cluster is an administrative and planning container; it is the level at which a Programme of Growth is tracked, resources are coordinated, and intensive phases are called. A single cluster in an African country may encompass dozens of villages. A single cluster in a major metropolitan area may encompass many urban neighbourhoods.

The Programme of Growth therefore operates cluster-wide in the sense that the ATC holds overall responsibility and the reflection-and-planning cycle runs at the cluster level. But the density of activity — the study circles, junior youth groups, children's classes, and devotional gatherings — is typically concentrated in specific villages or neighbourhoods where a critical mass of friends and families is being reached. The 28 December 2010 letter names the expectation: core activities "may well need to be maintained with assistance from outside the local population for a time. It is to be expected, however, that the multiplication of these core activities would soon be sustained by human resources indigenous to the neighbourhood or village itself." Those focal nodes are where community actually forms: participants know each other personally, can walk to each other's homes, and share a sense of belonging to a defined place.

Why the Distinction Matters Practically

(Tier F — editorial operationalisation grounded in UHJ locus-of-work letters.)

Confusing these three levels creates two opposite failure modes.

Over-centralisation occurs when a cluster team treats the entire cluster as the unit of community building — organising activities from a single hub, drawing participants from across a wide geography, and inadvertently creating a programme that no one truly inhabits. Participants travel to attend rather than host; the activity does not put down roots in any particular place; and when key individuals move or lose interest, nothing self-sustaining remains.

Under-coordination occurs in the opposite direction: each neighbourhood or village acts entirely on its own, without coherence with the broader cluster. Local friends may not benefit from the experience accumulated in neighbouring localities; resources (visiting teachers, materials, training opportunities) are not shared; and the ATC loses the ability to identify where the leading edge of growth actually is and how to support it.

The three-level vocabulary — cluster, neighbourhood/village, activity site — exists precisely to hold both scales simultaneously. The ATC coordinates, learns, and plans at the cluster level. Friends build authentic relationships, host families, and animate the spiritual life of a place at the neighbourhood or village level. The Riḍván 2012 message locates the Mashriqu'l-Adhkár at this intimate scale: "It is within these clusters that, in the coming years, the emergence of a local Mashriqu'l-Adhkár can be contemplated."
