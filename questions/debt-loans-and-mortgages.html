What does Bahá’í teaching say about debt, loans, and mortgages?

Bahá’í teaching does not forbid borrowing, lending, or paying interest on money — but it treats debt as a solemn obligation, not a lifestyle. Interest is explicitly lawful; usury in the sense of exploitative excess is not. A believer who can pay what he owes and neglects to do so has failed a binding command of trustworthiness. Mortgages and other long-term loans fall within the same framework: permitted instruments whose wisdom is measured by moderation, honest dealing, and the capacity to honour what one has undertaken.

The reversal on interest — binding law with a counsel of moderation

The question of loans begins with a ruling that many seekers find surprising. In prior dispensations, charging interest on money was forbidden; in this one, Bahá’u’lláh has made it lawful:

“…it is lawful and proper to charge interest on money, that the people of the world may, in a spirit of amity and fellowship and with joy and gladness, devotedly engage themselves in magnifying the Name of Him Who is the Well-Beloved of all mankind. Verily He ordaineth according to His Own choosing. He hath now made interest on money lawful, even as He had made it unlawful in the past.”

— Bahá’u‘lláh, Tablets of Bahá’u’lláh (the Ishráqát)

The social rationale is practical: without some prospect of return, lending would collapse, and ordinary commerce — including the financing of homes, enterprises, and productive investment — would suffer dislocation. The ruling is binding law. It does not, however, license predation. Bahá’u’lláh immediately counsels moderation and fairness, deliberately leaves the rate unset, and entrusts future regulation to the Universal House of Justice. Usury — illicit gain dressed in legal form — remains condemned. A mortgage whose interest rate reflects ordinary market terms is not, on that ground alone, objectionable; a loan whose terms exploit ignorance or desperation is another matter entirely, and falls under the wider prohibition of injustice that trustworthiness in business forbids.

Debt as moral obligation — not mere bookkeeping

Where the Writings speak with greatest force is not on whether one may borrow, but on whether one will pay. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá is unambiguous:

“It is manifestly clear that anyone who hath the ability to settle his debts, and yet neglecteth to do so, hath not acted in accordance with the good pleasure of the one true God. Those who incur debts should strive to settle them with all diligence and application.”

— ‘Abdu’l-Bahá, in Trustworthiness (compilation)

The same passage carries a critical qualification drawn from the Qur‘án: “Respite thy debtor till he findeth means to pay.” Ability is the operative condition. A demand that exceeds what a debtor can meet is itself impermissible. The believer who lends must exercise forbearance; the believer who borrows must exercise diligence. Both sides of the transaction are governed by the same virtue — trustworthiness — which Bahá’u‘lláh names among the highest of divine attributes and which Shoghi Effendi requires be “constantly reflected in the business dealings of all” Bahá’ís (The Advent of Divine Justice).

The practical corollary for personal finance is direct. Debt is not shameful in itself; evasion of debt when one is able to pay is. A believer who carries a mortgage, a student loan, or a business line of credit has undertaken a promise. Keeping that promise — making payments on time, communicating honestly with creditors when difficulty arises, restructuring rather than defaulting when restructuring is the honourable course — is an expression of faith, not a separate domain from it.

Estates, precedence, and the seriousness of what is owed

The Kitáb-i-Aqdas embeds debt settlement in the architecture of inheritance itself. When a believer dies, the estate is not divided among heirs until prior charges are met. The order is explicit: funeral and burial expenses first; then settlement of debts; then payment of any outstanding Ḥuqúqu‘lláh; only then division of what remains. If the estate is insufficient to cover all debts, the property is distributed among creditors in proportion to each claim. The Universal House of Justice (1 July 1996) confirms that unpaid Ḥuqúqu’lláh at death is itself a debt of the estate, payable like any other obligation before heirs receive their shares.

This ordering teaches something larger than probate procedure. Bahá’u’lláh treats what one owes others as prior to what one would pass on to one‘s children. A believer planning for the future — including the future beyond death — should reckon with debts as seriously as with Ḥuqúqu‘lláh and testamentary wishes. Leaving one’s affairs in order is not optional piety; it is part of the same law that enjoins the writing of a will (Aqdas ¶109, currently binding).

Mortgages and long-term borrowing — conscience within lawful bounds

No Tablet singles out mortgages, credit cards, or consumer finance by name. The guidance is principled. Three questions, applied with honesty, cover most cases.

First: is the borrowing productive or merely consumptive? Bahá’u’lláh exalts engagement in craft, trade, and agriculture to the rank of worship (Aqdas ¶33) and praises wealth acquired through honest effort in commerce, agriculture, art, and industry when it is expended for useful purposes (‘Abdu‘l-Bahá, The Secret of Divine Civilization). A mortgage that secures shelter for one’s family, or a loan that finances a legitimate enterprise or education, sits comfortably within that vision. Debt incurred to sustain a standard of living one cannot afford — or to feed attachment to luxury — runs against the counsel of moderation and the warnings against extravagance that run through the Writings.

Second: can the obligation be met without endangering one‘s other duties? The Codification of the Law of Ḥuqúqu‘lláh treats annual expenses, taxes, and necessary outlays as deductions before assessable surplus is calculated. A believer whose debt service consumes the margin that would otherwise constitute surplus — or who borrows in ways that foreclose observance of Ḥuqúqu’lláh, contributions to the Fund, or care of dependents — has likely crossed from prudent use of lawful instruments into self-imposition. The test is not a published debt-to-income ratio; it is conscience informed by the texts.

Third: are the terms honest and the counterparty treated fairly? Hidden fees, misrepresented conditions, and pressure tactics are not “how business is done”; they are violations of the trustworthiness that must “distinguish every phase” of economic life. A believer should read what he signs, refuse what he does not understand, and walk away from arrangements whose structure depends on another party’s confusion.

A Local or National Spiritual Assembly does not adjudicate personal borrowing decisions. It may become involved if debt arises in the context of Bahá’í institutional affairs — a failing project, a dispute among believers in a joint venture, or conduct that damages the good name of the Faith. The 2 September 1993 guidance on private business ventures (see bahai-owned-business-conduct) limits institutional intervention in commercial affairs to cases of difficulty among friends, misrepresentation of the Faith, or damage to its reputation. Ordinary household debt remains a matter between the believer, his creditors, and God.

Lending — the creditor’s side of trustworthiness

The Writings address borrowers and lenders with equal seriousness. Bahá’u‘lláh’s reversal on interest assumes a world in which lending continues — because without it, “the affairs of men would suffer collapse or dislocation.” The believer who lends, whether to a family member, a friend in business, or through a financial institution he owns or directs, bears the same standard of moderation and fairness that governs borrowing. Pressing a debtor who lacks means violates the Qur’anic principle ‘Abdu’l-Bahá cites; pressing a debtor who possesses means but prefers delay is the legitimate exercise of a right.

Private lending among believers calls for written clarity — amounts, terms, and expectations — even when affection makes paperwork feel awkward. The trustworthiness compilation’s counsel on dealings between friends of God is stern: domestic honesty must precede public reputation. Informal loans that end in bitterness are a staple of Assembly consultations; they are preventable through the same virtues that govern commercial paper.

Institutional borrowing by Bahá‘í communities follows civil law and Assembly governance, not a separate revealed finance code. The principles remain: contracts honoured, creditors not misled, debts discharged when due. A community that borrows for a centre or a project and neglects repayment damages the Faith’s name as surely as a believer who defaults on a personal loan.

Detachment, means, and the cord Bahá’u’lláh commands

Borrowing sits within a wider teaching on economic means. Bahá’u’lláh forbids idleness and begging (Aqdas ¶33, ¶184) and commands believers to “hold ye fast unto the cord of means and place your trust in God, the Provider of all means.” The cord of means is not the cord of debt; it is honest occupation and lawful instrumentality. Debt becomes a snare when it replaces earning with deferral, or when the believer mistakes leverage for providence.

Detachment does not require poverty. It requires that possessions not own the possessor. A mortgage on a modest home, carried by a household whose members work and serve, may express responsible means; a mountain of consumer debt funding status displays attachment. The Hidden Words’ warning that riches may be a “mighty barrier” applies to the heart, not to the balance sheet alone.

Misunderstandings worth correcting

“Bahá’ís should avoid debt as spiritually superior to borrowing.” The Writings praise earning and honest commerce; they condemn neglect of obligation, not the use of credit. A believer who pays a lawful mortgage on time while serving others is not inferior to one who owns outright through inheritance or extraordinary thrift.

“Interest is still usury, so Bahá‘ís should not charge or pay it.” The distinction is explicit in Bahá’u‘lláh’s own language: interest is lawful; abusive excess is not. Treating all interest as sin misreads a deliberate legislative change.

“If I cannot pay Ḥuqúqu‘lláh and my creditors, the creditors come first.” At death, funeral and debts precede Ḥuqúqu’lláh on the estate. During life, both obligations rest on conscience. The House of Justice has stated that if one cannot afford both Ḥuqúqu‘lláh and contributions to the Fund, Ḥuqúqu’lláh takes priority — a principle that should inform how one structures debt so that sacred obligations are not permanently deferred.

“Respite for debtors means I need not pay until I feel ready.” Respite applies when means are genuinely lacking. Ability plus neglect is condemned without qualification.
