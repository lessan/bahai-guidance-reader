What does the guidance say about handling Covenant-breaking material encountered online or socially?

Do not engage with Covenant-breaking material in public forums, do not circulate it, and do not treat it as material for casual reading. The foundational instruction is in ’Abdu’l-Bahá’s Will and Testament: "one of the greatest and most fundamental principles of the Cause of God is to shun and avoid entirely the Covenant-breakers, for they will utterly destroy the Cause of God, exterminate His Law and render of no account all efforts exerted in the past." The electronic context is new; the principle is not — and the Universal House of Justice has applied it directly to bulletin boards, chat rooms, and email lists.

What Covenant-breaking actually is

Covenant-breaking is not simply disagreeing with Bahá’í teachings or leaving the Faith. The Universal House of Justice defines a Covenant-breaker as "a Bahá’í who attempts to disrupt the unity of the Faith by defying and opposing the authority of Bahá’u’lláh… ’Abdu’l-Bahá… the Guardian and the Universal House of Justice." The community's posture toward such opposition is not incidental. Shoghi Effendi names the structural function of the Covenant itself: to "insure its integrity, safeguard it from schism, and stimulate its world-wide expansion." The twin institutions of the Administrative Order share the same object: "to safeguard the unity of its followers, and to maintain the integrity and flexibility of its teachings."

Why the guidance says to shun, not debate

The danger in engaging with Covenant-breaking material is not primarily intellectual — the arguments are not unanswerable. The danger is spiritual. Shoghi Effendi, writing on behalf of the Guardian, describes Covenant-breakers as afflicted with "what we might try and define as a contagious spiritual disease" and warns that associating with them "exposes one to grave danger of contagion"; "if we sympathize with them we only fortify their perversity and waywardness." The Universal House of Justice has called Covenant-breaking "a spiritual poison" whose calumnies can "undermine the faith of the believer and plant the seeds of doubt unless he is forearmed with an unshakable belief in Bahá’u’lláh and His Covenant and a knowledge of the true facts."

This is why a believer who feels obligated to publicly rebut Covenant-breaking content online has already misread the situation. The House of Justice has instructed that the friends "should avoid confrontation with them before the public, especially in news columns such as 'Letters to the Editor.'" On hostile books, it has advised that "no issue should be made of them… the best plan is to ignore them entirely." Public debate typically amplifies what the guidance counsels leaving alone.

Practical guidance for online encounters

If you encounter material online attributed to a Covenant-breaker or a splinter group — such as followers of Mason Remey (see custodial-period-1957-1963) or various claimant movements — the practical posture follows from the same principles:

  • Do not engage in public dialogue. The House of Justice, responding in 1995 to questions about electronic forums, stated that when enemies of the Faith or Covenant-breakers participate in online discussion, "it is not advisable for the institutions of the Faith or its individual members to become directly involved in such discussion."
  • Do not circulate the material. Sharing spreads it; the guidance to ignore hostile publications applies by inference to forwarding posts or links, even to warn others.
  • Bring genuine questions to trusted people — an Auxiliary Board member, a knowledgeable friend, or your Local Spiritual Assembly. Questions arising from such encounters are normal; the appropriate response is to bring them to the community, not to pursue further investigation in isolation.
  • Return to authoritative sources: the Writings, letters from the Guardian, guidance from the Universal House of Justice. The House of Justice placed authentic Texts on electronic servers precisely so believers could check claims against the Sacred Word.

The 1995 guidance names the positive protection directly: "The greatest protection to the Faith will not be through intervention on either open or closed electronic forums, but through ongoing deepening of the Bahá’í community in the Covenant and the history and Teachings of the Faith."

Scholarly inquiry versus casual reading

The guidance distinguishes purpose and preparation, not a simple binary between "academic" and "curious" reading. The Universal House of Justice has stated that "to read the writings of Covenant-breakers is not forbidden to the believers and does not constitute in itself an act of Covenant-breaking" — but that the friends are "warned in the strongest terms against reading such literature" because of the spiritual poison it carries. Bahá’í historians "have the obligation to present the truth" and "should not distort history by avoiding treatment of Covenant-breakers when this is called for." Where reading is "necessary for the purposes of your study… you should have no feeling of guilt." Scholars who must read such works are reminded they are "by no means immune to the spiritual poison."

Shoghi Effendi drew a related distinction: Covenant-breaker books "is better not to read"; books by well-meaning but inaccurate critics "may be read 'so as to refute [the] charges' made in them." The operative question is not whether one is a scholar, but whether one is forearmed with Covenant knowledge, reading for a defined purpose, and accountable to the institutions — rather than browsing out of curiosity or following a Covenant-breaker's social media feed.

The positive counterpart: proximity to the Covenant

Avoiding Covenant-breaking material is only half the picture. Bahá’u’lláh’s Kitáb-i-'Ahd states the Covenant's telos at its opening: "The religion of God is for love and union; make it not the cause of enmity or dissension." The positive practice the guidance pairs with avoidance is grounding oneself in prayer, study circles, community life, and regular engagement with the Writings — the deepening that makes divisive material recognizable for what it is without elaborate argument.
