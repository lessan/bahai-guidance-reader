How should a Bahá’í think about insurance — life, health, and property?

The Writings contain no revealed law for or against insurance. What they supply is a framework: trustworthiness in every contract; moderation in all things; the duty to care for one’s family and not impose needless hardship on others; detachment from the illusion that material security replaces reliance on God. Within that framework, life, health, and property insurance are, for most believers in modern economies, ordinarily prudent instruments of stewardship — not acts of faithlessness, but lawful arrangements of conscience whose specifics the sacred texts do not legislate.

What the corpus does and does not say

A search of the authoritative texts yields principles, not policies. Bahá’u’lláh does not mention insurance. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá does not classify it among forbidden acts. The Universal House of Justice has not issued guidance treating insurance as a religious question distinct from ordinary contractual life. Institutional records — including Bahá’í community administration of properties — show believers and assemblies purchasing liability, fire, and other coverage as a matter of practical management, not doctrinal controversy.

The honest starting point matters. A believer asking whether insurance is “allowed” is asking a question the Revelation does not answer directly. The better question is what virtues and cautions the Writings bring to bear when one decides whether, and how much, to insure.

Trustworthiness — the firmest hook for any policy

Insurance is a contract: premiums paid in exchange for defined benefits under defined conditions. The Writings’ treatment of commercial life therefore applies without adaptation:

“Every business company should be established on divine principles. Its foundations should be trustworthiness, piety and truthfulness in order to protect the rights of the people.” — ‘Abdu’l-Bahá, in Trustworthiness (compilation)

“Commerce is as a heaven, whose sun is trustworthiness and whose moon is truthfulness.” — ‘Abdu’l-Bahá, in Trustworthiness

For the policyholder, trustworthiness means honest application forms, accurate disclosure of material facts, and claims made in good faith. For the believer employed in the industry, it means the same rectitude Shoghi Effendi demands of every Bahá’í in professional life: no misrepresentation of products, no exploitation of fear, no pressure on the vulnerable. Insurance is not a domain where Bahá’í ethics are suspended because “everyone exaggerates on the form.” The sun of trustworthiness shines on this commerce as on any other.

Stewardship, trusteeship, and care for dependents

Economic teaching in the Faith consistently frames wealth as instrument, not idol. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s account of wealth in The Secret of Divine Civilization praises riches honestly acquired and philanthropically used, and condemns wealth that benefits no one. The Prosperity of Humankind statement extends the logic to civilisation itself — trusteeship in service of SocietyBuilding: humanity must recover “the capacity for contentment, the welcoming of moral discipline, and the devotion to duty” and use resources as trustees, not merely consumers.

Insurance sits naturally within stewardship. A parent who insures against the loss of income that dependents rely on is not denying God’s sovereignty; he is discharging a duty of foresight so that illness or death does not translate into preventable destitution for those under his care. A homeowner who carries fire coverage is acknowledging that catastrophe can be shared across a pool of policyholders more effectively than borne alone. A society without any mechanism for spreading catastrophic risk concentrates ruin on the unlucky and their kin — an outcome at odds with justice, which the Writings treat as the organising principle of a mature social order.

The spiritual danger lies not in the contract but in the disposition. Insurance purchased to feed anxiety, to finance recklessness (“I need not be careful — I‘m covered”), or to accumulate as an investment vehicle unrelated to genuine need, drifts toward the attachment to material security that Bahá’u’lláh warns against. Moderation governs both whether one insures and how much coverage one carries.

Contentment, providence, and the balance the Writings require

No honest account of insurance can ignore the counter-theme in Bahá’í teaching: reliance on God, contentment with divine decree, and the warning against letting material means obscure spiritual dependence. Bahá’u’lláh counsels believers to “be generous in your days of plenty, and be patient in the hour of loss” (Tablets of Bahá’u’lláh). Loss comes to the insured and uninsured alike; a policy does not repeal tests ordained by God.

The resolution is not either-or. The Writings consistently pair trust in God with the disciplined use of lawful means. Bahá’u’lláh exalts trade to worship; ‘Abdu’l-Bahá urges the creation of “new instrumentalities toward” human happiness (Secret of Divine Civilization). Insurance is such an instrument — a social technology for pooling risk that a prudent believer may employ without imagining that he has cancelled divine providence. The error is twofold: refusing all means while invoking faith, or trusting means so completely that prayer and detachment become empty forms.

Contentment also limits how much coverage one pursues. The believer who insures against every conceivable loss, at escalating premiums, to preserve a lifestyle of excess has mistaken insurance for entitlement. The believer who carries reasonable coverage and accepts that some losses lie beyond any policy’s scope has found the balance moderation requires.

Life, health, and property — three applications of one principle

Life insurance addresses the economic consequences of death. The Writings enjoin writing a will (Aqdas ¶109, binding) and making provision for children who are minors (Aqdas inheritance provisions). Life insurance can serve those ends — ensuring that funeral costs, debts, Ḥuqúqu’lláh, and support for dependents are not left to emergency improvisation. It is not a substitute for a will; benefits must be coordinated with testamentary plans and named beneficiaries. Term coverage aligned to defined obligations (mortgage balance, years until children reach adulthood) tends to fit the moderation principle better than complex permanent policies whose primary logic is tax avoidance or speculative accumulation — matters the Writings do not endorse and conscience must scrutinise.

Health insurance addresses the economic consequences of illness. The Faith affirms the dignity of the human body and the importance of medical care; it does not prescribe a health-financing model. In jurisdictions where care is privately financed, going without coverage when affordable imposes risk on family and community that foresight could mitigate. In jurisdictions with public systems, supplementary coverage may be unnecessary or may fill genuine gaps. The believer judges by circumstance, not by a universal rule imported from another country’s debate.

Property insurance — homeowner‘s, renter’s, automobile, liability — protects against loss that would otherwise fall entirely on the individual. Liability coverage deserves particular attention for believers in business: the trustworthiness standard that governs commerce does not eliminate honest mistakes, and a pool that absorbs catastrophic liability may protect both victims and the owner‘s capacity to continue serving through the enterprise (see bahai-owned-business-conduct). Skipping liability insurance to save premiums while exposing employees, customers, or the public to unrecoverable harm is difficult to reconcile with justice. The Kitáb-i-Aqdas exempts the principal residence from Ḥuqúqu’lláh assessment in certain conditions, signalling the Writings’ recognition of housing as a needful foundation of life, not mere investment. Insuring that foundation against insurable perils is consistent with treating the home as shelter rather than as a leveraged wager. Over-insuring for replacement values one would never rebuild, or under-insuring to save premiums while carrying debt secured on the property, are imbalances conscience should correct.

A Spiritual Assembly does not advise believers on policy choices. It may become involved if a believer in the insurance business misrepresents affiliation with the Faith, damages its reputation through dishonest practice, or creates disputes among friends in a joint enterprise. The criteria mirror those for other private commerce (2 September 1993, on private business ventures — see bahai-owned-business-conduct). A widow contesting a claim denial in a persecuting state — as Iranian believers have faced — is a matter of civil rights and legal advocacy, not of religious prohibition against insurance itself.

Contracts with institutions — reading the fine print as a spiritual act

Modern insurance is complex: exclusions, deductibles, waiting periods, subrogation clauses, and dispute-resolution forums buried in dense documents. The Writings do not excuse the believer from reading what he signs. Trustworthiness is not naïveté; it is honest dealing joined to informed consent. A believer who purchases coverage without understanding what is excluded, or who makes claims for losses not covered by the contract he accepted, fails the standard he applies to his own commercial honour.

Group coverage through employment or association raises additional questions of conscience but not of permission. Accepting employer-sponsored health or life benefit is ordinary prudence. Declining coverage when the cost is heavily subsidised, without alternative provision for dependents, requires a sober justification — not a slogan about faith.

Misunderstandings worth correcting

“Insurance shows lack of faith in God.” The Writings command trust in God and also command trustworthiness, foresight, and care for others. Bahá’u’lláh praises those who “initiate measures which would universally enrich the masses” (Secret of Divine Civilization). Pooling risk through lawful contracts is a social measure of that kind, not a theological statement that God will not test us.

“The Faith requires believers to carry insurance.” No text says so. Believers with other means of meeting the same obligations — substantial liquid assets, extended family support, public guarantees — may reasonably judge coverage redundant. The standard is prudent stewardship, not a universal mandate.

“Life insurance payouts replace inheritance law.” Beneficiary designations operate alongside, not instead of, wills and intestacy rules. Estate planning should integrate insurance, testament, Ḥuqúqu’lláh, and debt settlement as a single whole (see inheritance-and-family-wealth).

“Insurance companies are inherently exploitative, so Bahá’ís should opt out.” Some practices are exploitative; the industry as such is not forbidden. The believer’s task is to choose sound institutions, honour honest contracts, and — if working in the field — exemplify the standard ‘Abdu’l-Bahá sets for commerce.

“Charitable giving makes insurance unnecessary for society.” Voluntary philanthropy is central to Bahá’í life (see charitable-giving-beyond-huquq), but the Writings do not present charity as a complete substitute for lawful institutions that spread risk. Both can coexist; neither cancels the other.

“I should insure everything because catastrophe proves I lacked faith.” Tests and losses are not verdicts on whether one should have bought coverage. They are occasions for patience and generosity in loss, not for retroactive self-condemnation or for using spirituality to avoid prudent planning.
