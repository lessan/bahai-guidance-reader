What was the state of the Bahá’í community at the launch of the Nine Year Plan in 1964?

At Riḍván 1964 the newly formed Universal House of Justice launched the Nine Year Plan from a community that had just concluded a decade of explosive territorial expansion. The community then stood at 69 National Spiritual Assemblies and nearly four thousand six hundred Local Spiritual Assemblies — figures named in the Riḍván 1964 message itself. The Plan-opening baseline of 15,168 localities is cited retrospectively in the Riḍván 1965 message (per growth time-series), not in the 1964 launch letter. The Faith was spread across enough countries and territories that the UHJ could immediately set its sights on a further dramatic enlargement.

The Crusade completed

The Ten Year Crusade (1953–1963), launched by Shoghi Effendi at Riḍván 1953 and concluded at the Most Great Jubilee in London in April 1963, transformed the Administrative Order from a largely Western and Iranian infrastructure into a genuinely global one. The Guardian's original mandate to the twelve then-existing National Spiritual Assemblies called for opening 131 virgin territories, increasing the number of NSAs to 57, translating Bahá’í literature into 100 new languages, completing the superstructure of the Mother Temple of Europe in Frankfurt, and initiating the Mother Temple of Africa in Kampala.

The Custodians — the Hands of the Cause who stewarded the community from Shoghi Effendi's passing in November 1957 until the first UHJ election — brought the Crusade to its close. When the fifty-six National Spiritual Assemblies gathered in Haifa to elect the first Universal House of Justice at Riḍván 1963, those fifty-six bodies were themselves the administrative fruit of the Crusade's territorial work. The Crusade had, in the space of a decade, raised the NSA total from 12 to 56 — not quite reaching the goal of 57, but with the 57th body (that of Brunei) formed at Riḍván 1966.

The House of Justice's second message, addressed to the national conventions on 7 May 1963, named the moment exactly: "Beloved friends, we enter the second epoch of the Divine Plan blessed beyond compare, riding the crest of a great wave of victory produced for us by our beloved Guardian." The wave was real. What the Guardian had set in motion in 1953 had, by 1963, placed the Cause in lands and among peoples who had no Bahá’ís a decade earlier.

The Nine Year Plan's objectives

The Riḍván 1964 message set the twin objectives of the new Plan as "a huge expansion of the Cause of God and universal participation by all believers in the life of that Cause" — expansion and participation understood as inseparable. The concrete targets announced at launch were:

  • 108 National Spiritual Assemblies (from 69 at Plan start — an increase of 39)
  • Over 13,700 Local Spiritual Assemblies (from roughly 4,600 at Plan start)
  • Literature translated into 133 more languages
  • Two new Mashriqu'l-Adhkárs — one in Panama, one in Asia (site preparation on Mount Carmel)
  • 70 virgin territories opened (the community was called to consolidate recent gains and push into remaining territories)
  • Worldwide proclamation centred on the 1967–68 centenary of Bahá’u’lláh’s Proclamation to kings and rulers
  • Recognition of Bahá’í Holy Days as an independent religion in 95 countries

The UHJ's 13 July 1964 letter Teaching the Masses — the foundational teaching letter of the Plan — set out the operational framework: simplicity of message, teaching with love and without prejudice, nursing new believers into maturity, the inseparability of expansion and consolidation. Alongside the sheer territorial targets, this methodological guidance shaped how the community would pursue them.

The most telling column is the 1973 row. The community of 113 NSAs, 17,000+ LSAs, and 69,500 localities — compared with 69, ~4,600, and 15,168 at launch — represents what appears to be the largest proportional expansion in the community's administrative history across a single Plan (Tier F editorial comparison, not a UHJ claim). The NSA total grew by 64%. The localities total grew by more than four-fold.

Caveats on the numbers

The 1964 UHJ messages deliver institutional statistics — NSA counts, LSA counts, and locality counts — that are coherent across the Riḍván message series and the MUHJ-1963–1986 compilation. Several caveats govern their use.

LSA figures in the 1960s are approximations. The Riḍván 1964 message's goal of over 13,700 LSAs implies an opening baseline of roughly 4,600 — the figure preserved in growth time-series — but the messages do not deliver this as a precise audited count. LSA counts at this era depended on self-reporting by National Assemblies; administrative infrastructure in newly-opened territories was thin. Read the figure as a confident estimate, not a census.

The 69 NSA figure is well-sourced. The first UHJ election was conducted by 56 NSAs; the Riḍván 1964 message reports thirteen National Spiritual Assemblies added since the previous Riḍván, bringing the total to 69. The net count also reflects the dissolution of several regional National Spiritual Assemblies formed during the Crusade — administrative records, each requiring a UHJ directive. (growth time-series records both the 56 figure for 1963 and the 69 figure for 1964.)

No global membership figure exists in the primary corpus. The UHJ messages do not publish a cumulative worldwide Bahá’í population count for this era. The Riḍván 1965 message notes that India alone exceeded 140,000 believers — this is the most reliable partial figure from the Plan's first year. The frequently-cited "five million" or "seven million" global estimates are secondary (drawn from encyclopedias or the World Religion Database) and do not appear in the UHJ corpus.

The locality definition was expansive. A "locality" in the 1960s framework meant any place where at least one believer resided. The sharp rise from 15,168 to 69,500 between 1964 and 1973 records primarily the spread of individuals and families into new places — not the growth of communities. The 1970s–1980s push was about spreading the Faith geographically; the later cluster era reorganised that geographic spread on a different logic. The 2023 locality figure of 100,000 looks smaller than the 1980 peak of 106,000 not because the community shrank but because the definition narrowed to count localities with a residual community, not just a single resident.

Tier note. The statistics in this page are drawn from Tier C sources — Universal House of Justice Riḍván messages and the MUHJ-1963–1986 compilation — and from the growth time-series dataset, which aggregates those same sources. No secondary or institutional sources (ITC, scholarly) are used for the figures themselves. Where the data page attributes a row to "Riḍván 1965 (retrospective)" for the 15,168 localities figure, that is because the Riḍván 1965 message names the figure as the Plan's opening baseline.
