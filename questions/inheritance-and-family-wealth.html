What does the Faith teach about inheritance and passing wealth to children?

The Faith‘s answer has two layers that are often confused. During life, and by will, a believer has full discretion to dispose of property — including passing the whole of it to children if he wishes. If he dies without a will, a detailed law of intestacy applies — seven categories of heirs, fixed shares, the residence assigned to the eldest surviving son, and children’s portions held in trust if they are minors. Writing a will is a binding law (Aqdas ¶109). The intestacy provisions are the consequence of disobedience to that law, not the preferred path for passing wealth to the next generation.

The will comes first — binding law with spiritual weight

Bahá’u‘lláh’s command is direct:

“Unto everyone hath been enjoined the writing of a will. The testator should head this document with the adornment of the Most Great Name, bear witness therein unto the oneness of God in the Dayspring of His Revelation, and make mention, as he may wish, of that which is praiseworthy, so that it may be a testimony for him in the kingdoms of Revelation and Creation and a treasure with his Lord, the Supreme Protector, the Faithful.”

— The Kitáb-i-Aqdas, ¶109

The Universal House of Justice (1 July 1996) confirms that the writing of a testament is among the laws currently binding on the friends — not deferred, like some other Aqdas provisions. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá intensifies the stakes: making a will is “one of the binding laws of this mighty Dispensation”; failure to do so is “disobedience” and “non-fulfilment of the divine obligation.” The Research Department memorandum attached to that letter observes that a will allows the individual full discretion to specify how property — including the residence — is to be disposed of, and is “conducive to unity and agreement.”

The spiritual portion of the will — the testimony of faith headed with the Greatest Name — the believer drafts himself; the material provisions may follow local legal requirements but should reflect his own wishes. Aside from prerequisites in ¶109, the friends are free to formulate their wills as they please.

Full discretion in life and in the will — passing wealth to children

‘Abdu‘l-Bahá’s Tablets on inheritance, quoted in the 1996 House of Justice letter, remove ambiguity about children:

“In truth, one is entirely free during his lifetime to make provision in his will for the division and distribution of his property amongst his heirs in whatsoever manner he seeth fit… Under these conditions, should he bequeath his whole property to his children, he is but acting within his rights.”

— ‘Abdu’l-Bahá, Tablet on inheritance (extract in UHJ letter, 1 July 1996)

The primary obligation is therefore not to divide according to intestacy shares, but to write a will that carries out one‘s considered wishes with justice and equity. A parent who wishes children to inherit equally, or to receive support at different stages, or to benefit a daughter‘s education disproportionately because need is greater, may do so — provided Ḥuqúqu‘lláh and debts are honoured. Bahá’u‘lláh’s Question and Answer 69 confirms:

“A person hath full jurisdiction over his property. If he is able to discharge the Ḥuqúqu’lláh, and is free of debt, then all that is recorded in his will, and any declaration or avowal it containeth, shall be acceptable.”

— Kitáb-i-Aqdas, Q&A 69

Passing wealth to children is not merely permitted; it is one of the most natural expressions of the freedom the law affirms — so long as it is executed through a valid will rather than left to default.

When there is no will — the law of intestacy

If a believer dies intestate, the Aqdas distributes the estate among seven categories: children, spouse, father, mother, brothers, sisters, and teachers — in shares built on a system of 3,620 parts (Aqdas ¶20 and Q&A on inheritance). Salient features for family wealth include:

  • Children receive the largest collective portion; unborn children at the time of death receive doubled shares.
  • The residence and personal clothing of the deceased go to the male, not female, offspring — specifically the eldest surviving son; if he has died but left children, they inherit his share.
  • Minor children do not receive their portions outright; shares are entrusted to a reliable individual or company, invested in trade and business until they come of age, with the trustee receiving a due share of profit.
  • Division occurs only after funeral and burial expenses, settlement of debts, and payment of outstanding Ḥuqúqu’lláh.

‘Abdu’l-Bahá explains that the intestacy provisions — including the residence rule — are designed partly as incentive to write a will, not as the ideal pattern families should aim for. The residence assignment preserves the family home as a memorial and practical centre; it is not a general endorsement of primogeniture in civil law. In the absence of a will, however, it is binding.

Non-Bahá‘í heirs do not inherit under Bahá’í intestacy law (Synopsis, inheritance notes). A believer with non-Bahá’í relatives must address their provision explicitly in a will if he wishes them to receive property — another reason the House of Justice treats will-writing as urgent.

Ḥuqúqu’lláh, debts, and the order of obligations

Wealth passed to children is what remains after sacred and civil obligations. At death, unpaid Ḥuqúqu‘lláh is a debt of the estate, payable like any other before division (UHJ, 1 July 1996). The order confirmed in the Aqdas Questions and Answers: funeral and burial; debts; Ḥuqúqu’lláh; then inheritance.

A parent who intends to benefit children should therefore observe Ḥuqúqu’lláh during life where due, keep debts manageable, and make testamentary provision — or clear accounting — for any outstanding Right of God. Leaving children an inheritance encumbered by undisclosed obligations is a formula for discord precisely the will was meant to prevent.

The intestacy formula — what applies if one dies without a will

Because the will is the designed path, many believers never study intestacy until a death forces it. The summary below is not a substitute for legal counsel or future House of Justice legislation; it describes the binding outline in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas and Synopsis for when no testament exists.

Bahá’u’lláh divides inheritance into seven categories — children, spouse, father, mother, brothers, sisters, and teachers — using a base of 3,620 shares distributed in fixed proportions. Children collectively receive the largest portion; the spouse receives a defined share that varies depending on which other heirs survive; parents, siblings, and teachers receive smaller designated portions. If the deceased leaves offspring but no other specified heirs, children take two-thirds and the remaining third reverts to the House of Justice. If there are no specified heirs but nephews and nieces exist, two-thirds pass to them under stated conditions.

The residence and personal clothing of the deceased are not part of the general pool: they pass to the eldest surviving son, or to his children if he predeceased the parent. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá explains the wisdom: preserving the home as a place where family remember the deceased and pray for his soul. This rule applies in intestacy, not by moral recommendation alone.

Minor children do not receive lump sums; their shares are managed by a trustee and invested until majority, with the trustee entitled to a portion of profit — a provision that anticipates modern guardianship and trust law but is rooted in the Aqdas itself. The believer who writes a will may replicate or improve upon this pattern using civil instruments.

Understanding intestacy’s complexity is itself an incentive to write a will. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá describes the dispersion of an estate without testament as breeding “difficulties and disagreements” that the will was ordained to prevent.

Gender, equity, and the spirit of the law

The residence rule favouring the eldest son has troubled modern readers. ‘Abdu‘l-Bahá’s Tablets contextualise it: the law mirrors, without endorsing wholesale, historical distinctions accorded firstborn sons in prior dispensations and in civil law; the primary obligation remains the making of a will in which the testator may devise the residence to anyone. The Faith‘s principle of equality of men and women is not suspended in inheritance — the testator may equalise, favour a daughter’s need, or devise jointly. Intestacy‘s asymmetry is a default, not the Bahá’í ideal for family wealth.

Practical application for parents

Write a will early, revise it as life changes. Marriage, children, divorce, acquisition of property, and estrangement from relatives all require updated documents. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá counsels sealing and safeguarding the will in a secure place and treating the duty as continuous, not a once-in-old-age task.

Coordinate the will with Ḥuqúqu‘lláh records, insurance beneficiaries, and joint ownership. Beneficiary designations that contradict the will create the disputes the law seeks to avoid. The House of Justice advises legal wording for Ḥuqúqu’lláh provisions in wills, with support from Trustees’ Representatives where needed.

Provide for minors with the Aqdas model in view. Trust structures, guardianship nominations, and investment of minors‘ shares until maturity mirror the Tablet’s logic even when civil law offers different mechanisms.

Exercise freedom with justice, not with caprice. Full discretion is not a licence to punish heirs or to evade Ḥuqúqu’lláh. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá requires “due regard for the need to observe justice and equity.” Disinheritance out of spite fails that test even if it is legally possible in some jurisdictions.

Teach children the spiritual uses of wealth, not only its possession. ‘Abdu‘l-Bahá’s praise of wealth expended for knowledge, schools, orphans, and social welfare (Secret of Divine Civilization) frames inheritance as transmission of responsibility, not merely of assets.

Assemblies are not probate courts. They may assist believers with resources on will-writing, encourage observance of binding laws, and — where disputes among heirs who are Bahá’ís cannot be resolved — offer consultation. Testamentary disputes that require legal adjudication belong in civil courts unless the parties agree otherwise. The Assembly does not interpret shares or override a valid will.

Misunderstandings worth correcting

“Bahá’í inheritance law requires me to leave the house to my eldest son.” Only if you die without a will. A testator may devise the residence to anyone — jointly to all children, to a spouse, to a charity.

“I must divide wealth equally among my children.” The intestacy formula is not equal division. A will may equalise or differentiate; the Texts require equity, not arithmetic equality.

“My non-Bahá‘í spouse will automatically inherit.” Under Bahá’í intestacy, categories are defined and non-Bahá‘í heirs are excluded. A will must make express provision for a non-Bahá’í spouse or child if that is the testator’s intent.

“Inheritance law replaces Ḥuqúqu‘lláh.” The opposite. Ḥuqúqu’lláh and debts are prior charges; inheritance is calculated on what remains.

“Children automatically inherit the Faith of their parents.” They do not (UHJ guidance on registration). They may inherit property; belief is a separate matter.
