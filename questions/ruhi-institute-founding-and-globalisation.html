When was the Ruhi Institute founded, and how did its materials become the global sequence of courses?

The Ruhi Institute was formally established in 1976 in Puerto Tejada, Colombia, the product of a consolidation crisis in the Colombian Bahá’í community that began in the early 1970s. It was shaped by Farzam Arbab, a physicist who had pioneered to Colombia in 1969 and co-founded FUNDAEC in 1974, and directed from the outset by his wife Laurie Arbab, whose methods the Universal House of Justice later described as forging "the methods, morale, and materials of a dynamic approach to the advancement of the Cause." The Institute's materials were adopted worldwide not by design but by demonstrated performance: the UHJ's Four Year Plan (1996–2000) initiated a global institute-building movement, and by the time the Five Year Plan opened in 2001 it had become clear that the Ruhi sequence was filling that role more coherently than any alternative. The 28 December 2005 message formalised what the field had already decided.

Origins in Colombia and FUNDAEC

The impetus was a concrete problem. Following a period of mass teaching in Colombia, many newly enrolled Bahá’ís were finding that their communities lacked the capacity to sustain themselves between the visits of travelling teachers. Consultations among the Colombian National Spiritual Assembly in the early 1970s produced a diagnosis: the community needed not more teachers from outside but locally-formed servants who could anchor devotional life, educate children, and attract seekers without external support.

Two sister institutions emerged from this diagnosis in the same region and the same years. FUNDAEC (Fundación para la Aplicación y Enseñanza de la Ciencia — Foundation for the Application and Teaching of the Sciences) was co-founded by Farzam Arbab in 1974 in the Cali–Norte del Cauca region to address the broader challenge of rural education and human capacity-building, developing what became the Tutorial Learning System (SAT). The Ruhi Institute followed in 1976 in Puerto Tejada, focusing specifically on the spiritual and service capacity of the Bahá’í community. The cornerstone of its building was laid by Hand of the Cause Raḥmatu'lláh Muhájir, shortly before his passing in December 1979.

The Institute's name was a conscious memorial: Ruhí Arbáb, Farzam's father and secretary of the National Spiritual Assembly of Iran, had passed away in May 1975. The name joined a private act of remembrance to a public institutional mission.

FUNDAEC's pedagogical grammar — accompaniment, small groups, learning-in-action, low-cost reproducibility adapted to rural and post-colonial conditions — permeated the Ruhi materials from the beginning. Both institutions drew on the same insight: that education disconnected from the conditions and aspirations of a community produces passivity rather than agency.

Laurie Arbab served as founding director from 1976 to 1988, shaping the curriculum around what the Institute would later call paths of service: a basic deepening track, a community-development track, a youth-group track, and a children's education track. By 1985, nine courses had been published in Spanish, English, and French — and the Universal House of Justice, at the close of the Seven Year Plan (1979–1986), identified the Ruhi materials as a major factor in the consolidation of Bahá’í communities across Central America.

The UHJ's Endorsement

The Four Year Plan (1996–2000) named the training institute as the strategic instrument of the new era — the mechanism through which the process of entry by troops would be sustained rather than squandered. The 26 December 1995 letter to the Conference of the Continental Boards of Counsellors, launching the Plan, called for "permanent institutes designed to provide well-organized, formally conducted programs of training on a regular schedule." National Spiritual Assemblies were encouraged to develop their own materials; the Ruhi sequence was one option among several.

What changed during the Plan's execution was not the UHJ's recommendation but the community's experience. By the time the Five Year Plan opened, the evidence was in. The 9 January 2001 message to the Counsellors — the most consequential single letter of the institute era — theorised what the field had demonstrated: "The value of a sequence of courses, each one following the other in a logical pattern and each one building on the achievements of the previous ones, has become abundantly clear." The message did not name the Ruhi Institute explicitly. It named the principle: a coherent sequence whose internal logic moves participants from spiritual formation through specific acts of service, each book a formation unit that ends not at a certificate but at a practice.

The 17 January 2003 message confirmed the operational architecture: the "parallel process" of individuals moving through the sequence and clusters moving through stages of growth were to run simultaneously, each energising the other. Core activities — study circles, children's classes, devotional meetings — were identified as the fruits the sequence was expected to produce in the life of the cluster.

What completed the universalisation was the 28 December 2005 message to the Bahá’ís of the World (transmitted through National Spiritual Assemblies), which announced that the Ruhi materials "should constitute the main sequence of courses for institutes everywhere." The day before, the 27 December 2005 message to the Counsellors' Conference had theorised the cluster framework in which that sequence would operate — naming "two complementary, reinforcing movements" of individuals through institute courses and clusters advancing from stage to stage. By that point most national communities had already adopted the Ruhi sequence in practice. The 28 December letter made it canonical — not as an administrative directive but as a recognition that the community had, through field experience, resolved the question the Four Year Plan had left open.

From Regional to Global

The spread of the Ruhi materials preceded the UHJ's formal endorsement by more than a decade. Trinidad & Tobago adopted them in October 1982, Panama in February 1983, the Central African Republic in April 1984. By 1991, Hindi translations were being used in India to train children's class teachers. The first published English editions — what would become Books 1–4 — appeared between 1994 and 1996 through Palabra Publications in Florida.

This organic spread carried both an asset and a risk. The asset was demonstrated adaptability: the same materials were functioning in rural Colombia, Caribbean islands, and sub-Saharan Africa. The risk was that local adoption without institutional coordination could fragment the learning the materials were generating. The institute-process architecture introduced in the Four Year Plan — with regional institutes, coordinators, and reflection meetings — provided the coordination mechanism that the materials' global spread required.

The 28 December 2010 message marked the pedagogical maturation of this global system, consolidating how institutes worldwide were multiplying tutors and deepening the practice component of the sequence. Riḍván 2010 named the three irreducible elements: "the study circle, the tutor and the curriculum of the Ruhi Institute." The Institute had by this point ceased to be a Colombian institution that happened to export its materials; it had become the centre of a worldwide learning system in which the field's accumulated experience fed back into curriculum revision and the Institute's publications, translated into many languages worldwide, anchored a shared form of community life across every cluster engaged in systematic growth.

The Evolving Sequence

At the close of the Seven Year Plan, the Ruhi Institute had nine courses. The published main sequence that the community now knows took its formal shape between 1994 and 2006:

  • Books 1–4 appeared between 1994 and 1996.
  • Books 6 and 7 were published in 2000 and 2001, bringing the sequence to its operational core.
  • Book 5, Releasing the Powers of Junior Youth, was published in 2006, formally integrating the Junior Youth Spiritual Empowerment Programme into the sequence.

This left Books 8–14 as the sequence's open frontier. On 1 January 2022, the Universal House of Justice announced that the Ruhi Institute aims to complete its full fourteen-book curriculum by 2031, after which no further materials are planned — framing the sequence as a bounded, finite architecture rather than a perpetually-extending programme. The fourteen-book design, when complete, maps onto the three dimensions of endeavour introduced in the 2021 messages: Books 1–8 serving expansion and consolidation, Books 11–14 addressing social action and participation in the discourses of society.

The most recent major milestone was the publication of Book 8, The Covenant of Bahá’u’lláh, in a first edition (1.1.1.PE) in March 2026 — over five hundred pages, three units, spanning the Centre of the Covenant, the Guardianship, and the Universal House of Justice. Books 9 (Gaining an Historical Perspective) and 10 (Building Vibrant Communities) circulate in pre-publication form; Books 11–14 remain in development. The sequence that began as a set of deepening booklets for rural Colombian villages in the early 1970s has reached, in Book 8, the doctrinal centre of the Faith itself.
