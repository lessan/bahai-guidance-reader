What were the main milestones of Bahá’í World Centre development on Mount Carmel from 1953 to 2026?

The vision for Mount Carmel as the spiritual and administrative seat of the Bahá’í Faith was given scriptural grounding in Bahá’u’lláh’s own Lawḥ-i-Karmil (Tablet of Carmel), and Shoghi Effendi translated that vision into a concrete physical programme during the final decade of his ministry. The Arc project — the series of buildings on the arc-shaped pathway of Mount Carmel — unfolded across roughly fifty years, from the construction of the International Archives Building in the 1950s through to the completion of the Shrine of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá during the Nine Year Plan. These milestones mark not only architectural achievement but the progressive institutionalisation of the global Bahá’í administrative centre.

Shoghi Effendi’s vision and foundation (1950s)

The Lawḥ-i-Karmil, revealed by Bahá’u’lláh, designates Mount Carmel as the site of the Bahá’í world centre and contains imagery that Shoghi Effendi interpreted as a prophecy of the arc of institutions that would one day crown the mountain. As Guardian, Shoghi Effendi worked to translate this spiritual designation into physical reality.

The first enduring Arc building was the International Archives Building, completed under Shoghi Effendi‘s direct supervision. The Arc Project concept page, drawing on Priceless Pearl and the early message corpus, places this completion in 1957. The building was designed in the style of classical Greek architecture and housed the sacred relics, manuscripts, and photographs of the central figures of the Faith — making it both a repository and a monument to the early history of the Bahá’í Revelation.

During this same period the superstructure of the Shrine of the Báb was completed, as documented in Citadel of Faith (Shoghi Effendi‘s messages to America 1947–1957): “the construction of the Arcade and Superstructure of the Shrine of the Báb on Mount Carmel” is named as one of the “twin outward material achievements of the decade.” These two structures — the Archives Building and the completed Shrine — constituted Shoghi Effendi’s enduring legacy at the World Centre before his passing in November 1957.

The Arc buildings — timeline

The Universal House of Justice announced the initial steps for erection of the Seat of the Universal House of Justice at Riḍván 1973, marking the victorious conclusion of the Nine Year Plan. The design by architect Ḥusayn Amánat was accepted on 7 February 1974. Excavation was completed by 18 March 1976; the $5.5 million marble contract was placed in Italy, drawing on Pentelikon marble from Greece; and construction formally commenced when the first marble consignments arrived in Haifa on 24 March 1977. On 2 February 1983, the Universal House of Justice occupied its newly constructed permanent Seat on Mount Carmel — an event the House described as a fulfilment of the Tablet of Carmel. The Seat’s completion came during the Seven Year Plan (1979–1986).

Then, on 31 August 1987, the House of Justice launched the Mount Carmel Projects in a letter that named the full scope of what remained to be built: the Terraces of the Shrine of the Báb, the International Teaching Centre Building, the Centre for the Study of the Texts, and the International Bahá’í Library. Faríburz Sahbá was appointed Project Manager. The House assessed that a $50 million reserve and annual fundraising of $20–25 million would be needed — the largest single construction undertaking in the Faith’s history to that point.

Progress moved in stages through the Six Year Plan (1986–1992) and the Three and Four Year Plans that followed:

  • May 1990: Work commenced on extension of the main Terrace of the Shrine of the Báb, on the 146th anniversary of the Declaration of the Báb.
  • September 1991: Ground broken for construction of the Centre for the Study of the Texts and the extension to the International Archives Building.
  • Riḍván 1996: The Three Year Plan goal of $74 million was met; the structural framework of the Centre for the Study of the Texts and the Archives Extension had been raised; seven of the terraces below the Shrine of the Báb were completed.
  • 1999: The Centre for the Study of the Texts — the permanent seat of the Research Department of the Universal House of Justice — was completed on the Arc. The House had described the forthcoming Centre in 1987 as the “efflorescence” of the Research Department, language indicating it was not merely a physical home but the institutional setting in which textual-scholarly work would come to fullest expression.
  • 14 January 2001: The International Teaching Centre occupied its permanent seat in its new building on the Arc on Mount Carmel, ending its period of operation from 10 Haparsim Street, Haifa, where it had been based since 1984.

The Terraces

The Terraces of the Shrine of the Báb — eighteen terraces descending and ascending the slope of Mount Carmel, framing the golden-domed Shrine at their centre — were the most visually dramatic component of the Mount Carmel Projects. Construction spanned from 1990 through 2001, with seven lower terraces already complete by Riḍván 1996.

The full completion and inauguration took place at a gathering of approximately 4,500 friends from virtually all countries of the world, convened 21–25 May 2001 on Mount Carmel. The Universal House of Justice, in its message at those events, described the occasion as one in which “the Bahá’í Cause emerges as a visible and compelling reality on the global stage.” The Terraces, running nearly one kilometre along the northern face of the Carmel range and aligned with the historic downtown of Haifa, were later inscribed on UNESCO’s World Heritage List together with the Shrine of the Báb — a recognition the House of Justice noted in its 30 December 2021 message. The May 2001 gathering marked the formal completion of the Arc project as it had been defined since 1987: all principal buildings occupied, all terraces landscaped and open.

Shrine of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá

The question of a permanent Shrine for ‘Abdu’l-Bahá — who passed away in Haifa on 28 November 1921 and was interred in the Shrine of the Báb on Mount Carmel — had long been on the horizon of the world community. The construction of a dedicated Shrine came to the fore when the Universal House of Justice announced it at Riḍván 2019, locating the Shrine in the vicinity of the Riḍván Garden in ‘Akká. On 7 May 2019 the UHJ announced the appointment of Hossein Amanat (son of the Seat’s architect Ḥusayn Amánat) as architect, and the Shrine Fund was inaugurated.

Construction was interrupted: a major fire struck the site on 8 April 2022, though with no injuries and with insurance coverage; a 4 July 2022 update confirmed a substantial delay but stated no increased fundraising would be required. Plan documentation records the Shrine among anticipated Phase 1 achievements of the Nine Year Plan (Riḍván 2022 – Riḍván 2026), but no UHJ dedication message confirming its completion appears in the corpus as of Riḍván 2026. Construction was delayed by the April 2022 fire; the project’s status should be treated as reported in plan pages rather than as a confirmed dedication.

The Shrine of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá is distinct from the Mount Carmel Arc buildings and from the Terraces project. It is located near the Riḍván Garden in ‘Akká rather than on the Carmel slope, though it is associated with the same World Centre complex. Its completion represents the last of the major building objectives that had remained outstanding at the close of the 2001 Arc inauguration.
