How should a Bahá’í family or community prepare for Ayyám-i-Há?

Ayyám-i-Há — the Intercalary Days, the Days of Há — are the four or five days immediately before the Fast, ordained for hospitality, rejoicing, charity, and gift-giving. They are not among the nine work-free Holy Days; their character is festive and outward-turning rather than commemorative. Preparation is therefore practical: plan acts of generosity, open the home, involve children in giving, and coordinate community events without importing the solemnity of Riḍván or the suspension-of-work structure of the Twin Birthdays. The law is brief; the field of expression is wide.

The Nature of Ayyám-i-Há

The Badí' calendar consists of nineteen months of nineteen days, with intercalary days added so the year aligns with the solar cycle. The Báb did not specify where the excess days fell; the Kitáb-i-Aqdas resolved the question by placing them immediately before the month of 'Alá', the period of fasting, and designating them Ayyám-i-Há — days associated with the letter Há, whose abjad value is five, corresponding to the maximum number of intercalary days.

Bahá’u’lláh reveals the spiritual posture directly:

"It behooveth the people of Bahá, throughout these days, to provide good cheer for themselves, their kindred and, beyond them, the poor and needy, and with joy and exultation to hail and glorify their Lord, to sing His praise and magnify His Name; and when they end—these days of giving that precede the season of restraint—let them enter upon the Fast."

— Bahá’u’lláh, Kitáb-i-Aqdas, ¶16

The editorial notes to the Kitáb-i-Aqdas draw out three verbs — feasting, rejoicing, charity — and cite a letter written on behalf of Shoghi Effendi explaining that "the intercalary days are specially set aside for hospitality, the giving of gifts, etc." The days are therefore days of giving before the season of restraint: abundance turned outward, then the Fast's inward discipline.

Since the worldwide implementation of the Badí' calendar from Naw-Rúz 172 (2015), the number of intercalary days varies with the timing of the vernal equinox in successive years — four in most years, five in others. Communities consult the calendar issued by the Universal House of Justice for the exact Gregorian dates each year. The days always immediately precede the Fast; they are not movable for local convenience.

Unlike Naw-Rúz, which opens the new year as a feast after the Fast ends, Ayyám-i-Há belongs to the threshold — the last generosity before abstention. The tone is gratitude and extension, not historical commemoration. There is no Tablet of Visitation obligation, no fixed hour of observance, and no suspension of work comparable to the nine Holy Days treated in holy-days-essentials-and-scaling.

Shoghi Effendi's description of the Kitáb-i-Aqdas places the Intercalary Days among the book's festival ordinances alongside Naw-Rúz, the Nineteen Day Feast, and the established Holy Days. The calendar architecture is deliberate: nineteen months of nineteen days, the Days of Há, then nineteen days of fasting, then Naw-Rúz. A community that neglects the intercalary period arrives at the Fast without the law's intended transition — from open-handed joy to disciplined restraint. Families and clusters that mark the days poorly often report the Fast feels abrupt; those who prepare giving and hospitality well often describe the Fast as a natural continuation rather than a sudden deprivation.

Gift-Giving — Substance Over Scale

Gift-giving is established practice, not optional decoration. The House has not legislated what gifts must be, to whom, or in what amount. The Shoghi Effendi letter cited in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas notes sets the intercalary days aside for "hospitality, the giving of gifts, etc." — the etc. is deliberate room for cultural expression.

The principle is extension: gifts express the law's outward movement from family to kindred to the poor and needy. Extravagance is not the measure; intention is. A family might give children modest means to select gifts for siblings; a community might collect for a local shelter; a cluster might support a social action initiative. The Bahá’í World volumes record children delivering clothes to orphans, visiting hospital wards with songs and presents, and collecting goods as part of Ayyám-i-Há — field illustrations (Tier F) of the law's charitable vector, not prescribed formats.

Preparation tip: decide before the first intercalary day what the giving budget — of money, time, or attention — will be. Impulse scattered across five days is weaker than a plan made in consultation. Avoid commercial holiday inflation; Ayyám-i-Há is not a Bahá’í Christmas and loses its meaning when reduced to consumption.

Gifts within the family need not be expensive to be lawful. The verse pairs good cheer for "themselves, their kindred" with provision for the poor; intrafamily generosity and outward charity are one movement, not competitors. Some communities encourage children to make rather than buy gifts — drawings, baked goods, written prayers — so that the habit formed is attention rather than acquisition. Others designate one intercalary day for monetary collection and the remaining days for personal visits; the pattern matters less than the orientation toward others.

Hospitality — Opening the Home and the Community

Hospitality is the day's second pillar. Bahá’u’lláh’s verse moves from "themselves, their kindred" to "beyond them, the poor and needy." Hospitality begins at the dinner table and extends to whoever lacks cheer.

For a family, preparation may include: inviting friends or neighbours for a simple meal; including someone recently isolated or bereaved; coordinating with other families so children have companions during the school holiday that often overlaps these days in the Northern Hemisphere. For a community, it may mean a collective gathering — potluck, devotional, children's programme — whose primary purpose is warmth and welcome rather than formal outreach.

’Abdu’l-Bahá, in a Tablet in Days of Remembrance, links true hospitality to character: the heavenly table of God's loving-kindness is spread when people offer hospitality through "their character and their conduct." Preparation is therefore moral as well as logistical — clearing the calendar for presence, not only polishing the guest room.

Hospitality also scales down. A single widowed believer who shares tea with a neighbour has observed the spirit of the days. A student who cooks for housemates before the Fast begins extends good cheer without a banquet budget. The law does not require entertaining crowds; it requires turning outward. Isolated believers should reach out rather than wait to be included — and communities with capacity should notice who might otherwise spend the intercalary days alone.

Children's Activities

Children are natural protagonists of Ayyám-i-Há. The law asks the community to provide good cheer; children both receive and generate it. Age-appropriate preparation might include:

  • Giving projects — selecting toys or clothes to donate, preparing cards for neighbours, or contributing to a community collection with parental guidance.
  • Creative expression — songs, skits, or art that express joy and praise; no performance standard is required.
  • Story and devotion — brief readings about generosity, the Fast that follows, or the calendar's structure; keep tone celebratory, not instructional in the abstract.
  • Visits — nursing homes, hospitals, or shelters where local custom and safeguarding policies permit; accompanied visits model charity as presence, not only transaction.

Ruhi and children's class curricula do not prescribe an Ayyám-i-Há module; the absence of institutional programme is freedom. Parents and teachers draw on the Kitáb-i-Aqdas verse and on the community's own traditions. Simplicity suits the age group: a child who delivers one gift with understanding has observed the law better than a community that stages elaborate entertainment without giving.

Junior youth groups have sometimes organised service projects during this period — collecting for food banks, writing notes to elders, cleaning a shared space — because the age group grasps justice acutely and responds to concrete tasks. Children's class teachers can link the calendar story briefly: the excess days placed before fasting, the letter Há, the praise of God before restraint. The narrative gives children a place in the year's rhythm without burdening them with adult calendar complexity.

Community Events — Scaling Without a Template

The Universal House of Justice has not legislated community formats for Ayyám-i-Há. Unlike the Twin Holy Days, where work suspension and gathering are required, the intercalary days carry no parallel institutional checklist. Scaling follows capacity, as with Holy Day observance generally:

A few families might rotate open houses across the four or five days, ensuring someone isolated is included each day.

A cluster might hold one central gathering — devotional readings, music, shared meal, collection for a agreed charitable purpose — and invite friends of the Faith without reframing the event as teaching.

A larger community might coordinate service projects (food banks, care packages, visits) across the period, using the extra day in a five-day year for a distinct act of charity.

The Framework for Action observes that Holy Day commemorations, with prayers, stories, and songs, stir the heart; Ayyám-i-Há shares the festive element without the historical narrative anchor of Riḍván or the Twin Birthdays. Community planners lean on generosity and hospitality as the content, not on re-enactment.

Consultation in the Local Assembly or cluster agency should settle: dates and venues; fund handling if collections are taken; safeguarding for children's involvement; and inclusion of seekers and neighbours without pressure. Record lessons after the Fast begins, while memory is fresh, for the following year.

Where a community also runs an intensive programme of growth, Ayyám-i-Há should not be cancelled for lack of time; it should be simplified. A devotional circle with shared refreshments and a collection for the poor fulfils the law as surely as a citywide festival. The error is treating the days as optional padding in the calendar rather than as the ordained preparation for the Fast — a preparation that trains the community, year after year, in the habit of generosity the Faith will increasingly require of it.

Distinction From Other Holidays

Confusion often arises because Ayyám-i-Há falls in late February or early March in the Gregorian calendar — near secular carnival seasons, Christian Lent, or cultural gift-giving occasions. The distinctions worth preserving:

Ayyám-i-Há is not a substitute for missing Naw-Rúz or Riḍván observance. It is not the Day of the Covenant or the Ascension of ’Abdu’l-Bahá — those anniversaries have their own status. Importing Santa-Claus logic, Valentine commerce, or birthday-party egocentrism misreads the law: the centre is praise of God and provision for others, not receipt of goods.

The Fast follows immediately. Preparation should not schedule so heavily that the community arrives at the Fast exhausted, nor so lightly that the intercalary days pass as ordinary time. The rhythm is deliberate: generosity, then restraint.

Practical Planning — A Working Sequence

Four to six weeks before: Confirm dates from the official Badí' calendar. Brief the Local Assembly or cluster institute board if a community event is proposed.

Two to three weeks before: Families decide giving and hospitality plans; communities assign coordinators for venue, food, charitable recipient, and children's activities.

One week before: Shop or collect for gifts and charitable donations; send invitations; confirm safeguarding and permissions for visits.

During the days: Prioritize presence over production. Recite prayers and magnify God's Name with joy, as the verse directs. Extend hospitality beyond the usual circle.

After the last intercalary day: Enter the Fast without prolonging festivities. Debrief briefly for next year's learning.

Employers do not owe special leave for Ayyám-i-Há; believers do not press for work suspension that the law does not require. Where collective leave is possible — school holidays, for instance — use it for hospitality and charity rather than for retreat from community life.

Pioneers and cross-cultural families: customs differ widely. A family recently arrived in a new country should neither import a home culture's carnival wholesale nor adopt local secular habits that contradict the law's emphasis on charity and praise of God. Consultation with the Local Assembly and with experienced families in the jurisdiction helps find lawful expression that feels natural in the new setting. The constant is the Kitáb-i-Aqdas verse; the form is contextual.

Devotional content: communities sometimes read the Kitáb-i-Aqdas passage on the Intercalary Days, sing prayers from Bahá’í Prayers, or share passages from Days of Remembrance associated with joy and hospitality. No selection is mandatory. The praise and magnification the verse names are the standard; readings and music are means, not ends.

Points friends often raise

"Ayyám-i-Há is a Holy Day like Riḍván." It is ordained and spiritually significant, but it is not among the nine work-free Holy Days and does not carry the same gathering and work-suspension obligations.

"We must follow a set community programme." The House has not legislated one. Capacity and consultation determine form.

"Gift-giving is only for children." The law addresses the people of Bahá collectively; adults give and receive hospitality too.

"Charity should go only to Bahá’í funds." The verse names the poor and needy; charitable extension is broader than the Fund, though contributions to the Fund may form part of a community's plan.

"Five days always." Since Badí' calendar implementation, the count is four or five depending on the vernal equinox.

"Ayyám-i-Há replaces missing Fast discipline." The days precede the Fast; they do not attenuate it. Feasting ends; restraint begins.
