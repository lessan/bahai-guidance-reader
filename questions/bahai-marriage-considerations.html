How should a Bahá’í approach marriage — within and outside the Bahá’í community?

The Bahá’í approach to marriage rests on two Tier A provisions from the Kitáb-i-Aqdas: parental consent from all living parents is required, and the couple must recite the specific verse ordained by Bahá’u’lláh. Bahá’ís may marry non-Bahá’ís — the Faith does not require religious endogamy. The spiritual aspiration is a union that advances both partners and becomes, in Bahá’u’lláh’s words, “a fortress for well-being and salvation” (family-life compilation, from Bahá’í Prayers).

Parental consent — the binding requirement

Bahá’u’lláh prescribes in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas that before a Bahá’í marriage can proceed, consent must be obtained from all living parents of both parties — typically four people. This consent is not a formality or a courtesy; it is a legal requirement of Bahá’í law. If any living parent withholds consent, the marriage cannot take place under Bahá’í law, regardless of the couple’s age or circumstances.

Shoghi Effendi explained the rationale: Bahá’u’lláh required the consent of all living parents “to strengthen the social fabric, to knit closer the ties of the home, to place a certain gratitude and respect in the hearts of children for those who have given them life” — and this applies “whether the parents are Bahá’ís or non-Bahá’ís” (25 October 1947). Marriage is not merely a private arrangement between two individuals but an event that bonds two family lines.

Practically, this means the couple seeks consent before they set a date, not as a formality after the decision is made. Where a parent initially withholds consent, the appropriate response is patient, respectful consultation — not legal manoeuvre or waiting for the parent to relent under social pressure. In exceptional cases (e.g., estrangement, unknown parent), Local Spiritual Assemblies provide guidance on how to proceed.

The ordained verse

Every Bahá’í marriage ceremony requires both parties to recite, in the presence of witnesses, the verse ordained by Bahá’u’lláh: “We will all, verily, abide by the Will of God.” This verse must be spoken regardless of what other civil or religious ceremonies also take place. Many Bahá’í couples have a civil ceremony (required for legal recognition in most countries) and a Bahá’í ceremony; the Bahá’í ceremony requires only this verse, the presence of witnesses, and usually oversight by the Local Spiritual Assembly.

The verse is significant: both parties declare “We will all, verily, abide by the Will of God” — a joint submission to divine will rather than a mutual promise contingent on the couple’s feelings alone. The Covenant, not private sentiment, is the foundation the ceremony names.

Marrying outside the Bahá’í community

Bahá’ís may marry non-Bahá’ís, and many do. The non-Bahá’í partner is not required to convert, to study the Faith, or to express belief — but they must consent to the Bahá’í ceremony, including the recitation of the verse. The same parental-consent law applies to the non-Bahá’í partner’s parents: the Guardian ruled that “under all circumstances the consent of the parents of both parties is required” when one party is not a Bahá’í (12 August 1941). A non-Bahá’í who is not willing to participate in the Bahá’í ceremony presents a practical challenge; LSAs navigate these cases individually.

When children arrive in an inter-faith household, questions arise about their spiritual education — those are addressed in childrens-spiritual-education-mixed-faith-home. The non-Bahá’í partner’s religious practice is not treated as an obstacle; the Bahá’í principle of the oneness of religion means the Bahá’í partner honours the other faith’s validity.

Marriage is recommended to believers by Bahá’u’lláh (Shoghi Effendi, 6 March 1953), and the compilation on preserving Bahá’í marriages commends the spiritual depth of a union in which both partners share the Faith. Interfaith marriage is not prohibited; a shared spiritual framework has practical benefits for family life, but religious endogamy is not a legal requirement.

Permanence and divorce

The Bahá’í teachings regard marriage as a lifelong commitment. Bahá’u’lláh’s word is strong: divorce is “strongly condemned.” However, it is not prohibited in all circumstances. Where marriage becomes untenable, Bahá’í law requires a “year of patience” — a twelve-month period of separation during which the couple is encouraged to attempt reconciliation, ideally with community support. Divorce is only permissible after this year has elapsed without reconciliation.

The year of patience is not a bureaucratic delay; it is a spiritual discipline. The guidance is that a Bahá’í approaching separation should approach it with the same seriousness as the original marriage: honestly, prayerfully, and in consultation with trusted community members. The LSA may provide support during this period, though it does not adjudicate the couple’s relationship.

The family as a unit of civilisation

The larger context for Bahá’í marriage guidance is the understanding that the family is the basic unit of a new social order. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá explains that “a family is a nation in miniature” — the conditions surrounding the family surround the nation. Marriage built on consent, honesty, and mutual service to God provides the stable foundation this vision requires.
