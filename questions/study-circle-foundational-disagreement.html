How should a tutor handle a study circle where one participant disagrees with foundational teachings of the Faith?

A participant who disagrees with foundational teachings is not a facilitation problem (which the dominance question addresses) but a consultative-pedagogical one — the tutor‘s task is to honour the participant’s freedom of investigation while keeping the circle on the text, distinguishing genuine inquiry (which the Ruhi method actively welcomes) from a campaign that would redirect the circle for everyone else (which it does not). The standard move is to read the relevant passage together and recur to the meaning the text itself bears, separating personal opinion from textual reading; where the disagreement persists into a campaign that derails the circle, the tutor accompanies the participant privately and, where appropriate, refers the question upward through the channels the Covenant supplies.

Inquiry is what the institute process is for

Independent investigation is itself a foundational teaching, and the Ruhi materials assume it. A participant who arrives at Book 1 with serious questions about what Bahá’u’lláh teaches is doing what the institute process was designed to receive — they are not aberrant, and they are not to be silenced. Book 1 is explicit that the tutor’s posture is co-learning, not custody of the right answer: “The relationship between the tutor and the other participants is not one of teacher-to-student; all are consciously engaged in a process in which everyone is seeking to learn” (Ruhi Book 1, preface). The doctrinal grounding for this welcome is treated more fully in the sibling independent-investigation question; the present page is the meeting-prep application.

The accompanying discipline, in the same preface, is what the tutor must not do with the inquiry. “This challenge is seldom met by asking questions such as, ‘What does this mean to you?‘ Questions of this kind tend to reduce knowledge and truth to the level of opinion. And it then proves difficult to create an atmosphere in which consultation among the members of the group actually gives rise to increased understanding.” The tutor who answers a foundational question by polling the room for reactions has converted a question that has a textual answer into a contest of subjectivities. The Ruhi response is the opposite move: the participant‘s question is treated as serious enough to deserve the text — not the room’s feelings about it.

Distinguishing inquiry from campaign

The harder line to draw is between the participant who asks — “why does the Faith teach X?” — and the participant who systematically attempts to convince the others that X is false. The first is exactly what the institute process is designed to receive. The second is a different activity altogether: it has shifted from study of the text to advocacy against it, and it reshapes the purpose the circle has been gathered for. Book 1 is equally firm that “the tutor is not a detached and passive facilitator of discussion either” (Ruhi Book 1, preface) — passivity that lets a campaign redirect the circle is as much a failure of tutor discipline as authoritarianism. The tutor’s discipline is to honour the first and not to permit the second to colonise the room.

The 1999 compilation Issues Related to the Study of the Bahá’í Faith is the Universal House of Justice’s most direct treatment of where this line falls. Its forwarding letter (Department of the Secretariat, 7 April 1999) names the central problem as a “campaign of internal opposition to the Teachings” that seeks to “recast the entire Faith into a sociopolitical ideology alien to Bahá’u’lláh’s intent” — and that, while “purporting to accept the legitimacy of the Guardianship and the Universal House of Justice as twin successors of Bahá’u’lláh and the Center of His Covenant,” “attempts to cast doubt on the nature and scope of the authority conferred on them in the Writings.” The compilation’s INTRODUCTION is equally clear that academic and personal inquiry are welcome: “The letters in this booklet were written on behalf of the Universal House of Justice over the past few years to believers who, conscious of the high importance Bahá’u’lláh attaches to the pursuit of knowledge and the use of reason, had raised various questions regarding the scholarly study of the Faith” — and the line is drawn not between inquiry and obedience but between inquiry-within-the-Covenant and inquiry-deployed-against-it.

For the tutor in the room, the practical effect is to ask, quietly, what is actually happening. A participant who keeps returning to the same hard passage across several sessions, asking it differently each time, is investigating. A participant who is using each session as a pulpit to argue that other passages are wrong, that the institutions are illegitimate, or that other participants should withhold their assent until “more is known” has crossed into a different activity — and the tutor‘s responsibility shifts from accompaniment to protection of the circle’s purpose for everyone else.

The link to consultation — and where it ends

A study circle is not a consultation in the technical sense, but the same disciplines apply: detachment from one‘s own opinion once expressed, frankness without belittling, humility. The sibling page the consultation question carries the full treatment of those disciplines and the vote-as-backstop; it is not duplicated here. What must be added for this question is where the analogy stops. A foundational disagreement that extends to the legitimacy of the Covenant institutions is not a matter for the circle to deliberate to resolution — the 2 July 1996 letter in Issues Related to the Study of the Bahá’í Faith names “methods and channels for the voicing of such grievances or disagreements” and forbids the pattern in which believers, “instead of confining their protests… to the channels and agencies which are plentifully provided,” generalise their dissent until they “arouse widespread disaffection in the community.” Bahá’u’lláh’s prohibition of contention applies directly: “Conflict and contention are categorically forbidden in His Book.” The tutor applies consultation’s disciplines inside the circle; covenant-level disputes exit the circle to the channels the Covenant supplies.

The text as the discipline

The single most reliable move, in either case, is the one the sibling dominance page also names: let‘s read that passage again. This is not authoritarian; it is the form of the study circle reasserting itself. Where a participant’s disagreement is with what the text actually says, the slow honest reading often dissolves the disagreement, or at least clarifies what is in dispute. Where the disagreement is with what the text means, the next move is to read ‘Abdu’l-Bahá or Shoghi Effendi on the same passage — interpretation is conferred (see Authority and infallibility §2), and the tutor’s recourse to the interpretive tier is the form the Ruhi method uses to keep the question grounded.

Book 1 cautions tutors that prolonging deliberations “by introducing many related but peripheral concepts tends to diminish the effectiveness of the material”; the same caution applies here. A foundational disagreement that branches into ten more questions across one session is no longer being studied — it is being multiplied. The tutor‘s discipline is to follow the question to the relevant text, sit with it together, and not to follow each branching to its end inside the circle’s hour.

When the disagreement touches the Covenant

If the participant’s disagreement extends to the authority of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá, the Guardianship, or the Universal House of Justice — that is, to the Covenant itself — the tutor faces a different question, and the answer is not in the tutor’s hand. Issues Related to the Study of the Bahá’í Faith names this directly: “those who actually enter the Bahá’í community have accepted, by their own free will, to follow the Teachings in their entirety, understanding that, if doubts and disagreements arise in the process of translating the Teachings into practice, the final arbiter is, by the explicit authority of the Revealed Text, the Universal House of Justice.” The compilation also names the channels through which a believer with such a disagreement may raise it — “methods and channels for the voicing of such grievances or disagreements” — and notes that what is forbidden is not raising the question but bypassing those channels: believers who, “instead of confining their protests… to the channels and agencies which are plentifully provided,” go on “publicly and privily assailing” the institutions until they “arouse widespread disaffection in the community.”

The tutor‘s responsibility, in such a case, is twofold. Inside the circle, recur to the text and do not let the foundational question settle the circle’s reading of every other passage in Book 1. Outside the circle, accompany the participant privately, and — where the disagreement is serious — refer the question to the local Local Spiritual Assembly or to the institute coordinator and Auxiliary Board members, whose office is precisely this kind of accompaniment. The fuller doctrinal treatment lives in Authority and infallibility §4 and §7.

Accompanying the participant privately

Public correction is rarely the right move. Private accompaniment — in which the tutor and the participant read together at a deeper level than the group‘s pace permits — often is. The English regional junior-youth coordinator’s formulation in R.O.G. 26 — “The coordinators that I have learned the most from are those with whom I can act, reflect, and consult” — names the posture the tutor should bring to that private work: not adjudicating from above but accompanying with a learning posture, in time outside the formal session. That accompaniment is pre-referral, not a substitute for institutional channels when the disagreement crosses into covenant authority; at that threshold the tutor refers upward even while continuing to read with the participant privately.

A short conversation of the form “I‘d like us to look at that passage in more depth than the group‘s hour permits — would you read it with me this week?” converts the foundational question from a disruption of the circle into the participant’s own deepening, and frequently produces the second-order effect that the participant returns to the next session less invested in the disagreement and more invested in the inquiry that lay underneath it.

Pastoral caveats

Four counterfeits of the corrective move are worth naming. First, do not silence a sincere question — a participant who learns that their hard questions are unwelcome will either leave or learn to dissemble, and neither outcome serves the institute process. Second, do not treat doubt as bad faith — Book 1‘s discipline is co-learning, and the assumption that a participant’s question is an attack rather than an inquiry is itself a failure of the tutor‘s posture. Third, do not let one participant’s continued protest stall the rest of the group‘s progress through the book — the circle was gathered for everyone, and where the foundational disagreement has become a campaign, the tutor’s responsibility to the others overrides the responsibility to indulge a single participant‘s repetition. Fourth, do not pretend the foundational teachings are a matter of opinion — Book 1’s caution against opinion-surfacing prompts applies most acutely here, because the participant‘s foundational disagreement is precisely the kind of question that cannot be honestly answered by “and what does everyone else think?” The tutor’s discipline is to recur to what the text says, not to what each participant feels about it.
