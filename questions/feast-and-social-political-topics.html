How should the institution of the Feast handle social/political topics that come up in the consultative portion?

The consultative portion of the Feast is a forum on the life of the local community — its devotional rhythm, its plans, the work of expansion and consolidation, the welfare of its members — and the friends bring to it the social, civic, and professional concerns of their lives precisely insofar as those concerns bear on that life. What it is not, and was never intended to be, is a venue for partisan-political contestation. The chair's task — and behind the chair, the Local Spiritual Assembly's — is to keep the consultation focused on principle and on the community's own work, redirecting matters that have become attached to political faction without silencing the friend who has raised something of substance.

The consultative portion: what it is for

The 27 August 1989 letter of the Universal House of Justice to the followers of Bahá’u’lláh is the controlling text. It frames the Feast as an institution of which “all the people of Bahá are members” and names its second movement with precision: a “general meeting where the Local Spiritual Assembly reports its activities, plans and problems to the community, shares news and messages from the World Centre and the National Assembly, and receives the thoughts and recommendations of the friends through a process of consultation.” The same letter then enlarges the frame in a way that determines how social topics are to be received: “Here, for instance, the Feast is an arena of democracy at the very root of society, where the Local Spiritual Assembly and the members of the community meet on common ground, where individuals are free to offer their gifts of thought, whether as new ideas or constructive criticism, to the building processes of an advancing civilization.”

This is a sweeping warrant. The Feast's consultation is not narrow housekeeping; it is, in the House of Justice's own framing, the local pulse of an “advancing civilization” — and the friends' contribution to its building is welcomed. Social questions — education, the state of the neighbourhood, race relations, the welfare of women and youth, the texture of professional and civic life — are entirely proper material here. What is excluded is not the social but the partisan.

What is excluded — and why

Shoghi Effendi's letter of 21 March 1932 to the Bahá’ís of the United States and Canada, published in The World Order of Bahá’u’lláh, sets the operative discipline. He calls the friends to “refrain from associating themselves, whether by word or by deed, with the political pursuits of their respective nations, with the policies of their governments and the schemes and programmes of parties and factions. In such controversies they should assign no blame, take no side, further no design […].” The same letter places the doctrine on its civilisational ground: “Let them rise above all particularism and partisanship, above the vain disputes, the petty calculations, the transient passions that agitate the face, and engage the attention, of a changing world.”

The reason this prohibition reaches into the consultative portion of the Feast is not squeamishness about controversy. It is that the Feast convenes the local community as itself — not as a collection of the political affiliations its members happen to hold. A Feast in which one party's positions are pressed by some and contested by others has ceased to be the gathering Bahá’u’lláh ordained; it has become a faction-meeting on Bahá’í premises. In a letter written on his behalf, 16 March 1933, the same principle is generalised: “to enter the arena of party politics is surely detrimental to the best interests of the Faith and will harm the Cause.” The Feast boundary is distinct from the disciplinary pathway when an individual believer has entered partisan politics — treated at length in the companion question on partisan politics.

The 23 December 2008 letter of the Department of the Secretariat — written to a Bahá’í who was a political scientist, and the source of the much-quoted “co-opted” formulation — supplies the contemporary application: “you may find that an issue pertaining to social action has been co-opted by the political debate among competing factions, and wisdom will be required to determine whether to adjust your approach or let the matter rest for a time.” The letter's logic is not that the underlying social question is improper — quite the contrary, it names “safeguarding the rights of women, extending effective education to all children, curbing the spread of infectious disease, protecting the environment, and eliminating the extremes of wealth and poverty” as concerns the friends will rightly contribute to. The logic is that politicisation converts a question of principle into a question of faction, and the Bahá’í response is to step out of the factional contest while continuing to address the underlying matter on the level of principle.

How the Assembly handles this in the room

The following chair moves and channelling options are editorial operationalisation (Tier F) — sound assembly practice drawn from the doctrinal boundary above, not textual prescription from the Feast compilation.

The chair's first move when a topic with political charge arises is the redirect that names what the Feast is for. How does this bear on the life of our community and the work it has undertaken? If the topic resolves into a substantive concern — race-relations work in the neighbourhood, the equality of women in our junior-youth groups, environmental stewardship in the village, support for a friend whose family is harmed by a government policy — the consultation continues, drawing on principle from the Writings rather than on positions from the political arena. If it resolves into the urging of a partisan position, the chair names the boundary courteously and either redirects or sets the matter aside.

The Assembly's structural options when a topic has substance but cannot be done justice in the consultative window are well established. A working-group of the Assembly can pick up the matter for fuller study; the next reflection-meeting consultation can take it; the Counsellors or Auxiliary Board members can be consulted where guidance is needed. None of this is silencing — it is the proper channelling of a substantive concern into a forum that can carry it.

Two pastoral cautions, both load-bearing. Do not silence a friend who raises a topic with substance. In a letter written on his behalf, 13 December 1939 — collected in the Feast Compilation — Shoghi Effendi names “the need for open and constructive criticism and deliberation” as among the Feast's chief administrative purposes; an Assembly that polices the consultation into anaemia has misread its office as gravely as one that lets it become a partisan meeting. The same passage, however, warns that “all criticisms and discussions of a negative character which may result in undermining the authority of the Assembly as a body should be strictly avoided” — lest the order of the Cause itself be endangered. Do not equate “social” with “political.” The Faith engages society at every level — in community-building, in social action, and in the discourses of society — and the friends' professional and civic lives are part of that engagement. The Feast is not the forum in which the community as such takes a political position; it is, however, a forum in which the friends consult on how their lives — including their civic lives — bear on the work they share.
