What should a Bahá’í put in a will — and what can civil law not override?

Every believer is obliged to write a will during life and to dispose of property as conscience and justice direct — within the limits civil law imposes and after funeral costs, debts, and any balance of Huqúqu‘lláh are met. The testator enjoys wide freedom: charity, family provision, bequests to the Cause, burial instructions, and specific gifts are all permissible once prior charges are satisfied. What civil law cannot override is Bahá’í law itself: a provision requiring cremation, or refusing payment of Huqúqu‘lláh when a balance remains due, is null in the Faith’s reckoning even if a court would enforce it — though where civil law compels cremation, the Assembly takes no part and the believer‘s survivors follow the civil requirement as closely as Bahá’í practice permits.

The Writings on Wills and Inheritance

Bahá’u’lláh ordains the writing of a will as a binding law of the Kitáb-i-Aqdas. The testator should open the document with the adornment of the Most Great Name, bear witness to the oneness of God in the Dayspring of His Revelation, and make mention of what is praiseworthy — so that the will becomes “a testimony for him in the kingdoms of Revelation and Creation and a treasure with his Lord.”

“Unto everyone hath been enjoined the writing of a will. The testator should head this document with the adornment of the Most Great Name, bear witness therein unto the oneness of God in the Dayspring of His Revelation, and make mention, as he may wish, of that which is praiseworthy, so that it may be a testimony for him in the kingdoms of Revelation and Creation and a treasure with his Lord, the Supreme Protector, the Faithful.”

— Bahá’u’lláh, Kitáb-i-Aqdas, ¶109

The Synopsis and Codification adds the financial precondition that frames every disposition:

“Any person is at liberty to will his possessions as he sees fit provided he makes provisions for the payment of Huqúqu’lláh and the discharge of his debts.”

— Synopsis and Codification of the Laws and Ordinances of the Kitáb-i-Aqdas

‘Abdu‘l-Bahá, in a Tablet on the subject, names the will as the primary and fundamental obligation — not a secondary convenience. Where no will exists, inheritance is divided according to the law revealed in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas; where a will exists, that law governs distribution of what remains after the testator’s lawful charges. The testator is free to bequeath the residence to whomsoever he wisheth, or to devise it jointly among heirs. The revealed inheritance tables — seven categories of heirs, with children allotted the largest share — apply as the default when someone dies intestate, not as a ceiling on what a testator may otherwise arrange.

Bahá’u‘lláh’s Questions and Answers settle a common doubt about charitable bequests. A person has “full jurisdiction over his property”; if Huqúqu’lláh is discharged and debts settled, “all that is recorded in his will, and any declaration or avowal it containeth, shall be acceptable.” The testator may assign portions beyond funeral expenses to works of charity. The law of inheritance is therefore a safety net for the intestate, not a template the living must follow.

The order of settlement before any division is itself revealed. Division of the estate takes place only after Huqúqu‘lláh hath been paid, debts settled, funeral and burial expenses defrayed, and provision made that the deceased may be carried to the resting-place with dignity and honour. This sequence is not advisory; it structures both intestate distribution and the executor’s duties.

Huqúqu’lláh and the Estate

Huqúqu‘lláh is a lifetime obligation computed by the believer’s own conscience, as treated in huqququllah-not-a-tax and the Codification of the Law of Ḥuqúqu’lláh. Death does not extinguish it. Whatever portion remains due at the moment of passing is a debt of the estate, payable before general bequests and before heirs receive their shares — whether or not the will mentions it.

The Universal House of Justice has clarified the operative sequence repeatedly. Funeral and burial costs come first; then outstanding debts; then any balance of Huqúqu‘lláh; only then does the executor arrive at the property to be divided according to the will or, absent a will, according to the law of inheritance. A believer should pay Huqúqu’lláh during life and leave clear records of what has been rendered, so that at death the will need address only any residual balance. The House has observed that without such accounting, no one can calculate accurately what remains due.

Executors bear a specific duty. Within the limits civil law imposes, an executor or administrator must do his utmost to ensure that the balance due to Huqúqu‘lláh by the deceased is paid from the estate — after funeral and burial expenses and after all outstanding debts, and before the remainder is distributed. This applies when the deceased dies intestate, when the will makes no provision for Huqúqu’lláh, and even when the will states that no Huqúqu‘lláh should be paid: such a clause is contrary to Bahá’í law and is null and void; the executor proceeds to pay from the estate if assets suffice and civil law permits.

Practical wording for a will clause can direct the executor to calculate and pay any balance to the National Spiritual Assembly, earmarked “Right of God” or “Huqúqu’lláh.” Believers may consult a Representative of the Trustee for guidance on lifetime payment and on what records to leave for the executor. The Board of Trustees educates; it does not audit or demand.

What Civil Law Can and Cannot Override

Bahá‘í law and civil law operate in parallel, and the executor must honour both within each system’s limits. Civil law governs whether a will is validly executed — witnesses, formalities, age of majority, community-of-property rules, forced-heirship regimes, and tax treatment. Bahá’í law governs what dispositions are spiritually licit and what charges take priority in the conscience of the community.

The House of Justice has been explicit on the hierarchy. A believer cannot make a provision in a will contrary to Bahá‘í law; if he does, the executor must abide by Bahá’í law. Thus, if a testator directs cremation, burial should nevertheless proceed according to Bahá‘í law unless civil law prevents it — in which case civil law must be followed, but the Spiritual Assembly takes no part in the cremation. The same principle governs Huqúqu’lláh: a civil court might distribute an estate without regard to the Right of God, but Bahá’í relatives and institutions are not permitted to fulfil a will clause that refuses payment when a balance remains due.

Where civil forced-heirship limits how much a testator may bequeath away from statutory heirs, the believer works within those limits. The Writings do not promise that every spiritual preference will be legally enforceable in every jurisdiction. The testator’s task is to draft the will with qualified legal counsel in the country where property is situated, so that lawful wishes are carried out and spiritual obligations are not accidentally negated by invalid form.

The House has also confirmed that a person‘s will is sacred: a Bahá‘í is not permitted to challenge the provisions of another’s will. Disputes among heirs are a principal evil the law of wills was revealed to prevent; ‘Abdu’l-Bahá describes the will as “the settler of every dispute, and the cause of ease for all.”

A Practical Checklist

The House has not issued a standard form for Bahá’í wills and has directed National and Local Spiritual Assemblies not to prescribe provisions or give legal advice. Assemblies may remind believers in general terms of the obligation to make a will and may suggest consulting a qualified attorney. The following checklist reflects institutional guidance and field practice; it is not a substitute for legal counsel.

Before meeting an attorney, gather: full legal name and address; birth date and national identity number if applicable; names, addresses, and birth dates of spouse and children; marriage and divorce documents; a list of intended beneficiaries; all assets (real property, investments, insurance, pensions, safety deposit boxes, debts owed to or by the testator); and the names of proposed executors, trustees, and guardians for minor children.

Core clauses a Bahá’í will commonly includes:

  1. Opening invocation — the Most Great Name and testimony to the oneness of God, as the Kitáb-i-Aqdas directs.
  2. Burial instructions — body not cremated; burial within one hour‘s journey of the place of death; Prayer for the Dead recited if the deceased is a Bahá’í aged fifteen or older; funeral simple and dignified. Separate written instructions kept with a trusted friend or executor are advisable, since the will may not be read until after burial.
  3. Priority charges — debts, last illness expenses, funeral and administration costs, and any Huqúqu‘lláh balance, paid before other distributions, with reference to where Huqúqu’lláh records are kept.
  4. Specific legacies — jewellery, books, Bahá‘í papers and correspondence (especially important when non-Bahá’í relatives might otherwise inherit them); bequests to Archives or to a trusted Bahá’í friend.
  5. General and residuary bequests — to family, charities, or the Faith (stated sum, percentage of gross estate, or residue after family provision).
  6. Executor and alternates — relatives or trusted friends; a clause nominating the National Spiritual Assembly to appoint an executor if the named executor cannot serve. Neither the Local nor the National Assembly should be named as executor itself.
  7. Guardianship — for minor children if both parents may die together.
  8. Body to medical science — permissible if the will stipulates that remains not be cremated and not be transported more than one hour’s journey from the place of death, per guidance from Shoghi Effendi.

After execution: review the will when moving countries, marrying, having children, or when beneficiaries die. A sealed copy may be deposited with the National Assembly if it is named as beneficiary; deposit is appropriate but not required.

Joint property: holding assets in joint names does not remove the need for a will. Without one, survivors may face unnecessary court costs to administer assets that joint ownership alone does not fully settle.

Assembly Consultation — What to Ask, and What Not to Expect

Local and National Spiritual Assemblies are not will-drafting bodies. The House of Justice (1 October 1989) directed that Assemblies refrain from issuing specific instructions on the form and content of Bahá’í wills and from giving legal advice. They may draw attention, in a general way, to the obligation to make a will.

What a believer may appropriately bring to an Assembly or to the National Centre:

  • Bequest wording — anyone contemplating a bequest to a Bahá‘í institution should contact the National Centre to ensure correct legal phrasing and fund designation (local fund, national fund, international fund earmarked through the National Assembly, Continental Counsellors’ fund, and similar).
  • Huqúqu’lláh education — a Representative of the Trustee can advise on lifetime payment and on what documentation to leave for executors; this is counsel, not audit.
  • Burial coordination — where a Local Assembly exists, it has responsibility to help ensure burial laws are observed; advance conversation with the Assembly and with one’s executor prevents crisis confusion.

What not to expect: prescribed clauses, review of draft wills, or adjudication among heirs. Inheritance disputes among Bahá‘ís are civil matters for courts or private settlement; the Assembly does not divide estates. If a believer is named executor and is uncertain how to apply Huqúqu’lláh principles, the House has indicated that subsidiary legislation may come in future; for now the executor applies principles to the extent possible, using best judgment and available information.

Points friends often raise

“The Kitáb-i-Aqdas inheritance fractions dictate my will.” They dictate distribution only when no valid will exists. With a will, the testator disposes freely after Huqúqu’lláh, debts, and funeral charges.

“I can direct cremation in my will and my Bahá‘í family should honour it.” Cremation is forbidden in Bahá’í law. A cremation clause is void in the Faith; the executor buries according to Bahá’í law unless civil law compels otherwise.

“Huqúqu’lláh is personal — my estate owes nothing.” Lifetime payment is the norm, but death does not cancel an unpaid balance. The estate owes what remains due, before heirs inherit.

“I should name the Assembly as executor because it is trustworthy.” The House directs that Assemblies not be named executors. Trust is not the issue; the role is personal and legal, not institutional.

“A will is unnecessary if I have little property.” The obligation is universal. Complications at death — government agencies, family disagreement, intestacy rules — fall hardest on those who assumed a will was only for the wealthy.

“I may contest my relative‘s will because the distribution seems unjust.” Bahá‘í law forbids challenging another person’s will. Equity among heirs is pursued in life, through consultation and gift, not through posthumous litigation.

“Making a will is morbid.” Shoghi Effendi, quoted in House of Justice guidance, links execution of a will’s provisions to the spirit of the deceased rejoicing in the Abhá Kingdom. The act is an expression of love for those left behind.
