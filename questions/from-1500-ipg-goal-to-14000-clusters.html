What was the journey from the 1,500-IPG goal to the 14,000-cluster Nine Year Plan goal?

The distance between the 1,500-IPG target of 2005 and the 14,000-cluster goal of 2031 is not simply a matter of scale — it spans 21 years, two complete changes of metric, and a fundamental shift in what the Bahá'í world understands "growth" to mean. Each time the governing number changed, it was not because the old target had become irrelevant; it was because experience on the ground had made visible a richer and more differentiated picture than the earlier count could capture.

2001–2010 — the IPG count era

The 9 January 2001 message to the Conference of the Continental Boards of Counsellors introduced both the cluster as the basic unit of planning and a four-category scheme (known in field shorthand as A/B/C/D) for assessing a cluster's development. Only the topmost category — a cluster with "strong communities of deepened believers" and a functioning institute process — was ready to launch an intensive programme of growth. The message named the IPG as the target condition, but the concept was so new that no count existed.

By the 27 December 2005 message (Framework for Action) the House of Justice could articulate a concrete goal: at Riḍván 2006 it would call upon the friends "to establish, during the next Plan, intensive programs of growth in no less than 1,500 clusters" (27 December 2005). The number carried an almost symbolic weight — one IPG for every day of the year, as the friends came to put it — and it gave the worldwide community a single, legible metric by which to measure progress during a formative decade. By Riḍván 2009 approximately 1,000 clusters had crossed the threshold; by Riḍván 2010 the House of Justice announced that the 1,500-cluster target had been met one year early.

The achievement was real, and the House of Justice acknowledged it as evidence of a maturing pattern of learning. But reaching the target also exposed the scheme's limits: the A category had grown crowded and heterogeneous. Clusters that had launched IPGs years earlier and developed far beyond their first cycles were being counted the same way as clusters that had just crossed the threshold. A binary measure — has an IPG / does not have an IPG — could no longer do justice to a reality that was clearly a continuum.

2010–2021 — the milestone era

The 28 December 2010 message to the Counsellors' Conference retired the A/B/C/D scheme and replaced it with the three-milestone framework. The House of Justice wrote that the decade of experience had enabled the community "to conceive of the movement of a population, propelled by mounting spiritual forces, in terms of a rich and dynamic continuum" rather than a set of discrete stages (28 December 2010).

The new framework changed both what was counted and what was sought. The 1,500 IPG clusters were immediately re-read as second-milestone clusters — those with all the elements of an intensive programme functioning adequately. But now there was also a first milestone (a nascent programme of sustained expansion and consolidation, before the full IPG conditions are met) and a third milestone (a dynamic pattern of community life extending across whole populations, where social action and engagement with prevalent discourses begin to arise). The 2010 message set the next target not at a higher IPG number but at 5,000 clusters with a programme of growth at any level — a deliberate widening that acknowledged the breadth of the journey still ahead.

What followed was rapid expansion across all three levels. By Riḍván 2016 the worldwide total of clusters with a programme of growth had surpassed 5,000 — the target the 28 December 2010 message had set for the end of the 2011–2016 Plan. At the outset of the 2016–2021 Plan, roughly 200 clusters had passed the third milestone, spread across some 40 countries; by Riḍván 2021 that figure had risen to approximately 1,000 in nearly 100 countries. The Riḍván 2021 message reported the actual achievements of the completed Plan: approximately 4,000 intensive programmes of growth (second milestone), 300,000 core activities sustained at once, and participation in those activities risen above two million — a tableau of formation that a single IPG count could never have conveyed.

The 30 December 2021 message launching the Nine Year Plan's first year offered anticipations, not year-end results: it forecast that by the close of the One Year Plan programmes of growth would be under way in over 6,000 clusters, that close to 5,000 of these would have passed the second milestone, and that 1,300 would have advanced further. Those figures must be read as forward-looking planning language, distinct from the Riḍván 2021 audit of what the community had actually accomplished.

The 2021 Riḍván message also made explicit what had been implicit since 2010: the community was no longer measuring its progress primarily against a cluster-count target. It was observing, cluster by cluster, the degree to which whole populations were being engaged in their own spiritual and social transformation. The Nine Year Plan's framing would make this shift permanent.

The Nine Year Plan shift

The 30 December 2021 message to the Counsellors' Conference launched the Nine Year Plan (2022–2031) and retired the raw IPG count from goal-setting language. It asked National Assemblies and Regional Councils to forecast milestone progress over the coming nine years, but it did not yet publish the collective worldwide aspirations. Those figures appeared in the Riḍván 2022 message, which reported the outcome of cluster-boundary adjustments and the forecasts received. The House of Justice wrote that "by the end of the Plan, a programme of growth at some level of development will exist in around 14,000 of these clusters"; "the number where the programme of growth could be considered intensive is projected to climb to 11,000"; and "the number of clusters where the third milestone has been passed will rise above 5,000 by 2031" (Riḍván 2022).

These figures require careful reading on two axes. Milestone meaning: the 14,000-cluster aspiration is not a target of 14,000 intensive programmes of growth. It is 14,000 clusters with at least a nascent programme — the first milestone of the 2010 framework. The 11,000 figure corresponds to the second milestone (the old A-category IPG condition). The 5,000+ figure is the frontier where whole populations are engaged and the society-building power of the Faith has become visible. Forecast vs. actual: the 14,000 / 11,000 / 5,000 numbers are Riḍván 2022 aspirations toward 2031, not Riḍván 2021 achievements and not the One Year Plan anticipations of 30 December 2021 (>6,000 / ~5,000 / 1,300). Conflating these layers has been a common source of confusion.

The goals were also set against a new baseline. A global re-clustering exercise redrew cluster boundaries worldwide, raising the total count by roughly a quarter to over 22,000 (from a Riḍván 2003 / January 2003 baseline of approximately 17,000 clusters — see growth time-series). This was a definitional change, not organic growth: existing geographic units were subdivided, so that some clusters previously counted as one appeared as two or three in the revised map. Figures from before and after 2022 are therefore not directly comparable, and the ambition implied by "14,000 clusters" relative to the earlier "1,500 IPGs" is even greater than the raw numbers suggest.

What the metric evolution reveals

Read together, the three metrics tell a story about the maturation of an analytic framework alongside the growth of a community.

The IPG count (2005–2010) was a binary instrument suited to an early stage: it identified which clusters had crossed a threshold of readiness and held the wider community's attention on the single most urgent task. It did what it needed to do.

The milestone framework (2010–2021) replaced the binary with a gradient. It could hold the heterogeneity of a worldwide community that now included clusters barely past the first stirrings of activity and clusters where thousands of people were engaged in community-building at every level. It gave planners and cluster agencies a common vocabulary for articulating where a cluster stood and what it needed next.

The Nine Year Plan framing (2021–) adds a third dimension: the goals are now differentiated across three thresholds simultaneously, and the qualitative language of "society-building" frames what happens beyond the third milestone in terms that no single numerical target can fully capture. The 31 December 2025 midterm message to the Counsellors observed explicitly that in the most advanced clusters "the term 'programme of growth' no longer does full justice to such developments."

The journey from 1,500 to 14,000 is therefore not a straight line along a single axis. It is a series of expansions in what "growth" is understood to include — from readiness for an intensive programme, to developmental stage within an unfolding programme, to the degree to which a whole population has been drawn into a civilisation-building endeavour.
