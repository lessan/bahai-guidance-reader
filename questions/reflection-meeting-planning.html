What does the guidance say about how to plan a reflection meeting — agenda, tone, length, and the role of the institutions present?

The Cluster reflection meeting closes one three-month cycle of activity and opens the next. It is a learning instrument, not a status report. The Universal House of Justice‘s 27 December 2005 message names its character in a single compressed phrase — “as much a time of joyous celebration as it is of serious consultation” — and that phrase governs almost every planning decision. An agenda that produces neither celebration nor consultation has lost the meeting’s purpose, however efficient it may otherwise look. Plan for substance over speed, prayerfulness over briskness, the friends in the room over the institutions presiding, and what was learned over what was done.

The agenda — five movements

No single text prescribes a fixed agenda. The five movements below are an editorial synthesis of recurring patterns in the field accounts (R.O.G. 27, 33, 35) and in the framing texts (the 27 December 2005 “joyous celebration… serious consultation” pair and the 28 December 2010 “meeting for reflection and planning” in that order). Each movement does work the others cannot.

1. Devotional opening. Substantial, not perfunctory. A reflection meeting that reduces prayer to a single recitation before the business begins has already conceded the meeting‘s character. R.O.G. 35’s Alotau account links the cluster‘s reinvigoration directly to a strengthened devotional life — including dawn prayers — animated by study of the 18 December 2014 message on the Mashriqu’l-Adhkár. The devotional is not a frame; it is the medium in which consultation becomes possible.

2. Reading the cycle that has just closed. Stories from the field — successes, struggles, surprises — drawn out by the tutors, JuniorYouthSpiritualEmpowermentProgramme, children‘s-class teachers, ATC members, and friends active in core activities. Statistics are part of this reading, but only as evidence in a narrative; they are not the reading itself. R.O.G. 35’s Sanniquellie case turns precisely on the recovery of honest statistics: “As we consulted, we realized that our goals did not match the available human resources. To devise more realistic plans, we needed to establish a system to collect more reliable statistics.” Numbers are read against capacity, not against ambition.

3. Consultation on what was learned. This is the heart of the meeting and where most of its time should sit. The aim is not to declare what happened but to ask what the cluster‘s experience means — what the receptivity of a particular neighbourhood is telling them, why a Book 1 campaign caught fire in one sector and stalled in another, what tutors are seeing about how friends move through Book 2, where the institute pipeline is bottlenecked. Consultation here is the Bahá’í consultative method applied to the cluster’s own learning: detached, frank but courteous, oriented to truth rather than to vindication.

4. Planning the next cycle. Concrete commitments — which neighbourhoods, which lines of action, which campaigns, which goals against which human resources. Targets are modest and matched to capacity. R.O.G. 35’s Matéri, Sanniquellie, and Alotau accounts all show plans set realistically against the human-resources map — not ambitiously against an aspirational one. Plans without prior reading are wishes; a meeting that begins with planning has skipped the cycle that just closed.

5. Devotional closing. The meeting is bracketed in prayer. The closing devotional sends the friends back into the next cycle as participants in something larger than a planning exercise.

Tone

Three adjectives drawn from the guidance and from field practice: prayerful, candid, joyful.

Prayerful — the meeting opens and closes in devotion; consultation is conducted in the spirit of prayer rather than around it. Candid — failure is named without disguise. R.O.G. 35‘s eight recovery accounts (Matéri, Sanniquellie, Alotau, Bucharest, Cobly, Itaunja, Bogotá, Hamilton) all begin with frank admission that the programme had stalled; recovery began with that admission. Joyful — celebration of grace is not optional; the 27 December 2005 message itself names the meeting as “as much a time of joyous celebration as it is of serious consultation,” and R.O.G. 27’s field accounts (the United States gathering held “without any agenda — ‘just an opportunity for reflection’”; the Zambian weekend organised in small groups for shared reflection) treat both dimensions as requiring deliberate cultivation rather than emergence by chance. A meeting that is candid without being joyful is bureaucratic; a meeting that is joyful without being candid is sentimental.

What the tone is not: bureaucratic, self-congratulatory, evaluative. The reflection meeting is not the place where coordinators are graded or where the ATC defends its quarter. The 28 December 2010 message names the cycle’s punctuation as “a meeting for reflection and planning” — those two words, in that order.

Length

The guidance does not fix a duration. As a matter of field practice, smaller clusters with a single neighbourhood often complete the agenda in two to three hours; larger clusters with multiple sectors and many active friends commonly need a half-day, or organise sub-sessions — sector-level reflection gatherings feeding a shorter cluster-wide gathering. R.O.G. 35’s Alotau case is the canonical example of sectorisation, where a cluster of 54 → 103 activities in one cycle could no longer be reflected on in a single undifferentiated meeting and the cluster reorganised itself into sector reflections that feed a cluster-wide one.

What length should not be set by: a fixed slot on the calendar, the convenience of those convening, or the wish to “get to business.” A meeting compressed to ninety minutes to fit a Saturday afternoon will deliver ninety minutes of reflection; the cycle that follows will inherit the compression.

Role of the institutions present

The 27 December 2005 message and the field accounts are clear that institutions participate in the reflection meeting; they do not preside over it. The three protagonists — individual, institutions, community — are all present in the room.

  • Area Teaching Committee (ATC). Convenes the meeting; coordinates the agenda with the institute coordinator and Auxiliary Board member in advance. The ATC‘s posture in the meeting itself is facilitative — keeping the consultation moving, holding the cycle’s planning together, ensuring goals leave the room with owners attached. The ATC does not dominate.
  • Institute coordinator. Reports on the cluster‘s movement through the InstituteProcess — how many friends in Book 1, Book 6, Book 7; where the pipeline is healthy and where blocked; new tutor, animator, and children’s-class-teacher capacities emerging. The posture is one of learning shared rather than evaluation pronounced — a distinction R.O.G. 27 develops at length, calling on coordinators to visit tutors “in the field” and at “tutor reflection gatherings, rather than sit in an office and call them”. Tutors are accompanied, not graded.
  • Auxiliary Board member (and assistants). Accompanies pastorally; offers reflections on the cluster‘s overall health and on how its experience connects to neighbouring clusters and to Counsellor guidance the cluster may not yet have absorbed. The ABM’s voice is one of accompaniment — neither chair nor referee.
  • Local Spiritual Assembly representatives. Participate in the consultation, bringing the local-community perspective that cluster agencies alone cannot supply: the rhythm of the Nineteen Day Feast, the texture of community life, the friends and families whose names appear in cluster statistics only as numbers. R.O.G. 35 (Matéri, Alotau, Hamilton) repeatedly identifies LSAs working “shoulder to shoulder” with cluster agencies as a recovery condition. The LSA is not a guest at the meeting; the LSA is a co-protagonist.

The meeting belongs to all four — and to the friends. None of them owns it.

What NOT to do

Anti-patterns drawn from R.O.G. 35 and R.O.G. 27; each is a real failure that recovered when it was named.

  • Reduce the meeting to numerical reporting. Reading numbers without reading what they mean. R.O.G. 35‘s Sanniquellie recovered by combining reliable statistics with consultation on what they implied for the next cycle’s plan.
  • Allow one or two friends to dominate the consultation. The reflection meeting is the moment the cluster’s own voice should be loudest; if that voice is two people, the consultation has failed before it began. Plan facilitation accordingly — small-group rounds, naming who has not yet spoken, distributing the floor.
  • Plan the next cycle without first reading the previous one. A planning meeting that begins with goals is not a reflection meeting. The 28 December 2010 message’s order — reflection and planning — is operative.
  • Skip the devotional dimension to “get to business.” The 27 December 2005 message‘s pairing of “joyous celebration” with “serious consultation” puts devotion at the meeting‘s centre, not at its perimeter. Devotion is not the meeting’s frame; it is its medium.
  • Treat coordinators‘ reports as evaluative judgements. Tutors, animators, and children’s-class teachers are accompanied through their service; the reflection meeting is where their experience teaches the cluster. R.O.G. 26‘s Malaysian national junior youth coordinator names the recurrent pathology directly: the coordinator “would have to go from being a ‘statistician’ to being a friend” — counting tutors without accompanying them was the failure that recovery had to undo.
  • Hold the meeting only with institutions present. The friends active in core activities are participants, not absent constituents. R.O.G. 33‘s Differdange (Luxembourg) account identifies widening participation to families and the wider community as one of the cluster’s turning points. A meeting of institutions reflecting on the community is not a reflection meeting — it is something narrower.
