What is the longest-running Bahá’í periodical, and what other historical periodicals does the corpus include?

Bahá’í News — published by the National Spiritual Assembly of the United States — is the longest-running Bahá’í periodical in this knowledgebase corpus, spanning 1924 to 2009 in 714 issues. The broader landscape of English-language Bahá’í periodicals stretches back to 1910 with Star of the West, and continues into the present with the Journal of Bahá’í Studies (1988–ongoing). World Order and Persian/multilingual holdings in the corpus extend the picture further; longer runs may exist globally that are not fully represented here.

Star of the West (1910–1935)

Star of the West was the first Bahá’í periodical published in the Western world. Issued by the Bahá’í News Service in Chicago, it ran for 25 volumes and 384 issues over a twenty-five year span. Its opening volumes coincided with ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s Western travels (1911–1913) and the period in which His tablets were reaching English-speaking communities for the first time.

The publication’s historical importance lies partly in what it transmitted. Translations of tablets by ‘Abdu’l-Bahá and, in some cases, Bahá’u’lláh appeared here that have never been republished in standard English compilations. The corpus’s survey of Volumes 1–25 identified over 100 ‘Abdu’l-Bahá tablets, the majority being short community-encouragement addresses; one substantive theological tablet — a response to questions on the afterlife and levels of consciousness from the Ostburg family of Boston — was unique enough to warrant its own wiki page (sotw-brief-answers-seven-questions). Administrative foundation documents also appeared: early NSA elections, community formation, and the first House of Justice guidelines.

The magazine ceased in 1935. Its run overlaps the early years of Bahá’í News, with which it ran in parallel from 1924 onward.

Bahá’í News (1924–2009)

Bahá’í News began publication in 1924 as the official organ of the American NSA, and ran continuously — monthly or bi-monthly — until its final issue (no. 714) in 2009. That continuity of eighty-five years makes it the longest-running periodical in this corpus by a significant margin. From approximately issue 525 (December 1974) the publication appeared as The American Bahá’í (corpus header templates shift at that point), before eventually ceasing publication.

The range of the magazine‘s content shifted across eras. Early issues (1–50, 1924–1930) carried Shoghi Effendi’s letters to the American community in full, tablets by ‘Abdu’l-Bahá still circulating, and accounts of the first Bahá’í communities in North America. Issues from the Ten Year Crusade period (1953–1963, issues 301–450) serve as the primary chronicle of the Knights of Bahá’u’lláh and the pioneering campaigns that opened new territories. The later decades (issues 551–714, 1979–2009) document the response to the Iranian persecution and the succession of teaching plans under the Universal House of Justice.

Because many of the tablets and letters it published have not been republished elsewhere, many issues of Bahá’í News contain Tier A/B material embedded within what is otherwise an institutional-record publication (Tier E) — pending systematic issue-level cataloguing.

World Order Magazine (1935–2007)

World Order was published by the NSA of the United States as a scholarly and literary journal. Its 305 issues appeared across two distinct series separated by a two-decade gap: Series 1 ran as a monthly magazine from 1935 to 1949 (approximately Volumes 1–14); Series 2 resumed in 1969 as a quarterly academic journal and continued through 2007 (Volumes 1–39 of the second series). The total active run was roughly fifty-two years across the two series.

The journal‘s character was consistently more analytical and literary than Bahá’í News. Leading Bahá’í scholars — William Hatcher, Udo Schaefer, Moojan Momen, among others — published major essays here on theology, philosophy, and social principles. Some Series 1 issues contain letters or messages by Shoghi Effendi or the Universal House of Justice in their original published context, making those issues Tier B/C sources within a Tier D publication. The Journal of Bahá’í Studies, when it launched in 1988, was widely regarded in the field (Tier D/F — community and scholarly understanding, not UHJ policy) as the academic successor to World Order in intent, though the two overlapped for nearly two decades.

Journal of Bahá’í Studies (1988–present)

The Journal of Bahá’í Studies is published by the Association for Bahá’í Studies (ABS) and is the only periodical in this corpus that continues to be published as of 2026. Peer-reviewed, the corpus holds approximately 446 articles across 35+ volumes since its founding. Its character is explicitly academic: peer review, interdisciplinary engagement (law, medicine, education, arts, environmental sciences), and rigorous application of scholarly methodology to Bahá’í texts and history.

Some issues include plenary talks from the ABS annual conference — valuable for understanding evolving scholarly discourse, though not authoritative. The journal‘s coverage since Volume 16 (approximately 2005) includes extensive engagement with the institute process and youth empowerment, reflecting the community’s own development trajectory during those years.

Persian and multilingual periodicals in the corpus

The corpus holds several Persian-language and multilingual periodicals that expand the picture beyond the English tradition:

Andalib — a Persian-language Bahá’í literary and cultural journal published by the Bahá’ís of Germany. 99 issues are present in the corpus (under `raw/afnan_library/andalib/`). The issues span a substantial run; precise founding and closing dates are not confirmed from the files reviewed.

The Bahá’í Journal (UK) — published by the Bahá’ís of the United Kingdom. 263 issues are present in the corpus (`raw/afnan_library/bahai_journal_uk/`). Dating and continuity span not confirmed from the files reviewed, but the volume count suggests a multi-decade run.

Other early periodicals in the afnan_library corpus — the `journals_other/` collection includes first issues of several early-20th-century periodicals from diverse national communities: Kawakib-i-Hind (India, 1924), The Dawn (Burma, 1923), Bahá’í Bulletin (1908, apparently the earliest item), Bahá’í Magazin (Germany), Akbar-i-Amrí (Persian, 1930–31), Khurshid-i-Khavar (Ishqabad, 1926), New World Order (Vol. 1), and One Country (1989, BIC). These represent the breadth of early national Bahá’í publishing but most are represented only by first issues, not full runs.

Brilliant Star — the Bahá’í youth magazine published in the United States. The corpus holds numerous issues but it targets a younger audience and differs in character from the scholarly and news periodicals above.
