How should a small or isolated Bahá’í community adapt the Nineteen Day Feast?

The Nineteen Day Feast is an institution of the Bahá’í Administrative Order — not a gathering that scales down to a solitary believer or isolated family without an administrative body. A Local Spiritual Assembly, however small its community, holds the Feast in full; believers without an LSA remember the day in prayer and maintain their consultative connection through correspondence and attendance at neighbouring Feasts when travel permits.

The Feast as an Institution

The 27 August 1989 letter of the Universal House of Justice to the followers of Bahá’u’lláh is the controlling text. It names the Feast “an institution of which all the people of Bahá are members” — intended “to promote unity, ensure progress, and foster joy.” Shoghi Effendi, the letter recalls, described the Feast as “the foundation of the new World Order”; a letter written on his behalf names it “a vital medium for maintaining close and continued contact between the believers themselves, and also between them and the body of their elected representatives in the local community.”

The Feast has three distinct portions: “the devotional, the administrative, and the social.” The first entails prayer and the reading of the Holy Texts; the second is the Assembly‘s report and consultation with the community; the third is hospitality and fellowship. The House of Justice requires “strict adherence to the threefold aspects in the sequence in which they have been defined” — though within that structure, as the same letter notes, “the quality and range of the consultation are critical to the spirit of the occasion.” A letter written on the Guardian‘s behalf likewise cautions the friends neither to overstress nor to minimise the Feast’s importance.

The Administrative Threshold

The operative question is not headcount but whether a Local Spiritual Assembly exists to take charge of the Feast. The House of Justice has stated plainly that “the Nineteen Day Feast can only be an official administrative occasion where there is a Local Spiritual Assembly to take charge of it, present reports to the friends, and receive their recommendations.” A handful of believers with an LSA hold the Feast — devotional, consultative, and social — in full; no minimum attendance is legislated.

Believers in a recognised group without an LSA may observe the day and even hold a Feast “in the manner in which a Local Spiritual Assembly would do so, recognizing of course that it has no official administrative standing.” That is commemorative observance, not the official administrative Feast. The connecting distinction is administrative standing: where an Assembly exists, the institution proceeds under its charge; where it does not, the friends remember the day and gather in prayer without the Feast’s official consultative function.

Adapting the Three Portions in a Small Community

Devotional portion. The Feast is rooted in hospitality ordained in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas: “It hath been enjoined upon you once a month to offer hospitality, even should ye serve no more than water; for God hath purposed to bind your hearts together.” In small communities, the 1989 letter notes, “the aspect of personal hospitality is easy to carry out” — two believers reading from the Writings and offering prayer fulfil this portion.

Consultative portion. The LSA brings its agenda — reports from the national level, plans for the community, responses to individual concerns — and the friends respond. Without the procedural weight of large assemblies, a small group may consult more directly; the 1989 letter’s emphasis on consultation quality supports that possibility without legislating it.

Social portion. “Originality and variety in expressions of hospitality are possible” (1989 letter). Refreshments, conversation, and warmth do not require numbers. Simplicity here is consonant with the hospitality the Texts enjoin, not a compromise of the institution.

Isolated Individuals and Families Without a Local Community

A lone pioneer, a family in a remote area, or believers who have no LSA nearby cannot hold an official Feast. The House of Justice directs that “groups, spontaneous gatherings of the friends, and even isolated believers should certainly remember the day and say prayers together.”

What they can do:

  • Hold regular devotional gatherings that carry the same spirit of prayer and study, even if they are not Feasts in the administrative sense.
  • Convey suggestions and recommendations to the National Spiritual Assembly “at any time” and thus take part in the consultative dimension of community life; attend the Nineteen Day Feasts of neighbouring communities when they wish to.
  • Where travel is feasible, attending a neighbouring community‘s Feast is encouraged — any Bahá’í may attend a Feast, and isolated believers from the neighbourhood are expressly welcomed.

The form of observance adapts to circumstance; the institution itself remains the Assembly’s responsibility where an LSA exists.

When Numbers Are Low but the LSA Exists

A common concern is whether a poorly-attended Feast “counts” or whether it is worth holding at all. The guidance is to hold it. The House of Justice advises that “the Nineteen Day Feast should not be cancelled.” A letter written on the Guardian‘s behalf records that the Feasts “should also be observed according to the Bahá’í calendar every nineteen days” and “should be regularly attended by all confirmed believers” — though attendance is “not strictly obligatory” and believers “should endeavour to regularly attend.” The Assembly holds the Feast each Bahá’í month; individual attendance is highly desirable but conscience-based. Low attendance is itself a topic for the consultative portion — the Assembly and the believers present can reflect on why, and what might be done. Cancelling or deferring the Feast because only two or three people are expected inverts the logic of the institution.
