How should a Bahá'í engage with social media, particularly partisan or anti-Bahá'í content?

Social media is not named in the authoritative texts, but the underlying principles transfer directly: a Bahá'í's character online should be indistinguishable from their character offline. When partisan or hostile material appears, the instinct to respond publicly should almost always be resisted — amplification and escalation are the predictable outcomes of online debate, not illumination.

Online conduct mirrors offline character

The core expectation is consistency. Bahá'u'lláh admonishes: "O people of God! I admonish you to observe courtesy, for above all else it is the prince of virtues." He likewise commands: "Beautify your tongues, O people, with truthfulness, and adorn your souls with the ornament of honesty." On backbiting — which includes sharing or reposting mockery and gossip — He warns that "backbiting quencheth the light of the heart, and extinguisheth the life of the soul." Shoghi Effendi synthesises the standard: "Let truthfulness and courtesy be your adorning." None of this is relaxed because the medium is digital.

The Universal House of Justice's 19 May 1995 letter on electronic discussion is the corpus's closest authoritative statement to social-media conduct. The House of Justice instructs that the medium "imposes on participants the same requirements of moderation, candour, and courtesy as would be the case in any other discussion" — and that "the ease and relative impersonality of the electronic medium require in some ways an even higher level of self-discipline than is the case in situations where a spirit of unity is reinforced by the opportunity for direct personal contact and social interaction." Condescension, sarcasm, and divisive labelling sit no better in a comment thread than at a dinner table; the impersonality of the screen makes the discipline harder, not optional.

Bahá'ís who are publicly identified as Bahá'í carry an additional responsibility. Their conduct reflects on the community as a whole, and the potentially large audience of a public profile makes association especially consequential.

Partisan political content

The principle is the same as in any other context. Shoghi Effendi's 1932 letter, gathered in The World Order of Bahá'u'lláh, gives the doctrine its sharpest formulation:

"Let them refrain from associating themselves, whether by word or by deed, with the political pursuits of their respective nations, with the policies of their governments and the schemes and programs of parties and factions. In such controversies they should assign no blame, take no side, further no design, and identify themselves with no system prejudicial to the best interests of that world-wide Fellowship which it is their aim to guard and foster."

This is not passivity — it reflects a considered conviction that healing social fractures requires standing apart from the factional contest. The 30 December 2021 message, launching the Nine Year Plan, describes the friends advancing the Plans as "loyal citizens who eschew partisanship and the contest for worldly power" who "share their beliefs liberally with others, remaining respectful of the freedom of conscience of every soul" — a pairing that makes the standard explicit: freedom to share the Faith is not freedom to align the Bahá'í identity with partisan factions.

Individual Bahá'ís acting purely as private citizens have more latitude, but "purely private" is harder to maintain online than off. Once a platform profile links a name to the community, there is no clean separation. The 23 December 2008 letter of the Department of the Secretariat supplies a useful test: "You may find that an issue pertaining to social action has been co-opted by the political debate among competing factions, and wisdom will be required to determine whether to adjust your approach or let the matter rest for a time." Would sharing this content make it harder for the community to convene people of opposing political views around a common table? If so, the case for restraint is strong.

Hostile and defamatory material

When hostile or defamatory content about the Faith appears — whether it targets institutions, teachings, or individuals — the guiding instinct is not to engage it publicly. This category is not the same as Covenant-breaking material: Covenant-breaking is a defined institutional category with its own guidance on shunning and non-association. Hostile critics, defamatory posts, and well-meaning but inaccurate attacks are a separate case — though the posture toward public debate and amplification is parallel.

The Universal House of Justice has advised that when books are written against the Faith, the friends "should not be unduly exercised when these books appear and certainly no issue should be made of them." On confrontation more broadly, it has urged believers to "avoid confrontation and dissension; these would tend to increase the antagonism." Shoghi Effendi drew a related distinction: Covenant-breaker books "is better not to read"; books by well-meaning yet inaccurate critics "may be read 'so as to refute [the] charges' made in them" — but refutation belongs in private, prepared conversation, not in a public thread that extends the material's reach.

The International Teaching Centre, in a 24 February 1995 letter to a Continental Board of Counsellors, observed that when enemies of the Faith or Covenant-breakers participate in electronic forums, "it is not advisable for the institutions of the Faith or its individual members to become directly involved in such discussion." The 8 December 1988 letter on electronic forums for teaching reinforces the same boundary from the Guardian: believers should "refrain, under any circumstances, from involving yourselves, much less the Cause, in lengthy discussions of a controversial character, as these, besides being fruitless actually cause incalculable harm to the Faith."

Appropriate responses:

  • Use platform reporting tools to flag abuse or false content.
  • If the material raises genuine questions, address them privately with knowledgeable people.
  • Respond to sincere private inquiries with care; ignore public provocations.

The distinction that matters is between a sincere seeker who has encountered misleading content and wants to understand, and an adversary seeking a platform. The former deserves a private, patient conversation; the latter does not deserve a public reply. For Covenant-breaking material specifically, see covenant-breaking-material-online.

Digital tools and teaching contact

The tension between digital reach and embodied community is real, but it is not framed in a letter on "online and hybrid community-building." The operative pandemic-era statements are elsewhere. The Naw-Rúz 177 message (20 March 2020) acknowledged that believers, "though having to adapt to new circumstances," used creative means to sustain bonds, and that "the elevated conversations that have occurred as a result, whether remotely or in person, have been a source of comfort and inspiration to many." The Riḍván 2021 message named the cost honestly: restrictions "prevented an even higher total from being reached" in opening new villages and neighbourhoods — online tools sustained existing relationships; they did not substitute for the in-person rhythm of expansion. See online-and-hybrid-community-building for the full arc.

The 8 December 1988 letter sees "no objection to the utilisation of electronic forums for personal teaching activities" and encourages remaining mindful of the potentially large audience. Social media is most valuable on that basis — as a place to initiate contact and invite genuine investigation, not to publish positions or accumulate endorsements. A post that sparks a private message exchange with someone curious about the Faith is doing something real. The frame of meaningful conversation belongs to the institute process and neighbourhood community-building register; it orients social media as a possible prelude to private teaching contact, not as a substitute for placed work.
