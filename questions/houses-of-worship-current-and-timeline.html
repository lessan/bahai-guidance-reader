How many Houses of Worship currently exist (continental, national, local), and what is the timeline of their dedications?

As of Riḍván 2026, nine continental Houses of Worship have been dedicated (including the first, in ’Ishqábád, which no longer stands), two national Houses of Worship have been dedicated (in Kinshasa and Port Moresby), five local Houses of Worship have been dedicated from the seven originally designated in 2012, and at least four further sites — national and local — have been announced under the Nine Year Plan. The full arc of dedication spans from 1908 to the present, with the pace accelerating sharply from 2012 onward as the institution descends from the continental scale to the neighbourhood and village.

Continental Houses of Worship

The continental Mashriqu’l-Adhkár was the dominant form of the institution for most of the twentieth century. One per continent was the working objective; each dedication was a marker of a Plan’s achievements. Nine have been built; eight stand today.

The first was raised in ‘Ishqábád (modern Ashkhabad, Turkmenistan) and opened in 1908 — the only House of Worship constructed in Bahá’u’lláh’s own era and the only one subsequently lost. Soviet authorities expropriated the building in 1938 and used it as an art gallery; earthquakes in 1948 caused severe damage; demolition was completed by 1963. The Universal House of Justice noted the demolition in a letter of 25 August 1963, recording the passing of “the first Temple raised to the glory of Bahá’u’lláh.” (muhj-1963-1986)

The second, in Wilmette, Illinois — the Mother Temple of the West — was completed and consecrated in 1953 at the Bahá’í Centenary, under Shoghi Effendi’s direction. Its superstructure, the work of architect Louis Bourgeois, had stood unfinished for decades; the Guardian oversaw the completion of the interior and the formal opening.

Dedications then followed in clusters tied to the Plans:

  • Kampala, Uganda (14–15 January 1961) — Mother Temple of Africa; third continental Mashriqu’l-Adhkár.
  • Sydney, Australia (September 1961) — Mother Temple of Australasia; fourth.
  • Frankfurt, Germany (July 1964) — Mother Temple of Europe; fifth, dedicated in the opening year of the Nine Year Plan.
  • Panama City, Panama (Riḍván 1972) — Mother Temple of Latin America; architect Peter Tillotson; Amatu’l-Bahá Rúḥíyyih Khánum represented the Universal House of Justice at the dedication.
  • Apia, Samoa (30 August – 3 September 1984) — Mother Temple of the Pacific Islands; sixth continent.
  • New Delhi, India (December 1986) — Mother Temple of the Indian Subcontinent; the Lotus Temple, designed by Fariborz Sahba; received over 120,000 visitors in its first thirty days.
  • Santiago, Chile (13–16 October 2016) — the last continental House of Worship; the Universal House of Justice described it as marking a new chapter in the life of the institution. (muhj-2001-2022)

The continental series was completed with Santiago. Eight of the nine continental temples stand today.

National and Local Houses of Worship

The expansion of the institution below the continental level was announced at Riḍván 2012, in a message that marked the centenary of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s breaking of ground for the Wilmette Temple. The House of Justice announced seven new projects simultaneously: two national Houses of Worship (in the Democratic Republic of the Congo and Papua New Guinea) and five local Houses of Worship in specific localities where community life had matured to the point that the institution could take root at neighbourhood scale. (ridvan-messages-1950-2023)

The five local sites designated were: Battambang (Cambodia), Bihar Sharif (India), Matunda Soy (Kenya), Norte del Cauca (Colombia), and Tanna (Vanuatu). The Office of Temples and Sites was established at the Bahá’í World Centre the same year to coordinate the unprecedented simultaneous construction of seven buildings.

Dedications of the local Houses of Worship proceeded over the following decade:

  1. Battambang, Cambodia — dedicated 1 September 2017; first local Mashriqu’l-Adhkár of the new era.
  2. Norte del Cauca, Colombia — dedicated 22 July 2018.
  3. Matunda Soy, Kenya — dedicated 23 May 2021; first local House of Worship on the African continent.
  4. Tanna, Vanuatu — dedicated 13 November 2021; first local House of Worship in the Pacific.
  5. Bihar Sharif, India — not yet dedicated as of Riḍván 2026; the October 2024 UHJ message noted “progress made toward the realization” of this project among several still under construction.

On the national tier:

  • Kinshasa, Democratic Republic of the Congo — dedicated 25 March 2023; the first national Mashriqu’l-Adhkár ever raised in the Bahá’í world (UHJ message to friends gathered in Kinshasa, 25 March 2023).
  • Port Moresby, Papua New Guinea — dedicated 25 May 2024 (UHJ message to friends gathered in Port Moresby).

Under the Nine Year Plan, the Universal House of Justice has announced further projects: national Houses of Worship in Brasília (Brazil) and Lilongwe (Malawi), announced October 2024; a local House of Worship in Batouri (Cameroon), announced October 2024; and a national House of Worship in the Manila vicinity (Philippines), announced 8 April 2025. Riḍván 2022 had already named additional sites — Toronto (Canada), Kanchanpur (Nepal), and Mwinilunga (Zambia) — still under construction as of Riḍván 2026.

Spiritual and Community-Building Significance

The expansion of the institution to the national and local levels was not a logistical escalation. It was a doctrinal move. The House of Justice‘s 2012 message frames the seven new projects as the materialisation of a condition already present in communities that had crossed a threshold: “Bahá’í communities have significantly grown in size and vitality, and where engagement in social action is apparent.” The local House of Worship, in this reading, ratifies an emergence rather than manufacturing one.

The Neighbourhood concept page traces this arc: by 2012 the neighbourhood had been established as the focal unit of community-building activity, the arena where core activities — children‘s classes, junior youth groups, study circles, devotional gatherings — had multiplied until they constituted a pattern of community life. The Mashriqu’l-Adhkár is the institution that gives that pattern its architectural and devotional centre. As R.O.G. 34 observes of Tanna: the spiritual conditions for the House of Worship existed since 2012, even before the structure was built.

The Nine Year Plan message (30 December 2021) places the Mashriqu’l-Adhkár at the centre of the society-building aim of the current Plan. The institution’s emergence at the local level is treated as a milestone of cluster development, tied to the third milestone at which “the consciousness of vibrant community life” has become visible and the community is ready to radiate from a devotional centre into the surrounding population.

Shoghi Effendi‘s formulation in Bahá’í Administration (1929) remains the load-bearing statement of what the institution uniquely does: worship divorced from the Dependencies “can never hope to achieve beyond the meager and often transitory results produced by the contemplations of the ascetic,” and service divorced from worship is mere philanthropy. The House of Worship is the only Bahá’í institution that architecturally unites the two.
