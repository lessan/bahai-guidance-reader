What does Bahá’í law say about funerals, burial, and caskets — especially in the West?

For Bahá’ís in the West, four burial requirements are presently binding: the body must be buried, not cremated; it must not be transported more than one hour's journey from the place of death; the Prayer for the Dead must be recited if the deceased is a believer aged fifteen or older; and the funeral should be simple and dignified. The Kitáb-i-Aqdas reveals a fuller law — coffin, ring, silk wrappings, grave orientation — that remains Revelation but is not obligatory for Western believers at this time, though many choose to observe what circumstances allow. Where civil law requires cremation or mandates practices the Faith does not prescribe, believers follow civil law as closely as Bahá’í practice permits and consult their institutions.

The Prayer for the Dead

The Prayer for the Dead (Salát-i-Mayyit) is revealed in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas and is the only Bahá’í obligatory prayer recited in congregation. One believer recites it while all present stand in silence. It is required only for adults — believers who have attained the age of maturity, fifteen — and must precede interment. There is no requirement to face the Qiblih. If no one present can read the full prayer, a shortened form suffices.

The doctrinal ground is the soul's continuing journey after death, treated in soul-and-life-after-death. The prayer binds the community to the departed at the moment of physical separation. Where a Bahá’í community exists, the Local Spiritual Assembly has responsibility to ensure that the prayer is recited for qualifying believers. The Universal House of Justice, in its Riḍván 1981 message, directed National Spiritual Assemblies to educate the friends in burial requirements and to see, courteously and tactfully, that relatives who might otherwise neglect them are informed.

For a non-Bahá’í loved one, Shoghi Effendi clarified that an official Bahá’í funeral service should be given only for a believer, but that there is no objection to reading Bahá’í prayers or to a Bahá’í conducting a funeral at the family's request. The care, readings, and presence of friends remain appropriate; the obligatory congregational prayer is not required for those outside the Faith.

Burial Within One Hour — and the Spirit of the Law

Bahá’u’lláh forbids transporting the body of the deceased a greater distance than one hour's journey from the city. The Kitáb-i-Aqdas notes state that the intention is to limit duration, not mileage: one hour's time, whatever the means of transport. The place of death may be taken to encompass the city or town in which the person passes away, so the hour is calculated from city limits to the burial site. Bahá’u’lláh affirms that the sooner burial takes place, the more fitting and acceptable it will be. The spirit of the law is burial near where one dies — not repatriation across continents, however beloved the distant cemetery.

Western practice often conflicts with this. Funeral homes may delay interment; families may wish burial in a homeland or a family plot far from the deathbed. The binding Western standard is the House of Justice's application: one hour's journey from the place of death. Believers prepare executors and families in advance — through wills, separate burial instruction sheets, and conversation — so that grief does not become the moment for first explaining why repatriation is not an option. When civil or logistical reality makes strict observance impossible, the believer does what the law permits and consults the Assembly.

No Cremation

Cremation is precluded by Bahá’í burial law. The Synopsis and Codification, reflecting guidance from ’Abdu’l-Bahá and Shoghi Effendi, states explicitly that the law forbids carrying the body more than one hour's journey, requires the Prayer for the Dead for those of age, and precludes cremation of the dead. The human body was once exalted by the immortal soul; interment in durable materials expresses respect for that exaltation.

Cremation is increasingly the civil default in many Western countries — sometimes mandated for public health, cost, or environmental regulation. The House of Justice (9 December 1984) has ruled that if a believer's will directs cremation, burial should nevertheless proceed according to Bahá’í law unless civil law prevents it; in that case civil law is followed, but the Assembly takes no part. The believer cannot participate in arranging a cremation that civil law does not compel.

Shoghi Effendi permitted leaving the body to medical science for investigation, provided the will stipulates that remains not be cremated and not be taken more than one hour's journey from the place of death. The sequence — service to science, then burial near the place of death — honours both civic contribution and religious law.

Western Application and the Full Law

The distinction between full Revelation and present Western obligation is institutional, not doctrinal. The Universal House of Justice stated (9 June 1974, restated 2001) that for believers in the West “the only requirements now binding are to bury the body (not to cremate it), not to carry it more than a distance of one hour's journey from the place of death, and to say the Prayer for the Dead if the deceased is a believer over the age of 15.” The April 1981 letter to all National Assemblies added that the funeral should be carried out in a simple and dignified manner that would be a credit to the community.

The fuller provisions revealed in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas remain in force as Revelation and are binding in Iran and other Middle Eastern countries. Western believers are not required to observe them at present, though some do when means and circumstances allow. The fuller law includes:

Caskets — Crystal, Stone, Wood, and What the West Uses

Bahá’u’lláh decrees that the dead should be interred in coffins made of crystal, of hard resistant stone, or of wood that is both fine and durable, with graven rings upon their fingers. The Báb had prescribed crystal or polished stone as a sign of honour to the body the soul once animated.

The Universal House of Justice has explained that the spirit of the coffin law is durability. In addition to crystal, stone, and hard fine wood, there is no objection to using the hardest wood available or concrete. For the present, Bahá’ís are left free to make their own choices within that spirit. A simple wooden coffin or an approved concrete liner satisfies the principle when a crystal casket is neither obligatory nor practicable.

Western commercial funeral practice often pushes ornate sealed caskets, vaults, and embalming. Bahá’í law does not prescribe embalming; the House's Western requirements do not mandate it. Simplicity is the operative standard. Families negotiating with funeral directors should know the four binding points before grief and sales pressure converge. A dignified burial does not require the most expensive casket in the catalogue.

Silk Wrappings and the Burial Ring

The deceased should be enfolded in five sheets of silk or cotton; for those whose means are limited, a single sheet of either fabric suffices. Bahá’u’lláh confirmed the Báb's provision and added the concession for limited means. When asked whether “five sheets” meant five full-length shrouds or five cloths customarily used, Bahá’u’lláh indicated the use of five cloths. The Writings do not define the method of wrapping; Bahá’ís are free to use their judgment.

The ring bears the inscription: “I came forth from God, and return unto Him, detached from all save Him, holding fast to His Name, the Merciful, the Compassionate.” It is placed on the finger of the deceased. The formal prayer and the ring are meant for those who have attained the age of maturity. Western believers who omit the ring are not failing a presently binding requirement; those who include it honour the full text of the Revelation.

In many Western settings, funeral homes control preparation of the body. Families who wish shrouding rather than embalming, or who wish a ring placed, must state this in advance — in wills, in executor instructions, and in direct conversation with the funeral provider.

Civil Law — When the State Constrains Practice

Civil law governs death certificates, coroner procedures, cemetery permits, and sometimes cremation mandates. The Bahá’í approach is not civil disobedience for its own sake; it is faithful observance of revealed law within the law of the land, and candid consultation when the two cannot fully align.

Common friction points include: mandatory cremation in certain jurisdictions or institutions; waiting periods before release of the body; cemetery rules requiring vaults or prohibiting shrouds; and transport restrictions that exceed one hour's journey to the only available Bahá’í cemetery. The House has directed that believers follow the law as closely as possible and consult their institutions. Assemblies do not participate in cremations civil law compels. Families document their efforts — Prayer for the Dead recited, burial where possible, simplicity maintained — rather than treating civil constraint as permission to ignore the spirit of the law when choice remains.

Advance planning reduces harm: identifying a burial site within one hour of home, registering wishes with the Local Assembly where one exists, and ensuring non-Bahá’í relatives understand burial requirements before the crisis arrives. Wills and separate burial instruction sheets serve this purpose.

Believers who travel or live far from a Bahá’í cemetery should inquire before illness where burial within one hour remains feasible. In rural areas the nearest lawful plot may be a municipal cemetery rather than a dedicated Bahá’í section; the law is satisfied by burial, not by a particular deed holder. When a Bahá’í section exists, it expresses community dignity; when it does not, simple burial near the place of death still fulfils the binding Western requirements.

Memorial Gatherings

Burial law is precise; memorial practice is deliberately open. Shoghi Effendi counselled (10 January 1936) that “the utmost simplicity and flexibility should be observed” in memorial matters and warned against developing “a definite system of rigid rituals and practices among the believers.” There is no prescribed memorial format. Communities typically gather for readings from the Writings, music, and shared remembrance; gatherings may occur later, on anniversaries, or in forms meaningful to the family.

Cultural observances — ninth-day or fortieth-day gatherings familiar in some communities — are inherited custom, not Bahá’í law. The Holy Family observed such days after ’Abdu’l-Bahá’s passing; families who find meaning in them may hold them without contradicting the Teachings.

The community's role extends beyond the day of burial. grief-and-loss-in-the-community treats grief as spiritually valid, the Assembly's educational duty, and the layered care — practical help, sustained presence, prayers for the departed — that the framework supports. Burial law answers how the body is committed; memorial gatherings answer how the living continue to honour the bond.

Gifts and good deeds done in memory of the departed are, a letter written on behalf of Shoghi Effendi records, helpful to the soul's development in the realms beyond. Memorial gatherings may therefore include charitable acts in the deceased's name — a practical bridge between the open format Shoghi Effendi counselled and the Writings' teaching on the continuing bond between living and dead.

Points friends often raise

“Cremation is acceptable if the family prefers it.” It is not, unless civil law compels it and the Assembly does not participate.

“The Prayer for the Dead is optional.” For Bahá’ís aged fifteen and older, it is obligatory and must precede interment.

“We may fly the body home for burial.” The one-hour journey provision, measured by time from the place of death, rules out repatriation in most cases.

“Western Bahá’ís must use a crystal casket.” The full coffin law is not presently binding in the West; durable wood or concrete is acceptable in the spirit of the law.

“An official Bahá’í funeral is appropriate for any loved one.” Official service is for believers; prayers and Bahá’í presence at a non-believer's funeral are welcome when requested.

“Memorial services must follow a set programme.” Flexibility and simplicity are the Guardian's explicit counsel; rigidity is the danger to avoid.
