How are the goals of the Nine Year Plan being tracked, and what is the data discipline behind those numbers?

Direct answer

The Nine Year Plan's three numerical goals — 14,000 clusters past the first milestone, 11,000 past the second, and 5,000+ past the third by Riḍván 2031, with at least one third-milestone cluster in every country and territory — are tracked through a four-tier institutional pipeline: cluster agencies and Auxiliary Boards report to Regional Bahá’í Councils and National Spiritual Assemblies, who report in turn to the Universal House of Justice via the International Teaching Centre. The reading instrument at the cluster level is the reflection meeting, not a metrics dashboard. The data discipline behind the figures rests on three principles that have to be understood together: milestones are read rather than awarded; growth is measured by flows (declarations, new programmes, clusters crossing thresholds) rather than by a published stock of believers; and definitional changes — most notably the 2022 re-clustering — must be marked, not smoothed. The House of Justice does not penalise short-falls. The goal is the direction of movement and the discipline of learning, not bureaucratic compliance.

What the goals are, as stated

The framing for the Nine Year Plan was set out in the Universal House of Justice's 30 December 2021 message launching the Plan; the numerical targets themselves were fixed at Riḍván 2022, after the national re-clustering exercise the 30 December 2021 letter had requested. Keeping these two messages distinct is itself part of the data discipline this page is about. The 30 December 2021 letter asked NSAs and Regional Councils to "forecast the numbers of clusters where progress could be made past the first, second, and third milestones, respectively, during the Plan," with the explicit caveat that these "are only intended to be well-informed estimates; they can be refined later as necessary and need not be laboured over at length." The Riḍván 2022 message, after national re-clustering, fixed the global denominator at "over 22,000" clusters and translated those forecasts into the projections that have stood ever since: a programme of growth at some level of development in around 14,000 clusters by 2031, intensive activity in 11,000 of them, and "the number of clusters where the third milestone has been passed will rise above 5,000 by 2031." The same Riḍván 2022 message describes a two-horizon goal-setting frame, asking NSAs to consider "the advances they expect to see in their respective communities by Riḍván 2026 and then by Riḍván 2031."

Within that, the 30 December 2021 message names a stricter sub-goal: "during the Nine Year Plan the process of growth reach this level of intensity in at least one cluster in every country and every region. This constitutes one of the Plan's chief objectives." The grounds the message gives — "the immense value, to a region, of a cluster where the third milestone has been passed" — anticipate what the Riḍván 2021 message had already framed as a hope, observing that "clusters of marked strength, as had been hoped, have proven to be reservoirs of knowledge and resources for their neighbours." The 31 December 2025 message confirms the formulation in present-tense form: many third-milestone clusters "are acting as reservoirs of knowledge and resources to the clusters that surround them."

The numerical goals sit underneath a qualitative aim, which the same message states as the framing of the entire 2022–2046 arc of Plans: "the release of the society-building power of the Faith in ever-greater measures." The numbers track movement toward that aim; they are not the aim itself.

The tracking architecture

Reporting flows up four tiers, each adding a different kind of reading.

  • Cluster level. Area Teaching Committees, institute coordinator teams, and Auxiliary Board members and their assistants gather what is happening in the cluster. The instrument is the quarterly reflection meeting, where friends "read their own reality" before settling on next steps. Numbers — participants, study-circle completions, new declarations, new neighbourhoods — are visible at this level, but only as the legible surface of a deeper question: has the work matured?
  • Regional and national level. Cluster aggregates are consulted by Regional Bahá’í Councils and consolidated by National Spiritual Assemblies. The forecasting exercise the 30 December 2021 message asked of every NSA — to "forecast the numbers of clusters where progress could be made past the first, second, and third milestones, respectively, during the Plan" — produced the national contributions that, viewed under Riḍván 2022's two-horizon frame ("the advances they expect to see in their respective communities by Riḍván 2026 and then by Riḍván 2031"), built up to the global 14,000 / 11,000 / 5,000+ figure.
  • Continental level. The Continental Boards of Counsellors add a pastoral reading: assessment of cluster spirit, of community quality, of where the work is alive and where it has stalled. This sits alongside the numerical aggregation, not subordinate to it.
  • Global level. The International Teaching Centre consolidates the picture for the Universal House of Justice. The UHJ's Riḍván messages and end-of-phase letters report the worldwide state.

The architecture is unusual in that the upward channel is matched by an equally important downward channel: the Counsellors' Conferences and the messages that emerge from them — the 31 December 2025 letter being the most recent — return interpretation, not just instructions, to the field.

The data discipline

Milestones are read, not awarded. The 28 December 2010 message established the first two milestones as part of "a rich and dynamic continuum," not categorical states. The 29 December 2015 message, characterising the second milestone five years on, insisted that the quantitative advances that accompany it — "a rise in the number of those involved in conversations… in how many homes are being visited, in core activities and participation" — are "the more visible signs of a much finer development: the gradual spread, within a population, of a pattern of community life based on Bahá’u’lláh’s teachings." The third milestone is named later in the corpus — appearing, for instance, in the Riḍván 2018 message's reference to "the friends to pass the third milestone along a continuum of development" — and the same logic applies upward: the qualitative reading carries the weight, the quantitative signs make it visible. No institution issues a milestone certificate. The cluster agencies and the Counsellors converge on a reading.

No cumulative membership figure is published. The Universal House of Justice reports flows — declarations per Plan, new intensive programmes of growth, new third-milestone clusters, core-activity participants — but does not publish a worldwide Bahá’í population total. The frequently-quoted "five to eight million Bahá’ís" figures are secondary, drawn from the World Religion Database or Britannica; they do not appear in the primary corpus and are not how the Plan's progress is measured. (See How many Bahá’ís are there in the world? for the full treatment.)

Definitional shifts must be marked. The most consequential is the 2022 re-clustering. Riḍván 2022 announced: "the total number of clusters in the world has risen by a quarter and now stands at over 22,000." That is not organic growth. National Assemblies, asked to reassess their clustering schemes as part of the goal-setting exercise, re-drew cluster boundaries — typically by subdividing larger clusters into more workable units. Any time-series chart that crosses 2021/2022 without a discontinuity is misleading. (growth time-series flags this in the data-quality notes.) The same care applies to the localities series: the 2023 figure of 100,000 is smaller than the 1980 figure of 106,000 because the definition narrowed, not because the community shrank.

Self-assessment, with discipline. The cluster's reading of itself is the primary input — but with discipline. The reflection meeting is the reading instrument; reports are the writing instrument; the two must answer to each other. The 30 December 2021 letter is itself the load-bearing authority on the discipline of numbers, framing the cluster forecasts as "only intended to be well-informed estimates" that "can be refined later as necessary and need not be laboured over at length." The numbers are real; they are not exact, and they are not the point. Reflections on Growth Issue 35 illustrates the failure mode that justifies that caution. In the Sanniquellie account it carries, the friends report that the turn came when they recognised their "goals did not match the available human resources" and that they needed "to establish a system to collect more reliable statistics" — the reflection meeting having to do its work as a space for consultation before any number it generated could mean anything. The House of Justice's habit of qualifying every figure — "no fewer than", "approximately", "more than" — is the same discipline at the global tier: every exact-looking figure should be read as a well-informed estimate unless the source says otherwise.

The UHJ does not penalise short-falls. This is structural, not cultural. A community that misses a numerical goal but has clarified what the goal requires has succeeded in the only way the Plan asks it to succeed. The 30 December 2021 message's sub-goal of "at least one cluster in every country and every region" past the third milestone is a case in point: by Phase 1 close, the 31 December 2025 message reports that "by Riḍván there will be a cluster where the third milestone has been passed in around half of the 160 countries and regions where, at the start of the Plan, still no such cluster existed—a dramatic accomplishment." Half is not all. The message names this as success because the direction and the discipline are right, even where the specific number is not yet met.

Phase 1 close and Phase 2 launch

The Riḍván 2026 message confirms the closure of Phase 1 and opens Phase 2 without setting fresh numerical targets: "Now begins the second, greater phase of the Nine Year Plan." The 2031 projections set at Riḍván 2022 stand. What the message does is sharpen the qualitative orientation under which the existing numerical goals will be pursued — naming the emergence of "the community as a more conspicuous protagonist of the Plan," the recognition of the work as "the cultivation of a way of life patterned on the divine teachings—a shaping of actions, interactions, and aspirations," and identifying "third milestone clusters [as] proving to be fertile ground for rich experience to be harvested and propagated."

The most telling sentence about the limits of numerical tracking comes from the 31 December 2025 message: "Given the far-reaching implications of what is occurring, the term 'programme of growth' no longer does full justice to such developments." In the most advanced clusters, the framework's own vocabulary is starting to lag the reality it was built to describe. That is the answer, in the end, to what the data discipline is for: it exists so that the institutions know when the framework needs to grow.
