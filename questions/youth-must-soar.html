What does the guidance say about the role of Bahá’í youth in cluster activities — and the recent emphasis that "the youth must soar"?

Across the Plans the Universal House of Justice has framed the Bahá’í youth — broadly, those in the post-junior-youth arc from roughly fifteen into early adulthood — as at the forefront of cluster work: the age group leading community-building in advanced clusters, the principal source of animators for the junior youth programme and tutors for the main sequence, and the cohort whose service most readily kindles others. The Riḍván 2024 message names this with unusual sharpness — "All must surge, but the youth must soar" — not as a put-down of the wider community but as a summons to the youth themselves and an instruction to the cluster and its institutions to make space for that soaring. The phrase belongs to a longer arc anchored in the 1 July 2013 message to the youth conferences and developed across Riḍván 2010, the 12 December 2011 letter, the Nine Year Plan messages of 2021, and the ISGP letter of 26 May 2024.

The 1 July 2013 message — the load-bearing text

The load-bearing UHJ letter on this question is the 1 July 2013 message "To the participants in the forthcoming 114 youth conferences throughout the world." It opens with the historical pattern: "When the exalted figure of the Báb, aged just twenty-five, arose to deliver His revolutionizing message to the world, many among those who accepted and spread His teachings were young, even younger than the Báb Himself." The House then states the present continuity: "every generation of youth, drawing inspiration from the same divine impulse to cast the world anew, has seized the opportunity to contribute to the latest stage in the unfolding process… It is a pattern that has suffered no interruption from the time of the Báb to this present hour."

The letter then names what the youth in fact carry. "Not surprisingly, it is your age group that is gaining the most experience at aiding junior youth, and children too, with their moral and spiritual development, fostering in them capacity for collective service and true friendship." And on the work in receptive places: "The possibilities presented by collective action are especially evident in the work of community building, a process that is gaining momentum in many a cluster and in neighbourhoods and villages throughout the world that have become centres of intense activity. Youth are often at the forefront of the work in these settings — not only Bahá’í youth, but those of like mind who can see the positive effects of what the Bahá’ís have initiated and grasp the underlying vision of unity and spiritual transformation." The two claims — that the youth are currently the most experienced cohort at the children's-and-junior-youth end of the educational process, and that they are currently at the forefront of community-building in receptive settings — are the empirical premise the later phrase "the youth must soar" presupposes.

A pastoral aside from the same letter: "To follow a path of service, whatever form one's activity assumes, requires faith and tenacity. In this connection, the benefit of walking that path in the company of others is immense." The youth's role is given as a collective role; the soaring is not solo flight.

"The youth must soar" — Riḍván 2024 in its own context

The phrase comes from the Riḍván 2024 message (19 April 2024), two paragraphs after the House writes that "there must be a sustained, rapid rise in the number of those committing their time, their energy, their concentration to the success of this work." The full passage:

"We are conscious of how much vibrancy and strength is generated in any society by its youth being awakened to the vision of Bahá’u’lláh and becoming protagonists of the Plan. And so, with what immense kindliness, courage, and complete reliance on God must Bahá’í youth resolve to reach out to their peers and bring them into this work! All must surge, but the youth must soar."

— Universal House of Justice, Riḍván 2024

The following guardrails are interpretive — they read the grammar of the passage, not an official UHJ exegesis. Three readings to refuse and one to accept. Refuse: that the call diminishes the service of older friends — the verb "must surge" is itself addressed to the entire community, and the youth are named as one cohort within a wider rising, not its replacement. Refuse: that the call instrumentalises the youth as a recruitment workforce — the qualifying clause "with what immense kindliness, courage, and complete reliance on God" is doing real work, and the action named is "reach out to their peers," not generic activity. Refuse: that the call frames the youth as future protagonists — the grammar is present-tense throughout ("becoming protagonists of the Plan"), and the 1 July 2013 letter had already established that they are currently at the forefront of the work in advanced settings. Accept: that the House is naming, in a single image, both an empirical observation (a society's youth, when awakened, generate disproportionate vibrancy) and a charge (the youth themselves are to commit their time, energy, and concentration at a higher pitch than the wider community).

The follow-up letter from the Department of the Secretariat to the Institute for Studies in Global Prosperity (26 May 2024) glosses the phrase by linking it to a talk of ’Abdu’l-Bahá: "This counsel calls to mind a talk by ’Abdu’l-Bahá in which He describes how a faithful soul should aspire to flight: 'But human flight is through faith. Human flight is through the virtues of the world of humanity. Human flight is through the acquisition of perfections. Human flight is through adherence to the divine teachings. Human flight is through service to the oneness of the world of humanity.'" The soaring is moral and spiritual, not careerist or activist; it is faith, virtue, perfection, adherence, service.

What this looks like in cluster life

The field record (Tier F) is consistent with the framing. The Khujand, Tajikistan account in R.O.G. 30 reports that nearly 70% of junior youth programme graduates have entered the main sequence of institute courses, and "many are now at the forefront of the work in the cluster," with one former participant later serving on the National Spiritual Assembly. This is the JY-graduate-into-youth-protagonist pipeline that the 1 July 2013 message presupposes: the youth cohort in an advanced cluster is largely composed of recent JY graduates who have moved through Books 1, 3, 5, and into 6 and 7, and whose first serious acts of service were animating a JY group or teaching a children's class.

The same R.O.G. 30 records that the age-15 transition is best prepared before age 15 — through conversations between animator and junior youth about paths of service and through invitations to co-facilitate sections of the JY texts. R.O.G. 31 notes that a single animator training in Unity cluster (Nigeria) produced fourteen new groups; it is the youth who supply that animator pool. R.O.G. 31 also describes the animators in the Madrid learning site as drawn largely from the youth of nearby clusters who travel for accompaniment, return, and animate.

Three practical implications for cluster life follow.

The youth belong in the consultation of the cluster as participants in their own right. The 1 July 2013 letter addresses the youth directly as those "taking an increasing level of responsibility upon your shoulders." In practice this means inviting youth into reflection meetings, cluster planning consultations, and Counsellor visits not as observers or as a symbolic delegation but as the friends doing the substantive work. A reflection meeting whose youth-aged participants only attend the closing devotional is a reflection meeting underestimating who in fact carries its plans.

Institutional confidence is the resource the youth most need. Core activities led by youth — junior youth groups, study circles, children's classes — succeed when an animator or tutor is accompanied by an Auxiliary Board member or coordinator with real time to give, and where the institutions visibly trust the youth to carry the work. Under-resourcing youth-led activities while exhorting the youth to soar is a contradiction the cluster's institutions are responsible to resolve.

The youth's natural energy does not substitute for accompaniment. The pattern across R.O.G. 30 and R.O.G. 31 is that high-functioning youth movements are accompanied by sustained coordinator presence, regular seminars, and one-on-one conversation. Youth burn out when they are admired for their energy but left to carry the work alone; the coherent life of service the institute process is shaping cannot be improvised.

Pastoral notes

Three cautions are worth holding alongside the Riḍván 2024 phrase.

First, "the youth must soar" is not a comparative slight against older friends. The same Riḍván 2024 paragraph began "All must surge." The ISGP letter of 26 May 2024 is similarly careful — it speaks of "the efforts that have been made through the decades by the followers of the Blessed Beauty, youth and adults alike." A cluster that uses the phrase to imply that older friends should defer is misreading the text.

Second, the youth's protagonism is exercised in the company of others. The 1 July 2013 letter is explicit: "the benefit of walking that path in the company of others is immense." A youth movement that loses the company of its elders, or the company of its junior-youth-graduate cohort coming up behind it, becomes brittle.

Third, the phrase does not authorise self-romanticising activism. The ISGP-letter gloss — "human flight is through faith… through the virtues of the world of humanity… through service to the oneness of the world of humanity" — relocates the soaring from external profile to interior discipline. The youth are not asked to be visible; they are asked to be faithful, courageous, kind, and reliant on God in their service to peers.
