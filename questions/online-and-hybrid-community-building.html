What does the guidance say about adapting community-building activities for online or hybrid settings (post-COVID)?

Online tools can extend the reach of devotional meetings, study circles, deepenings, and consultative meetings — but they do not substitute for the embodied community-building work in a neighbourhood, which proceeds through home visits, walking through the neighbourhood, and the patient face-to-face cultivation of relationship. The post-COVID guidance points toward integration, not replacement. The default location of the core activities remains the home, the neighbourhood, the village; online is a supplement that earns its place where the in-person rhythm cannot reach.

What the pandemic letters said

The Universal House of Justice's Naw-Rúz 177 message (20 March 2020), written as restrictions descended, framed the constraint as both real and survivable. "Though having to adapt to new circumstances, the believers have used creative means to strengthen bonds of friendship, and to foster among themselves and those known to them spiritual consciousness and qualities of tranquillity, confidence, and reliance on God. The elevated conversations that have occurred as a result, whether remotely or in person, have been a source of comfort and inspiration to many." The phrase "whether remotely or in person" is the operative one: remote contact is admitted into the grammar of the work, in the same breath as in-person contact, and on the same footing of being a vehicle for elevated conversation. It is not elevated above the in-person; nor is the in-person fenced off as the only legitimate medium.

Riḍván 2020 extended the same posture — noting that where conditions allow or communication tools make it possible, "a sense of extraordinary solidarity is being actively nurtured among souls sharing similar circumstances," and affirming that "the dynamics of community life, so important for collective progress, will not be subdued." The 25 November 2020 message looked past the disruption to the One Year Plan ahead, naming the conditions for advance not as an online toolkit but as "a flourishing community, especially a centre of intense activity in a village or neighbourhood." The locus of the work is named, even mid-pandemic, in placed terms.

What the post-pandemic letters said

The Riḍván 2021 message, surveying the close of the Five Year Plan, named the cost honestly. "The restrictions on personal interaction that waxed and waned in most countries over this period could have dealt the community's collective efforts a severe blow… Difficulties involved in opening up new villages and neighbourhoods to the Faith in the midst of a global health crisis, or expanding activities that were at an early stage when the pandemic began, prevented an even higher total from being reached." The community had adapted; the framework had held; but the new work — opening new neighbourhoods, breaking ground in early-stage clusters — had been the casualty. The implication is exact: online tools sustained existing relationships well; they did not generate new ones at the rate the in-person rhythm had.

The 30 December 2021 message, launching the Nine Year Plan, makes no concession to a permanent online turn. The setting it names for the Plan's central work is "receptive neighbourhoods and villages"; the activity it describes is bodily:

"Within each centre of intense activity, collaborative arrangements emerge among groups of families, who organize community-building activities among themselves with a view to widening the embrace of such activities to many nearby households."

— Universal House of Justice, 30 December 2021

The Riḍván 2022 message confirms the same orientation across the new epoch.

What field experience confirms

The R.O.G. 35 accounts of clusters that had stalled — Bogotá, Hamilton, Napoli-Portici, Bucharest — converge on a recovery pattern that is structural rather than technological: "concentrating effort at the neighbourhood level allows a dynamic pattern of expansion and consolidation to emerge." In Itaunja, where local activities had ceased entirely, recovery came through weekly visits from Lucknow — a team physically travelling, not a video call. In Alotau, restoration began with a face-to-face institutional meeting that reconvened the cluster agencies. The corpus does not denigrate online tools; it simply does not name them as the lever that moves a stalled cluster.

What works online

(Tier F — field practice and editorial inference grounded in UHJ locus-of-work letters.)

In practice, online tools have settled into a stable supplementary role:

  • Devotional gatherings that gather geographically distant friends, often around a particular reading or theme.
  • Study circles for working adults whose schedules will not permit a weekly in-person meeting; cross-cluster cohorts where no local critical mass exists.
  • Institute training across regions — animator training, tutor reflection, coordinator gatherings.
  • Consultative meetings between cluster agencies, between reflection-meeting organisers across sectors, and across regional institutions.
  • Hybrid reflection meetings in clusters with sectors, where a plenary feed lets dispersed participants join while sectors meet locally.

What does not transfer

(Tier F — field practice and editorial inference grounded in UHJ locus-of-work letters.)

A first meaningful conversation with a stranger is the rite of hospitality of a particular place; it does not survive translation to a screen. A junior-youth group is a relationship with a neighbourhood, not with an animator on a webcam. A home visit done by video keeps a friendship alive but does not deepen one. The texture of fellowship at a Feast — the embrace, the food, the shared space — is what the Feast is for; a webcam Feast preserves the form and loses the substance.

Pastoral caveats

Do not let online convenience become the reason a cluster never returns to its neighbourhood — the gravity is real and the recovery is harder than the lapse. Do not exclude friends without good internet from the cluster's life by quietly relocating its centre of gravity to Zoom. Do not treat hybrid as a substitute for the disciplined in-person rhythm; it is a bridge across a particular obstacle, not a new normal. Do not treat the convenience of online as evidence of its sufficiency. The 30 December 2021 message's image — "families, who organize community-building activities among themselves with a view to widening the embrace of such activities to many nearby households" — is the test: a cluster's online activity is healthy in proportion as it serves, and is anchored by, that placed work.
