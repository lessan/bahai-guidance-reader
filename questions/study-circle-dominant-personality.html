What does the guidance say about handling situations where one or two strong personalities dominate a study circle?

Dominance in a study circle is, before anything else, a facilitation problem rather than an interpersonal one. The Ruhi method itself is constructed to disperse the floor — everyone reads aloud, everyone answers the exercises, the text carries authority rather than the most articulate voice — and the tutor’s primary task is to keep that method working. The repair is small, repeated facilitation moves: rotating who reads, calling on quieter participants by name, returning to the text when discussion drifts into opinion, and accompanying a chronically dominant participant privately rather than confronting the pattern in the room.

The method itself disperses the floor

A study circle is not a discussion group, and the Ruhi books are explicit that the difference is structural. Book 1’s prefatory notes to the tutor name the antimatter directly: “this challenge is seldom met by asking questions such as, ‘What does this mean to you?‘ Questions of this kind tend to reduce knowledge and truth to the level of opinion. And it then proves difficult to create an atmosphere in which consultation among the members of the group actually gives rise to increased understanding” (Ruhi Book 1, preface). The reason this line is load-bearing for the dominance question is that opinion-surfacing prompts reward the loudest voice. The text carries authority — not as a decorative claim but as the operative centre of authority in the Bahá’í framework. Once the tutor concedes that authority to whoever speaks first, longest, or with the most confidence, the structural ground that protects every other participant has gone — and there is no facilitation move that can recover it from inside that frame.

The corresponding move that does work is the one Book 1 names two paragraphs earlier: “It falls on the tutor to ensure that every member of the group remains engaged in the process of learning fostered by the material. To elicit participation without pressuring any individual to speak is often the challenge” (Ruhi Book 1, preface). This is the operative sentence for almost every practical move below. The tutor’s discipline is to elicit, not to invite an opinion and not to put a quiet participant on the spot. Eliciting is achieved by structure — rotating reading, sequenced exercises, paired work — far more reliably than by personal charisma.

The tutor’s role: companion-not-instructor, but not passive either

The Ruhi methodology is firm about what the tutor is not. “The relationship between the tutor and the other participants is not one of teacher-to-student; all are consciously engaged in a process in which everyone is seeking to learn. But the tutor is not a detached and passive facilitator of discussion either” (Ruhi Book 1, preface). That second sentence is what saves the dominated study circle. A tutor who interprets the companion-not-instructor commitment as “let the discussion go where it wants” has misread the form. The tutor is responsible for the environment — and that environment exists precisely so that every participant comes to see themselves, in the Universal House of Justice’s formulation in its Riḍván 2010 message, “as active agents of their own learning, as protagonists of a constant effort to apply knowledge to effect individual and collective transformation. Failing this, no matter how many study circles are formed in a cluster, the force necessary to propel change will not be generated.” A study circle in which one or two voices speak for the whole has failed this test in miniature.

Ruhi Book 7 develops this stance further as the discipline of accompaniment rather than instruction. Its guidance on participatory dynamics names what passive facilitation looks like from the inside: “the tutor must be alert, attentive, and observant. His or her thoughts should be focused at all times on how to help each of the participants gain a more complete understanding of what is being studied” (Ruhi Book 7, Unit 2). A dominated circle is often a tutor who has stopped watching the room.

The same Riḍván 2010 message extends the frame beyond the individual tutor: the training institute must “lay equal stress on the effectiveness of the system in its entirety,” and at cluster level the coordinator must “accompany those who serve as tutors” and “arrange periodic gatherings for them to reflect on their endeavours.” A dominance pattern that persists session after session is therefore not only a facilitation problem in the room — it is a signal worth bringing to the coordinator’s reflection gatherings, where tutors consult together about what is working.

Specific facilitation moves that work

The following items are Tier F editorial operationalisation — sound field practice derived from Book 1’s structural logic (rotation, exercises, text-centred elicitation), not verbatim Ruhi prescriptions. R.O.G. quotations below are Tier F field record with explicit attribution. None of these moves is a clever trick; each is a small disciplined habit repeated session after session.

  • Rotate who reads aloud. Paragraph by paragraph or quotation by quotation. Reading aloud is itself a form of engagement; the dominant participant who has read the section aloud is less likely to monologue about it afterwards, and the quiet participant who has read aloud has already used their voice in the room.
  • Wait after a question is asked. Silence pulls in voices that quick discussion shuts out. The dominant participant typically fills silence first; resisting the urge to call on them — or to fill the silence yourself — is the move.
  • Call on quieter participants by exercise, not by opinion. “What does the second paragraph say is true of every soul?” invites the participant‘s actual reading. “What do you think about this?” invites them to compete with the dominant participant on the dominant participant’s preferred ground.
  • Return to the text when discussion drifts. “Let’s read that passage again.” Book 1 is exact about why: prolonging deliberations “by introducing many related but peripheral concepts tends to diminish the effectiveness of the material.” The text is the discipline; recurring to it is not authoritarian, it is the form of the study circle reasserting itself.
  • Accompany the chronically dominant participant privately. Not in the circle. R.O.G. 27‘s account of the Mongolian tutor reflection gathering surfaced exactly the same pattern at the level of coordinators role-playing tutors: “the coordinators acting as tutors were doing the same as before—mainly they were speaking more than the participants and giving lectures rather than listening.” The remedy was not public correction but reflection in a separate gathering. The same applies one level down: a short private conversation, framed as an invitation into the tutor’s consultative work rather than a complaint, often proves helpful in field practice.
  • Consider co-tutoring. R.O.G. 26’s regional junior-youth coordinator in England names the accompaniment pattern at the coordinator level — “The coordinators that I have learned the most from are those with whom I can act, reflect, and consult” — not study-circle co-tutoring directly. A second tutor in the room is pastoral inference from that pattern: it can shift the social texture of the circle and give the primary tutor someone to consult with after each session about which voices are being heard.

The link to consultation — and where it ends

A study circle is not an Assembly consultation in the institutional sense — there is no vote, no binding decision — but Book 1’s preface names “consultation among the members of the group” as the pedagogical form through which shared understanding of the text emerges. The same member-conduct disciplines apply in that room. ‘Abdu‘l-Bahá’s prerequisites — “purity of motive, radiance of spirit, detachment from all else save God… humility and lowliness amongst His loved ones, patience and long-suffering” (Selections from the Writings of ‘Abdu‘l-Bahá, §43) — apply to the tutor and to every participant. The Compilation on Consultation items [8], [9], [20], and [22] gather the material most directly relevant here — detachment from one’s own opinion once expressed, frankness without belittling — and the dominance pattern is, at root, a failure of that first discipline: the moment a contribution has been offered, it ceases to be the speaker’s property. The fuller treatment of detachment-from-opinion as the technical move in institutional consultation, including the vote-as-backstop and the support-the-decision discipline, lives in the sibling page the consultation question and is not duplicated here.

Pastoral caveats

The following are Tier F pastoral inference — sound field guidance aligned with Book 1‘s spirit and R.O.G. 27’s practice of private reflection, not direct UHJ or Ruhi prescription. Three counterfeits of the corrective move are worth naming. First, do not embarrass the dominant participant publicly — a public correction in the circle creates two casualties (the dominant participant who feels rebuked, and the quieter participants who learn to fear what might happen to them next). Second, do not solve the pattern by quietly dropping the participant from invitations to subsequent circles; the dominant participant is often the most uncertain about their own grasp of the text, and exclusion confirms a deficit story rather than addressing it. Third, do not let the pattern drift forward into the next book; a circle that completes Book 1 with the same dominance pattern intact will reproduce it in Book 2, where the stakes — outward acts of service — are higher.

The frame to hold throughout is the one Book 1 supplies. The tutor’s task is to ensure that every member remains engaged in the process of learning fostered by the material. Dominance is the test case for that task. Met with structure, repeated, accompanied with patience, it dissolves into the consultative space the Ruhi method was designed to create.
