What is the headline statistic from Riḍván 2021, and how does that compare to a decade earlier?

The Riḍván 2021 message reports 300,000 core activities sustained at once, participation above two million, approximately 4,000 intensive programmes of growth, and 1,000 clusters past the third milestone in nearly 100 countries. A decade earlier, the 28 December 2010 message anchored the comparable figures at roughly half a million participants sustained by close to 70,000 tutors of study circles, with the 1,500-IPG goal of the 2006–2011 Five Year Plan having just been met a year early. The decadal shift is roughly four-fold in participation and more than doubled in intensive programmes — the House’s own phrase for the 1,500→4,000 IPG movement — a change in density, not merely scale.

What the Riḍván 2021 message actually says

The Universal House of Justice writes, of the just-concluded 2016–2021 Five Year Plan:

“At the start of this Plan, the capacity existed for conducting just over 100,000 core activities at a given time… Now, 300,000 core activities are being sustained at once. Participation in those activities has risen above two million, which is also close to a threefold increase. There are 329 national and regional training institutes in operation, and their capacity is evidenced by the fact that three-quarters of a million people have been enabled to complete at least one book of the sequence.” — Universal House of Justice, Riḍván 2021

The message goes on: “the number of intensive programmes of growth more than doubled and now stands at approximately 4,000,” and the number of third-milestone clusters has grown from “around 200, spread across some 40 countries” to “an astonishing 1,000 in nearly 100 countries — a quarter of all the intensive programmes of growth in the world.” The House describes this as “the capstone to an accomplishment twenty-five years in the making” — capacity built since the 1996 Four Year Plan.

A decade earlier: the 28 December 2010 anchor

The decade-prior comparator is the Universal House of Justice‘s letter of 28 December 2010 to the Conference of the Continental Boards of Counsellors. That letter retired the older A/B/C/D cluster classification in favour of the three-milestone framework and delivered the era’s headline figures:

“Fuelling the systematic multiplication of core activities across the globe, with no fewer than half a million known participants at a given time, are the efforts of close to 70,000 friends capable of serving as tutors of study circles.” — Universal House of Justice, 28 December 2010

Riḍván 2010 adds the IPG count: “in every continent of the globe a fresh complement of intensive programmes of growth under way, raising the total number worldwide over the 1,500 mark and securing the goal of the Five Year Plan, one year in advance of its conclusion.” The 1,500-IPG goal — set in 2006, reached a year early in 2010 — is the era’s defining numeric milestone.

The metrics aren‘t perfectly parallel — the 2010 messages report what was salient then (IPG count, participants, tutors); the 2021 message reports what was salient then (total core activities, participants, third-milestone clusters). All figures are lower bounds — the House’s habitual modifier is “no fewer than” or “approximately.”

What the figures register — and what they do not

The 300,000 / two-million figure registers a change in the density of community-building activity. A core activity is one of four small, sustained units of community life — study circle, children’s class, junior-youth group, devotional meeting (see CoreActivities). Tripling participation while holding the institute pipeline open means the institute process is producing human resources at the rate the programme of growth requires.

What the figures do not register is total Bahá’í membership. The Universal House of Justice does not publish a cumulative worldwide enrolment figure; the often-cited “five to eight million Bahá’ís” estimates are secondary (the World Religion Database, Britannica) and are not in the primary corpus. Participation in core activities includes seekers, family members, and friends, many not (yet) Bahá’ís — and that breadth is the point of the metric.

Caveats on time-series interpretation

Three caveats. First, the cluster baseline shifted in 2022: a global re-clustering exercise raised the cluster count from roughly 17,000 to 22,000 — a definitional change, not organic growth. Any comparison crossing the 2021/2022 boundary should mark this break. Second, the figures anticipated by close of the One Year Plan in the 30 December 2021 message — over 6,000 clusters with programmes of growth, close to 5,000 past the second milestone, 1,300 past the third — are projections delivered after Riḍván 2021, not part of the Riḍván 2021 snapshot. Third, “core-activity participants,” “tutors,” and “Bahá’ís” are three different counts; conflating them has produced confused journalism for two decades.
