How does the society-building framing differ from "entry by troops", and what continuities exist?

The two framings represent successive stages in a single arc rather than competing visions. "Entry by troops" — prophesied by ’Abdu’l-Bahá and elaborated by Shoghi Effendi and the Universal House of Justice — oriented the community toward numerical expansion and mass conversion as a necessary threshold; society-building, the frame of the Nine Year Plan, presupposes that threshold has been meaningfully crossed and asks what the community does with the capacity it has now developed. The shift is from growing the community inward to transforming its environment outward.

From numerical growth to qualitative transformation

During the mass-teaching era, "entry by troops" functioned as both a prophecy and a mobilising call. Shoghi Effendi, in his 18 July 1953 letter, described the Crusade as a period in which the Bahá’í world "as prophesied by ’Abdu’l-Bahá, will witness the entry by troops of peoples of diverse nations and races into the Bahá’í world." The Riḍván 1964 message likewise records "that entry by troops into the Cause of God prophesied by ’Abdu’l-Bahá." Communities organised large-scale teaching campaigns aimed at rapid mass conversion. In certain contexts — rural Africa and parts of India (Tier F generalisation) — something genuinely approaching that scale was achieved. The dominant question was: How many people can we bring in? Success was measured in enrollment figures, and the horizon was a quantitative breakthrough that would change the numerical standing of the Faith worldwide.

By the early 2000s the Universal House of Justice had begun reframing the work around systematic process. Language such as "intensive programmes of growth," "milestones," and "capacity building" supplemented — rather than replaced — the entry-by-troops idiom. The Framework for Action introduction still describes entry by troops as a process "promised by the Master" that "began in the Ten Year Crusade." The emphasis shifted toward whether participants were deepening, whether core activities were sustainable, whether the community could absorb and accompany new souls rather than simply enroll them. This was not a retreat from growth but a maturation of what growth meant.

Society-building as the next stage of the same vision

The NineYearPlan frame takes the cluster-era gains as its starting point. Enough clusters now have functioning programmes of growth — enough neighbourhoods have active children's classes, junior youth groups, and study circles — that the question is no longer primarily Can we generate growth? but What does that growth produce in the world around it? Society-building is the claim that Bahá’í community life, at sufficient depth and scale, begins genuinely reshaping how people relate to one another: patterns of consultation replace conflict, a moral culture emerges, institutions of the social order are influenced from within.

The Universal House of Justice's 30 December 2021 message launching the Twenty-Five-Year arc names the operative aim: "the release of the society-building power of the Faith in ever-greater measures." The same letter explains that raising vibrant communities, pursuing spiritual and material progress, and contributing to prevalent discourses are "means of releasing what the Guardian described as 'the society-building power' of the Faith." This is a qualitative claim. Where "entry by troops" measured success by counting who had crossed a threshold into the community, society-building measures success by observing whether the community is generating a transformed social environment — not merely a larger membership roll. The locus of attention moves from the boundary of the community (who joins) to its neighbourhood and village context (what changes). Both framings are grounded in the same eschatological vision of a spiritually transformed civilisation. The progression — preparation, growth, transformation — is continuous, and the UHJ has not repudiated "entry by troops" language; it still appears in Plan discourse as a description of one stage in a longer story rather than a distinct programme now superseded.
