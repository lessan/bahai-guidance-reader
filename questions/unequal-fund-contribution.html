How should an institution handle unequal Bahá’í Fund contributions without judgment or pressure?

The institution's role is to make education about the Fund equal and consistent — ensuring every believer understands the privilege of contributing — while accepting that the amounts given will naturally vary with individual means. Contributions are voluntary in amount and method, private, and carry no minimum; the guidance explicitly warns against any form of social pressure or comparison. An institution that creates the conditions for informed, free giving has fulfilled its responsibility, regardless of what each member chooses to do.

The Nature of the Fund

The Bahá’í Fund rests on three pillars that shape everything an institution does around it. First, only Bahá’ís may contribute — Shoghi Effendi treats this as an axiom of Bahá’í administration: only those who have "identified themselves with the Faith and are regarded as its avowed and unreserved supporters" should be invited to finance institutions of a strictly Bahá’í character (25 October 1929, Bahá’í Administration, pp. 171–172). The Universal House of Justice restates the point: giving to the Fund is "a spiritual privilege not open to those who have not accepted Bahá’u’lláh" (7 August 1985). Second, there is no required amount; the governing principle is contributing "according to their means," as the House of Justice appealed at Naw-Rúz 1979 — which by definition produces unequal contributions. Third, the privilege of giving is both a responsibility and a source of bounty, not merely optional goodwill:

"Giving to the Fund, therefore, is a spiritual privilege not open to those who have not accepted Bahá’u’lláh, of which no believer should deny himself. It is both a responsibility and a source of bounty… The importance of contributing resides in the degree of sacrifice of the giver, the spirit of devotion with which the contribution is made and the unity of the friends in this service."

— Universal House of Justice, 7 August 1985, to National Spiritual Assemblies

Shoghi Effendi pairs Fund contribution with Ḥuqúqu’lláh as "the obligation of every Bahá’í" — yet the same corpus insists friends should, "under no circumstances, be required" to contribute and that all gifts are "purely and strictly voluntary in character" (31 December 1935; 10 January 1926). The resolution is two senses of obligation: a conscience-driven spiritual duty of membership, not an institutional levy with fixed amounts. The institution is not collecting what is owed; it is inviting participation in something valuable — while still teaching that the voluntary act of contributing is "both a privilege and a spiritual obligation" (Universal House of Justice, 2 February 1966).

Note that the Fund is distinct from Ḥuqúqu’lláh, which is a separate Tier A law with its own conditions and administration. Conflating the two in educational materials muddies the picture; yet the distinction is complementary, not a licence to treat the Fund as spiritually indifferent.

What the Institution Should Do

Shoghi Effendi's guidance on the Mashriqu'l-Adhkár — generalised across Bahá’í institutions — underscores that spiritual influence depends on breadth and sacrifice, not on a few large donors:

"So vast and significant an enterprise… should be supported, not by the munificence of a few but by the joint contributions of the entire mass of the convinced followers of the Faith… [Its] emanations of spiritual power and inspiration… will to a very large extent depend upon the range and variety of the contributing believers, as well as upon the nature and degree of self-abnegation which their unsolicited offerings will entail."

— Shoghi Effendi, 25 October 1929, to the Bahá’ís of the United States and Canada (Bahá’í Administration, pp. 171–172)

In his handwriting appended to a letter of 8 August 1957, he states the same principle in institutional terms: "All, no matter how modest their resources, must participate." Even the smallest contribution carries spiritual reward and keeps the Fund from depending on a narrow base.

Feasts are the standing venue for this education. The Universal House of Justice pairs Fund teaching with Feast deepening for new believers (2 February 1966), and the administrative portion of the Feast is where Local Funds are reported on and contributions received. A brief, warm report at each Feast — what the Fund supports, what has been accomplished, what the current needs are — normalises giving as part of community life without singling anyone out. LSA letters and national Bahá’í news channels serve the same purpose at scale.

When presenting needs, specificity helps: framing contributions around concrete purposes connects giving to visible outcomes. Appeals should remain "of a general character, carefully-worded and moving and dignified in tone" (Shoghi Effendi, 10 January 1926). Expressing genuine appreciation for contributions already received — without disclosing amounts or naming donors — reinforces the culture the institution is trying to build.

For believers who genuinely cannot contribute financially, Shoghi Effendi counsels that the best support may be "moral help" — devotion, sincerity, and genuine enthusiasm — when material means are lacking (30 December 1933, Temple context). Service in the Cause remains valued; it does not, however, substitute for the believer's own relationship to the Fund institution.

What the Institution Must Not Do

The guidance is unambiguous on several prohibitions:

  • No public lists or pressure to give. A letter written on behalf of Shoghi Effendi permits Assemblies to keep internal records of contributors and sums received, but insists that "no pressure must ever be brought on the Bahá’ís to contribute, it must be voluntary, and should be considered confidential, unless the friends themselves wish to mention it openly" (26 October 1945). Announcing who has and has not contributed, or using contribution as a spiritual litmus test, crosses the line.
  • No direct or indirect pressure. "Any form of compulsion, however slight and indirect, strikes at the very root of the principle underlying the formation of the Fund" (Shoghi Effendi, 10 January 1926). Asking individuals why they have not given, suggesting they give more, or allowing community culture to treat generosity as a mark of spiritual seriousness — and its absence as a deficiency — all contradict the guidance.
  • No disclosure of amounts. Contributions are confidential. The LSA does not reveal who gives what to the community, for any reason.
  • No comparisons between members. Even framing like "some of us are carrying more of the load" implies judgment and should be avoided.

The test is simple: does the communication leave every member feeling invited and respected, regardless of what they give? If a believer who contributes nothing would feel welcomed and un-scrutinised by reading the institution's Fund communications, the tone is right.

Holding the Tension

The institution faces a genuine tension: it cares about the Fund's health and knows participation matters, yet it cannot engineer the outcome it wants. The resolution is to invest fully in education and trust the process. An institution that consistently explains the Fund's purpose, celebrates its work, and invites participation without coercion has done everything within its proper role. The decision belongs to each believer, made in the privacy of their own conscience before God.

Making education equal while accepting unequal contributions is not a compromise — it is precisely the model the guidance describes.
