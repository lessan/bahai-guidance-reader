How do you introduce someone to Anna’s Presentation or to a study circle without it feeling like a sales pitch?

The introduction does not begin with the presentation, and it does not begin with the study-circle invitation — it begins with a relationship in which the friend‘s own questions about meaning have begun to surface, and proceeds at the pace those questions set. Anna’s presentation is a resource the believer offers when the friend‘s inquiry has reached the kind of question Anna’s presentation answers; the study circle is the natural next step when the friend has expressed interest in studying further with others. The discipline is to let the substance of the conversation set the offer, not to let the desire to make the offer set the conversation.

Why the sales-pitch feel arises in the first place

The sales-pitch register is what conversation degrades into when the believer is more invested in the outcome — the declaration, the registration card, the new participant in the cycle‘s count — than in the substance of what is being shared. Ruhi Book 6 names this anti-pattern at the head of Unit 1 and warns the tutor explicitly: “The act of teaching should not be approached as though one were recruiting members for a progressive social cause, much less be conceived in terms of selling a product, no matter how beneficial to the user.” Book 6‘s positive framing of the act — that teaching is “the opening of the city of the human heart to His Revelation with the key of utterance” — names the inversion that the sales register cannot survive. The tutor notes add the operative image: the friends “are interacting with two sacred things — the human heart and His Revelation.” A salesperson treats the listener as a means to a transaction; a teacher in the Bahá’í sense treats the listener’s heart as itself the sacred object of the encounter.

The corrective is not less enthusiasm. The corrective is more substance. The believer who never names Bahá’u’lláh out of fear of being pitchy has not avoided the failure mode — they have produced a different one: the friend who says, as the Dhaka relatives in R.O.G. 19 did, that they only never became Bahá’ís because “no one had ever invited them.”

The Universal House of Justice’s framing: a conversation between two souls

The 28 December 2010 message to the Counsellors‘ Conference gives the corrective its load-bearing formulation. Describing what happens when believers internalise Anna’s presentation rightly — “where the logic underlying that presentation is appreciated, and the urge to convert it into a formula overcome” — the House writes: “it gives rise to a conversation between two souls — a conversation distinguished by the depth of understanding achieved and the nature of the relationship established.” Two notes are decisive. First, the unit of teaching is not the presentation but the conversation — the relationship is the carrier; the presentation is what enters that carrier when the moment is right. Second, the same letter relativises the introductory move‘s apparent stakes: “Whether the first contact with such newly found friends elicits an invitation for them to enrol in the Bahá’í community or to participate in one of its activities is not an overwhelming concern. More important is that every soul feel welcome to join the community in contributing to the betterment of society.” The believer who hears that sentence and absorbs it has the antidote to the pitch instinct already in hand: the immediate enrolment is not the test of the encounter.

The 29 December 2015 message develops the same point through the vocabulary of meaningful conversation. Friends, the House writes, “become more adept at discerning when they have found a hearing ear, at deciding when to be more direct in sharing the message, at removing obstacles to understanding.” The discernment named here is exactly what governs whether and when Anna‘s presentation is offered: it is offered when the friend’s own questions have reached the kind of subject the presentation addresses — God, the Manifestation, the purpose of life, the renewal of religion — and not as a default move whenever a substantive conversation breaks out.

Anna’s presentation as resource, not script

The wiki page on Anna‘s presentation notes that the presentation is “not a script to be memorised mechanically, but a pattern — a way of offering the message directly, reverently, and without beating about the bush — that friends can adapt in their own words across languages and cultures.” The Vancouver believer in R.O.G. 18 captured the operative posture in seven words: “Anna doesn‘t beat around the bush — she just goes for it.” The bluntness is in service of clarity, not pressure. Once the friend has expressed interest in what Bahá’ís believe — not merely in spirituality in general, not merely in the believer as a person — Anna‘s presentation is the way the believer answers the question they have been asked. Indirection at that point becomes its own discourtesy: the friend has signalled receptivity, and a believer who continues to talk around Bahá’u’lláh is failing to give the friend what the friend has come for.

The framing offer can be as ordinary as (Tier F illustrative phrasing): “There‘s a way I’d like to describe what we believe — it takes about half an hour. Could I share it with you?” The presentation is offered as a gift the friend is free to receive or to decline. It is not slipped in unannounced over coffee, nor is it presented as a course the friend has unwittingly enrolled in.

The study circle as the natural next step

The study circle is offered when the friend has expressed interest in studying further, ideally with others. Ruhi Book 2 trains the believer in the conversational register from which such interest naturally surfaces — the practice of “meaningful and uplifting conversations on spiritual themes with friends and neighbours,” anchored in ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s own utterances. Reflections on the Life of the Spirit (Book 1) is the standard entry point for the study circle itself: it is offered as “would you like to study this with me and a few friends? We read short passages and talk about how they apply to our lives,” not as “would you take this Bahá’í course?” The naming of what the activity is — small, friendly, weekly, a shared reading — relieves the invitation of the institutional weight that creates the pitch register.

The chain in field practice

The pattern that the R.O.G. record documents is consistent across very different cultures. The relationship comes first; the meaningful conversation produces a question; the believer offers Anna‘s presentation as their best account of the answer; the study-circle invitation follows when the friend wants to go deeper together. In rural Burkina Faso (R.O.G. 18), in San Diego’s City Heights (R.O.G. 19), in Dhaka, in Sungei Petani — the sequence is the same; the medium adapts. The invitation never carries an implicit declaration contract. The 30 December 2021 message reaffirms the point: enrolment is “a singular moment in a person‘s spiritual development, quite distinct from regular involvement in Bahá’í activities or voicing support for Bahá’í principles.” A friend who attends a study circle with no intention of declaring is exactly where they should be; the community welcomes them without quietly counting the cycles until they are expected to convert.

Pastoral caveats

Three counterfeits of the introduction are worth naming.

Do not skip the relationship to deliver the presentation. A presentation given to someone whose interior life the believer has not yet engaged is the pitch in pure form, regardless of how reverent the tone.

Do not skip the presentation when the friend has reached a question it would answer. The opposite anti-pattern is the believer who, in the name of respect, never makes the offer. R.O.G. 18 is direct about this failure mode: friends used home visits and firesides for years without ever sharing the message of Bahá’u’lláh, and missed the receptivity in front of them. Respect that withholds the invitation is not respect; it is omission.

Do not pre-commit the friend to declaration as the implicit contract of attendance. The study-circle invitation must mean what it says — an invitation to study. If the friend‘s continued attendance is conditional in the believer’s mind on eventual enrolment, the friend will sense it, and the study circle will register as the long form of the pitch.
