What guidance applies to running a business as a Bahá’í?

Running a business as a Bahá’í is not a compartment of life governed by softer rules. Commerce, agriculture, and industry are explicitly named as fields in which trustworthiness and piety must appear; engagement in a craft or trade is exalted to the rank of worship when undertaken in the spirit of service. The guidance is not a compliance checklist for industries — it is a standard of character: honesty in every transaction, fair dealing with employees and competitors, refusal to exploit, and conduct that may “lead the people to God’s path” by its example. Private ventures remain private; Spiritual Assemblies intervene only when difficulties among believers, misrepresentation of the Faith, or damage to its good name require it.

Work and commerce as worship — the doctrinal floor

Bahá’u’lláh removes any excuse for treating business as spiritually second-class:

“It is incumbent upon each one of you to engage in some occupation — such as a craft, a trade or the like. We have exalted your engagement in such work to the rank of worship of the one true God.” — The Kitáb-i-Aqdas, ¶33

‘Abdu’l-Bahá extends the point to enterprise itself:

“Commerce, agriculture and industry should not, in truth, be a bar to service of the one true God. Indeed, such occupations are most potent instruments and clear proofs for the manifestation of the evidences of one‘s piety, of one’s trustworthiness and of the virtues of the All-Merciful Lord.” — ‘Abdu’l-Bahá, in Trustworthiness (compilation)

A Bahá’í business owner is therefore not awaiting evenings and weekends to live spiritually; the firm is a site of worship whose validity is measured by integrity, not by whether its product is religious. The spiritual meaning of work is the parent doctrine; this question is its commercial application.

Trustworthiness — the non-negotiable standard

The trustworthiness compilation concentrates the bulk of practical guidance. Foundations first:

“Every business company should be established on divine principles. Its foundations should be trustworthiness, piety and truthfulness in order to protect the rights of the people.” — ‘Abdu’l-Bahá, in Trustworthiness

“Your criterion should be to pursue your commercial activities with such fairness and equity as to be a cause of guidance to others. The friends of God should, through the instrumentality of their business, lead the people to God’s path, and make them so astonished as to exclaim: ‘How great is their truthfulness, how high their trustworthiness, and how true is their good will!’” — ‘Abdu’l-Bahá, in Trustworthiness

Shoghi Effendi raises the standard for the entire community:

“This rectitude of conduct, with its implications of justice, equity, truthfulness, honesty, fair-mindedness, reliability, and trustworthiness… must be constantly reflected in the business dealings of all its members…” — Shoghi Effendi, The Advent of Divine Justice

Concrete implications follow without ambiguity. Billing for work not performed, deceiving customers about quality, concealing defects, bribing officials, evading lawful taxes, predatory pricing aimed at ruin rather than fair competition, wage theft, and exploitation of the desperate are not sharp practice — they are breaches of worship. Debts incurred in business must be settled with diligence when ability exists (‘Abdu’l-Bahá on debt in Trustworthiness). Creditors — including employees owed wages — have rights that ability and honesty must satisfy.

Among Bahá’ís in business with one another, the bar is higher still: dealings with fellow believers must exhibit “the utmost trustworthiness and integrity”; treating friends as if their trust were optional invalidates the very community one’s faith professes to build.

Competition, excellence, and moderation

The Writings do not forbid competition; they forbid the ethics of the pit. Shoghi Effendi, addressing a merchant facing predatory rivals, counsels forbearance without surrender — maintaining “the one true standard of business conduct” while refusing to be driven out by unethical pressure (Trustworthiness compilation, 31 October 1938). Malice cannot succeed in the long run; compromise of integrity can fail immediately.

Excellence in one’s field is a form of service. The Universal House of Justice has commended honest pursuit of a useful trade or profession undertaken in the spirit of service — even under persecution. Mediocrity when better is possible is a failure of orientation, not of talent.

Moderation governs scale and appetite. Bahá’u’lláh’s counsel to “exercise moderation in all things” applies to growth targets, personal draw from the firm, and the luxuries funded by profit. Wealth honestly earned and socially useful is praised (Secret of Divine Civilization); wealth extracted without benefit to anyone is a liability. A Bahá’í owner asks not only “Is this legal?” but “Does this wealth serve anyone beyond me?”

Scale, social action, and the wider horizon

The Prosperity of Humankind statement frames economic activity as capacity-building for a global civilisation — one expression of SocietyBuilding through honest enterprise: work undertaken in a spirit of service is “a form of prayer”; wealth must equip people and institutions, not merely accumulate. A Bahá’í-owned business participates in that horizon when it trains workers, serves genuine needs, and avoids extractive models that widen the gap between affluence and misery.

Private firms founded by believers to apply Bahá’í principles to social and economic issues are welcomed by the House of Justice (2 September 1993) as part of a “growing phenomenon” — distinct from institutional projects, not a replacement for the Funds, and subject to Bahá’í moral and ethical principles in their operations. A consulting practice, a cooperative, a technology firm with equitable ownership, or a social enterprise may all be legitimate expressions of commerce-as-worship when integrity governs them.

Scale itself is not condemned. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá praises the individual whose measures “would universally enrich the masses of the people.” A business that grows while elevating standards of honesty in its sector, paying fair wages, and returning value to customers is closer to that praise than a small firm built on hidden exploitation.

Employees, partners, and the public face of the Faith

Employers carry obligations of fairness, safety, and honest communication. Consultation — listening before deciding, seeking truth rather than victory — is the Bahá’í method of deliberation and applies to workplace governance where owners have discretion. Partisan political activity in the firm’s name falls under the same prohibition that governs individual believers (bahai-and-partisan-politics); the business is not a vehicle for campaigning.

Teaching through business is example-first, not proselytisation. A Bahá’í who uses client meetings to pressure declarations, or advertises primarily as religious outreach, crosses the line the House of Justice draws between teaching and undue pressure (bahai-conduct-in-workplace). Identity may be known; excellence and integrity are the witness.

Partnerships among believers should be entered with written clarity and consulted exit paths. The 2 September 1993 letter acknowledges that private ventures will sometimes generate disputes; prevention through transparency is a spiritual duty.

Institutional boundaries — when the Assembly is involved

Private business ventures by believers are not overseen day-to-day by Spiritual Assemblies. The Universal House of Justice (2 September 1993) states:

“When an initiative is in the form of a private business venture undertaken by an individual or group, the institutions of the Faith have little reason to interfere with their daily affairs. Only if difficulties arise among the friends involved in such an enterprise, if their activities could damage the good name of the Faith, or if they misrepresent their relationship to the Faith, would a Local or National Spiritual Assembly intervene.” — Universal House of Justice, 2 September 1993 — The Prosperity of Humankind

Assemblies may applaud application of the Teachings, offer guidance when requested, and help develop potential for the Cause — but they do not manage payroll, pricing, or product lines. Believers should not present commercial enterprises as Bahá’í institutions when they are not, should not imply institutional endorsement without cause, and should not route business disputes to Assembly tables when civil courts or commercial arbitration are the proper forum — except where consultation among friends may heal the breach without litigation.

Contributions to the Funds from a business owned solely by Bahá’ís are permissible (Ḥuqúqu’lláh compilation). Ḥuqúqu’lláh on business surplus follows the same conscience rules as personal wealth; needful equipment producing income is exempt per the Codification.

A believer running a restaurant need not serve alcohol because customers expect it; the binding prohibition on intoxicating drink is clear. A believer in construction must still decide, by conscience, how to handle contracts that subsidise armaments or environmental harm — inferential territory treated at length in bahai-aligned-investing. The method is the same: name what is binding, name what is inference, and do not present conscience as doctrine.

Holy Days, contracts, and the calendar of closure

Believers who own independent businesses receive practical guidance: those with shops or enterprises should refrain from working on the nine Holy Days (UHJ guidance on Holy Days observance). Closure on these days is a community marker, not a suggestion that commerce is impure on other days. Employers who can grant Bahá’í employees leave on Holy Days without hardship do so as an expression of the same consideration expected within the Bahá’í family.

Contractual deadlines that fall on Holy Days are resolved by foresight — scheduling, consultation with clients, and honest communication — not by the abrogation of either commercial obligation or sacred observance.

Daily decisions — a conscience checklist

When no explicit ruling exists, the owner may ask:

  1. Truth — Have I represented product, price, and terms honestly?
  2. Justice — Would I accept these wages, these prices, these penalties if positions were reversed?
  3. Service — Does this enterprise meet a genuine need, or merely manipulate appetite?
  4. Means — Are debts, including tax and wage obligations, being settled with diligence?
  5. Witness — Would a fair-minded observer see Bahá’í character here, or its counterfeit?
  6. Humility — Am I growing wealth that serves others, or attachment dressed as enterprise?

The checklist is not legislation; it is the operational face of trustworthiness applied to commerce.

Misunderstandings worth correcting

“My business is secular, so Bahá’í ethics apply only at home and at Feast.” ¶33 explicitly exalts trade to worship. There is no secular compartment.

“If I donate to the Fund, sharp practice is offset.” Ḥuqúqu’lláh and Fund contributions do not absolve dishonest dealing. Trustworthiness is prior.

“Bahá’ís must never compete hard.” Competition is not forbidden; unethical competition is. Forbearance is not capitulation.

“The Assembly should bless my business plan.” Institutions encourage principle; they do not vet commercial viability or endorse ventures.

“I should hire only Bahá’ís.” No text requires it. Fairness to all employees, regardless of belief, is required.

“Profit is suspect.” Profit honestly earned and beneficially used is praised. Profit without social fruit or through exploitation is condemned.
