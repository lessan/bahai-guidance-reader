How does Bahá’í social action differ from secular development and from missionary charity?

Bahá’í social action holds that material and spiritual development are inseparable dimensions of a single process, and that the community itself — not an external agency or benevolent donor — is the protagonist of its own transformation. This distinguishes it from mainstream secular development, which often treats human beings primarily as economic agents and measures progress in material outputs alone, and from missionary-style charity, which uses material aid instrumentally while positioning recipients as objects of benevolence rather than as partners.

Versus Secular Development

The mainstream development paradigm, for all its sophistication, rests on an implicit anthropology: the human being is fundamentally an economic agent whose well-being can be adequately described by income, health metrics, and access to services. Success is therefore measurable in those same terms, and improvement is delivered from outside — by experts, institutions, or capital — to a population whose role is to receive.

Bahá’í social action challenges this at the root. The Universal House of Justice’s 26 November 2012 statement on social action articulates the animating principle: “that the world civilization now on humanity’s horizon must achieve a dynamic coherence between the material and spiritual requirements of life.” Material well-being cannot be fully achieved, let alone sustained, unless the spiritual and moral capacities of individuals and communities are also cultivated. The same statement insists that “Social change is not a project that one group of people carries out for the benefit of another” — the community is the protagonist, not the beneficiary. A community that has been given a water pump but has not developed the understanding, the sense of ownership, and the organisational capacity to maintain it has not been developed — it has been made dependent.

FUNDAEC’s work in rural Colombia, conceived by Farzam Arbab, illustrates the alternative at field level (Tier F): when rural communities were equipped with conceptual tools drawn from the Writings and invited to investigate their own conditions, they were able to diagnose problems and generate solutions that reflected their own values and circumstances. The distinction is between the community as beneficiary and the community as protagonist.

This also reframes what “capacity-building” means. In secular development, capacity-building typically means training individuals in technical skills so that an externally designed project can continue. In Bahá’í social action, capacity refers to the community’s growing ability to apply principles, reflect on experience, and take ever more complex action toward its own vision of a just and flourishing life — a capacity that does not depend on any external actor remaining present.

The 2012 statement articulates three levels of engagement: individual and family initiatives carried out as a natural expression of a God-conscious life; small-scale projects that a cluster or neighbourhood undertakes collectively; and complex organisations like FUNDAEC that require their own institutional structures and expertise. Each level differs in scope and organisational requirements, but all share the same animating logic: the community as the unit of transformation, and material and spiritual progress as two dimensions of one reality.

Versus Missionary Charity

The missionary charity model shares with Bahá’í social action a recognition that human beings are more than economic agents — there is a spiritual dimension to life that matters. Where it diverges is in the relationship between material aid and religious purpose. In that model, aid is either a vehicle for conversion or an expression of a duty to relieve suffering that is owed to those outside the faith. Either way, the recipient occupies a subordinate position: they are the object of another’s benevolence or the target of another’s mission.

Bahá’í social action makes a principled separation between service and teaching. The UHJ’s 2012 statement is explicit: “the value of engaging in social action and public discourse is not to be judged by the ability to bring enrolments. Though endeavours in these two areas of activity may well effect an increase in the size of the Bahá’í community, they are not undertaken for this purpose. Sincerity in this respect is an imperative.” People are partners in a shared endeavour, not beneficiaries whose gratitude or spiritual openness is the return on an investment. This is not merely a strategic preference — it follows from the Bahá’í understanding that all human beings are members of one family, and that service to any part of that family is service to the whole. The power asymmetry that mars so much charity work is dissolved not by pretending it does not exist, but by genuinely ceding the locus of initiative to the community itself.
