What does the guidance say about how to receive a new declaration — the day of, the week after, the first month?

Receive a declaration as the beginning of a relationship with community life, not the end of a teaching effort: on the day of, the load-bearing moves are prayer together and a quiet recording of the declaration; in the week after, sustained home visits — “with efficiency and love” — become the deepening instrument; in the first month, the new believer is invited into a study circle (typically Book 1), into a devotional gathering, and into the next Nineteen Day Feast. The Universal House of Justice's 27 December 2005 message is the operative text and frames the whole arc; the early weeks centre on relational consolidation, not bureaucratic overload — though a quiet recording of the declaration for the LSA or cluster agency is an appropriate administrative step, not a teaching trophy.

The day of declaration

The 27 December 2005 message names the simplest version of what should now happen. Describing the consolidation phase that catches what an expansion phase has produced, the House writes: “Those not participating in study circles are nurtured through a series of home visits, and all are invited to devotional meetings, to the celebration of the Nineteen Day Feast and to Holy Day observances and are gradually introduced to the patterns of community life.” The verb gradually is doing real work. The day a soul recognises Bahá’u’lláh is not the day to enrol them in everything at once.

The 2005 message describes the consolidation pattern — not a day-of checklist — but field accounts across the Reflections on Growth corpus converge on what the day of declaration should hold: a few prayers said together; the introduction of the Greatest Name and the obligatory prayer if these have not yet been shared; a quiet, unhurried recording of the declaration so the Local Spiritual Assembly (or, where there is none, the cluster agency) knows the friend is now a member of the community. These moves belong to field practice; they translate the 2005 framework into the relational moment without substituting for it.

The 28 December 2010 message names what receptivity into the community really asks of the believer they are receiving: “every soul feel welcome to join the community in contributing to the betterment of society, commencing a path of service to humanity on which, at the outset or further along, formal enrolment can occur.” If formal enrolment is not the gate, neither is it the finishing line — it is one moment in a larger movement.

The first week

The week after a declaration is when the home visit comes into its own. The early Reflections on Growth binder describes the attitude required of the visitor in a phrase the global community has carried ever since: home visits are to be carried out “with 'efficiency and love'”, in “the spirit of simple and whole-hearted cooperation” the Guardian named for visiting teachers, and with humility and “the joy one feels at having the privilege of sharing God's Message with others.” The visit is not an administrative follow-up; it is the relational unit through which a new believer is woven into the life of the community.

Two patterns from R.O.G. 21 set realistic expectations for the week. First, frequency is not the constraint a busy adult life often assumes it to be. Reporting on the field's experience, the issue notes that “those new to the Faith respond well to systematic consolidation, they appreciate having regular, set times for deepening visits and want them to occur more frequently — even daily. This yearning to learn among the new believers demonstrates that love for the Faith and engagement in the process of the Five Year Plan can transcend the lack of time and other limitations that we sometimes assume exist.” The mistake, in other words, is usually under-visiting, not over-programming. Second, the conversational substance of the visits is to be the Word — Book 2's six themes are widely used; in some clusters illustrated pamphlets accompany them. R.O.G. 18 had named the failure mode the field was trying to leave behind: “the friends have not utilized these opportunities for directly sharing the message of Bahá’u’lláh but have been indirect in approach, never openly teaching seekers about the station and mission of Bahá’u’lláh.” A home visit that becomes mere sociability has not yet become a deepening visit.

In a cluster operating a cycle of activity, the first week of a new believer's life often coincides with the early consolidation phase that follows an expansion campaign. R.O.G. 19's Nyaruyoba account in rural Tanzania shows the discipline: when 57 declarations were secured in four days against a planned consolidation capacity of 65, “having set that ceiling, the cluster agencies stopped expansion on day six and immediately began consolidation” — pivoted, that is, into precisely the home-visit and study-circle work the new believers now needed. The cluster had decided in advance how many declarations its consolidation capacity could carry; the first week of each new believer's life is what that decision was protecting.

The first month

By the end of the first month, three movements should have taken shape. First, the new believer should have been invited — not pressured — into a Book 1 study circle. R.O.G. 21's central operational finding is that “those who entered Book 1 remained involved in the Faith… Those who did not enter Book 1, who often only received an introductory presentation, generally drifted away.” The same issue records the field's experience that beginning Book 1 during an expansion phase, not after it, produced new believers who themselves led teachers to other souls within the same cycle. In France a referent system — “individual Bahá’ís are assigned to accompany each new believer, as well as those seekers who are close to the Faith” — drove 73 percent of declarants into institute courses. The first month is when that accompaniment becomes structural, not improvised.

Second, the new believer should have attended at least one devotional meeting and been welcomed at the next Nineteen Day Feast. The Feast deserves a brief framing word in advance, since its three parts (devotional, administrative, social) are unfamiliar and the consultative middle portion can feel bewildering without it. The 27 December 2005 message captures what is actually being received in mature clusters: as consolidation deepens, “a sense of common purpose inspires their interactions”, the “quality of the Nineteen Day Feast has improved”, and the friends are “delving into the Writings and exploring their implications for their lives.” That is the texture the new believer is being invited into.

Third — and this is where a community most commonly errs — the relationship that already led the new believer to the Faith should deepen in its original setting. If the new believer came in through a children's class their family was attending, through a junior youth group, or through a neighbourhood activity already meeting nearby, the first move is not to relocate them into a new Bahá’í social geography but to deepen them where they already are. R.O.G. 19 records that one-third of Phoenix declarants were parents of children in classes; the activity that brought them in is also the activity through which they are received.

The cluster's reflection meeting is the right place for the declaration to be shared with the wider body of the friends and for the cluster agencies to consult on accompaniment — particularly if the new believer themselves shows the early movement R.O.G. 33 records in its Gonamutawa account: “Combining study with service—an essential feature of the institute process” from the earliest stage, with local friends “being empowered to arise” to serve. New believers are not always served best by a long apprenticeship; some of them are protagonists from the first month.

Pastoral caveats

Five things not to do.

Do not turn the new believer into a teaching trophy. Paraded at the next Feast, introduced as a victory, asked to share their story before a relationship is in place — these are gestures the community sometimes makes for itself, not for the new believer. The 27 December 2005 message's framing is the corrective: a “nurturing environment is being cultivated in which each individual is encouraged to progress at his or her own pace without the pressure of unreasonable expectations.”

Do not gate the deeper community-life invitation on the new believer asking. The 27 December 2005 message specifies the active verb: those not in study circles are nurtured; all are invited. The community moves toward the new believer; it does not wait for the new believer to learn the request form.

Do not delay introducing the Holy Texts and the obligatory prayer. The Word is the substance of the relationship; nothing in the consolidation pattern asks the believer to wait for it.

Do not assume the family will follow. In some cases they will — R.O.G. 18 records a Sungei Petani parent whose child had brought the prayers home: “Why have you not shared these wonderful teachings with me earlier?” In many cases they will not, and the new believer's path is their own. The community accompanies the believer the family did not produce as readily as it accompanies one whose family declared together.

Do not forget the LSA. In the Administrative Order Shoghi Effendi named Local Spiritual Assemblies the “bedrock upon which the strength of the Universal House is… established” (February 1924); in consolidation, the Local Spiritual Assembly remains the believer's institutional home — the cluster agencies did not displace it. A declaration that the LSA never quite hears about is one a community has half-received.
