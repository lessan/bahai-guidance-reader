What is the recommended approach to fundraising for the Bahá’í Fund at the cluster level — what works, what should be avoided?

There is, strictly, no such thing as “fundraising at the cluster level.” The Universal House of Justice has ruled explicitly that “A ‘Cluster Fund‘ cannot be established” and that cluster agencies such as an Area Teaching Committee “would not engage in fund-raising or solicit contributions from the believers” (18 November 2015). The Bahá’í Fund is a spiritual institution of the Administrative Order, not a charitable appeal: contribution is a privilege of the declared believer, discretionary in amount and “purely and strictly voluntary” in character (Shoghi Effendi, 1926), and never to be extracted by pressure however slight. The Cluster’s role with regard to the Fund is therefore education and consultation — keeping the Fund visible in community life, helping the friends understand it as an act of devotion, and making the practical machinery of regular giving easy — not the setting of targets or the running of campaigns. Where cluster activities require resources, the same letter points to legitimate channels: Local Assemblies hosting events, direct gifts toward specific projects, contributions earmarked for cluster support to the National or Local Fund, and regional treasurers appointing assistants in advanced clusters to collect and disburse earmarked funds on their behalf.

The character of the Fund

Shoghi Effendi establishes the doctrinal frame. In the very letter (12 March 1923) that calls the Bahá’í Fund into existence, he attaches to it an unconditional clause — “to be placed under the exclusive control of the Spiritual Assembly” — and within three years had to write a corrective letter against the first attempts to enthuse contribution by exhortation:

“I feel urged to remind you of the necessity of ever bearing in mind the cardinal principle that all contributions to the Fund are to be purely and strictly voluntary in character. It should be made clear and evident to every one that any form of compulsion, however slight and indirect, strikes at the very root of the principle underlying the formation of the Fund ever since its inception. While appeals of a general character, carefully-worded and moving and dignified in tone are welcome under all circumstances, it should be left entirely to the discretion of every conscientious believer to decide upon the nature, the amount, and purpose of his or her contribution for the propagation of the Cause.” — Shoghi Effendi, letter of 10 January 1926 to the National Spiritual Assembly of the United States and Canada (Bahá’í Administration, p. 101)

This is the operative passage. Two clauses must hold simultaneously: general appeals are permitted; compulsion and indirect pressure, in any form however slight, are forbidden. The line is delicate, but it is the line that defines whether the Fund is functioning as a Bahá’í institution at all. Six months later, in the Guardian’s handwriting appended to a letter written on his behalf, the same principle is stated even more flatly: “In matters of contribution we should not use any compulsion whatsoever and ascertain clearly the desire of the donor. We should appeal to but not coerce the friends” (9 July 1926). And in the most striking field instance — gatherings during a Convention at which donations were collected — a letter written on behalf of the Guardian distinguished a “true quiet, prayerful atmosphere when a person is of his own accord aroused to make some sacrifice” from gatherings “often used by religious bodies” in which “the audience is especially aroused to a frenzy and mob psychology is used to induce them to pay.” He notes that “the distinction is very delicate, but it is for the chairman to use his power to see that one desirable form is not corrupted into the other” (28 May 1932). A cluster reflection meeting that ran a fundraising appeal in the second style would have crossed the line. A cluster consultation that named the Fund’s needs and let the friends respond at their own pace would not.

The privilege restriction — Bahá’ís only

The most load-bearing modern statement is the Universal House of Justice’s letter of 7 August 1985 to all National Spiritual Assemblies, which restates the spiritual character of the relationship between believer and Fund:

“There is a profound aspect to the relationship between a believer and the Fund, which holds true irrespective of his or her economic condition… Giving to the Fund, therefore, is a spiritual privilege not open to those who have not accepted Bahá’u’lláh, of which no believer should deny himself. It is both a responsibility and a source of bounty… The importance of contributing resides in the degree of sacrifice of the giver, the spirit of devotion with which the contribution is made and the unity of the friends in this service.” — Universal House of Justice, 7 August 1985, to National Spiritual Assemblies

Three implications follow directly. First, the Bahá’í Fund — Local, National, Continental, International, and the Arc and World Centre Funds — is closed to non-Bahá’í contributions. Letters written on behalf of Shoghi Effendi worked this out exhaustively as the U.S. Mashriqu’l-Adhkár was being financed: “under no circumstances should the believers accept any financial help from non-Bahá’ís for use in connection with specific administrative activities of the Faith” (14 April 1934), with the proviso that gifts from sympathisers may be accepted only into a separately maintained section of the treasury devoted to “philanthropic or charitable purposes” (25 October 1929). A cluster fundraising scheme that opened the Fund to outside contributions, however well-intentioned, would have misread the institution. Second, the privilege is not means-tested — the Fund‘s spiritual function is the same for the materially poor and the materially comfortable. Third, contribution is of the believer, made to the Fund — never of the cluster as cluster; one does not contribute on someone else‘s behalf, and one does not chase someone else’s contribution.

Universal participation, not heavy donors

The institutional aim is breadth, not magnitude. In the Guardian‘s handwriting appended to a letter written on his behalf: “All, no matter how modest their resources, must participate. Upon the degree of self-sacrifice involved in these individual contributions will directly depend the efficacy and the spiritual influence which these nascent administrative institutions, called into being through the power of Bahá’u’lláh, and by virtue of the Design conceived by the Center of His Covenant, will exert” (8 August 1957, to the NSA of Central and East Africa). The Universal House of Justice extends the same principle in its message of November 1976 to the International Teaching Conference in Hong Kong: “Participation of all believers in supporting the Bahá’í Funds, the lifeblood of the Cause, must be given adequate attention, and the blessings which reward the act of voluntary giving for the promotion of the Faith, no matter how small the amount may be, must be lovingly and wisely explained.” The phrase “no matter how small the amount” is doctrinal, not pastoral softening: the substance of contribution lies in “the degree of sacrifice of the giver,” not in the sum.

The implication for cluster practice is sharp. A cluster that meets its budget through three large donors has not understood the Fund. A cluster in which every declared believer — including children, who “have always given to the Cause, everywhere” (letter written on behalf of Shoghi Effendi, 18 August 1949) — gives whatever they can give, regularly and freely, is a cluster whose contribution pattern matches what the institution is for.

What the cluster is actually for, with respect to the Fund

The cluster is not a treasurer; the Local Spiritual Assembly is. The cluster’s contribution to the life of the Fund is consultative and educational, and it lives in two visible places.

The Nineteen Day Feast. The administrative portion of the Feast is the standing place where Local Funds are reported on, where appeals of a general character are made, where contributions are received, and where the friends learn what their Local Assembly’s resources are doing in the locality. The second part of the Feast — not the cluster reflection meeting — is where this work belongs by long-standing institutional design.

The cluster reflection meeting and consultations. The reflection meeting may consult on whether cluster plans require resources, where children‘s-class materials or pioneer support are bottlenecking, and how to keep awareness of the Fund alive in the friends‘ devotional life. Field narratives such as R.O.G. 35 record rising Local Fund contributions as one indicator of consolidation returning — “an additional indicator of the return of the community’s vibrancy has been an increase in contributions to the Fund” — but this is a signal of Local Assembly health, not evidence of a cluster treasury. What the reflection meeting must not do: establish or solicit through a Cluster Fund; present per-believer targets; announce who has and has not contributed; or treat the Fund as a barometer of spiritual sincerity. A letter written on behalf of Shoghi Effendi is explicit that contributions “should be considered confidential, unless the friends themselves wish to mention it openly” (26 October 1945); a cluster apparatus that publishes contributions has converted a private spiritual act into a public marker of belief, and crossed exactly the line the Guardian warned against in 1926.

The complementarity with Huqúqu’lláh

The Right of God is a different institution. Huqúqu’lláh is computed by the believer’s own conscience as 19% of accumulated surplus wealth and rendered to the Centre of the Cause; it is administered by the Board of Trustees, separately from the Bahá’í Funds, with its own network of Representatives. The cluster has no role in Huqúqu’lláh — it is an offering between the believer and God, and the question of its spiritual significance treats it in detail. The two institutions are complementary: contribution to the Fund is the believer‘s collaborative gift to the work of the institutions; Huqúqu’lláh is the believer‘s purification of their own wealth. They do not substitute for each other, and a cluster’s awareness of one is not a discharge of the other.

What to avoid — the failure-mode list

Compiled directly from Shoghi Effendi’s corrective letters, the 2015 ruling on cluster funds, and the doctrinal architecture set by the 1985 letter:

  • Establishing or soliciting through a Cluster Fund. “A ‘Cluster Fund’ cannot be established”; cluster agencies “would not engage in fund-raising or solicit contributions from the believers” (Universal House of Justice, 18 November 2015).
  • Per-believer targets. Tying a believer’s expected contribution to a calculated share — by household, by income band, by core-activity participation — converts an offering into a levy. “It should be left entirely to the discretion of every conscientious believer to decide upon the nature, the amount, and purpose of his or her contribution” (Shoghi Effendi, 1926).
  • Public lists of contributors. The 1945 guidance is precise: records may be kept, “but no pressure must ever be brought on the Bahá’ís to contribute, it must be voluntary, and should be considered confidential, unless the friends themselves wish to mention it openly” (letter written on behalf of Shoghi Effendi, 26 October 1945).
  • Pressure on new believers. Spiritual sincerity must never become legible through financial contribution. The 1985 letter restates that the act of giving is essential to the basic teaching and deepening of new believers — but as “a spiritual privilege” of which they should not deprive themselves, not as a measure they must meet.
  • Accepting contributions from non-Bahá’ís into the Fund. Authoritative throughout the corpus; sympathiser gifts may be received only into a separately tracked charitable account, not into the Bahá’í Fund proper.
  • Mob-psychology gatherings. The 1932 distinction between a “prayerful” gathering and one in which “mob psychology is used to induce them to pay” governs every cluster Fund consultation.
  • Treating contribution as a barometer of cluster health. Rising Local Fund contributions are a downstream signal of vibrancy (the R.O.G. 35 indicator), not its cause. A cluster that pursues the signal directly distorts the institution.
  • Substituting for the LSA. The Local Fund is the Local Spiritual Assembly‘s responsibility. Cluster agencies do not replace the LSA‘s treasurer in the believers’ relationship to the Fund.

What works

The pattern that holds across a century of guidance is humble, regular, educative, and pastoral. Make the Fund visible in consultation without making it the centre. Make giving easy and habitual — small, regular, freely chosen. Educate new believers about the Fund as part of basic deepening, with the 1985 letter‘s framing of contribution as “a spiritual privilege.” Trust the friends to respond. Let the LSA hold the resources, report on them at Feast, and use them at its own discretion. Where cluster activities need support, use the channels the 2015 letter names — hosting, project-specific gifts, earmarked National or Local Fund contributions — without creating a cluster treasury or soliciting the friends. Watch the Fund’s health as a symptom of community vitality, not as a goal to be hit.
