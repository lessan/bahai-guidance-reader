How many UHJ messages have been issued specifically to the Bahá’ís of Iran, and what are the major themes?

The Universal House of Justice has issued approximately 237 messages directly to the Bahá’ís of Iran in the payamha-iran archive alone — likely the largest single-community correspondence in the UHJ's history — spanning from at least the 1979 Revolution to the present. The corpus is defined by a pastoral urgency unlike the annual global Riḍván and Naw-Rúz messages: it is addressed to a community living under sustained, systematic persecution and asks them to be not merely survivors but exemplars.

The corpus — how many and when

The primary aggregator of this correspondence is payamha-iran.org, which serves authorised English translations and Persian originals of UHJ messages to Iran. The knowledge base holds 237 Persian originals and 59 authorised English translations — downloaded from the site's ID space of 1–858 (many IDs are absent or reserved; the site does not number continuously). Additional Iran-specific guidance appears in global Naw-Rúz letters and in pre-2011 compilations such as muhj-1963-1986.

The collection's English-translation coverage begins clearly with a 17 June 2011 message on the arrests of Bahá’í Institute for Higher Education (BIHE) co-workers, but the Persian originals push coverage further back. Letters to Iranian Bahá’ís also appear in the Messages from the Universal House of Justice, 1963–1986 compilation, which documents "sustained guidance on the Iranian persecutions" during the Seven Year Plan (1979–1986). The correspondence thus spans roughly five decades, with the period from 1979 onward representing by far the most intensive exchange — the years when two successive National Spiritual Assemblies of Iran were martyred in their entirety and over one hundred Bahá’ís were killed within four years.

Major theme 1 — Consolation and spiritual empowerment

The most persistent note in the corpus is pastoral: the House of Justice writes to a community whose institutions have been dissolved, whose holy places have been demolished, and whose members face imprisonment for practicing their Faith. The tone is neither managerial nor merely sympathetic — it names the community's suffering and simultaneously reframes it within the Bahá’í understanding of the relationship between sacrifice and spiritual advance.

The 23 August 2022 letter to the Bahá’ís of Iran, responding to the destruction of properties in Mázindarán province, captures this voice precisely:

"The Bahá’ís seek justice and long for fairness and equity, but never pursue retaliation and revenge. Their hearts are filled with pain, but wholly free of hatred and rancour."

— Universal House of Justice, 23 August 2022

The same letter identifies the conduct of the community — not its statements, but its observable life — as the strongest evidence for the power of the Cause:

"…were a person to observe with a pure eye unclouded by prejudice, the conduct and example of the Bahá’í community in Iran would be sufficient evidence and abiding proof of Bahá’u’lláh’s miracle and of the immense power of His sacred Cause to unify and promote peace."

— Universal House of Justice, 23 August 2022

This is a recurring structural move: consolation is never offered as an end in itself but always turned outward — the community's endurance is simultaneously witness to the Faith.

Major theme 2 — Maintaining Bahá’í principles under oppression

The House of Justice has been direct about how Bahá’ís in Iran are to respond to persecution. Non-retaliation, recourse to lawful means, and forbearance toward oppressors are not presented as strategic choices but as expressions of the Faith's own character.

The Naw-Rúz 180 letter (21 March 2023) characterises the community's response to persecution in a compressed image that also implies an instruction: "To poison, it responded with sweetest honey." The Mázindarán letter is more explicit: "The Bahá’ís seek justice through recourse to the law and appeals to authority, never through retaliation and revenge." This principle is grounded in Bahá’u’lláh’s own formulation, quoted in that letter:

"The fundamental purpose animating the Faith of God and His Religion is to safeguard the interests and promote the unity of the human race, and to foster the spirit of love and fellowship amongst men. Suffer it not to become a source of dissension and discord, of hate and enmity."

— Bahá’u’lláh, Gleanings from the Writings of Bahá’u’lláh

The letters also attend to the mechanics of Bahá’í life under restriction — observing the Fast, the Nineteen-Day Feast, holy days, and the right to higher education through BIHE. A 11 May 2012 message (en_0015) marks the anniversary of the Yárán arrests while documenting the ongoing persecution of BIHE staff and students. Maintaining these observances under oppression is not treated as passive endurance but as active affirmation of the community's identity.

Major theme 3 — The community's unique heritage

A third strand, woven through decades of letters, identifies the Iranian Bahá’í community as uniquely positioned in the Bahá’í narrative — not merely because of its suffering, but because of its origin. Iran is the Cradle of the Faith, the country where Bahá’u’lláh was born and where the Báb declared His mission. The community holds a spiritual heritage that the House of Justice returns to repeatedly as both source of identity and ground of responsibility.

The Bahá’í World, Volume 18, records Geoffrey Nash's assessment that the Iranian community's conduct under the 1979–1983 persecution had "raised them, in the eyes of the Bahá’í community outside the Cradle of the Faith, to rank alongside the Dawn-Breakers of the Heroic Age." The Naw-Rúz 180 letter (2023) names the community's 180-year record of service — schools, hospitals, literacy work, equality — and the Naw-Rúz 182 letter (2025) extends the praise to family life and consultation within marriage. The House of Justice frames this heritage not as grounds for pride but as grounds for service: what the Iranian community has learned through suffering it is called to share.

The recent Naw-Rúz letters

The Naw-Rúz letters of 180, 181, and 182 B.E. (2023–2025) reveal how the tone has evolved. Where earlier letters responded directly to specific persecution events, these situate the Iranian community within the global arc of the Nine Year Plan. Naw-Rúz 180 names society-building power as the era's central aim and presents the training institute as an instrument for "the upliftment of the neighbourhood." Naw-Rúz 181 commemorates the fortieth anniversary of the martyrdom of ten Bahá’í women in Shíráz; Naw-Rúz 182 pivots to family life and engagement in social discourse. Persecution is acknowledged — the 180 letter notes "renewed attempts to weaken community dynamism and target the youth" — but the frame is now forward-facing: what does the Iranian community's hard-won experience offer to the world?
