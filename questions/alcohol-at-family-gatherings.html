How should Bahá’ís navigate alcohol at family gatherings and social events?

Bahá’u’lláh forbids the believer to drink what deprives him of intelligence. The law is absolute for Bahá’ís; the social field around it is not. Family gatherings — weddings of non-Bahá’í relatives, holiday meals, receptions where others toast with wine — place the believer at the intersection of binding personal law and the relationships the Writings exalt. The guidance distinguishes what a Bahá’í may never do, what a Bahá’í should not do as host or institutional representative, and what conscience governs when others drink in the believer's presence or at the believer's table. Clarity on that distinction prevents both rigid isolation and silent compromise.

The binding law — no intoxication for the believer

The Kitáb-i-Aqdas states that it is forbidden "for an intelligent person to drink that which depriveth him of his intelligence." ’Abdu’l-Bahá explains that both light and strong drinks are prohibited because alcohol "leadeth the mind astray and causeth the weakening of the body" — and that whatever is truly beneficial exists in creation; alcohol's harm is medically established.

Shoghi Effendi confirmed that the law is binding on all Bahá’ís, not a personal preference. "Under no circumstances should Bahá’ís drink. It is so unambiguously forbidden in the Tablets of Bahá’u’lláh that there is no excuse for them even touching it in the form of a toast, or in a burning plum pudding." A believer who persists in drinking, after loving exhortation and warning, may face loss of administrative rights. The law is not suspended at family gatherings, national holidays, or interfaith ceremonies.

The Writings also forbid foods flavoured with alcoholic liquors for believers. Extracts in cooking are not a loophole the Guardian recognised. Medical exception exists where a competent physician prescribes alcohol as treatment; social pressure is not medicine.

Why the law exists — more than arbitrary taboo

Bahá’u’lláh distinguishes the "Mystic Wine" of divine love from the wine that stupefies. Intoxication that diminishes intelligence is the opposite of the enlightenment the Faith seeks. ’Abdu’l-Bahá writes that intellect and comprehension are God's gifts whereby man is distinguished from animals; the wise man does not "lose this Light in the darkness of intoxication."

Shoghi Effendi surveyed civilisational decline and listed drunkenness among the signs of moral downfall alongside divorce, lawlessness, and the weakening of family solidarity. Abstinence is therefore not sectarian oddity but participation in the moral reconstruction the Cause advances. A believer who drinks at a relative's wedding has not merely broken a rule; he has surrendered the clarity the law protects — often in the very settings where family bonds are celebrated.

The chaste and holy life the Guardian describes also requires "total abstinence from all alcoholic drinks" — one element in a pattern of modesty, purity, and self-mastery that includes dress, language, and amusement. Alcohol at family events is not isolated from the household's spiritual climate.

As host — what the believer serves and what the believer names

Guidance diverges by role.

Bahá’í institutions must never serve alcohol. The Universal House of Justice has stated that "on all occasions officially sponsored by Bahá’í Institutions or where the host is acting as a representative of the Cause alcohol should not be served." Receptions should not be called "cocktail parties"; "tea" or "reception" is preferable.

Bahá’í hosts in private homes face a gradation. Shoghi Effendi wrote in 1957 that "there is no reason why Bahá’ís may not serve some alcoholic refreshment to their guests, if they feel sincerely that this will further their teaching work. If they can obtain their objectives without doing so, it would be better." The same letter forbids Bahá’ís to drink even in toast form. Later, the House of Justice stated that "alcohol should not be served at any reception, either at home or in a public place, at which you are host" and that "it would be inappropriate for a Bahá’í to serve such beverages at a function given by him." The trend of guidance moves toward abstinence as host; the believer planning a family gathering should weigh the later letters heavily and prefer hospitality that does not centre alcohol.

Practical hosting. A Bahá’í hosting a mixed family meal may offer non-alcoholic drinks abundantly, explain dietary choices without lecturing, and arrange seating and timing so that abstinence does not read as disapproval of guests. The Writings prize courtesy; a host who humiliates a wine-drinking uncle has not advanced the Faith — but a host who opens every gathering with champagne has made alcohol the symbol of welcome the law rejects.

As guest — presence without participation

When the believer attends gatherings hosted by others — non-Bahá’í parents, siblings, in-laws, employers — the law on personal drinking remains absolute. The believer does not toast with wine, does not sip beer for politeness, and does not accept trifle laced with spirits without inquiry.

Presence where others drink is not forbidden. The Writings do not require the believer to leave every table where a glass of wine appears. They require that his own conduct be unmistakable. A quiet refusal — "I don't drink" — suffices in most families without extended theology. Where relatives press, the believer may explain briefly that his faith forbids alcohol; where pressure continues, firmness without contempt is the model.

A baptism or wedding in another tradition may include champagne; the Bahá’í parent or relative attends, honours the occasion, and does not drink. The House of Justice has confirmed that a Bahá’í parent need not object to a Catholic mother's wish to baptise a child or use champagne on that occasion — while Bahá’ís themselves would not partake.

Children in mixed households require clarity. A Bahá’í parent teaches abstinence by example; denigrating non-Bahá’í relatives who drink teaches resentment rather than principle. childrens-spiritual-education-mixed-faith-home applies: foster family unity while holding the line on law.

Extended family, culture, and conscience

Alcohol's role in family life varies by culture. In some traditions, refusal is insult; in others, drinking is casual. The Writings do not prescribe one script for every dinner. They prescribe:

  • Absolute personal abstinence for believers.
  • Strong obligation not to serve as host or institutional representative.
  • Conscience on marginal cases — whether to attend an event whose sole purpose is drinking, whether to remain when intoxication becomes dangerous, whether to transport alcohol as part of employment.

On employment involving sale or service of alcohol, the House of Justice has left discretion to conscience where involvement varies in degree, while stating that believers should not enter partnerships whose business is alcohol and should work to dissociate from such activity. A waiter serving wine is a different case from an owner profiting from a bar; Assemblies may be consulted in doubt.

On advertising, illustrating, or promoting alcohol, the House of Justice urges wisdom and may restrict conduct that injures the Faith's good name. A believer asked to design a wine company's manual decides in conscience; where doubt exists, the Assembly may decide.

Teaching the Faith without leading with abstinence

Shoghi Effendi advised that in teaching, abstinence from wine should not be "the first" difficult point presented; yet when a person wishes to join the Faith, he must be told the law. Family relationships follow a similar arc. Relatives who know the believer's character may ask about alcohol before they ask about theology. A brief, calm explanation — "My religion forbids it; I am happy to celebrate with you in other ways" — often suffices. Extended sermons at the table convert no one and sour the hospitality the Writings commend.

Youth at family parties require modelling. A teenager who sees parents negotiate abstinence with dignity learns law as strength; one who sees parents drink "when it matters" learns law as negotiable.

Institutional and public settings contrasted with kinship

The clarity increases as the believer's representative role increases. A Feast host, Assembly officer, or teacher at a Bahá’í event must not serve alcohol — full stop. A believer attending a conference dinner where wine is poured for others but not pressed on her exercises personal law without insisting the venue change. A believer hosting a non-Bahá’í sibling's engagement party in her own home faces the conscience questions the House of Justice left open in the 1970s but increasingly discouraged: prefer tea, fruit drinks, and festive food; if relatives bring wine, the believer need not drink; serving it remains the harder question on which later guidance weighs against.

Alcoholism, recovery, and community response

The law forbids drinking; it does not forbid the alcoholic. Shoghi Effendi praised work helping people overcome drinking — including programmes such as Alcoholics Anonymous — as "excellent" and to be viewed "with sympathy and approval." The Universal House of Justice has counselled Assemblies to show "especial patience" toward believers battling addiction, to suggest professional counselling, and to distinguish flagrant disregard of law from the struggle of recovery.

A family gathering that includes a recovering relative may require additional sensitivity — avoiding centre-stage drinking, offering alternatives without drawing attention. Kindness and discretion serve the same standard abstinence serves: protection of human dignity.

Pastoral caveats

Do not drink "just this once" for family peace. The law does not admit seasonal exception. One toast teaches children that Covenant yields to pressure.

Do not preach at the table. Brief explanation suffices; sustained condemnation of relatives alienates without converting.

Do not assume abstinence requires absence. The believer who skips every family wedding to avoid wine may damage the very relationships the Faith calls him to honour. Attend; do not drink.

Do not serve as host what you forbid yourself to consume without grave reason. The later UHJ guidance on hosting weighs against normalising alcohol in Bahá’í-led gatherings.

Do not confuse cultural sophistication with spiritual maturity. Wine culture is not superior culture; it is simply the culture the law asks Bahá’ís to transcend in their own conduct.

Huqúqu’lláh, Fund, and household economy

Money spent on alcohol for guests is money not spent on the Fund, on debts honourably owed, on children's needs, or on Ḥuqúqu’lláh. The Writings do not itemise hospitality budgets, but moderation in expenditure applies. A believer who feels obliged to serve expensive wine to impress relatives might consult whether that impresses at the cost of sacred obligation — and whether grape juice and dignified welcome would serve the relationship better.

Believers in recovery may choose to attend family events late, after toasts, or with a supportive spouse who shares the exit signal — arrangements made in consultation, not isolation. The family that protects a recovering member's dignity practises the hospitality the Writings commend in a different key.

Seasonal peaks — year-end parties, weddings, reunions — concentrate pressure. Planning responses in advance ("We will bring sparkling juice; we do not drink, but we are glad to celebrate with you") reduces ambush at the door. Spouses should agree in private consultation before arriving so one partner is not surprised by the other's refusal or compliance.
