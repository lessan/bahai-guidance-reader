What does the guidance say about Bahá’ís in the military, police, or armed service?

Bahá’í teaching on military and police service rests on two obligations that must be held together: obedience to government — a binding provision of the Kitáb-i-Aqdas — and refusal to take human life where the law permits an alternative. The community is not pacifist in the sense of evading civic duty; believers accept the dangers and hardships that citizenship may require. They do, however, apply for non-combatant status whenever their government provides it on grounds of religious belief, and they do not voluntarily enlist in branches of the armed forces whose defining function is combat. Police service is generally permitted, but forces vary so widely by country that no universal rule can replace local judgment and, where needed, consultation with the National Spiritual Assembly or the Universal House of Justice.

Obedience to government and the duty of service

The Synopsis of the Kitáb-i-Aqdas lists “strict obedience to one‘s government” among Bahá’u’lláh’s ordinances. That obedience extends to lawful civic obligations, including military service where the state requires it. bahai-taxes-and-civic-obligations develops the distinction between administrative compliance and endorsement of policy: paying tax, registering for selective service, and fulfilling conscription are acts of lawful obedience, not votes of confidence in every use the state makes of revenue or force.

Shoghi Effendi, addressing believers under restrictive governments, draws a line of fundamental importance:

“Whereas the friends should obey the government under which they live, even at the risk of sacrificing all their administrative affairs and interests, they should under no circumstances suffer their inner religious beliefs and convictions to be violated and transgressed by any authority whatever. A distinction of a fundamental importance must, therefore, be made between spiritual and administrative matters.”

— Shoghi Effendi, The World Order of Bahá’u’lláh

Military registration and service fall on the administrative side when the state lawfully requires them. Spiritual matters — recanting faith, violating Bahá’í marriage law, participating in partisan political activity as a Bahá’í — remain inviolable. Where the two collide, the believer preserves conscience and accepts the civil consequences; national communities consult the Universal House of Justice on circumstances that are acute or unprecedented.

The Bahá’í position on military service

The National Spiritual Assembly of the United States, summarising decades of Guardian guidance for publication in The Bahá’í World, states the operative principles with clarity:

“The Bahá’í teachings require that followers of the Faith obey the laws of the government under which they live, and this requirement includes the obligation for military service which rests upon all citizens. However, Bahá’ís are also required to apply for noncombatant service whenever the opportunity to do so is legally provided by their government on the basis of religious training and belief.”

— National Spiritual Assembly of the United States, The Bahá’í World

The same statement insists that Bahá’ís are “not pacifists in the sense of refusal to cooperate with and obey the laws of an established government.” They do not, on grounds of religious conviction, seek to abandon their obligation as citizens in time of war or national emergency, nor do they attempt to avoid the dangers to which citizens of military age are liable. What they do seek — and what the Guardian made obligatory where the law allows — is exemption from duties that necessitate the taking of life.

In 1946, replying to a question whether the United Nations altered this stance, Shoghi Effendi answered:

“As there is neither an International Police Force nor any immediate prospect of one coming into being, the Bahá’ís should continue to apply, under all circumstances, for exemption from any military duties that necessitate the taking of life. There is no justification for any change of attitude on our part at the present time.”

— Shoghi Effendi, Bahá’í Administration

That instruction was confirmed again in 1951. The Guardian’s consistent position: applying for and maintaining non-combatant status is obligatory, not optional, where the law provides for it; voluntary enlistment in a branch of the armed forces where one would be subject to orders to take life is not permitted under that guidance.

Conscription, voluntary enlistment, and service already begun

Where selective-service machinery exists, non-combatant classification is ordinarily secured through the legal process of registration and classification — not by avoiding registration altogether. A 1965 National Spiritual Assembly statement for the United States notes that volunteering for service ordinarily forfeits the right to non-combatant status secured through that machinery; believers should not voluntarily enlist in any branch that is, by definition, entirely a combatant service.

A person who becomes a Bahá’í while already in military service and who does not already hold non-combatant status has an obligation to finish the term honourably undertaken. It may be possible to obtain de facto non-combatant status by transferring to duties that do not require physical fighting; such transfer should be effected without impairing relationships with responsible superiors and without any appearance of unwillingness to serve the country in assignments compatible with conscience. Identification as a Bahá’í — not as a generic Protestant, Catholic, or Jew — is appropriate where military authorities recognise the Faith as a separate religion.

When exempted from combatant service, the believer serves in other capacities the conscription law provides — medical, administrative, engineering, logistics, or civil-defence work. A 1940 National Spiritual Assembly instruction summarises the logic: the Bahá’í obeys the government; the religion obliges the believer to perform combatant duty only if the government will not exempt; when exemption is granted, service in non-combatant fields is undertaken willingly. The exemption claim rests on membership in a religious body whose teachings require it — not on individual conscience alone, which would fragment the community’s public witness.

Where no non-combatant alternative exists

National guidance varies with local law. In some countries no religious exemption is available; in others compulsory military service is total and armed. Historical practice in several communities — under direction from the Guardian and the Universal House of Justice — has been to comply with conscription while continuing to seek, where possible, assignments that do not require killing. Each national community consults the House of Justice on its circumstances; believers should not infer a personal policy from another country’s practice.

Persecuted communities illustrate the tension without dissolving the principle. In Iran, Bahá’ís have faced compulsory military service alongside systematic exclusion from civil employment; believers have been imprisoned and, in documented cases, killed in connection with military obligations combined with refusal to recant faith. The community’s obedience to government has never included obedience to orders that violate spiritual law. Those are exceptional contexts; in ordinary civic life the pairing stands: register, apply for non-combatant status where available, serve faithfully in permitted roles, consult institutions when the law offers no honourable path.

Police and other armed civilian forces

Police forces occupy a distinct category in Bahá’í guidance — related to military service but not identical. In 1984 the Universal House of Justice, replying to questions about Northern Ireland, confirmed that the Ulster Defence Regiment and the police force there are armed forces for purposes of the Guardian’s military instructions. A separate letter the same month addressed police enlistment more broadly:

“Concerning your question as to whether Bahá’ís may join the police force of a country where this force bears arms, there is in general no objection to Bahá’ís volunteering to serve in the police force. However, police forces vary considerably from country to country, and according to local situations, and conditions can change from time to time. Therefore the House of Justice feels that under current world conditions no hard and fast rules can be laid down, and if the friends have a strong desire to participate in law enforcement they should in such matters come to their own conclusions.”

— Universal House of Justice, 1984 — Messages from the Universal House of Justice, 1963–1986

That latitude is not licence to ignore the combatant principle. It is recognition that policing in one country may mean neighbourhood patrol and traffic regulation, while in another it may mean routine carriage of firearms in an internal-security role indistinguishable from military occupation. The believer asks: Will my duties require me to kill? Will identification with this force at this time associate the Faith with a partisan or oppressive apparatus? National Spiritual Assemblies familiar with local conditions are the first resource; unusual cases go to the House of Justice.

‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s tablets on government service, compiled in Trustworthiness, supply the ethical floor for any public role. Those selected for administrative positions should act with “true servitude and ready compliance,” distinguished by “goodly disposition and virtuous character,” content with allotted remuneration, and “far removed above covetous designs.” Bribery, extortion, and abuse of authority are condemned in terms that apply as sharply to a police uniform as to a minister’s desk:

“If a man deal faithlessly with a just government he shall have dealt faithlessly with God; and if he render it faithful service he shall have rendered that service to God.”

— ‘Abdu’l-Bahá, Trustworthiness

Law enforcement undertaken as service — protecting the vulnerable, upholding lawful order, refusing corruption — can be among the most socially useful of careers. Law enforcement undertaken as cover for prejudice, partisan enforcement, or personal power is incompatible with Bahá’í character regardless of the title on the badge.

The wider teaching on war and arms

Bahá’í opposition to taking life in military service is not indifference to peace. The Universal House of Justice’s 1985 statement The Promise of World Peace restates ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s vision of a binding international covenant limiting armaments:

“The size of the armaments of every government should be strictly limited, for if the preparations for war and the military forces of any nation should be allowed to increase, they will arouse the suspicion of others.”

— ‘Abdu’l-Bahá, cited in The Promise of World Peace

Bahá’ís work toward that future through spiritual and social means — not through capture of partisan power (spiritual-conquest-vs-political-non-involvement). Individual military and police decisions sit inside that horizon: obey the law, refuse killing where exemption exists, serve with integrity where it does not, and never confuse national service with partisan alignment (bahai-and-partisan-politics).

Pastoral caveats

Do not treat pacifism as Bahá’í doctrine. The Faith requires civic obedience and willing non-combatant service, not evasion of duty.

Do not volunteer for combat service to advance career or patriotism where national guidance forbids it. The Guardian’s instructions on voluntary enlistment are load-bearing.

Do not assume police work is automatically permissible. Armed internal-security forces may fall under the same moral analysis as combat units.

Do not publicise the Faith through draft boards or recruitment in ways that embarrass the community. A 1940 National Assembly letter notes that appearance before a draft board can be an opportunity to place Bahá’í teachings on official record — but answers must be consistent with authentic teaching, not improvised from personal opinion.

Do not judge believers in other countries by one’s own selective-service law. Consult the institutions that know the local statute and practice.
