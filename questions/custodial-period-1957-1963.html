How did the Ministry of the Custodians (1957–1963) operate in practice, and what role did the Hands of the Cause play after the first UHJ election?

The six years between Shoghi Effendi‘s unexpected passing on 4 November 1957 and the election of the first Universal House of Justice on 21 April 1963 constitute a period the Bahá’í world calls the custodianship — an interval without a Guardian, navigated by the twenty-seven Hands of the Cause of God acting in collective stewardship. The Ten Year Crusade (1953–1963) was completed under their care, its goals largely met or exceeded. When the Universal House of Justice was elected, the custodianship ended; the Hands continued as Hands until their passing but no new ones could be appointed.

Shoghi Effendi’s unexpected passing

Shoghi Effendi died in London on 4 November 1957 of Asian influenza. He had not appointed a successor Guardian, had not convened the International Bahá’í Council as a precursor to the Universal House of Justice, and left no Will designating what should follow. The Universal House of Justice, whose election he had been preparing the community for, did not yet exist — it could only be elected once a sufficient number of National Spiritual Assemblies had been established, a threshold the Ten Year Crusade was designed to reach.

The shock of this situation was acute. The Guardianship — the institution through which the Covenant had been protected since ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s passing — had ended with no named successor. The Will and Testament of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá had anticipated this situation only insofar as it called for the Hands of the Cause to “be under the direction of the Guardian.” With no Guardian, the Hands convened at the Bahá’í World Centre to decide what to do.

The declaration of custodianship

The twenty-seven Hands of the Cause, assembled in conclave at the World Centre in November 1957, issued a proclamation declaring themselves “Chief Stewards of the embryonic World Commonwealth of Bahá’u’lláh” (Hands‘ Proclamation, November 1957). The same document constituted “a body of nine Hands to serve at the Bahá’í World Centre” to deal with protection of the Faith and to correspond with National Assemblies “on matters connected with the prosecution of the objectives of the Ten Year Plan” — a temporary stewardship bounded to completing the Crusade and evolving the International Bahá’í Council into the Universal House of Justice, not a claim to Guardianship authority.

Nine Hands were stationed permanently at the World Centre as a Custodial Body, with a rotating membership. They administered the properties and affairs of the Faith at its international seat, coordinated with National Spiritual Assemblies worldwide, and maintained the momentum of the Crusade. The other Hands continued their work in their respective continental zones.

The Universal House of Justice later affirmed the Hands‘ custodianship as in full keeping with the Covenant. In the Naw-Rúz 1965 message on the establishment of the institution (The Establishment of the Universal House of Justice compilation), the House wrote that following Shoghi Effendi’s passing “the international administration of the Faith was carried on by the Hands of the Cause of God with the complete agreement and loyalty of the National Spiritual Assemblies and the body of the believers” — in accordance with the Guardian‘s designation of the Hands as the “Chief Stewards of Bahá’u’lláh’s embryonic World Commonwealth.” The six years between 1957 and 1963 demonstrated that the Administrative Order could function — and even flourish — without a living Guardian.

The Mason Remey claim

In 1960, Charles Mason Remey, an American Hand of the Cause and architect who had designed the superstructure of the Shrine of the Báb, declared himself the second Guardian of the Bahá’í Faith on the basis that he was the president of the International Bahá’í Council. The claim was rejected unanimously by the other Hands, who excommunicated him as a Covenant-breaker. Remey‘s followers constituted a small splinter group; the overwhelming body of the Bahá’í world continued under the Hands’ custodianship without disruption.

The episode illustrated the kind of Covenant-challenging situation the Hands’ protection role had always been designed to address. Their swift and united response reinforced the institutional integrity of the custodianship.

Completing the Ten Year Crusade

Under the Hands‘ stewardship, the Ten Year Crusade goals were substantially met. The goal of establishing fifty-six National Spiritual Assemblies — the electoral bodies that would elect the Universal House of Justice — was achieved. The Crusade had opened the Faith in 131 additional countries and territories (from 128 in 1953 to 259 by 1963, per the Crusade statistical record); by Riḍván 1963, fifty-six National Spiritual Assemblies stood ready to elect the Universal House of Justice, with communities established on every continent. The Crusade’s expansion had created the institutional basis for that election.

The Hands exercised no legislative authority — they could not issue binding guidance, legislate on matters not covered in the Texts, or interpret the Writings. Their role was custodial in the precise sense: preserving, protecting, and completing what the Guardian had set in motion.

The first UHJ election (April 1963)

On 21–29 April 1963, the first International Bahá’í Convention was held in Haifa. Delegates from the fifty-six National Spiritual Assemblies gathered to elect the nine members of the first Universal House of Justice. The election, by secret ballot, marked the end of the custodianship and the beginning of the institution that would guide the Bahá’í world from that point forward.

The Universal House of Justice‘s first message to the Bahá’í world was issued on 21 April 1963 — Riḍván 1963. It expressed the community’s gratitude to the Hands and affirmed the continuity of the Covenant.

The Hands after 1963

After the Universal House of Justice was elected, the surviving Hands of the Cause continued to serve in their capacity as Hands until their deaths. They could not be replaced — the Will and Testament specified that only the Guardian could appoint new Hands, and the institution had ended with Shoghi Effendi’s passing. The last Hand of the Cause, ‘Alí-Akbar Furútan, passed away in 2003.

To continue the propagation and protection functions historically served by the Hands, the Universal House of Justice established the Continental Boards of Counsellors on 21 June 1968, and the International Teaching Centre on 8 June 1973 to coordinate the Counsellors‘ work from the World Centre. These institutions carry forward the Hands’ protective and teaching mandate in institutional form.
