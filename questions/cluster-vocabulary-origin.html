Where did the vocabulary of "clusters" come from, and when did it enter the Bahá'í planning framework?

The word "cluster" as a defined Bahá'í planning term entered operative use in the 9 January 2001 letter from the Universal House of Justice to the Conference of the Continental Boards of Counsellors, gathered at the Holy Land as the Twelve Month Plan drew to a close. That letter set the features of the Five Year Plan (2001–2006) and, in doing so, introduced a new unit of geographic organisation that would reshape every subsequent Plan. The concept page Cluster gives the full twenty-five-year arc; this question focuses on the founding moment — what the 2001 letter actually said, what came before it, and why the Bahá'í world needed a new word at precisely that stage.

The 9 January 2001 letter

The 9 January 2001 letter proposed dividing national territories into "smaller geographic areas," each consisting of "a cluster of villages and towns" or, in an urban context, "a large city and its suburbs." The boundaries were to be drawn by "culture, language, patterns of transport, infrastructure, and the social and economic life of the inhabitants" — not by civil administration. This was the founding definition: a cluster is a manageable geographic unit whose coherence is social and human, not legal or political.

Within that frame the letter immediately established a classification. The areas into which a region would divide "will fall into various categories of development" — from those not yet open to the Faith, through areas with a few isolated localities and groups, to areas where communities were gaining strength through the institute process, and finally those where "strong communities of deepened believers will be in a position to take on the challenges of systematic and accelerated expansion and consolidation." This fourth category named what would later be called the Intensive Programme of Growth — and the cluster was announced as the geographic unit within which that programme would unfold.

The letter also named the coordinating triad that would operate at cluster level: the training institute, the Auxiliary Board members and their assistants, and an Area Teaching Committee working in close collaboration — the arrangement the Bahá'í world now knows as the cluster agencies. The cluster was therefore not merely a new label for an existing geography; it was a new administrative-pastoral unit with its own internal structure.

The problem the letter was solving was tractable in scale. Teaching plans in the 1990s worked at the level of national communities, regions, and localities, but had no stable intermediate unit — a geography that was small enough to allow mutual support and coordination among believers, yet large enough to hold a community capable of operating an institute, a teaching committee, and a programme of growth simultaneously. The cluster filled that gap.

Pre-2001 vocabulary

The plans Shoghi Effendi designed between 1937 and 1963 — and the successive plans of the Four Year Plan era (1996–2000) — operated in a different vocabulary. The units were: goal countries, goal cities, virgin territories, localities, regions, and teaching zones. Shoghi Effendi's Citadel of Faith articulates the Ten Year Crusade in terms of "virgin territories … to be opened" and territories "to be consolidated" — an ordinal taxonomy of places by their stage of opening, not a lateral division of a national territory into manageable peer units.

The Four Year Plan (1996–2000) named the training institute as the central instrument and launched the first serious effort at systematic expansion, but it still thought in terms of national communities and regional institutes. The 26 December 1995 letter to the Counsellors' Conference, which launched the Four Year Plan and is the pivot point of the Turning Point compilation, does not use "cluster" as a planning term. It names regions and localities; it calls for training institutes in every national community; it does not yet propose an intermediate geographic unit between the locality and the region.

The word "cluster" does appear in the writings before 2001, but not as a technical planning term. In the Tablets of the Divine Plan, 'Abdu'l-Bahá refers to "the cluster of the Greater Antilles" and "the cluster of the islands of Bermuda" — loose geographic groupings, not operationalised planning units. Shoghi Effendi occasionally uses the word in a similarly informal geographic sense. The 9 January 2001 letter is the moment this ordinary English word acquired a precise technical definition and was placed at the centre of the global Plan.

Evolution of the vocabulary 2001–2022

The cluster vocabulary did not arrive complete in 2001; it matured with use across four subsequent Plans.

By 2003, two years into the first Five Year Plan, the 17 January 2003 message ("Insights Gained in the Unfoldment of the Five Year Plan") reported that National Assemblies had divided their territories into clusters "using criteria that were purely geographic and social and did not relate to the strength of local Bahá'í communities" — clarifying an important ambiguity in how the 2001 letter had been read. The global count was by then "close to 17,000 clusters worldwide." The letter also refined the meaning of the four categories: "To assign a cluster to one or another category is not to make a statement about status. Rather, it is a way of evaluating its capacity for growth." The vocabulary had already needed its first precision adjustment within two years.

The 27 December 2005 message codified the cluster's role inside the image of two movements: "the steady flow of individuals through the sequence of institute courses and the movement of clusters from one stage of development to the next." The cluster was now not just a planning unit but the unit of the second movement — a concept around which a coherent planning discourse could grow. The 1,500-cluster target for intensive programmes of growth gave the vocabulary a measurable dimension.

The decisive conceptual shift came with the 28 December 2010 message, which explicitly retired the 2001 four-category classification: "A decade ago, when we introduced the concept of a cluster … we provided an outline of four broad stages along the path of its development … The abundant experience which has since accrued enables the believers now to conceive of the movement of a population, propelled by mounting spiritual forces, in terms of a rich and dynamic continuum." The four categories gave way to the milestone framework — first two milestones in 2010, then a third added by the 29 December 2015 message — and the vocabulary shifted from asking "what kind of cluster is this?" to asking "where on the arc of development is this population moving?"

From the 30 December 2021 message onward, the vocabulary stretched further. That message announced a reassessment of cluster boundaries; the Riḍván 2022 message then reported the outcome of the re-clustering exercise, raising the global count from roughly 17,000 to "over 22,000" clusters — reflecting sharper sub-national divisions as the network of Plans matured. The Nine Year Plan also brought a qualitative frame — the "release of the society-building power of the Faith" — as the register for clusters at or beyond the third milestone, a frame the 31 December 2025 message to the Counsellors' Conference extended with the observation that in the most advanced centres of activity "the term 'programme of growth' no longer does full justice to such developments." (Universal House of Justice, 31 December 2025; [source].) What began in 2001 as a manageable geographic frame for systematic teaching had become, by 2025, the unit in which a Bahá'í community might achieve a "self-perpetuating" pattern of social transformation.

Why the cluster concept was necessary

The cluster was not an administrative novelty for its own sake. It was the answer to a specific question the 1990s experience had posed: at what scale can a community actually learn?

Teaching plans up to 2001 had produced significant expansion in some places, but the gains were fragile because they rested on the efforts of a small number of individuals rather than a coherent community with shared institutional infrastructure. The training institute, introduced in the Four Year Plan, addressed part of the problem by producing human resources at scale — but those resources needed a geography in which to act. A national community was too large for the institute's graduates to hold accountable to one another; an individual locality was too small to sustain the full triad of institute, teaching committee, and Auxiliary Board accompaniment. The cluster resolved this by creating a unit large enough to hold that triad and small enough for its members to know one another.

The 2001 letter's insistence on drawing boundaries by "culture, language, patterns of transport, infrastructure, and the social and economic life of the inhabitants" — and explicitly not by the strength of the existing Bahá'í community — was the second crucial move. Cluster boundaries were to describe a human social reality, not an administrative convenience. This meant that when a cluster grew, its growth could be read as a real change in how a real population was responding to the institute and the core activities, not merely as an artifact of drawing lines around existing believers.

The deeper necessity was the institute process itself. The Four Year Plan had named the institute as the primary instrument; what it did not yet have was a unit in which the institute's output — trained teachers, tutors, animators — could aggregate into a self-sustaining programme. The cluster provided exactly that: a space in which the institute process and the programme of growth could be co-present and mutually sustaining rather than sequential. This is the analytical core of the 9 January 2001 letter, and it is why the cluster concept did not simply name a new geographic category but created a new mode of action.
