How should an Assembly handle a situation where a Bahá’í has been involved in partisan politics?

The Local Spiritual Assembly‘s first move is pastoral consultation with the friend, not censure: two members visit, listen before naming, and seek to understand what the involvement actually is, what circumstances led to it, and whether the friend was aware of the principle at stake. The matter is serious because the non-partisan character of the Faith is structural — an expression of religion’s constructive role in society, bound to its mission of unifying humankind across forty-some governments through the Administrative Order — but the path runs through accompaniment, deepening, and, where withdrawal is needed, support in carrying it out. The severe sanction of deprivation of administrative rights is reserved to the NSA and is invoked only after sustained warning and obduracy; the LSA’s office is to consult, educate, and accompany.

Why this is not an ordinary disciplinary matter

Shoghi Effendi‘s 1932 letter to the American friends, gathered in The World Order of Bahá’u’lláh, gives the doctrine its sharpest contemporary formulation. The Guardian writes that it is the friends’ “first and sacred obligation to abstain from any word or deed that might be construed as a violation of this vital principle” — the non-participation of the adherents of the Faith “in any form of activity that might be interpreted, either directly or indirectly, as an interference in the political affairs of any particular government.” He continues:

“Let them refrain from associating themselves, whether by word or by deed, with the political pursuits of their respective nations, with the policies of their governments and the schemes and programs of parties and factions. In such controversies they should assign no blame, take no side, further no design, and identify themselves with no system prejudicial to the best interests of that world-wide Fellowship which it is their aim to guard and foster.”

— Shoghi Effendi, 21 March 1932 (The World Order of Bahá’u’lláh)

The 1947 statement The Faith of Bahá’u’lláh: A World Religion, also in the same volume, supplies the structural reason: a Faith whose divinely-ordained institutions have been “established within the jurisdiction of no less than forty different countries, the policies and interests of whose governments are continually clashing,” cannot, “by allowing its adherents… to meddle in political activities, succeed in preserving the integrity of its teachings and in safeguarding the unity of its followers.” The prohibition is not squeamishness about controversy; it is the load-bearing condition of a globally unified community. A friend who joins a party or accepts a partisan office has — perhaps unknowingly — taken a step that puts that condition at risk.

What is partisan, and what is not

The line is not between civic engagement and withdrawal. The Guardian himself instructs that the friends are “to strive to distinguish, as clearly as they possibly can… such posts and functions as are either diplomatic or political from those that are purely administrative in character.” Bahá’ís hold civil-service posts, professional offices, academic chairs, and civic associations of every kind; they vote where the franchise permits voting on the basis of qualifications rather than party; they contribute to the prevalent discourses of society from within their workplaces and disciplines. The doctrinal companion to this question — on the spiritual conquest of the planet — develops the positive register at length.

What is excluded is partisan alignment: membership of a political party, public advocacy of a party‘s platform, holding an office whose occupancy is determined by partisan competition, or aligning the Bahá’í voice with a faction in the political contest. The 23 December 2008 letter of the Department of the Secretariat, written to a Bahá’í political scientist, supplies the most useful contemporary application. “You may find that an issue pertaining to social action has been co-opted by the political debate among competing factions, and wisdom will be required to determine whether to adjust your approach or let the matter rest for a time.” Co-option is the live risk in our era — a question of principle (the equality of women, the welfare of children, the protection of the environment) becomes attached to a partisan position, and the Bahá’í who pursues the principle is read as endorsing the position. The Assembly’s pastoral conversation often turns on exactly this distinction.

The consultation pathway

The 9 December 1991 letter of the Universal House of Justice, The Role of Assemblies in Dealing with Violations of Bahá’í Law, is the controlling text on the procedural shape. Citing a letter on behalf of the Guardian, it sets out the basic principle:

“Before anyone is deprived of their voting rights, they should be consulted with and lovingly admonished at first, given repeated warnings if they do not mend their immoral ways, or whatever other extremely serious misdemeanor they are committing, and finally, after these repeated warnings, be deprived of their voting rights.”

— Universal House of Justice, 9 December 1991

In practice this unfolds as a sequence the LSA holds with patience.

First contact is pastoral. Two members of the Assembly meet the friend privately. They listen. They seek to understand: was the friend aware of the prohibition? What is the actual nature of the involvement — a registered party membership, a candidacy, a paid staff position, an enthusiastic public association? What circumstances — political pressure, a workplace expectation, a family situation, a deeply felt civic concern — led to it? The conversation is not an interrogation; it is the Assembly initiating pastoral care. A letter on behalf of the Guardian (28 September 1941) names the Assembly’s standing duty to “help, advise, protect, and guide the believers in every way within their power when appealed to” — a duty the Assembly discharges proactively here, not only when the friend has first sought help.

If the friend was unaware, the appropriate response is a brief, loving deepening on the principle, with the relevant passages from the Guardian and the House of Justice shared. Most situations resolve here. The friend was not consciously violating a teaching; once the principle is understood, withdrawal follows naturally and the Assembly accompanies the steps — resignation from a party position, a quiet clarification where the friend’s name has been associated with the party, a re-anchoring in community life and the core activities.

If the friend was aware and is prepared to withdraw, the Assembly consults with the friend on what withdrawal looks like in their particular situation. The 1991 letter is explicit that a single isolated act is not, in general, ground for sanction; the Assembly’s task is to ascertain attitude and to assist. On the logic of sanctions generally — in an example concerning immorality, not partisanship — the House writes: “If she is contrite and is determined to lead a moral life henceforth, there would be no question of sanctions.” The procedural point transfers: contrition and a firm resolve to amend conduct ordinarily preclude administrative action.

If the friend persists despite consultation, the Assembly continues to accompany — often drawing on the Auxiliary Board member or an assistant for additional pastoral support, since the appointed arm’s specific gift is the protection and propagation of the Faith through accompaniment. Repeated warnings are given; time for reflection is allowed. If the conduct continues to be flagrant, the matter is referred to the National Spiritual Assembly for consideration. The 1991 letter is candid about the threshold: the severe sanction of deprivation of administrative rights is reserved for “such matters as ‘disgraceful conduct, flagrantly contrary to our Teachings,’ ‘seriously injuring the Faith in the eyes of the public through his conduct or flagrantly breaking the laws of God,’ ‘gross immorality and open opposition to the administrative functions of the Faith,’” and similar.

The reservation of administrative rights to the NSA

This point is load-bearing and easy to misstate. The authority to deprive a believer of administrative rights, or to restore them, rests with the National Spiritual Assembly — not with the LSA. The 30 May 1997 letter on Regional Bahá’í Councils (in turning-point) makes the boundary explicit even where authority has been widely delegated to a Council: “in any case the authority to deprive a believer of his or her administrative rights, or to restore them, remains with the National Assembly.”

The LSA‘s role on the way to such an outcome is therefore particular. It consults pastorally with the friend; it seeks to understand and to encourage; it issues the warnings the 1991 letter envisages; and, where the matter is not resolved, it brings the case to the NSA with its own assessment and its own consultation on file. The decision to remove administrative rights — the right to vote in Bahá’í elections, to be elected to office, to attend the consultative portion of the Feast, to contribute to the Bahá’í funds — sits with the national institution. This reservation is itself a safeguard: it prevents local heat from converting itself into administrative action, and it places the heaviest pastoral instrument in the hands of the body best positioned to weigh the wider context.

Pastoral cautions

Three are load-bearing.

Do not punish the friend in the name of the Faith. The 1991 letter is direct that the removal of voting rights is “a serious action which an Assembly should take reluctantly,” always with the “hope and prayer… that the believer who has been administratively expelled from membership in the Bahá’í community will come to see that his behavior is in violation of the teachings, will endeavor to rectify his conduct, and will thus open the way to being welcomed back.” The instrument is restorative in intention, never punitive.

Do not let the consultation become a faction. The Guardian‘s instruction (1924) that “the keynote of the Cause of God is not dictatorial authority, but humble fellowship, not arbitrary power, but the spirit of frank and loving consultation” applies inside the Assembly room as much as outside. An LSA that splits along its members’ own political sympathies has misread the consultation; the discipline of consultation requires that personal political views are set down at the door.

Do not under-name what is at stake — and do not over-name it. The non-partisan character of the Faith is not a stylistic preference; it is what makes the Faith translatable across forty governments and a thousand cultures. Where a friend‘s involvement risks that character publicly, the Assembly must act. Equally, most situations do not reach the threshold of administrative action; they resolve in the first pastoral conversation. The Assembly’s discernment is how to give each situation what it actually needs.
