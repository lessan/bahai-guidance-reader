How should a Bahá’í think about investing — what values should a portfolio lean toward, and what should it avoid?

The writings give an investor a direction of travel, not a screening rulebook. Wealth is honourable when honestly earned and generously, usefully spent; trustworthiness must distinguish every phase of business life; and interest, dividends, and ordinary returns on capital are explicitly lawful. A believer has clean, binding-law warrant to regard three activities as forbidden — alcohol, habit-forming drugs, and gambling — and may, as a matter of conscience, screen the industries that supply them. Almost everything beyond that (the arms industry, environmental harm, exploitative business) is a defensible inference from principle, not an explicit ruling — and the texts themselves prefer the voluntary over the imposed and counsel moderation in all things, including how strictly one interprets.

Wealth is conditionally honourable

The clearest statement is positive, with two conditions — honest acquisition and useful expenditure:

"Wealth is praiseworthy in the highest degree, if it is acquired by an individual's own efforts and the grace of God, in commerce, agriculture, art and industry, and if it be expended for philanthropic purposes."

— ’Abdu’l-Bahá, The Secret of Divine Civilization

Held against this is the counterweight that wealth is a test and a potential "mighty barrier" (Bahá’u’lláh, Hidden Words, Persian No. 53). The reconciling principle is attachment versus detachment and use, not the quantity of money. Earning a living is itself raised "to the rank of worship of the one true God" (Kitáb-i-Aqdas, ¶33) — a binding law — with agriculture singled out for "special regard" (Bahá’u’lláh, Tablet of the World). Productive enterprise — farms, workshops, firms that make goods and services — sits closer to the texts' positive image of wealth than speculation pursued for its own sake, though the Writings do not forbid holding financial assets as such.

The firmest hook: trustworthiness in business

This is the teaching that maps most directly onto an investor's own conduct, and it is framed as obligatory. Bahá’u’lláh names trustworthiness the "goodliest vesture" and the fruit of faith itself:

"O people! The goodliest vesture in the sight of God in this day is trustworthiness."

— Bahá’u’lláh, in Trustworthiness (compilation)

The same compilation gathers a passage from the Tarázát that applies the virtue to the whole economic order:

"The fourth Ṭaráz concerneth trustworthiness. Verily it is the door of security for all that dwell on earth and a token of glory on the part of the All-Merciful. He who partaketh thereof hath indeed partaken of the treasures of wealth and prosperity. Trustworthiness is the greatest portal leading unto the tranquillity and security of the people. In truth the stability of every affair hath depended and doth depend upon it."

— Bahá’u’lláh, Tablets of Bahá’u’lláh

Commerce itself is pictured as a domain whose health depends on this virtue:

"Commerce is as a heaven, whose sun is trustworthiness and whose moon is truthfulness."

— Bahá’u’lláh, cited in Trustworthiness

’Abdu’l-Bahá extends the principle from personal character to institutional design:

"Every business company should be established on divine principles. Its foundations should be trustworthiness, piety and truthfulness in order to protect the rights of the people."

— ’Abdu’l-Bahá, in Trustworthiness (compilation)

Shoghi Effendi makes the obligation operational for every Bahá’í engaged in commerce:

"This rectitude of conduct, with its implications of justice, equity, truthfulness, honesty, fair-mindedness, reliability, and trustworthiness… must be constantly reflected in the business dealings of all its members…"

— Shoghi Effendi, The Advent of Divine Justice

Fraud, bribery, embezzlement, extortion, kickbacks, and exploitation of the poor are categorically condemned; debts must be settled when one is able. These are principles of conduct, not portfolio screens — their application to security selection is the believer's inference. A practical case: a believer who learns that a company systematically misstates earnings has stronger textual ground to divest than one who merely dislikes its sector. The investor's own honesty in reporting income, paying Ḥuqúqu’lláh, and meeting obligations is the non-negotiable floor; screening holdings is the discretionary ceiling built above it.

Moderation — including in how one invests

Bahá’u’lláh’s reversal of the historical prohibition on interest (below) carries an explicit counsel of "moderation and fairness." The wider corpus repeats the pattern. Shoghi Effendi, summarising Bahá’u’lláh’s social teachings, states that He "inculcates the principle of 'moderation in all things'" and warns that whatever "passeth beyond the limits of moderation" — "Liberty, civilization and the like" — "must exercise a pernicious influence upon men" (God Passes By). Applied to investing, moderation cuts in several directions at once. It counsels against compulsive trading, against treating portfolio returns as the measure of a life, and — equally — against turning ethical screening into a rigid ideology that breeds self-righteousness or division among friends. A believer who declines a broad-market fund because it holds a brewery stake, and another who holds that fund while giving generously to the Cause, may both be acting in good faith; the texts supply principles, not a single correct allocation.

A passage from the trustworthiness compilation names the interior disposition that moderation protects:

"Be ye God's manifestations of trustworthiness in every land. So perfectly should ye mirror forth this quality that even were ye to travel through cities heaped with gold, your gaze would not for a single moment be seduced by its allure."

— Bahá’u’lláh, in Trustworthiness

The image is not ascetic renunciation of wealth but freedom from its seduction — a distinction that matters when markets rise and the temptation is to let returns redefine one's identity.

Interest is lawful

Bahá’u’lláh reversed the historical prohibition:

"…it is lawful and proper to charge interest on money… He hath now made interest on money lawful, even as He had made it unlawful in the past."

— Bahá’u’lláh, Tablets of Bahá’u’lláh (the Ishráqát)

The ruling is binding; it carries a counsel of "moderation and fairness," the rate is deliberately left unset, and regulation is entrusted to the Universal House of Justice. Usury (abusive excess) remains condemned, but ordinary interest, bonds, dividends, and lending exposure are permitted. Retirement accounts, pension funds, and balanced portfolios that include fixed-income instruments are therefore not, by that fact alone, out of step with the law — though how heavily to weight such instruments, and at what cost to other priorities, remains a matter of judgement and circumstance.

What is cleanly forbidden — and what is inference

Three activities are forbidden by binding law of the Aqdas: alcohol/intoxicating drink (¶119), opium and habit-forming drugs (¶155, ¶190), and gambling (¶155). For opium, ’Abdu’l-Bahá extends culpability explicitly: "the user, the buyer and the seller are all deprived of the bounty and grace of God" (cited in Aqdas Note 170). These are the strongest grounds for an industry screen — though even here, moving from "the act is forbidden" to "do not hold the shares" is an inference, and the scope of "gambling" is officially left "to the conscience of the individual believers" pending future legislation (Aqdas Note 169).

Everything else is weaker-footed inference: the arms industry (no explicit prohibition, but clear moral disapproval in the peace teachings — "the malignant fruits of material civilization," ’Abdu’l-Bahá, Tablet to the Hague — while legitimate defensive force is preserved); environmentally destructive enterprise (supported only by the moderation/anti-extravagance principle, the most interpretive of all); and tobacco (explicitly discouraged, not forbidden). State these as conscience, not doctrine.

ESG screens as conscience, not canon

Environmental, social, and governance (ESG) criteria are a modern vocabulary for questions the Writings already pose in older terms: Is wealth honestly acquired? Are workers treated justly? Does enterprise serve human need or merely extract? No Bahá’í institution has endorsed a particular ESG rating system or mandated shareholder activism. The believer who adopts such screens is exercising the same faculty the Aqdas reserves for gambling's scope — individual conscience informed by principle — not obeying a new law.

The honest framing runs as follows: "On the basis of trustworthiness, the condemnation of exploitation, and the binding prohibitions on alcohol, drugs, and gambling, I choose to avoid direct holdings in these industries and to favour enterprises whose conduct I can regard as honestly productive." That statement is faithful. "The Faith requires every Bahá’í to use ESG fund X" is not. Index funds that hold a small weight in a forbidden industry force a further judgement: is minimal passive exposure acceptable, or does conscience require a customised portfolio? The texts do not decide; the investor must, without imposing the answer on others.

Mutual funds, retirement accounts, and pooled vehicles

Most believers today invest through pooled vehicles — employer pensions, national retirement schemes, mutual funds, exchange-traded funds — rather than by selecting individual securities. The Writings do not address these instruments by name; application is by principle. Three points follow.

Lawful returns. Because interest and dividends are permitted, a diversified fund is not suspect merely for holding bonds or bank stocks. The question is proportion and purpose, not category.

Imperfect purity. Broad funds almost always contain some exposure to screened-out industries. A believer may accept trace passive exposure, seek "ethical" fund variants, or build a direct portfolio — each option trades convenience against control. None is legislated.

Ḥuqúqu’lláh and valuation. Appreciating investments complicate the Right of God; unrealised gains are generally not assessed until realised, and believers may defer payment on illiquid assets until disposal (see Ḥuqúqu’lláh — computation and worked examples). Retirement accounts raise open questions — locked savings, employer matching, tax wrappers — that Representatives of the Trustee can help think through; the Board of Trustees exists to educate, not to audit portfolios.

Voluntary sharing beyond law. Contribution to the Bahá’í Fund is a separate institution from Ḥuqúqu’lláh — discretionary, spiritual, never solicited by pressure (see cluster-fund-raising). Investment returns that flow to the Fund or to philanthropic uses fulfil the expenditure side of ’Abdu’l-Bahá’s praise of wealth as much as the acquisition side.

Real estate and productive property

Land and buildings are the oldest form of "portfolio," and the Writings treat productive property favourably. Agriculture enjoys "special regard." Business and agricultural equipment that produces income for subsistence is exempt from Ḥuqúqu’lláh once assessed (Codification III; Aqdas Q&A 95). The principal residence is exempt — a rule that shapes how believers think about home ownership versus rental investment property. Income property is not automatically exempt: capital used to earn income remains assessable by conscience ([57] in Ḥuqúqu’lláh: The Right of God).

A practical case: a couple who own their home and one rental unit must distinguish exempt residence from assessable investment. A believer who flips houses for profit is engaged in commerce — lawful if honest — but the gains enter the Ḥuqúq frame as surplus, not as sheltered housing. Real estate investment trusts and property funds extend the same logic at scale: the asset class is permitted; the believer's conscience governs how much to hold and how returns are purified and spent.

Built in to a Bahá’í's wealth, whatever the holdings

Ḥuqúqu’lláh — 19% on net surplus growth above one's needs (residence, needed furnishings, and tools of trade exempt), rendered to the Universal House of Justice as a matter of conscience — is the one binding law that directly touches how accumulated wealth and its profits are held. Beyond it, voluntary sharing is rated above mere accumulation (’Abdu’l-Bahá, Selections, §79): the use of returns matters as much as their source.

What the Assembly cannot advise on

Local and National Spiritual Assemblies are responsible for the affairs of the community — the Fund, properties, teaching plans, the observance of burial law, the protection of the Covenant. They are not financial advisers to individual believers. The law of Ḥuqúqu’lláh makes the point structurally: computation and payment are "exclusively a matter of conscience between the individual and God," and "demanding or soliciting the Ḥuqúqu’lláh is prohibited" (Codification I). Representatives of the Trustees may counsel on the Right of God in confidence; Assemblies do not audit personal wealth.

On investment selection, the same boundary holds by extension. The principle of independent investigation of truth requires each believer to study, consult available expertise, and decide. An Assembly may not tell a friend which fund to buy, whether to hold defence stocks, or how to structure a retirement account — and a believer should not ask it to. Consultation with knowledgeable friends, professional advisers, and Ḥuqúqu’lláh Representatives is appropriate; transferring personal financial judgement to elected institutions is not. Historical warnings against business ventures promoted in the name of the Cause — speculative mines, "Bahá’í" corporations that lost believers' savings — reinforce the point: the institutions guard the community's integrity; they do not underwrite individual investment choices.

When ethical questions touch community life — accepting a donation, purchasing property for a centre, hiring practices — the Assembly's role is active. When they touch a believer's private portfolio, the role is, at most, to uphold general standards of honesty and to avoid pressuring anyone to contribute beyond the Fund's voluntary character.

Practical cases

The new employee in a pension plan. A believer whose employer enrolls them in a default fund with no ethical screen may participate without guilt, set aside a portion of returns for Ḥuqúqu’lláh and the Fund, and — if conscience requires — redirect future contributions to a screened option where one exists. Perfect purity is often unavailable; moderation includes accepting imperfection without abandoning effort.

The inheritance. A believer who inherits shares in a tobacco company has not sinned by receiving them. They may sell and redirect proceeds, hold and give returns to charity, or consult on timing with attention to Ḥuqúq on realised gains. The texts address character and use, not the mechanics of every windfall.

The friend who asks what you hold. Shoghi Effendi's guidance that Fund contributions "should be considered confidential" (26 October 1945) reflects a wider sensibility: financial matters are private spiritual territory. Sharing one's screening principles is welcome; interrogating another believer's portfolio is not.

The honest limit

The writings supply principles and a direction, not operational rules for security selection. A policy that says "I choose, as a matter of conscience, to weight toward honestly-run productive enterprise and to avoid alcohol, drugs, and gambling, on this textual basis" is faithful to the sources. A policy that asserts "the Faith prohibits investing in X" generally over-reaches — except for the narrow, act-level prohibitions. Moderation, trustworthiness, purification through Ḥuqúqu’lláh, and generous use of wealth: these are the load-bearing pillars. The specific allocation is the believer's work before God.
