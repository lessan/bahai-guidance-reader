How should a Bahá'í think about investing — what values should a portfolio lean toward, and what should it avoid?

The writings give an investor a direction of travel, not a screening rulebook. Wealth is honourable when honestly earned and generously, usefully spent; trustworthiness must "distinguish every phase" of business life; and interest, dividends, and ordinary returns on capital are explicitly lawful. A believer has clean, binding-law warrant to regard three activities as forbidden — alcohol, habit-forming drugs, and gambling — and may, as a matter of conscience, screen the industries that supply them. Almost everything beyond that (the arms industry, environmental harm, exploitative business) is a defensible inference from principle, not an explicit ruling — and the texts themselves prefer the voluntary over the imposed and counsel moderation in all things, including how strictly one interprets.

Wealth is conditionally honourable

The clearest statement is positive, with two conditions — honest acquisition and useful expenditure:

"Wealth is praiseworthy in the highest degree, if it is acquired by an individual's own efforts and the grace of God, in commerce, agriculture, art and industry, and if it be expended for philanthropic purposes."

>

— 'Abdu'l-Bahá, The Secret of Divine Civilization

Held against this is the counterweight that wealth is a test and a potential "mighty barrier" (Bahá'u'lláh, Hidden Words, Persian No. 53). The reconciling principle is attachment versus detachment and use, not the quantity of money. Earning a living is itself raised "to the rank of worship of the one true God" (Kitáb-i-Aqdas, ¶33) — a binding law — with agriculture singled out for "special regard" (Bahá'u'lláh, Tablet of the World).

The firmest hook: trustworthiness in business

This is the teaching that maps most directly onto an investor's own conduct, and it is framed as obligatory:

"Every business company should be established on divine principles. Its foundations should be trustworthiness, piety and truthfulness in order to protect the rights of the people."

>

— 'Abdu'l-Bahá, in Trustworthiness (compilation)

"This rectitude of conduct, with its implications of justice, equity, truthfulness, honesty, fair-mindedness, reliability, and trustworthiness… must be constantly reflected in the business dealings of all its members…"

>

— Shoghi Effendi, The Advent of Divine Justice

Fraud, bribery, embezzlement, extortion, kickbacks, and exploitation of the poor are categorically condemned; debts must be settled when one is able. These are principles of conduct, not portfolio screens — their application to security selection is the believer's inference.

Interest is lawful — the most-misunderstood point

Bahá'u'lláh reversed the historical prohibition:

"…it is lawful and proper to charge interest on money… He hath now made interest on money lawful, even as He had made it unlawful in the past."

>

— Bahá'u'lláh, Tablets of Bahá'u'lláh (the Ishráqát)

The ruling is binding; it carries a counsel of "moderation and fairness," the rate is deliberately left unset, and regulation is entrusted to the Universal House of Justice. Usury (abusive excess) remains condemned, but ordinary interest, bonds, dividends, and lending exposure are permitted. Bahá'u'lláh's wider principle — "exercise moderation in all things" (Gleanings) — governs the spirit in which returns are pursued.

What is cleanly forbidden — and what is inference

Three activities are forbidden by binding law of the Aqdas: alcohol/intoxicating drink (¶119), opium and habit-forming drugs (¶155, ¶190), and gambling (¶155). For opium, 'Abdu'l-Bahá extends culpability explicitly: "the user, the buyer and the seller are all deprived of the bounty and grace of God" (cited in Aqdas Note 170). These are the strongest grounds for an industry screen — though even here, moving from "the act is forbidden" to "do not hold the shares" is an inference, and the scope of "gambling" is officially left "to the conscience of the individual believers" pending future legislation (Aqdas Note 169).

Everything else is weaker-footed inference: the arms industry (no explicit prohibition, but clear moral disapproval in the peace teachings — "the malignant fruits of material civilization," 'Abdu'l-Bahá, Tablet to the Hague — while legitimate defensive force is preserved); environmentally destructive enterprise (supported only by the moderation/anti-extravagance principle, the most interpretive of all); and tobacco (explicitly discouraged, not forbidden). State these as conscience, not doctrine.

Built in to a Bahá'í's wealth, whatever the holdings

Ḥuqúqu'lláh — 19% on net surplus growth above one's needs (residence, needed furnishings, and tools of trade exempt), rendered to the Universal House of Justice as a matter of conscience — is the one binding law that directly touches how accumulated wealth and its profits are held. Beyond it, voluntary sharing is rated above mere accumulation ('Abdu'l-Bahá, Selections, §79): the use of returns matters as much as their source.

The honest limit

The writings supply principles and a direction, not operational rules for security selection. A policy that says "I choose, as a matter of conscience, to weight toward honestly-run productive enterprise and to avoid alcohol, drugs, and gambling, on this textual basis" is faithful to the sources. A policy that asserts "the Faith prohibits investing in X" generally over-reaches — except for the narrow, act-level prohibitions.
