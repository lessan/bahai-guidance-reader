How should the question of who is in charge in a cluster be handled?

The question is its own corrective: no single institution is in charge of everything in a cluster, by design. The work is carried by a triad of agencies — the training institute, the Area Teaching Committee, and the Auxiliary Board member with her assistants — operating alongside the Local Spiritual Assembly(ies) and under the strategic frame of the Regional Bahá’í Council or the National Spiritual Assembly. A mature cluster does not search for a chief; it sustains consultation among these agencies and trusts the friends to serve in the spaces that consultation opens.

Why the question keeps surfacing

The question almost always arrives in a particular emotional register — frustration at perceived inaction, or confusion about whom to approach when something needs to move. The temptation is to import a secular org-chart instinct: identify the office at the top, assign accountability there, escalate. The Bahá‘í architecture refuses that move. The Institution of the Counsellors (2001), the Universal House of Justice’s definitive statement on the appointed arm, makes the refusal explicit. The institution of the Counsellors and the Auxiliary Boards comprises individuals “who play such a vital role in advancing the interests of the Faith, but who have no legislative, executive or judicial authority, and are entirely devoid of priestly functions or the right to make authoritative interpretations” — a feature the same passage calls “unparalleled in the religions of the past”. The architecture’s most active institutional presence at cluster level is, by design, an arm with no power of command.

Authority over the affairs of the community sits with the elected institutions — the LSA, the RBC (where one exists), the National Spiritual Assembly, the UHJ — but no one of those bodies “runs” the cluster either. The LSA is rooted in a single locality; an RBC oversees many clusters across a region; the NSA sets the national plan. The cluster itself is a coordinated working space, not a jurisdiction.

The architecture, briefly

Three agencies do the cluster‘s coordinating work, and each has a distinct conferred-authority story. The triad was named from the outset in the Universal House of Justice’s 9 January 2001 message: “The implementation of such a program will require the close collaboration of the institute, the Auxiliary Board members and their assistants, and an Area Teaching Committee.”

The training institute coordinator(s) answer to the institute‘s board — The Institution of the Counsellors (2001) states that when a board of directors is named, “its membership is decided upon by the National Spiritual Assembly in consultation with the Counsellors and with their full support” — and run the sequence of courses, the children’s class pipeline, and the junior-youth animator pipeline. Their medium is the study circle and the tutor.

The Area Teaching Committee is appointed by the RBC (or the NSA where there is no RBC). The Universal House of Justice‘s 27 December 2005 message states directly: “An Area Teaching Committee appointed by the Regional Council, or by the National Assembly itself, is also required to administer other aspects of systematic effort to achieve accelerated expansion and consolidation.” The ATC plans the cluster’s three-month cycles of expansion and consolidation. Its medium is the teaching campaign, the receptive contact, the new declarant.

The Auxiliary Board member and her assistants are appointed by the Counsellors and the Board member respectively. Their work is pastoral and consultative. They cannot direct the other two; they accompany.

Alongside these three sit the LSA(s) within the cluster — divinely-ordained, locally elected, holding marriages, funds, the Feast, pastoral care. None of these four sits above the others in the cluster’s coordinating work; the LSAs are peers of the cluster agencies in consultation, while retaining their own locality-bounded divine warrant over the affairs of their jurisdictions. As The Institution of the Counsellors states: “The authority to direct the affairs of the Faith locally, nationally and internationally is divinely conferred on elected institutions. The power to act, however, resides primarily in the entire body of the believers.” The same document stresses that Auxiliary Board members “do not direct Local Spiritual Assemblies or individual believers in their activities” but accompany and advise; at cluster level both arms operate together within those boundaries. The wider architecture is set out in the institutions-architecture synthesis.

Why this is good design

The architecture protects against any single agency becoming the cluster‘s bottleneck. The Universal House of Justice’s 20 October 2008 message names the point directly: “Let them not underestimate the power inherent in the system they are putting in place for the propagation of His Faith.” Power is in the system, not in a heroic individual or a dominant office. R.O.G. 23 draws out the same lesson from field experience. When the institute pipeline, the ATC‘s planning, the ABM‘s accompaniment, and the LSA’s pastoral life all run, the cluster moves; when one of them seizes the work of another, it deforms.

The consultative forums that answer the question

How is consultation actually maintained among bodies none of which commands the others? At the national and regional scale, The Institution of the Counsellors (2001) names a practice that has become standard:

A highly fruitful practice has developed in many parts of the world whereby members of a number of institutions and agencies of a country, or a region thereof, come together in a meeting of consultation to reach a common vision for the growth of their community and discuss strategies for action. These “institutional meetings” help to steer the friends away from thinking merely in terms of the mechanics of projects and to infuse their plans and subsequent action with the spirit of the Faith.

— Universal House of Justice, The Institution of the Counsellors (1 January 2001)

At cluster level — the scale at which this question is usually asked — the Universal House of Justice‘s Riḍván 2010 message describes the analogous space in terms of the agencies’ joint interactions: “Intimately connected to this grassroots consultative process are the agencies of the training institute and the Area Teaching Committee, together with the Auxiliary Board members, whose joint interactions provide another space in which decisions pertaining to growth are taken, in this case with a higher degree of formality.” The cluster reflection meeting, at the close of each three-month cycle, is the wider forum where the friends and the agencies meet on common ground; the joint interactions of the triad are its smaller and more frequent counterpart.

Field practice has extended the “institutional meeting” label to cluster-scale gatherings of the LSA(s), ATC, institute coordinators, and ABM — R.O.G. 35’s Alotau account is a clear example — but that usage elaborates the Riḍván 2010 architecture; it is not what the 2001 passage itself names. Where the triad and the LSA(s) sit together regularly and consult on a shared reading of the cluster, no chief is needed. Where they do not, the question recurs — and recurs unanswerably. Detail on the reflection meeting itself sits at the reflection-meeting question.

Characteristic failure modes

Three patterns surface in the R.O.G. corpus when the architecture is misread.

An ATC that absorbs the institute pipeline — running its own teaching events without the institute coordinator, or scheduling cycle activities that crowd out the sequence of courses — produces short bursts of declarations that the cluster cannot consolidate. The Universal House of Justice‘s 27 December 2005 message to the Counsellors’ Conference names the corrective: “capacity at the level of the cluster to sustain growth will remain the most compelling concern in the coming years.” An ATC cannot manufacture that capacity by other means. R.O.G. 26 illustrates the point from field experience.

An LSA that tries to direct cluster expansion in neighbourhoods or villages outside its own jurisdiction confuses its locality-bounded mandate with the cluster-wide mandate of the ATC. The Universal House of Justice‘s Riḍván 2010 message describes the healthy alternative: evolving administrative relationships have brought the LSA to “the threshold of a new stage in the exercise of its responsibilities to diffuse the Word of God, to mobilize the energies of the believers, and to forge an environment that is spiritually edifying” — a maturation that unfolds alongside, not over, the cluster agencies’ joint work.

An ABM (or assistant) who steps into administrative authority — instructing the ATC, second-guessing the LSA — collapses the very distinction The Institution of the Counsellors insists on: Auxiliary Board members “do not direct Local Spiritual Assemblies or individual believers in their activities.” A parallel field image appears in R.O.G. 26‘s account of a national junior youth coordinator, who had to move from being a “statistician” to being a “friend” — but that account belongs to coordinator accompaniment, not to the ABM‘s mandate; the doctrinal boundary is the 2001 document’s.

Pastoral note

When a friend asks “who is in charge here”, the right move is rarely to identify the chief. It is to identify the consultation that the cluster has not yet had — between the LSA and the ATC, between the institute coordinator and the ABM, between the agencies and the friends doing the work — and to help convene it. Authority in this system is not control of the work; it is responsibility for the consultation that orients it.
