How should Bahá’í parents help children navigate other-faith holidays at school?

Public schools in many countries weave other-faith holidays into the calendar — Christmas concerts, Easter crafts, Diwali assemblies, Passover stories, Halloween parties — and Bahá‘í children are often expected to participate as a matter of belonging. Parents need a distinction the guidance already supplies elsewhere: between witnessing a tradition’s celebration as a respectful member of a diverse community and performing a ritual act that declares faith in a creed inconsistent with being Bahá‘í. Children can learn, attend, contribute art or song in a cultural mode, and honour their classmates without accepting that every holiday activity is neutral. Parents teach that distinction through conversation, consultation with teachers where needed, and the positive cultivation of Bahá’í Holy Days — not through isolation or contempt for others’ joy.

The theological ground parents are teaching

The Bahá’í teaching is not that all religions are the same but that each proceeds from the same divine Source. ‘Abdu‘l-Bahá frames the Greater Covenant: Manifestations form an unbroken chain; recognising the light in another tradition is compatible with belonging distinctly to this one. Attending another faith’s ceremony as a respectful guest follows from that recognition. School holiday activities are a lower-stakes, more ambiguous zone than a baptism or communion service — but the same question applies: Is this attendance and respect, or is it a ritual act that implies a faith commitment inconsistent with being Bahá’í?

Parents are also teaching independent investigation of truth: the child should learn how to think about religious difference — with respect, clarity about their own identity, and freedom from both blind imitation and arrogant dismissal. The mixed-faith home guidance in childrens-spiritual-education-mixed-faith-home applies by extension: do not let the child absorb an implicit hierarchy in which the Bahá‘í parent’s beliefs are correct and everyone else’s are merely tolerated. Speak of other traditions with the sincerity the Writings require.

What the Guardian and the House have said to believers — not to children

Two guidance streams orient parents; neither is a school dress code.

Among Bahá‘ís, Christian holiday celebrations are discouraged. Shoghi Effendi, in a letter written on his behalf (19 March 1938), advised that it is “surely preferable and even highly advisable that the friends should in their relation to each other discontinue observing such holidays as Christmas and New Years, and to have their festal gatherings of this nature instead during the intercalary days and Naw-Rúz.” The counsel is about Bahá’í social life, not about expelling children from music class. It reminds parents that the community has its own calendar — nine work-free Holy Days, Ayyám-i-Há, Naw-Rúz — and that importing another tradition‘s festal centre into Bahá’í identity blurs what should be distinct.

Bahá‘í children should observe Bahá’í Holy Days where arrangement is possible. Shoghi Effendi wrote (25 October 1947) that children under fifteen “should certainly observe the Bahá‘í Holy Days, and not go to school, if this can be arranged on these nine days.” The symmetrical logic matters for parents: the child’s right to absence on Bahá‘í Holy Days is worth pursuing with the school; the child’s participation in others’ holidays is a separate question governed by the witness-or-declarant distinction, not by automatic reciprocity.

Orthodox religious schooling is generally to be avoided. A letter written on behalf of the Guardian (30 May 1947) counsels avoiding sending Bahá‘í children to orthodox religious schools, especially Catholic, because children “receive the imprint of religious beliefs we as believers know are out-dated and no longer for this age.” The Universal House of Justice (10 May 1966) permits attendance at a parochial school “if circumstances require,” while reaffirming the Bahá’í parent‘s obligation to offer Bahá’í education and not to vow to raise the child in another religion. The school-holiday question is milder than full-time parochial education but belongs on the same spectrum: how much formation, how young, with what understanding.

The lines are not always bright. A Christmas pageant in which the child plays an angel may be cultural theatre in one school and devotional worship in another. Parents read the programme, ask what the child is expected to say and believe, and consult the Spiritual Assembly if uncertain.

Practical scenarios

Winter concerts and Christmas pageants. Many schools treat these as community arts events. If the child sings generic seasonal music without professing Christ as personal saviour, participation is usually consistent with respectful attendance. If the child is assigned a role that requires a creedal statement, parents request an alternative assignment — the same reasonable accommodation they seek for Bahá’í Holy Days.

Easter, Passover, Diwali, Eid units. Study units are opportunities for the child to become literate in neighbours‘ traditions — precisely what Bahá’u‘lláh’s counsel to consort in friendliness supports. The parent adds the Bahá‘í frame at home: “We respect the light in this tradition; we are Bahá’ís, so we observe our own Holy Days and laws.”

Halloween. Guidance does not address Halloween directly. Parents weigh whether the activity is neighbourhood festivity or imagery the family finds incompatible with dignity and refinement. The question is not whether other families enjoy it but whether this child, at this age, can participate without confusion about fear, death imagery, or cruelty as entertainment. Reasonable minds differ; consultation in the family precedes consultation with the school.

Food, gifts, and class parties. Accepting goodwill food or exchanging cards is social courtesy, not religious declaration — unless the act is explicitly tied to a ritual the Bahá’í child would be misread as professing.

When the child is the only Bahá‘í in the class. Loneliness is real. The parent validates the feeling, secures Holy Day accommodation where possible, and connects the child to Bahá’í community life — children’s classes, Holy Day observances, Ayyám-i-Há hospitality — so school is not the only calendar the child knows.

What to say to the child — by age

Young children need simple clarity: “Your classmates celebrate their holy days; we celebrate ours. We can be kind and join some school activities that are for friendship and learning. We don‘t say things that mean we are not Bahá’ís.”

Older children need the principle: progressive revelation, respect for Manifestations, distinct Bahá’í laws, independent investigation. They can be invited to notice when a school activity is teaching about a religion versus asking them to be part of it.

Youth can read short guidance with a parent, discuss a pageant contract before signing, and advocate for their own Holy Day absences — practising the dignity the Faith expects.

Across ages, the House of Justice (14 October 1982, to a National Spiritual Assembly) reminds parents that they are responsible for children‘s behaviour at Bahá’í meetings — training in courtesy, reverence, and obedience. The same training equips children to navigate school: not disruptive superiority, not ashamed secrecy, but clear and kind identity.

Consultation with schools and institutions

Parents approach teachers and administrators as collaborators, not adversaries. Request letters explaining Bahá’í Holy Days — widely available through national offices — mirror the accommodation other faiths seek. Where a holiday activity crosses into declarant territory, propose alternatives: a research project, a stage role without creedal content, attendance as audience rather than participant.

Bring genuinely unclear cases to the Local Spiritual Assembly. Do not circulate rumours about the school’s motives; do not let a single awkward December craft project become a grudge that teaches the child contempt.

What this is not

Not isolation. Keeping the child home from every event that mentions another religion teaches fear, not faith.

Not syncretism. Letting the child treat every holiday as equally theirs dissolves Bahá‘í identity. The Guardian’s counsel on Christian holidays among believers points the other way: the community has its own festal calendar.

Not contempt. Mocking classmates‘ traditions violates the oneness of religion and damages the child‘s character. The Bahá’í parent speaks of other faiths with the respect due to divine origins.

Not a campaign against the school. The aim is the child’s clarity and kindness, not a precedent for culture-war parenting.

Building the positive calendar at home

Children who only hear what they do not do at school learn religion as restriction. Parents balance boundary with celebration: observe Naw-Rúz and Ayyám-i-Há with joy; mark the Twin Birthdays; explain the Fast as the child matures; connect the child to a children‘s class open to all where virtue, prayer, and friendship are practised. The Riḍván 2010 arc pairs study circles and children‘s classes as core activities “open to all” — the child who knows vibrant Bahá’í community life navigates school holidays from strength, not from deficit.

Ayyám-i-Há offers a natural counter-season to winter‘s commercial and religious saturation in the Northern Hemisphere: hospitality, gift-giving, charity, and rejoicing ordained in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas without importing another tradition‘s symbols. A child who helps prepare gifts for neighbours during the Intercalary Days learns that the Bahá’í calendar is generous and outward-turning, not merely a list of absences from school pageants.

Mixed-faith households and the non-Bahá‘í parent’s family

In homes where one parent observes another tradition, school holidays may coincide with grandparents‘ expectations — Christmas dinner, Eid visits, Passover with extended family. The guidance on mixed-faith homes applies: foster family unity, be transparent about what the child will encounter, do not press registration against a parent’s objection, and speak of both traditions without ranking one as merely tolerated. Attending a grandparent‘s holiday meal as family love is not the same as the child professing a creed; the Bahá’í parent prepares the child beforehand and debriefs afterward, naming what was honouring and what would cross the line if asked.

Consultation between spouses precedes unilateral decisions. A child dragged into a parental conflict over symbols learns religion as warfare — the opposite of what the Writings intend.

Registration, identity, and the school form

School census forms sometimes ask religion. Registration of a child as Bahá‘í in community records follows the House of Justice’s 19 July 1982 guidelines: children of two Bahá‘í parents are Bahá’ís from birth; a child of one Bahá‘í parent may be registered unless the non-Bahá’í parent objects. Answering a school questionnaire is a separate matter — parents describe the child‘s upbringing accurately without treating a government form as declaration. A child not registered in Bahá’í records may still attend children‘s classes and Holy Days; registration and participation are distinct, and the school does not need to know the community’s internal census.
