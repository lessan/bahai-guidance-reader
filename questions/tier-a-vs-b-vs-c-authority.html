How do Tier A, Tier B, and Tier C authority differ, and why does Bahá’í epistemology require all three?

Bahá’í epistemology rests on three structurally distinct tiers of authority: Divine Revelation (Tier A), authoritative interpretation (Tier B), and binding legislation (Tier C). Each tier operates in a different mode — revealed, interpreted, and legislated — and each fills a function that the other two cannot supply. Together they constitute a complete and self-sustaining architecture for guiding a global community across centuries.

The Three-Tier Architecture

Tier A — Bahá’u’lláh, the Báb, and ‘Abdu‘l-Bahá — constitutes the revealed foundation. The Báb and Bahá’u’lláh are Manifestations of God; their Word is Divine Revelation, essentially infallible and beyond the reach of any later authority to supersede or alter. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá occupies a unique station as Centre of the Covenant and fully authorised Interpreter: his infallibility is conferred rather than essential, yet his interpretations carry the authority of the Text itself. Nothing written by Shoghi Effendi or enacted by the Universal House of Justice can override what Tier A has established. The Text is permanent, and everything downstream flows from it.

‘Abdu’l-Bahá distinguishes the two kinds of infallibility in Some Answered Questions ch. 45: “infallibility in essence is confined to the universal Manifestations of God,” while “infallibility as an attribute is conferred upon sanctified souls” — including the Universal House of Justice when “elected by the entire community,” whose decisions on matters not in the Book are “guarded from error.”

Tier B — Shoghi Effendi as Guardian of the Cause — provides authoritative interpretation of that Text. His authority is not revelation; he does not add to the Word but illuminates its meaning with a guidance that is infallible within its proper scope. Shoghi Effendi formulates the division in The Dispensation of Bahá’u’lláh: “the Guardian of the Faith has been made the Interpreter of the Word and… the Universal House of Justice has been invested with the function of legislating on matters not expressly revealed in the teachings… Neither can, nor will ever, infringe upon the sacred and prescribed domain of the other.” Because his interpretations stand permanently — they cannot be reversed by the Universal House of Justice or by any future authority — they form a stable doctrinal layer between the living Text and the evolving decisions of the legislative body. Since Shoghi Effendi‘s passing in 1957, Tier B is a closed corpus: no living individual holds the interpretive office, but the Guardian’s writings remain binding in perpetuity (see guardianship-not-continued).

Tier C — the Universal House of Justice — legislates on matters the Texts do not explicitly address and applies Bahá’í principles to the realities of each age. ‘Abdu‘l-Bahá’s Will and Testament ordains that this body “enacteth the laws” and “must be elected by universal suffrage.” Its freedom from error in its designated functions is institutional in character: the collective body, not any individual member, is sheltered from error when discharging its assigned duties. The 1972 Constitution specifies that the House may “enact laws and ordinances not expressly recorded in the Sacred Texts; to abrogate, according to the changes and requirements of the time, its own enactments” — revisability that is not a weakness but a design feature, enabling the community to adapt without violating the permanent layers above.

Why Each Tier Is Necessary

Revelation alone cannot govern a global community through every contingency across centuries. The Holy Writings do not specify rules for every situation that will arise in human society; a body capable of extending and applying the law is indispensable. Interpretation alone cannot evolve: precisely because the Guardian’s interpretations are permanent, they anchor meaning but cannot respond to new circumstances. Legislation alone — without an authoritative interpretive layer — would fragment over time, as competing readings of the Text pulled the community in incompatible directions.

The necessity of all three tiers is therefore structural, not merely historical. Tier A supplies the eternal foundation — truths that no later authority can revise. Tier B supplies permanent interpretation — fixed definitions of what the Text means, shielding the community from doctrinal drift. Tier C supplies adaptive legislation — a living capacity to meet new conditions without abandoning the foundation. Remove any one tier and the architecture collapses: without Tier A there is nothing authoritative to interpret or apply; without Tier B interpretation becomes contested and the Text becomes a battleground; without Tier C the community would be frozen, unable to respond to historical change.

What Happens Without Any One of Them

(Editorial synthesis — Tier F.) A tradition with only revelation and no authoritative interpreter produces the denominational fragmentation visible in earlier religions — multiple communities, each claiming to read the same text correctly, with no institutional means of resolution. A tradition with revelation and interpretation but no legislative body would be administratively paralysed, unable to address the practical and social questions that every community must answer. A tradition with legislation and no revealed text — or with legislation allowed to override the text — drifts into institutional self-authorisation, the law becoming whatever the governing body decides. Shoghi Effendi‘s comparative survey in The Dispensation of Bahá’u‘lláh locates the distinctive Bahá’í provision in the written appointment of twin successors; the three-tier structure forecloses each of these failure modes simultaneously.
