How does the Bahá’í concept of gender equality differ from secular feminism?

The Bahá’í Faith and secular feminism share a commitment to the equal dignity of women and men and the dismantling of structural barriers to women's full participation in society, but they diverge sharply in their foundations, methods, and ultimate scope. For the Bahá’í teachings, equality is not a political construct won through advocacy but a spiritual reality inscribed in the nature of the soul itself — the soul has no gender, and in the sight of God all humans are equal. This is a Tier A/B principle of the Faith, not a secondary or negotiable one.

’Abdu’l-Bahá articulates the civilisational argument: “The world of humanity is possessed of two wings: the male and the female. So long as these two wings are not equivalent in strength, the bird will not fly. Until womankind reaches the same degree as man, until she enjoys the same arena of activity, extraordinary attainment for humanity will not be realized.” (Promulgation of Universal Peace, p. 375.) The Universal House of Justice, in The Promise of World Peace (1985), names equality as a prerequisite of peace: “The emancipation of women, the achievement of full equality between the sexes, is one of the most important, though less acknowledged prerequisites of peace… There are no grounds, moral, practical, or biological, upon which such denial can be justified.” Riḍván 2022 grounds the principle ontologically: “The soul has no gender, no ethnicity, no race. God sees no differences among human beings except in relation to the conscious effort of each individual to purify his or her soul and to express its full powers.”

Shared Ground and Where It Ends

Both traditions reject the notion that women are inferior in capacity, intellect, or moral standing. Both are concerned with the concrete conditions of women's lives — access to education, economic participation, freedom from violence, representation in public life. The Bahá’í community is expected to actively embody these commitments in its own institutions and practices, not merely endorse them in principle.

The divergence begins at the root. Secular feminism — whether liberal, socialist, or radical — derives equality from philosophical frameworks developed by human reason: natural rights theory, social contract thinking, or critique of patriarchal power structures. The Bahá’í position is that equality is a fact about the soul and therefore about reality, disclosed by divine revelation. This means it cannot be voted away, culturally relativised, or treated as one value to be balanced against others. It is prior to politics.

The Two Wings of a Bird

’Abdu’l-Bahá’s two-wings framing goes well beyond a rights argument. It locates the equality of women and men not in fairness to individuals alone but in the capacity of the human race as a whole to advance. A civilisation that suppresses half its members is not merely unjust — it is structurally impaired, unable to reach the degree of maturity its potential allows.

This places the Bahá’í teaching closer in some respects to cultural feminism than to liberal feminism, in that it is concerned with the transformation of attitudes, relationships, and culture rather than primarily with legal reform. The difference is that this cultural transformation is grounded in revealed religion and the discipline of community life, not in feminist theory.

The UHJ and the Limits of Human Understanding

The most frequently raised apparent tension is the composition of the Universal House of Justice, whose membership is restricted to men by Bahá’u’lláh’s own legislation. Secular feminism would classify this as a straightforward instance of structural inequality at the apex of an institution. The Bahá’í response is that this is a specific divine provision that coexists with the equality principle rather than negating it. ’Abdu’l-Bahá, in Selections from the Writings of ’Abdu’l-Bahá (no. 38.4), states: “The House of Justice, however, according to the explicit text of the Law of God, is confined to men; this for a wisdom of the Lord God's, which will erelong be made manifest as clearly as the sun at high noon.” The Universal House of Justice's 31 May 1988 letter to New Zealand adds its own exposition: the matter “will, no doubt, become clear in the future” — and affirms that the ineligibility of women for UHJ membership “does not constitute evidence of the superiority of men over women.” The Bahá’í framework asks the believer to hold both truths: that women and men are spiritually equal in all respects, and that this particular institutional provision reflects a wisdom not yet disclosed. This is a posture of epistemic humility before divine legislation, a category that secular feminist analysis does not recognise and therefore cannot evaluate on its own terms.
