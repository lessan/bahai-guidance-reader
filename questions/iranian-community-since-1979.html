What was the state of the Iranian Bahá’í community at the time of the 1979 Revolution, and how has it changed since?

Iran was the homeland of the Faith and held the largest single national Bahá’í community in the world at the moment the Revolution succeeded in February 1979. The Islamic Republic that emerged from the Revolution declared Bahá’í institutions illegal and initiated the systematic campaign of persecution — executions, imprisonment, institutional dissolution, and educational exclusion — that has not ended. Through all of this the community has responded with what the Universal House of Justice, in its 23 August 2022 letter to the Iranian friends, described as conduct that would be, “were a person to observe with a pure eye unclouded by prejudice,” “sufficient evidence and abiding proof of Bahá’u’lláh’s miracle.” (payamha-iran, `en_0365`.)

Before 1979 — the community at its height

Iran is the birthplace of the Cause. The Báb declared His mission in Shíráz in 1844; Bahá’u’lláh was born in Núr in 1817 and received the first intimations of His revelation in the Síyáh-Chál dungeon of Ṭihrán in 1852. The community that grew from those declarations was, by the mid-twentieth century, the largest and most institutionally developed Bahá’í community in the world. No authoritative census exists; secondary estimates place the pre-1979 population at roughly three hundred thousand to four hundred thousand believers (scholarly and BIC sources; not a UHJ-published figure).

The community had built a significant institutional life across the Pahlavi era. The Tarbíyat schools — including girls‘ schools in Tehran founded in 1911 — extended literacy and education to communities the state had not reached, and placed Bahá’í women‘s education decades ahead of regional norms. Bahá’í hospitals and dispensaries served the broader Iranian public. Professionals from the community contributed to law, medicine, government service, and the arts in proportions far exceeding the community‘s size. The national Ḥaẓíratu‘l-Quds in Tehran was a functioning administrative and cultural centre; local Bahá’í communities across the country ran their own assemblies, feasts, and Holy Day observances. Five generations of institutional development stood behind the community that entered 1979.

That strength was not purchased without cost. Periodic pogroms had struck through the entire period — the Yazd massacre of 1903 in which more than a hundred Bahá’ís were killed is among the most severe — and the Pahlavi era was uneven in its posture, ranging from tacit toleration to formal exclusion from government employment. The community had learned, over 130 years, both to build and to absorb. What it had not encountered was a state with the reach and institutional capacity of the Islamic Republic.

1979 and the decade of persecution

The revolution‘s Bahá’í chapter began before it formally concluded. On 15 December 1978, the Universal House of Justice cabled the world that Bahá’ís in Iran were in grave peril: homes in Nayriz, Shíráz, and Sarvistán had been looted and burned, and in at least one location Bahá’ís had been forced to recant in mosques (chronology, December 1978; muhj-1963-1986). By 12 January 1979, more than 300 homes in Shíráz alone had been destroyed and some 700 people displaced; the UHJ contributed $135,000 to an Iran relief fund (chronology, January 1979).

The regime‘s victory on 11 February 1979 inaugurated systematic dismantling. What The Bahá’í World, Volume 18 documents — as the first contemporaneous account — spans the period Riḍván 1979 to Riḍván 1983 and includes:

  • 15 February 1979: Revolutionary Guards seized the national Ḥaẓíratu’l-Quds in Tehran.
  • May–September 1979: The House of the Báb in Shíráz — one of the two most sacred Bahá’í sites — was seized, then attacked and partially demolished; the site was announced fully obliterated by the UHJ on 10 December 1981.
  • 30 September 1981: A Ministry of Education decree (published in the Kayhán daily) barred Bahá’í professors and students from universities as members of a “misled and heretical sect” (chronology, September 1981; muhj-1963-1986).
  • August 1980: Nine members of the National Spiritual Assembly of Iran disappeared from a meeting in Tehran; their fate was never officially confirmed. All are presumed martyred.
  • 27 December 1981: Eight members of the replacement NSA — the second body elected after the first disappeared — were executed by firing squad in Tehran, following their arrest on 13 December.
  • 18 June 1983: Ten Bahá’í women, including Mona Mahmúdnízhád (17 years old), were hanged in Shíráz after refusing to recant. Six Bahá’í men had been executed two days earlier (chronology, June 1983; muhj-1963-1986).
  • 29 August 1983: The Iranian Attorney-General ordered the disbanding of all Bahá’í administrative institutions.

By the time BW Vol. 18 was compiled, over a hundred Bahá’ís had been martyred or kidnapped by Riḍván 1983, two NSAs had been entirely destroyed, and the community’s five generations of institutional development had been stripped away — holy places, schools, centres, endowments, and the formal right to any organised community life (BW V18, chapter 11). The decade that followed brought the total of executed believers to over two hundred by the late 1980s (chronology, 1979–1988; bahai-iran-persecution).

Geoffrey Nash, opening the persecution chapter in BW V18, placed the community in its proper historical frame: “This latest pogrom developed into the most severe onslaught in this century against the Bahá’í Faith… The Bahá’ís of Iran… have retained a consciousness of their heritage in the arena of persecution that befittingly has raised them, in the eyes of the Bahá’í community outside the Cradle of the Faith, to rank alongside the Dawn-Breakers of the Heroic Age.”

The BIHE and community resilience

The 1981 university ban created the immediate problem the Bahá’í Institute for Higher Education was founded to answer. In 1987 — the same year the Six Year Plan was launched and the Mount Carmel Projects announced — the community built a clandestine university operating out of private homes across Iran, with Bahá’í professionals, many of them dismissed academics, serving as volunteer faculty (chronology, Q3 1987; bahai-iran-persecution). By 2010 the Institute had roughly three thousand students enrolled in courses across more than thirty fields (For the Betterment of the World, BIDO 2023). Degrees from the BIHE have been recognised by some foreign universities for graduate admission.

The Institute has been raided repeatedly — most prominently in 1998, in 2011 (the occasion of the UHJ’s 17 June 2011 letter in payamha-iran, `en_0022.md`), and again in 2022 — with faculty arrested and materials seized. It has continued to operate after each raid.

The BIHE is best read not as an anomaly but as one instance of a pattern the Universal House of Justice has addressed consistently across its entire correspondence with the Iranian community: the community continues. Where the state dissolved formal institutions, informal coordination replaced them. Where university education was blocked, a clandestine university was built. Where the Yárán — the informal national coordinating body that served in the absence of an elected NSA — were arrested in 2008, correspondence with the World Centre continued without them. The House of Justice’s framing in its 19 May 2008 letter to the Iranian believers captures the underlying logic: “the authorities remain unaware of the operation of spiritual forces that can bring about the greatness of that blessed land.”

The UHJ has issued a sustained series of pastoral letters to the Iranian community across the entire post-1979 period — the payamha-iran corpus holds 237 Persian originals and 59 authorised English translations (with additional Iran-specific guidance in global Naw-Rúz letters and pre-2011 compilations such as muhj-1963-1986). These letters function not as crisis communications but as a continuous relationship: the House addresses the community in the second person, placing it inside a spiritual interpretation of its own circumstances rather than treating it as an object of concern from outside. The discipline the letters consistently commend is the same across decades: continue the community‘s inner life, observe the Fast and Holy Days and the Nineteen-Day Feast, participate in the Nine Year Plan’s activities within whatever constraints apply, and never respond to oppression with political opposition or retaliation.

Diaspora and global impact

The post-1979 exodus produced a substantial diaspora of Iranian Bahá’ís across North America, Western Europe, and Australia. That community-in-dispersion has been, by any measure, a significant force in the worldwide Bahá’í community. Iranian Bahá’ís have served on Continental Boards of Counsellors, in translation and publication work, in the development of the Ruhi sequence, and in Bahá’í academic and social-action institutions. The experience of persecution they carried out of Iran — intimate knowledge of maintaining Bahá’í community life under hostile conditions, along with the spiritual formation that non-retaliation requires — proved directly applicable to the cluster-building work the UHJ launched from the early 2000s onward, sustained by the worldwide institute process. The UHJ’s Naw-Rúz 180 letter (21 March 2023) names this contribution explicitly, noting that Iranian Bahá’ís have, over 180 years, built schools, hospitals, and dispensaries and promoted literacy, equality, and the abolition of prejudices — a record that spans both the community inside Iran and those who carried its culture outward.

Recent UHJ letters

The pastoral correspondence continues. The Naw-Rúz 180 letter (21 March 2023; nawruz-180-letter-iran) situates the community within the Nine Year Plan and names its history of response to oppression in a single phrase: “To poison, it responded with sweetest honey.” Subsequent Naw-Rúz letters address the culture of peace (nawruz-181-letter-iran, 2024) and the institution of the family (nawruz-182-letter-iran, 2025). Across all of them the House writes to the Iranian friends not as a persecuted community to be pitied but as “the protectors of the Cause of God and the standard-bearers of the Law of God in the Cradle of His Faith.” The Covenant — the axis that holds the community’s non-retaliatory disposition, its institutional continuity, and its correspondence with the World Centre together — is the connecting concept that makes this correspondence intelligible across its entire span.
