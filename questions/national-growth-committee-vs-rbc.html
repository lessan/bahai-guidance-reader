What is the role of the National Growth Committee that the Nine Year Plan introduced, and how does it relate to the Regional Bahá’í Council?

A National Growth Committee (NGC) is a body of three, five, or seven members appointed by a NationalSpiritualAssembly in a country that does not have RegionalBahaiCouncils, charged with helping clusters advance. The Universal House of Justice formalised it in its 30 December 2021 message to the Conference of the Continental Boards of Counsellors, at the launch of the Nine Year Plan. The NGC and the RBC are parallel institutions — calibrated to different national scales but charged with the same cluster-coordination work. Where size and complexity warrant multiple regional bodies, the RBC architecture continues; where the cluster work needs focused coordination but not sub-division into regions, the NGC plays the analogous role at the national-aggregate level.

How the NGC was introduced

The 30 December 2021 message names the precedent and the new step in one continuous passage. The House of Justice first observes that "where a Regional Council has developed an enhanced capacity for administration, including an ability to provide appropriate kinds of support to many clusters at once, this has been conducive to the accelerated progress of the whole region." It then turns to the smaller communities:

Our message to your 2015 conference indicated that in smaller countries where the establishment of Regional Councils is not required, a formal structure would need to emerge at the national level which would be charged with helping clusters to advance. We ask that, in countries where this has not yet occurred, you now consult with National Assemblies about the steps that can be taken to appoint that formal structure, namely, a National Growth Committee with three, five, or seven members.

Two things are worth noting. First, the NGC is not a new idea in 2021 — the 29 December 2015 message to the Conference of the Counsellors had already noted that, in "smaller countries that do not require the establishment of Regional Councils," National Assemblies were "increasingly giving thought to ways of helping clusters advance, in some instances by forming a working group charged with this task," and asked the Counsellors to "stimulate learning in this area, with the aim that, in due course, formal structures can be defined that would assume this responsibility in much the same way that Councils do in other countries." The 30 December 2021 message names the formal structure and asks for it to be established now. Second, the NGC is also asked of countries "where Councils have not been formed but could be established eventually" — so the NGC is, in some places, a precursor to a future Council rather than a permanent alternative.

Between Riḍván 2022 and Riḍván 2025, NGCs were progressively appointed in countries that lacked them. By the close of the Plan's first phase, the institutional coverage is uniform: the 30 December 2025 message to the Counsellors observes plainly, "With Regional Bahá’í Councils or a National Growth Committee now established in every country, the institutional means are in place everywhere for the process of growth to be systematically advanced."

What the NGC does

The 30 December 2021 message specifies the brief and grants the latitude. The National Assembly "will need to give this agency the necessary latitude to foster the movement of clusters, drawing relevant insights from what has been learned about Regional Councils in this regard." The substantive responsibilities the message lists track closely with those of an RBC: "appointing Area Teaching Committees and encouraging them in their plans, arranging for the deployment of homefront pioneers, supporting teaching projects, and distributing core literature." The relational arrangements also track: "The Committee will benefit from being able to collaborate closely with the training institute, itself an agency of the National Assembly, and with the Auxiliary Board members serving the country, and it will also be able to communicate directly with the relevant Counsellor."

The rationale is administrative: "creating an entity that is wholly occupied with promoting growth should enable an Assembly to give greater attention to other important matters." The NSA in a smaller country has many duties — external affairs, properties, fund, government relations, communications with the World Centre — and direct cluster coordination at cluster-era depth competes with these duties for the NSA's attention. The NGC is the device by which the NSA devolves the cluster-coordination work without losing the national picture.

The parallel with the Regional Bahá’í Council

The relationship between NGC and RBC is parallel, not hierarchical. Both exist in countries that have NSAs; neither stands above the other.

The 30 May 1997 letter described the original problem the RBC was designed to address: "the expansion of the Bahá’í community and the growing complexity of the issues which are facing National Spiritual Assemblies in certain countries" had brought the Cause to a stage where purely national coordination was too distant from the grassroots. The letter answered with a "new element of Bahá’í administration, between the local and national levels … to be designated as 'Regional Bahá’í Councils.'" That answer fits a country whose cluster count and geographic spread justify multiple regional bodies — the United States, India, Brazil, several large African and Latin American countries.

For a smaller country — many Pacific island nations, smaller European countries, parts of Africa with single national communities — the RBC architecture would over-decentralise. There is not enough scale to justify multiple Councils, but the cluster work still needs more focused coordination than the NSA itself can give it. The NGC is calibrated to that scale: a single national-level coordinating body, smaller than a Council, with a narrower remit and a closer working relationship with the NSA that appointed it.

Three asymmetries are worth holding clearly. Size: an RBC has nine members, "or, in certain cases, seven or even five" — a provision set out in the enclosure to the 30 May 1997 letter, The Establishment of Regional Bahá’í Councils in Certain Countries, Their Characteristics and Functions; an NGC has three, five, or seven. Composition route: per the same enclosure, an RBC is, in some places, elected by the LSAs of the region on 23 May and, in others, appointed by the NSA, with the UHJ deciding which mode; an NGC is appointed by the NSA. Authority: the 30 May 1997 cover letter formulates the RBC's brief as "autonomous decision making on both teaching and administrative matters … below the National Assembly and above the Local Assemblies"; the 30 December 2021 message asks the NGC to assume its function "in much the same way that Councils do in other countries," with latitude granted by the NSA, without repeating the precise "autonomous decision making" formulation.

The continuity is more important than the asymmetries. The 30 December 2025 message treats the two as functionally equivalent for the purposes of growth coordination — the institute's periodic consultative gatherings, for example, bring together "Auxiliary Board members, representatives of the Regional Bahá’í Council or National Growth Committee, resource persons from the learning site, and other individuals whose experience has become a valued asset." The same paragraph, the same role, either body.

What this means for the architecture

In the cluster-era division of labour mapped in Bahá’í institutions architecture, the NGC fills the cell that an RBC would otherwise occupy. The NSA still holds national-strategic oversight, external affairs, property, the fund, and the relationship with the Universal House of Justice. The training institute remains an agency of the NSA. AreaTeachingCommittees, institute coordinators, and Auxiliary Board members continue at their own scales. What changes in a non-RBC country is who appoints the ATCs, arranges pioneer deployment, distributes core literature, and holds the cross-cluster picture: the NGC, not the NSA directly. The practical answer to "what coordinates the cluster work above the level of a single cluster?" is now uniform across the Bahá’í world — an RBC where the cluster count and geographic spread justify multiple regional bodies, an NGC where they do not.
