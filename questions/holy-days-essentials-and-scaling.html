How should one approach the Holy Days — what is essential, and how does it scale?

Bahá’í Holy Days anchor the community calendar in sacred history. What binds across all nine work-free days is suspending employment where circumstances permit; gathering is also obligatory on specific anniversaries, while on others the friends may meet at any convenient hour. Beyond these obligations, the House has not legislated a fixed liturgical form — which means observance can expand or contract with community capacity without changing what the day requires.

The Nine Work-Free Days — and the Twin-Day Change

Nine Holy Days carry the obligation to suspend work. The Kitáb-i-Aqdas names the Riḍván festivals and the Declaration of the Báb; editorial notes record that ‘Abdu’l-Bahá added the Ascension of Bahá’u’lláh, completing the nine days on which work is forbidden. The list below follows a pedagogical order, not the sequence of the Text:

  1. Naw-Rúz (1 Bahá — Bahá’í New Year, fixed at the vernal equinox)
  2. First Day of Riḍván (13 Jalál)
  3. Ninth Day of Riḍván (2 Jamál)
  4. Twelfth Day of Riḍván (5 Jamál)
  5. Declaration of the Báb (8 ’Aẓamat)
  6. Ascension of Bahá’u’lláh (13 ’Aẓamat)
  7. Martyrdom of the Báb (17 Raḥmat)
  8. Birth of the Báb
  9. Birth of Bahá’u’lláh

On the first, ninth, and twelfth days of the Festival of Riḍván, Bahá’u’lláh states in the Questions and Answers: “On the first, ninth and twelfth days of this Festival, work is forbidden.” The Universal House of Justice, repeating the Guardian’s instruction, has affirmed that “work is forbidden on our nine Holy Days” and that believers should make every effort to have their right to these days recognised by employers, though they are not required to forfeit employment if refused.

Since the adoption of the Badí‘ calendar on Naw-Rúz 172 (21 March 2015), all Holy Days except Naw-Rúz and the Twin Birthdays are fixed in Badí’ months; their Gregorian equivalents shift from year to year. Naw-Rúz remains tied to the vernal equinox.

The Twin Holy Days — the Birth of the Báb and the Birth of Bahá’u’lláh — underwent a significant calendar reform in the signed letter of 10 July 2014. Eastern communities had traditionally observed them according to their correspondence to the first and second days of Muḥarram; Western communities had used solar Gregorian dates. The House decided that they “will now be observed on the first and the second day following the occurrence of the eighth new moon after Naw-Rúz,” accommodating their lunar character within the solar Badí’ calendar and unifying worldwide observance. For the first commemoration under this rule, a letter written on behalf of the House on 8 September 2015 advised that “refraining from work and gathering for observances are required on both days.”

Two additional days are commemorated but are not work-free: the Day of the Covenant (4 Qawl) and the Ascension of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá (6 Qawl). Editorial notes to the Kitáb-i-Aqdas record that these anniversaries “are observed, but on which work is not suspended.” Shoghi Effendi, in a letter written on his behalf on 21 January 1951, clarified that the friends “must regard observance of these two anniversaries as obligatory — but suspension of work not to be regarded as obligatory.”

What Is Essential

The binding constant across the nine work-free Holy Days is work suspension. Gathering obligations vary by day. On the Day of the Covenant and the Ascension of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá, coming together is obligatory though work need not cease. On the Twin Birthdays, both work suspension and gathering are required on each of the two days. On other Holy Days, the friends may gather at any time during the day they find convenient.

Beyond these obligations, Bahá’í Holy Day observance carries no prescribed liturgical form in the sense of mandatory rites, programme structures, or officiants. The House of Justice has confirmed that reciting the Tablets of Visitation is not mandatory at anniversary commemorations. Communities have developed occasion-appropriate devotional content — prayers, readings, music, stories — as interpretive practice rather than as law alongside work suspension.

One commemoration that does carry a specific traditional timing is the Ascension of Bahá’u’lláh. In a response dated 26 September 2011, the House of Justice noted that the Guardian “has advised that, if feasible, the friends should commemorate” this day “at 3:00 a.m.” — local standard time, with a clock exception in high latitudes. This timing is advised tradition, not a suspension-of-work-level obligation; small communities often honour it in the predawn hours, but feasibility governs.

Scaling from a Small Group to a Large Community

The following describes field practice (Tier F), not institutional prescription. The House has not legislated programme formats by community size; clusters at different stages of development have found the patterns below useful illustrations of how the same obligations can be expressed.

Two to five friends. A home gathering with prayers, passages from the Writings, conversation, and shared refreshment can express the day’s spirit. No formal programme is required.

A neighbourhood or cluster community. Some communities at this scale invite seekers and friends of the Faith to modest programmes — a brief historical framing, readings, music, informal conversation. The devotional portion of the Nineteen Day Feast offers a parallel model for tone, though the Feast and Holy Days are distinct institutions.

A larger regional or national event. Well-resourced communities have sometimes organised public commemorations with artistic presentations, children’s activities, and outreach to civic or interfaith contacts. Holy Days carry inherent narrative — the Declaration of the Báb, the Martyrdom, the Twin Birthdays — that lends itself to accessible public programmes. The Framework for Action notes that Holy Day commemorations, with their prayers, stories, and songs, “stir the heart and fill the soul with awe and wonder,” and that major anniversaries offer opportunities to attract “believers, their families, friends, and collaborators, as well as others from the wider society.”

Elaboration reflects community capacity, not a second standard of validity. A handful of friends and a citywide gathering both fulfil the same obligation set.

Holy Days as Teaching Opportunities

Holy Days are grounded in history — real figures, real events — and the House has directed that the Twin Festivals in particular be regarded as “a sacred moment to gather and strengthen the devotional and social life of the community and as a priceless opportunity to reach out to friends, family, and the wider society.” A community that opens its gatherings extends an invitation that is both welcoming and instructive; seekers encounter the character of the Faith through atmosphere and story. The inherent content of the commemorations carries much of that work without requiring events to be reframed as outreach programmes.
