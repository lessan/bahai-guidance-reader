What is an Intensive Programme of Growth, what makes one intensive, and what are its constituent elements?

An intensive programme of growth (IPG) is the form a programme of growth takes when, in a Cluster, the elements that produce expansion and consolidation are not only present but functioning together at a sustained tempo — three-month cycles disciplined by a reflection meeting, an institute process producing human resources at the rate the cycles require, and a coordinating triad of cluster agencies in regular consultation. What makes it intensive is not the volume of activity in any one moment but the rhythm, coordination, density, and disciplined learning that hold a cluster‘s work together cycle on cycle. The Universal House of Justice’s 28 December 2010 message places the launch of an IPG at the second milestone of cluster development: the moment a cluster moves from a programme of growth in nascent form to a self-sustaining system.

Where the term comes from

The phrase “intensive programme of growth” is introduced in the 9 January 2001 message of the Universal House of Justice to the Conference of the Continental Boards of Counsellors, the letter that launched the first Five Year Plan of the new century. The 26 December 1995 message had already named clusters and the training institute; what 9 January 2001 added was the diagnostic distinction between clusters where an intensive programme could be undertaken and those where preparatory work was still needed. The condition the House of Justice attached to the launch of an IPG was specific: among the prerequisite conditions, “above all, the strong presence of the training institute with a scheme of coordination that supports the systematic multiplication of study circles.” An IPG was not a campaign that any cluster could declare; it presupposed an institute able to feed the cluster‘s growth at the cycle’s pace.

Five years later, the 27 December 2005 message gave the IPG its operative definition: “It consists of cycles of activity, in general of three months‘ duration each, which proceed according to distinct phases of expansion, consolidation, reflection and planning.” The same letter named the reflection meeting at the cycle’s hinge — “as much a time of joyous celebration as it is of serious consultation” — and added JuniorYouthSpiritualEmpowermentProgramme to the three earlier core activities. From 2005 onward the IPG is recognisable everywhere by this signature: three-month cycles with a reflection meeting at the end, the four core activities held together by an AreaTeachingCommittee and an institute coordinator team.

The 28 December 2010 message then re-located the IPG within the three-milestone framework. The first milestone was named as the appearance of “a nascent programme for the sustained expansion and consolidation of the Faith” — “in whatever combination and however small in number,” devotional gatherings, children‘s classes, and junior youth groups maintained by friends moving through the institute sequence. The second milestone was the “’launching‘ of an intensive programme of growth” — “conscious recognition that all the elements necessary to accelerate the expansion and consolidation of the Faith are not only in place but also functioning with an adequate degree of effectiveness.” The IPG, from 2010 forward, is the M2 form of a programme of growth, distinguished from the M1 form by the presence and coordinated functioning of all the elements, not by any single element’s strength.

What “intensive” actually means

Four operative qualities together make a programme of growth intensive.

Tempo. The cluster runs three-month cycles consistently, cycle on cycle, without interruption. The 28 December 2010 message names the diagnostic image: by the time a cluster’s growth conforms to it, the rhythm is “established by pronounced cycles of expansion and consolidation, which, punctuated every three months by a meeting for reflection and planning, are unfolding without interruption.” A cluster that runs one or two campaigns a year, however vigorous, has not yet acquired the tempo. Three months is the operative period because it is short enough that the cluster can read its own progress while the previous cycle is still in living memory, and long enough for an expansion phase to mature into consolidated activity.

Coordination. A coordinator (or coordinator team) for the main institute sequence, one for children’s classes, one for JuniorYouthSpiritualEmpowermentProgramme groups, and an AreaTeachingCommittee, all consult together. The 9 January 2001 letter is exact about this: an IPG is launched only where a “scheme of coordination” supports the systematic multiplication of activity. The cluster agencies do not run as parallel streams; they meet, reflect, and plan as one.

Density. Enough core activities operate at once — devotional meetings, study circles, children’s classes, junior youth groups — that a population, not just a small core of friends, is engaged. The 29 December 2015 message gives the scale at second-milestone clusters: “perhaps tens of individuals active in the work of expansion and consolidation, while those participating might well surpass a hundred.”

Disciplined learning. Each reflection meeting reads the cluster‘s current cycle against its previous cycle, in light of what the friends have learned about the global Plan. The 29 December 2015 message describes the reflection gathering as the meeting “that regulates” the cluster‘s “pulsating heartbeat,” the locus where the impulse to learn through action is concentrated and from which it spreads to other points of the cycle. Reflection is not a report-back to institutions but a consultation in which the next cycle’s plans are formed from what the last cycle taught.

The 28 December 2010 message ties these qualities together in a single phrase: an IPG is an “ever-expanding, self-sustaining system for the spiritual edification of a population.” “Intensive” denotes the systemness — the cluster’s capacity to keep its own pattern of growth going — not the loudness of any one campaign.

The constituent elements

The constituent elements of an IPG are formulated by the Universal House of Justice in the 9 January 2001 and 27 December 2005 letters, and elaborated operationally in Reflections on Growth — most directly Issues 7, 17, 28, and 33. The elements that must be in place for an IPG to be said to have been launched are:

  1. A functioning institute process with coordinators — for the main sequence of courses, for children’s classes, and for junior youth animators. Not a single coordinator carrying everything; a team that can support the activities the cluster sustains.
  2. An AreaTeachingCommittee — a small group of friends responsible for the cluster’s teaching efforts, in regular consultation with the institute and the Local Assembly (or Assemblies) within the cluster.
  3. A pattern of activity — the four core activities (devotional meetings, study circles, children’s classes, junior youth groups) operating at sufficient density that more than a small core of friends is engaged.
  4. Three-month cycles with reflection meetings — the cycle‘s tempo settled into the cluster’s life.
  5. A growing flow of human resources — friends moving through the InstituteProcess at a rate matching the cluster’s cycle, with Book 7 graduates routinely producing new tutors rather than tutoring being the bottleneck.

These elements are listed separately for analytic clarity; in practice the IPG is recognisable when they function as one system. The 9 January 2001 letter’s careful condition — that an IPG be launched only where the strong presence of the training institute supports systematic multiplication — is the structural insistence that the institute is the engine. Without it, the other elements lack what feeds them.

Distinguishing the IPG from a programme of growth in nascent form

The 28 December 2010 message draws the line precisely. A programme of growth is discernible “in however small a form” at the first milestone; the IPG is the form it takes at the second. The 29 December 2015 message reflects on what the intervening years had taught: a decisive demand for the first-to-second passage is the rooting of the institute process locally, with “the first cadre of tutors from the cluster” raised up from within the population. Until that rooting has happened, even an active cluster cannot sustain an IPG; visiting tutors and external campaigns can simulate the cycle but cannot keep it running. (See also How does a cluster move from the first to the second milestone?.)

A field illustration: Tanna, Vanuatu

The Universal House of Justice‘s Riḍván 2014 message offers Tanna as an extended illustration of an IPG functioning as a coherent whole. By the time of that message, “the thirtieth cycle of the intensive programme of growth has recently concluded.” The friends had engaged “no less than a third of the island‘s 30,000 inhabitants” in a sustained conversation about the planned local Mashriqu’l-Adhkár; junior youth groups were “particularly thriving” with the support of village chiefs; Local Spiritual Assemblies had become trusted across the wider society. The message draws the analytic point: “There is much here to indicate that, when the elements of the Plan‘s framework for action are combined into a coherent whole, the impact on a population can be profound.” What Tanna illustrates is not a single cycle’s success but the systemness an IPG names — thirty consecutive three-month cycles, the institute process rooted locally, the cluster agencies coordinating in their work, and a population, not only the active core, drawn into community-building.
