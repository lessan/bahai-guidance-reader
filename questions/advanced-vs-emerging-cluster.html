What is the difference between an advanced cluster and an emerging cluster — and how should each be approached?

An emerging cluster is one in which a programme of growth is nascent or not yet visible — typically a cluster at, or short of, the first milestone, with limited human resources and reliance on neighbouring clusters or national agencies for tutors and accompaniment. An advanced cluster is one whose programme of growth has matured into a self-sustaining intensive programme of growth (second milestone) or a dynamic pattern of community life extending into the wider population (third milestone). The distinction is not a ranking of communities but a developmental reading of where the work is at; the appropriate approach in each case is correspondingly different.

A continuum, not two boxes

The first thing to say is that “emerging” and “advanced” are poles on a continuum, not labels stamped on clusters. The Universal House of Justice was explicit on this point in its 28 December 2010 message to the Conference of the Continental Boards of Counsellors, the letter that retired the prior four-stage A/B/C/D classification and replaced it with the milestone framework:

“The abundant experience which has since accrued enables the believers now to conceive of the movement of a population, propelled by mounting spiritual forces, in terms of a rich and dynamic continuum.”

The phrase rich and dynamic continuum governs the rest. Cluster development is movement through phases that admit of every gradation; “emerging” and “advanced” are useful only as analytic poles for organising guidance and resourcing. Later messages — the 30 December 2021 launch of the Nine Year Plan, in particular — take this for granted and treat the milestone vocabulary as the operative tool, with “emerging” and “advanced” serving as everyday shorthands for the two ends of the continuum. The poles cluster around the first milestone (emerging) and the second-and-third (advanced); the centre of gravity of any given cluster moves over time and is read from the ground, not awarded.

A practical corollary follows. The same locality may be “emerging” in one decade and “advanced” in the next, and may also recede; R.O.G. Issue 35 (Reinvigorating Programs of Growth, 2018) is built around the experience of clusters that had reached the second milestone and then lost it. The continuum runs both directions.

The emerging cluster: establishing the pipeline

In an emerging cluster the operative task is to establish the conditions under which a programme of growth can take root and the first milestone can be crossed. The decisive variable is the local presence of the institute process. R.O.G. Issue 32 (Strategies for Growth in Emerging Clusters, August 2014) — written explicitly in response to the Riḍván 2014 call to raise to 5,000 the number of programmes of growth — collects the strategies that had proved effective: travelling teachers and “mobile tutors” dispatched from stronger clusters, homefront pioneers settling in the locality, visiting teams from neighbouring clusters, the catalytic effect of youth conferences, and patient cross-cluster reciprocity.

A representative case from R.O.G. 32: Sullana, Peru. A homefront pioneer settled in the cluster, identified a small group of receptive youth, and within months had drawn them through Books 1–3 of the institute sequence while opening the first core activities. The case is small in scale; what made it the first-milestone passage was not the number of declarations but the appearance of local capacity to help others study the sequence and accompany them as they began core activities. R.O.G. Issue 33 (Beginning and Sustaining a Program of Growth, 2015) names this in the language of “two nascent capacities” drawn from Insights from the Frontiers of Learning: first, friends able to help others study the institute sequence; second, those individuals able to attract others to participate in core activities. The first-milestone passage is the moment those two capacities take root locally.

The approach to an emerging cluster is therefore importing — tutors from neighbouring clusters, visiting teams that catalyse the first cycle, campaigns to begin Books 1–3 study, a homefront pioneer if one is available — done in a way that transfers initiative to local protagonists rather than cementing their dependence on outside life-support. The work is conducted with the locality, not upon it.

The advanced cluster: deepening and propagation

In an advanced cluster — second-milestone or third-milestone — the operative task changes. The institute pipeline is no longer the bottleneck; pronounced three-month cycles are settled; an Area Teaching Committee is functioning; a full slate of coordinators is in place. The work of the moment shifts to deepening — the qualitative concentration of activity in neighbourhoods and villages where receptivity wells up — and to propagation, the radiation of capacity outward to sister clusters.

The 29 December 2015 message — which named the third milestone — describes what this looks like in figural terms: a cluster operates “a system for extending, in centre after centre, a dynamic pattern of community life that can engage a people”. At the third-milestone end, the society-building work is visibly extending into the broader population — generations of children moving grade by grade through children‘s classes, junior youth animator capacity self-replicating across whole neighbourhoods, social and economic action arising organically out of community life, the Local Spiritual Assembly consulting on the spiritual and material life of the locality as a whole. R.O.G. Issue 36 (Stories from Around the World Shared During the Counsellors’ Conference, May 2016) supplies field illustrations from the 2015–2016 Counsellors‘ Conference — the village of Mboché in the Central African Republic, for instance, where Bahá’í activities reshaped village-council consultations around education, water, and agriculture, the cluster itself becoming a reservoir for its neighbours.

The 30 December 2021 message takes this further. It identifies the community-building, social-action, and discourse endeavours of the Plan as together being “means of releasing what the Guardian described as ‘the society-building power’ of the Faith”, and frames the entire twenty-five-year arc to Riḍván 2046 as oriented toward “the release of the society-building power of the Faith in ever-greater measures”. Within that horizon, it sets the goal that “during the Nine Year Plan the process of growth reach this level of intensity in at least one cluster in every country and every region”, observing that clusters of marked strength have already “proven to be reservoirs of knowledge and resources for their neighbours”. The advanced cluster is, by the design of the Plan itself, a propagator — its third-milestone learning is meant to flow outward.

The approach to an advanced cluster is correspondingly outward-facing: the integration of social action with community-building, participation in prevalent discourses of society, the eventual emergence of local Houses of Worship in mature centres, and conscious responsibility for the development of sister clusters in the surrounding region. R.O.G. 35 flags the inverse risk: an advanced cluster that turns inward, lets reflection meetings dwindle into reports, and stops radiating, will recede. Advancement carries an obligation to propagate.

Care needed in using the distinction

Before the cautions, a note on vocabulary: “advanced cluster” has not been a fixed category. The 9 January 2001 message inaugurated four broad qualitative stages of cluster development; the 28 December 2010 message replaced that scheme with the M1/M2/M3 milestones; the 29 December 2015 message named the third milestone and the society-building/centre-after-centre figure; and the 30 December 2021 message recast the third-milestone cluster as a reservoir for surrounding clusters across a region. The shorthand “advanced” refers in present (post-2021) usage to that latest, most demanding picture; readers tracing the term across two decades of guidance should expect the operative content to have grown.

Three cautions follow from the framework’s own logic.

The distinction is developmental, not evaluative. Writing under the prior A/B/C/D scheme — not yet under the milestone vocabulary that came in 2010 — the 17 January 2003 message had already insisted that “[t]o assign a cluster to one or another category is not to make a statement about status. Rather, it is a way of evaluating its capacity for growth, in order that an approach compatible with its evolving development can be adopted.” The vocabulary changed; the principle was inherited. The same logic holds for emerging-versus-advanced. An emerging cluster is not a deficient version of an advanced one; it is a community at a particular phase of an organic process whose unfolding has its own dignity. Triumphalist or hierarchical readings of the distinction misread the framework.

Resources flow both ways. Advanced clusters tutor and accompany emerging ones — that is the explicit logic of the third-milestone “reservoir” image and of the second movement of human resources. But the reverse is also operative: emerging clusters teach advanced ones humility about new beginnings, recall them to the freshness of first encounters with the core activities, and reveal where advanced-cluster vocabulary has drifted from the actual movement of populations. The flow is mutual, not one-directional charity.

All clusters participate in the Plan. The 30 December 2021 message addresses every cluster on earth — naming as one of the Plan‘s chief objectives that “the process of growth reach this level of intensity in at least one cluster in every country and every region”, and setting the longer-horizon aspiration to “establish intensive programmes of growth in all the clusters in the world” — with advanced clusters carrying the additional responsibility of accompaniment. Universal participation is the design; intensive programmes of growth in every cluster is the goal toward which that participation is graduated. The Plan’s success therefore depends as much on the emerging-cluster end of the continuum as on the advanced. The poles are interdependent; neither does its work alone.
