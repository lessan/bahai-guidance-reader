What is the relationship between the Greater Covenant and the Lesser Covenant?

The Greater Covenant is the perennial compact between God and humanity, mediated through the unbroken succession of Manifestations — each Manifestation announcing the next, each generation bound to recognise Him when He appears. The Lesser Covenant is the compact a given Manifestation makes within His own Dispensation regarding succession — Bahá’u’lláh’s Kitáb-i-'Ahd designating ’Abdu’l-Bahá, then ’Abdu’l-Bahá’s Will and Testament designating Shoghi Effendi and the Universal House of Justice. The two are not separate covenants but two aspects of one phenomenon: the Greater orients the individual toward the Manifestation in His age; the Lesser orders the community after His ascension. Faithfulness to the first is recognising Him; faithfulness to the second is turning to the Centre He has appointed.

’Abdu’l-Bahá’s own framing — the Greater Covenant as pattern

’Abdu’l-Bahá sets the Bahá’í Covenant within a universal pattern running the length of religious history: "Abraham, on Him be peace, made a covenant concerning Moses and gave the glad-tidings of His coming. Moses made a covenant concerning the promised Christ, and announced the good news of His Manifestation to the world. Christ made a covenant concerning the Paraclete and gave the tidings of His coming. The Prophet Muḥammad made a covenant concerning the Báb, and the Báb was the One promised by Muḥammad… The Báb made a Covenant concerning the Blessed Beauty, Bahá’u’lláh, and gave the glad-tidings of His coming."[^abraham-chain]

This is the Greater Covenant in its operative form. The compact is greater because its parties are God and humankind, and because its term is the unbroken arc of revelation from Adam to the Day of God. It is the doctrinal name for what is elsewhere called progressive revelation — reframed here as a covenant, a contract with stipulated parties and obligations, not merely a historical pattern.

Bahá’u’lláh names the human side of this Greater Covenant as the first duty incumbent on every soul. The opening verse of the Kitáb-i-Aqdas states: "The first duty prescribed by God for His servants is the recognition of Him Who is the Dayspring of His Revelation and the Fountain of His laws, Who representeth the Godhead in both the Kingdom of His Cause and the world of creation."[^aqdas-1] Recognition is not a sentimental act; it is the discharge of an obligation the Greater Covenant has placed on every generation since Abraham. The Manifestation appears; humanity is bound to recognise Him; that recognition fulfils the Greater Covenant.

The Lesser Covenant — a Manifestation's compact within His Dispensation

The Lesser Covenant operates one level below. Where the Greater Covenant binds humanity to recognise the Manifestation across the ages, the Lesser Covenant binds the followers of a particular Manifestation to a designated Centre after His ascension. Bahá’u’lláh’s Lesser Covenant is the Kitáb-i-'Ahd — the Book of My Covenant — sealed by His own hand and opened nine days after His passing. The designation is unequivocal: "It is incumbent upon the Aghsán, the Afnán and My Kindred to turn, one and all, their faces towards the Most Mighty Branch." ’Abdu’l-Bahá’s Will and Testament then continues the Lesser Covenant, instituting the Guardianship and the Universal House of Justice as the twin successors of the Centre.

The Lesser Covenant is lesser not in importance but in scope: its parties are the Manifestation and His own followers; its term is His own Dispensation; its concern is succession and the preservation of unity. Every prior Dispensation entered into one in some form — Christ designated Peter, Muḥammad's succession divided Sunni from Shíʿí, the Báb named Mírzá Yaḥyá. What differs is how it is recorded.

The continuity — two aspects of one phenomenon

The Greater and Lesser Covenants are not two religions stacked on top of each other; they are two aspects of one ongoing relationship. Faithfulness to the Greater Covenant is recognising the Manifestation when He appears; faithfulness to the Lesser Covenant is turning to the appointed Centre once He has ascended. A believer who recognises Bahá’u’lláh but rejects His written designation of ’Abdu’l-Bahá has half-fulfilled the Greater and broken the Lesser — historically, the precise fault line along which Covenant-breakers have fallen. The recognition was real; the obedience to the Manifestation's own ordinance for the post-ascension community was refused.

Both Covenants serve the same telos. Bahá’u’lláh names it in the Kitáb-i-'Ahd itself: "O ye that dwell on earth! The religion of God is for love and unity; make it not the cause of enmity or dissension." The Greater Covenant unites humanity across the ages around the recognition of God's progressive Self-disclosure; the Lesser Covenant unites a community across a Dispensation around the designated Centre. Both are instruments of unity — the same instrument operating at different scales.

The structural innovation — written naṣṣ

Every prior Dispensation observed the Greater Covenant in some form: most Manifestations were eventually recognised, even if by a remnant. But every prior Dispensation ruptured at the Lesser Covenant — succession contested, sects formed, the community fractured within a generation or two. The Bábí community fragmented in the years after the Báb's martyrdom around no fewer than twenty-five persons who, on ’Abdu’l-Bahá’s testimony, had the presumption to declare themselves the Promised One foretold by the Báb[^twenty-five] — the negative case Bahá’u’lláh’s Covenant was designed to prevent.

Bahá’u’lláh’s structural innovation is to have written down the Lesser Covenant, in the Manifestation's own hand, in a sealed document with no ambiguity about its referent. Shoghi Effendi draws this out as the Faith's distinguishing feature in religious history: the Administrative Order "must and will, in a manner unparalleled in any previous religion, safeguard from schism the Faith from which it has sprung."[^gpb-schism] ’Abdu’l-Bahá’s own formulation, cited by Shoghi Effendi in The Dispensation of Bahá’u’lláh, locates the unprecedented character at the level of the Lesser Covenant itself: "In accordance with the explicit text of the Kitáb-i-Aqdas Bahá’u’lláh hath made the Center of the Covenant the Interpreter of His Word — a Covenant so firm and mighty that from the beginning of time until the present day no religious Dispensation hath produced its like."[^firm-mighty]

The Greater Covenant in Bahá’u’lláh’s Dispensation is no different in kind from any prior one. What is unprecedented is the Lesser Covenant: the Manifestation's hand-penned designation of His Successor, ratified in a document the community treats as constitutional. Three generations of attempted schism have failed against this architecture, where every prior Dispensation fractured.
