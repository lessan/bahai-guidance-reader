What is meant by “the spiritual conquest of the planet,” and how is that compatible with the Bahá’í prohibition on partisan political involvement?

The phrase belongs to a body of imagery that runs from ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s Tablets of the Divine Plan through Shoghi Effendi‘s letters: the diffusion of Bahá’u’lláh’s revelation to every country and people is figured as a conquest, but the means are strictly spiritual — teaching, transformation of character, and the building of community life. There is no contradiction with the prohibition on partisanship because the conquest is not a contest for political power. It does not aim to capture the state, win elections, or align the community with a faction. It works in a register the political arena does not occupy: the recognition of Bahá’u’lláh and the slow re-formation of human relationships under His teaching.

‘Abdu’l-Bahá: an army whose weapons are love and divine teachings

The conquest imagery enters the Bahá’í corpus in ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s Tablets of the Divine Plan, fourteen tablets revealed to the Bahá’ís of North America between 1916 and 1917 and unveiled at the 1919 Convention. In the eighth tablet (revealed 19–22 April 1916, addressed to the Bahá’ís of the United States and Canada), the Master writes of “these souls” — the believers who arise to teach — as “the armies of God and the conquerors of the East and the West” who, “singly and alone,” will “break through the lines of the legions of all the nations.” The military register is unmistakable; so too is the disclosure, two tablets earlier, of what the army‘s weapons actually are. In the supplication closing the sixth tablet (revealed 8 April 1916, also to the Bahá’ís of the United States and Canada), ‘Abdu’l-Bahá prays that the believers may “become like unto a regiment and conquer these countries through the love of God and the illumination of divine teachings.” The means are named in the same idiom as the image. There is no campaign, no organisation of force; there is teaching, character, and the love of God.

Shoghi Effendi: the spiritual conquest of mankind

The Guardian carries the phrase forward and gives it its sharpest formulation. In The Unfoldment of World Civilization (11 March 1936), the long survey of the Bahá’í community’s progress that closes The World Order of Bahá’u’lláh, Shoghi Effendi writes that the Faith, “this healing Agency, this leavening Power, this cementing Force,”

“intensely alive and all-pervasive, has been taking shape, is crystallizing into institutions, is mobilizing its forces, and is preparing for the spiritual conquest and the complete redemption of mankind.”

The same letter, surveying the work of “itinerant teachers and pioneer administrators,” describes them as “strengthening the basis of its spiritual conquests and triumphs.” The sequence is consistent: institutions, teachers, pioneers, redemption. The conquest is the diffusion and reception of the Revelation, not the displacement of one political authority by another.

The prohibition on partisan involvement

The same Guardian who deploys the conquest imagery also articulates, in the most forceful language, the doctrine of non-partisanship. In the same letter — The Unfoldment of World Civilization — under the section heading “A World Religion,” he writes that the Faith

“is essentially non-political, supra-national in character, rigidly non-partisan, and entirely dissociated from nationalistic ambitions, pursuits, and purposes. Such a Faith knows no division of class or of party.”

Earlier in the same volume he spells out the operational implications. The friends are to “rise above all particularism and partisanship, above the vain disputes, the petty calculations, the transient passions that agitate the face, and engage the attention, of a changing world.” They are not to identify with political factions, nor to hold posts whose occupancy is determined by partisan competition. The grounds the Guardian gives are doctrinal — Bahá’u’lláh’s teaching of obedience to government, of the unity of mankind transcending factions — but they are also structural: a Faith whose divinely-ordained institutions are “established within the jurisdiction of no less than forty different countries” cannot, without losing its integrity, allow its adherents to “meddle in political activities.”

This does not mean withdrawal from civic life. The Guardian elsewhere instructs that Bahá’ís may participate in their communities where the franchise is non-partisan or where the office concerned is administrative in character; the Universal House of Justice has likewise affirmed that voting in non-partisan ballots is permissible and often appropriate. The line is not between engagement and non-engagement; it is between principled contribution and partisan alignment.

The compatibility — and why it holds

The two strands cohere because they describe different registers of action.

Spiritual conquest operates through (a) the recognition of Bahá’u’lláh’s revelation by individuals, (b) the transformation of character and conduct that follows from that recognition, and (c) the building of Bahá’í community life in cluster after cluster. None of these is a political act. None requires the community to seize, contest, or align with state power. Even the Universal House of Justice — the apex of the AdministrativeOrder whose maturation is itself part of the arc — exercises spiritual rather than political authority over the believers, and projects no claim of jurisdiction over the unrecognised.

The prohibition on partisan involvement exists because the means of conquest themselves require it. A community whose voice is read as politically aligned cannot speak to all parties; a community known to be campaigning for a faction cannot offer to a divided society the unity it claims to embody. The Universal House of Justice‘s contemporary guidance frames this exactly. Engagement with the discourses of society — the third dimension of Bahá’í endeavour named in the 29 December 2015 message and unified with growth and social action in the 30 December 2021 letter launching the Nine Year Plan — is offered as insight, not advocacy. The community contributes to the conversations of its surrounding society on peace, justice, equality, and governance, but does so by drawing on the Writings, not by aligning with platforms. The 2 March 2013 letter to the Bahá’ís of Iran on non-involvement in partisan politics frames the believers’ contribution to the discourses prevalent in society as a collegial labour of women and men consulting together — principle-bearing, not factional.

What is being won, then, is not a polity but a population‘s capacity to live under the principle of the oneness of humanity. The conquest is gradual, peaceful, and parallel to — not competitive with — the political institutions of any country in which it proceeds. The Most Great Peace, when it comes, will be the consummation of this transformation; it will not be installed by victory in an election, and the community knows this from the start. That is why the imagery of an army can sit alongside the firmest possible refusal to be a political party. The army’s victory is recognition; its weapons are “the love of God and the illumination of divine teachings”; its battlefield is the human heart and the slow shape of community life.
