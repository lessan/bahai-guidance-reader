How does an Area Teaching Committee actually plan a three-month cycle?

An Area Teaching Committee plans a three-month cycle as a single integrated movement of reflection → planning → expansion → consolidation → reflection, anchored at both ends by the cluster's reflection meeting and consulted on with the institute coordinator and the Auxiliary Board member. The plan is read against the cluster's actual human-resources reality — not against an aspirational one — and concretised into specific lines of action, named teams, scheduled dates, and modest goals owned by particular friends. The discipline is honest counting before goal-setting, a small number of priorities held to across the cycle, and a teaching campaign sized to what the consolidation phase can actually carry.

The cycle the ATC is planning into

The ATC is not inventing a structure; it is planning into one the Universal House of Justice specified in its 27 December 2005 message, which defines the intensive programme of growth as consisting of "cycles of activity, in general of three months' duration each, which proceed according to distinct phases of expansion, consolidation, reflection and planning." The same message names the reflection meeting as the cycle's hinge — "as much a time of joyous celebration as it is of serious consultation" — and specifies what the consolidation phase is for: "to bring a percentage of the new believers into the institute process… Those not participating in study circles are nurtured through a series of home visits, and all are invited to devotional meetings, to the celebration of the Nineteen Day Feast and to Holy Day observances."

The ATC plans within those phases — typically a brief expansion phase of often about two weeks (clusters may extend slightly by experience), a longer consolidation phase of six to eight weeks in typical practice, and a closing reflection week or two. The why of the three-month tempo is treated under the cycle-of-activity question; the what of the ATC itself under the ATC question. This page is the how of the planning work between those two.

The seven steps below are editorial operationalisation of UHJ and R.O.G. field practice, not a textual prescription.

Step one: the institutional meeting before the reflection meeting

Cycle planning does not begin at the reflection meeting; it begins one to two weeks before it, when the ATC, the institute coordinator, and the Auxiliary Board member (with LSA representatives where appropriate) meet to consult on what should be brought to the reflection meeting and what kind of next-cycle plan the cluster's experience is reading for. R.O.G. 35's Alotau account names this institutional meeting explicitly as the recovery move: the LSA convened "an institutional meeting to which its entire membership, the cluster institute coordinators, the Area Teaching Committee, and the Auxiliary Board members were invited" — the triad reassembling itself before the cluster gathered. The institutional meeting prepares the data and the questions; the reflection meeting belongs to the friends.

Step two: read the cycle that has just closed — honestly

The ATC arrives at planning carrying a particular discipline — honest counting before goal-setting. Four readings need to be in hand: the institute pipeline (how many friends in Book 1, Book 6, Book 7; new tutors and animators emerging); core-activity counts by neighbourhood; declarations and follow-up status; and the qualitative texture of the friends' service. R.O.G. 35's Sanniquellie cluster names the discipline directly:

"As we consulted, we realized that our goals did not match the available human resources. To devise more realistic plans, we needed to establish a system to collect more reliable statistics."

— Field account, Sanniquellie cluster (Rising to the Challenge, R.O.G. 35)

The Sanniquellie passage is load-bearing for ATC planning everywhere. A cycle whose goals exceeded its capacity does not need bigger goals next time; it needs an honest map of capacity and goals matched to it. R.O.G. 26 adds the warning from the other side: a coordinator who treats reflection as a place to present numbers without reading them — the coordinator-as-statistician pathology — leaves the cluster planning blind.

Step three: name a small number of priorities

A cluster cannot pursue everything in one cycle. The ATC's planning discipline is to name two or three lines of action the cluster will hold to — perhaps a Book 6 campaign in one neighbourhood, a junior-youth expansion in another, and a focused home-visit effort with the previous cycle's declarants. R.O.G. 28 reports the Port Alberni learning that frames the scale appropriately: "It is amazing what can happen when only two or three people study the guidance, consult, act, and reflect together in a consistent, purposeful way." Concentration produces movement; diffusion produces tiredness. The ATC chooses what not to do for this cycle as deliberately as what to do.

Step four: build teams around the priorities

Each line of action has named friends responsible. The Harris County (Houston) Area Teaching Committee secretary, in R.O.G. 23, describes the posture:

"We started about a year ago to learn how to learn. We wanted to be students of the Five Year Plan by studying the guidance and constantly acting, reflecting, consulting, and then acting again within the framework of the Plan."

— Harris County ATC secretary (Rising to the Challenge, R.O.G. 23)

The grammar — act, reflect, consult, act again — describes how a small team carries a line of action through a cycle. The ATC's planning produces team rosters, not just goals. Goals without owners are aspirations; owners without goals are activity without direction. Both fail. R.O.G. 26 names the right relational stance for the ATC to those teams: a coordinator must move from being a "statistician" to being a friend — and the same applies to the ATC's relation to the friends carrying its plan.

Step five: schedule the cycle

The plan becomes a schedule. Dates for the expansion phase (often about two weeks); the composition of campaign teams; the consolidation visits (which families, who from the cluster will accompany whom); the institute campaigns timed to the cycle (a Book 1 campaign opening during the expansion phase, on the R.O.G. 21 Tashkent pattern, so that new believers begin the institute sequence within days of declaration); the date and venue of the next reflection meeting. The schedule is concrete enough that any friend in the cluster can answer the question "what is happening in our cluster the second week of next month?"

Step six: coordinate with the institute pipeline

The ATC's plan and the institute coordinator's plan are not separate plans. An expansion phase that produces declarations the consolidation phase cannot carry leaves new believers without a Book 1 study circle to enter, and the cluster integrates fewer Bahá’ís a year later than if it had paced expansion to capacity. R.O.G. 21 cites the Universal House of Justice's Riḍván 2007 phrase for the discipline: "carrying forward the work of expansion and consolidation hand in hand" is "the key to sustained growth." The institute coordinator's pipeline — how many Book 1 tutors are ready, how many Book 6 graduates can teach in the campaign, how many new animators can absorb new junior-youth participants — is the capacity envelope inside which the ATC sets its expansion targets.

Step seven: coordinate with the LSAs

Where the cluster contains Local Spiritual Assemblies, their pastoral life and the cluster's plan should not be in tension. The Feast schedule, Holy Day observances, locality teaching projects, and any LSA-led consolidation work all belong inside the cluster's three-month rhythm rather than running on a parallel track. R.O.G. 35's recovery accounts (Matéri, Alotau, Hamilton) repeatedly identify LSAs and cluster agencies working "shoulder to shoulder" as a recovery condition. The ATC consults with the LSA(s); it does not issue.

Pastoral caveats

Three failure modes are worth naming in advance, because each one produces a plausibly competent cycle plan that nevertheless does not work.

Don't over-plan. A cycle plan that fills every weekend with a campaign or an institute event has no slack for the friends' actual lives — illnesses, work, family obligations, the rhythms of a real community — and produces friends who arrive at the reflection meeting tired rather than alive. A cycle is a learning unit, not a productivity unit.

Don't under-plan. A cycle plan with vague goals ("strengthen the institute process") and unowned actions leaves the friends without a frame and the next reflection meeting without anything specific to read. The ATC's job is to name what the cluster will hold itself to — not so it can be measured against the plan, but so it can read its own movement.

Don't separate the ATC's planning from the institute's pipeline. A teaching campaign without consolidation produces declarations the cluster cannot integrate; a consolidation phase without expansion produces an inward loop of study without new contact (the R.O.G. 35 Bucharest pattern). The ATC and the institute coordinator plan one cycle, not two.
