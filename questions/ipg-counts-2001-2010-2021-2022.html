How many intensive programmes of growth existed in 2001, in 2010, in 2021, and at the start of the Nine Year Plan in 2022?

In 2001 the concept of an intensive programme of growth did not yet exist as a counted quantity — the 9 January 2001 message introduced the idea, and the 27 December 2005 message set a target of 1,500 such programmes for the end of the 2006–2011 Plan. By Riḍván 2010 the community had reached approximately 1,500 clusters with intensive programmes (the Riḍván 2010 message confirms the target was met one year early, with the 28 December 2010 message reporting roughly 1,600). By Riḍván 2021 approximately 4,000 intensive programmes of growth were under way and approximately 1,000 clusters had crossed the third milestone — alongside 300,000 core activities and participation above two million. The 30 December 2021 message then launched the Nine Year Plan and shifted the governing metric to milestone-keyed goals; it also anticipated that by the close of the One Year Plan the community would reach over 6,000 first-milestone clusters, close to 5,000 second-milestone clusters, and 1,300 third-milestone clusters — forecasts, not Riḍván 2021 actuals. The Riḍván 2022 message reported a global re-clustering exercise that raised the total cluster count from roughly 17,000 to over 22,000 and set the 2031 milestone targets.

2001 — the concept introduced

The 9 January 2001 message to the Conference of the Continental Boards of Counsellors is the founding document. It introduced the Cluster as the basic unit of planning and described four broad categories of cluster development, from areas not yet open to the Faith up to those “strong communities of deepened believers… in a position to take on the challenges of systematic and accelerated expansion and consolidation.” Only in that topmost category was an intensive programme of growth to be launched.

At the time of the message, no count of intensive programmes existed: the concept was being named for the first time. The 17 January 2003 message reported back on the first two years, noting that Bahá’ís worldwide had now divided their territories into roughly 17,000 clusters, and that while still “relatively few” in number, some clusters were “now ready to launch intensive programmes of growth.” The figure of 1,500 — one per day of the year, as Bahá’ís sometimes put it — was set as the target for the end of the following Five Year Plan (2006–2011), not the current one. It was announced in the 27 December 2005 message.

The 2001 figure is therefore not a number but a threshold just crossed: the first clusters to operate an intensive programme existed somewhere in this early period, but they were few enough that the House of Justice was still describing them in qualitative rather than numerical terms.

2010 — the 1,500-IPG milestone

The Riḍván 2010 message reports that the worldwide community had met the 1,500-cluster goal one year before the end of the Five Year Plan — a fact the House of Justice described not as surprising but as confirmation of the pattern of learning the community had developed. The 28 December 2010 message to the following Counsellors’ Conference gives the figure with slightly more precision, situating roughly 1,600 clusters as having reached the intensive stage.

The significance of the 1,500-IPG milestone lay less in the number than in what reaching it revealed. The House of Justice wrote in December 2010 that “a decade ago, when we introduced the concept of a cluster… we provided an outline of four broad stages along the path of its development. As the Bahá’í community set about implementing the provisions of the Plan, this outline proved immensely useful… The abundant experience which has since accrued enables the believers now to conceive of the movement of a population… in terms of a rich and dynamic continuum.”

In other words, reaching 1,500 IPGs closed the chapter on the A/B/C/D classification scheme and opened the chapter on the three-milestone framework. The 28 December 2010 message named the first two milestones directly — the first being the emergence of a nascent programme of sustained expansion and consolidation, the second (equivalent to the old A-category) being the mature intensive programme. The 1,500-cluster count was immediately re-read as a second-milestone count. The 28 December letter set the next target at 5,000 clusters with a programme of growth at any level — a deliberate widening of scope that acknowledged the community could not simply keep building A-category clusters while leaving hundreds of developing clusters without a framework.

2021 — 4,000 intensive programmes of growth

The Riḍván 2021 message closes the Five Year Plan 2016–2021 and delivers the densest statistical snapshot the UHJ had then issued. It reports approximately 4,000 intensive programmes of growth worldwide — clusters operating at or past the second milestone — and around 1,000 clusters that had crossed the third milestone. The structural picture was further sharpened by the core-activity figures: 300,000 core activities were under way globally, involving more than 2 million participants, with three-quarters of a million people having completed at least one book of the institute sequence.

The Riḍván 2021 message does not publish a global first-milestone count; that figure appears only in the 30 December 2021 One Year Plan forecasts (over 6,000 clusters with programmes of growth). For growth ratios over the preceding five years, the 28 December 2010 message to the Counsellors‘ Conference provides the verified comparator: a near doubling in the reserve of children’s-class teachers and a sixfold increase in capacity to assist junior youth. The 4,000 IPG figure was therefore not an isolated headline but one count within a tableau of human-resource formation at unprecedented scale.

The Riḍván 2021 language marks the threshold of the Nine Year Plan‘s society-building frame. The House of Justice was already articulating, in cluster after cluster, a reality in which “a significant proportion of the population is engaged” and in which the Bahá’í community had “become a highly visible protagonist in society in its own right.” The 4,000-cluster count captured where the second movement stood; it did not yet capture the qualitative turn that the 30 December 2021 message would make explicit.

2022 and the Nine Year Plan

The 30 December 2021 message, launching the Nine Year Plan (2022–2031), marks a deliberate shift in how the community measures its own progress. The raw IPG count — which had been the governing metric since 2005 — gives way to milestone-keyed targets for three distinct thresholds of development.

The message also set a One Year Plan (Riḍván 2021–Riḍván 2022) with anticipated outcomes: programmes of growth in over 6,000 clusters, close to 5,000 past the second milestone, and 1,300 past the third. These are forward-looking forecasts delivered at the Nine Year Plan‘s launch, not a census of the community’s state at that moment. The Riḍván 2021 message — issued eight months earlier — remains the authoritative snapshot of actual achievement: ~4,000 IPGs, ~1,000 third-milestone clusters, 300,000 core activities, and participation above two million.

The goals set for Riḍván 2031 are: programmes of growth in approximately 14,000 clusters (past the first milestone); intensive programmes of growth — the second-milestone condition — in approximately 11,000 clusters; and more than 5,000 clusters past the third milestone, with at least one in every country and region. These numbers are not sequential addenda to the 4,000-IPG count of 2021; they represent a recalibration of what is being measured and a dramatically expanded ambition about how many clusters the Bahá’í world can bring into activity over a decade.

The recalibration was compounded by a structural change reported in the Riḍván 2022 message. A global re-clustering exercise redrew cluster boundaries, raising the total count from roughly 17,000 to over 22,000. This is a definitional jump, not organic growth: new clusters were formed by subdividing existing geographic units, meaning that some clusters previously counted as one now count as two or three. The Riḍván 2022 message reports the 2031 goals in the context of this new baseline, making direct comparison with pre-2022 IPG counts methodologically treacherous. A cluster that was operating an intensive programme before 2022 and was subdivided into three clusters after it may now appear as one second-milestone cluster and two first-milestone clusters, even if the pattern of activity on the ground has not changed.

The terminological shift also carries a conceptual signal. The 31 December 2025 midterm message to the Counsellors‘ Conference observed that in the most advanced clusters “the term ‘programme of growth’ no longer does full justice to such developments” — an explicit acknowledgement that the vocabulary introduced in 2001 was reaching the limits of what it could describe. The trajectory leads from a count of intensive programmes (2001–2010) to a milestone-framed count (2010–2022) to a society-building framing that progressively exceeds what any single count can capture.
