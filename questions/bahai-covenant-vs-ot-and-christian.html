How does the Bahá’í Covenant differ from the Old Testament covenant or the Christian new covenant?

The Bahá’í understanding of covenant operates on two distinct levels — a Greater Covenant spanning all of human history and a Lesser Covenant specific to each dispensation — neither of which maps neatly onto either the Mosaic/Abrahamic model or the Christian “new covenant.” Where the Hebrew Bible presents covenant as a conditional bilateral agreement with a particular people, and Christian theology presents it as a once-for-all redemptive act replacing the old law, the Bahá’í framework presents covenant as an ongoing divine guarantee built into the structure of progressive revelation itself, reinforced in each age by an institutional mechanism for preserving unity.

The Universal House of Justice’s 23 March 1975 letter supplies the canonical definitions:

“There is… the Greater Covenant which every Manifestation of God makes with His followers, promising that in the fulness of time a new Manifestation will be sent, and taking from them the undertaking to accept Him when this occurs. There is also the Lesser Covenant that a Manifestation of God makes with His followers that they will accept His appointed successor after Him. If they do so, the Faith can remain united and pure. If not, the Faith becomes divided and its force spent.” — Universal House of Justice, 23 March 1975

The Greater Covenant: A Divine Architecture, Not a Contract

In Bahá’í thought, the Greater Covenant is not an agreement negotiated between God and a specific group at a specific historical moment. It is better understood as a built-in promise inherent to creation: that God will never leave humanity without guidance, and that whenever a new Manifestation of God appears, humanity’s obligation is to recognise and follow that Manifestation. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá traces the pattern across dispensations: “Abraham, on Him be peace, made a covenant concerning Moses… Moses made a covenant concerning the promised Christ… Christ made a covenant concerning the Paraclete… The Prophet Muḥammad made a covenant concerning the Báb… The Báb made a Covenant concerning the Blessed Beauty, Bahá’u’lláh.” This makes the Greater Covenant simultaneously more expansive and less transactional than either the Sinai covenant or the Christian new covenant. Moses, Christ, Muhammad, and Bahá’u’lláh all stand within the same covenant — each representing a new chapter rather than a replacement or rupture.

The Lesser Covenant: An Institutional Innovation

What is historically unprecedented in the Bahá’í Faith is the Lesser Covenant — the specific, written provisions Bahá’u’lláh made to guard the unity of His community after His passing. In every previous dispensation, the death of the founder was followed by division: schism in Judaism over temple and diaspora, the great Christological controversies and denominational fractures in Christianity, the Sunni-Shia split in Islam. Bahá’u’lláh addressed this pattern directly by explicitly designating ‘Abdu’l-Bahá as the sole authorised interpreter of His teachings, who in turn appointed Shoghi Effendi as Guardian, whose constitution established the Universal House of Justice as the supreme governing body. Shoghi Effendi names the structural consequence: the Administrative Order “must and will, in a manner unparalleled in any previous religion, safeguard from schism the Faith from which it has sprung.” This chain of documented succession — grounded in Bahá’u’lláh’s own writings — means the Bahá’í Covenant has an administrative dimension that neither the Mosaic nor the Christian covenant possesses. Violation of the Lesser Covenant (i.e., rejection of the appointed Centre) is therefore not merely a moral failing but a structural rupture with the community’s source of unity.

What All Three Share

Despite these differences, all three covenant traditions share a common logic: divine initiative, a relational commitment between God and humanity, and consequences — whether framed as curses, spiritual loss, or alienation from the community — for those who break or ignore it. They also share the conviction that the covenant is not merely a legal instrument but the foundation of a transformed human identity and way of life. The Bahá’í framework does not dismiss the earlier covenants as false; it situates them as authentic and necessary stages within the same unfolding divine purpose.
