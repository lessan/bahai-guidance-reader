What does it actually mean to “release the society-building power of the Faith” — what is being released, into what, and how is success measurable?

Direct answer

To “release the society-building power of the Faith” means to allow the capacity latent in Bahá’u’lláh‘s Revelation for the gradual reordering of human relationships — around the principles of unity, justice, and the oneness of humanity — to begin propagating outward from a community that has built sufficient internal coherence to embody it. What is being released is capacity, not influence; the medium of release is the surrounding population — neighbourhood, village, cluster, and at larger scale the discourses and institutions of society; and success is read by qualitative shifts in how a population organises its life, by the proliferation of third-milestone clusters and their local Mashriqu’l-Adhkárs, and by the propagation of learning between clusters — not by membership counts, which the Universal House of Justice does not publish.

What is latent in the Revelation

The doctrine of a society-building power held in the Faith rests on Bahá’u‘lláh’s own statements about what the Revelation contains and what humanity has been created to do. In the Lawḥ-i-Maqṣúd, Bahá’u‘lláh writes: “Man is the supreme Talisman. Lack of a proper education hath, however, deprived him of that which he doth inherently possess… Regard man as a mine rich in gems of inestimable value. Education can, alone, cause it to reveal its treasures, and enable mankind to benefit therefrom.” The grammar is one of latent capacity awaiting release. In the same Tablet — released also among the Words of Paradise and the Tarázát — Bahá’u’lláh declares that “All men have been created to carry forward an ever-advancing civilization,” and that “such means as lead to the elevation, the advancement, the education, the protection and the regeneration of the peoples of the earth have been clearly set forth by Us and are revealed in the Holy Books and Tablets by the Pen of Glory.” ‘Abdu’l-Bahá, opening The Secret of Divine Civilization, treats this advancement of civilisation as the very purpose of the Revelation — the work of bringing the human family into a true social order. It is this Tier A and Tier A deposit that Shoghi Effendi and the Universal House of Justice are pointing to when they speak of a society-building power in* the Faith.

Where the phrase comes from

The phrase is not a UHJ coinage of the cluster era. It is Shoghi Effendi‘s, from The World Order of Bahá’u‘lláh — the 1936 letter “The Unfoldment of World Civilization.” Reflecting on the steady advance of the worldwide Bahá’í community, the Guardian writes that the friends, “conscious of their high calling, confident in the society-building power which their Faith possesses, … press forward, undeterred and undismayed, in their efforts to fashion and perfect the necessary instruments wherein the embryonic World Order of Bahá’u‘lláh can mature and develop.” The surrounding paragraphs name the community as the “people of the right” whose “fabric they themselves are weaving” is the “orderly world polity” of the future. The phrase, in its original setting, names a power latent in the Revelation, already operative in the construction of the Administrative Order, waiting on the community’s growing capacity to express it.

The Universal House of Justice‘s 28 December 2010 message to the Conference of the Continental Boards of Counsellors is the cluster-era doctrinal anchor. That letter quotes the Guardian’s “Conscious of their high calling…” passage at length, and observes that “the current approach to growth, effective as it is, must evolve still further in complexity and sophistication once it has taken root in a cluster, demonstrating ever more notably the ‘society-building power’ inherent in the Faith.” Thereafter the phrase becomes the recurring vocabulary of the global Plans.

The summons to release

Three years later, the 1 July 2013 Message to the 114 Youth Conferences of the Universal House of Justice introduces the phrase to the worldwide gathered youth as a charge to be deliberately undertaken. The friends, the House writes, “through perseverance in their implementation” of the framework for action “will already have seen for yourselves signs of the society-building power of the divine teachings,” and the message asks each young person to “consider the contribution that can be made by any young person who wishes to answer Bahá’u‘lláh’s summons and help to release that power.” The grammar moves from what the Faith possesses to what the friends are summoned to help release. By the 5 December 2013 message Centenary of ‘Abdu‘l-Bahá’s Return to the Holy Land, the formulation hardens into an operational target: “During the second half of the Five Year Plan, the society-building power of the Faith must be released within thousands of clusters where programmes of growth need to be initiated, reinforced, or extended.”

What is being released, and into what

The 30 December 2021 letter launching the Twenty-Five-Year arc, and through it the Nine Year Plan, is the most precise statement of the framework. Two passages of that letter, set sixteen sub-paragraphs apart, name the same reality from two angles. Speaking of the worldwide community’s areas of learning, the House writes:

“It means learning how to raise up vibrant, outward-looking communities; it means those communities learning how to bring about spiritual and material progress; it means learning how to contribute to the discourses that influence the direction of that progress… Together, they are means of releasing what the Guardian described as ‘the society-building power’ of the Faith.”

Then, framing the same reality at the cluster level, the letter observes:

“Within clusters, expansion and consolidation, social action, and contributing to prevalent discourses are dimensions of a single, unified, outward-looking endeavour carried out at the grassroots of society.”

The two passages cohere: the three areas of learning at the worldwide level — community-building, spiritual and material progress, contribution to discourses — find their cluster-level expression in the three dimensions of expansion and consolidation, social action, and discourse. What is being released, in either grammar, is a capacity latent in Bahá’u‘lláh’s Revelation — for the gradual reordering of human relationships and patterns of life around the principles the Revelation contains. The capacity is described as latent because it is not automatically operative in any community that holds Bahá’u‘lláh’s name; it manifests when the institute process has matured, when the cluster cycle is settled, when the three protagonists are working in coherence. Below that threshold the community is consolidating itself; above it the community begins to act outwardly on its environment.

The medium of release is concentric. At the innermost ring sits the neighbourhood or village — the small centre of population where, the 29 December 2015 message observes, “the process of collective transformation is most keenly felt—here that, in time, the society-building power inherent in the Faith becomes most visible.” At the next ring sits the cluster as a whole, the cluster‘s wider social fabric, and the local civic life its activities touch. At a wider ring sit the discourses of society — engaged through the BahaiInternationalCommunity, through ISGP, through the participation of individual believers in their professions. And at the institutional level sits the local Mashriqu‘l-Adhkár, named in successive UHJ messages as an architectural embodiment of the third-milestone cluster’s life, drawing the surrounding population into the new pattern.

How success is measurable

This is where the framework most clearly diverges from the secular development paradigm. The Universal House of Justice does not publish a worldwide membership figure and treats numerical conversions as a poor proxy for the work’s reality. Three legible markers track the release.

First, qualitative shifts in how a population organises its life. The Riḍván 2021 message describes what is now visible in the most advanced clusters: institutional teams that “oversee the unfoldment of educational programmes that cater to practically all the children and junior youth in a village,” engagements with the Revelation that have “transcended individuals, families and extended kinships—what is being witnessed is the movement of populations towards a common centre,” and contexts in which “age-old hostilities between opposing groups are being left behind, and certain social structures and dynamics are being transformed in the light of the divine teachings.” The unit of measurement is the pattern of life, not the headcount.

Second, the proliferation of third-milestone clusters. The Riḍván 2021 message reports that “around 200, spread across some 40 countries” clusters had passed the third milestone five years earlier, and that the figure had risen to “an astonishing 1,000 in nearly 100 countries” by 2021 — “a quarter of all the intensive programmes of growth in the world and an achievement far surpassing our expectations.” The Nine Year Plan‘s specific sub-goal, set out in the 30 December 2021 letter, is that the process of growth reach this level of intensity in “at least one cluster in every country and every region.” Each third-milestone cluster is, on the framework‘s own logic, a site at which the society-building power has begun to release outward; the count of such sites is the framework’s most direct quantitative reading.

Third, the propagation of learning between clusters. The Riḍván 2021 message frames advanced clusters as “reservoirs of knowledge and resources for their neighbours,” and notes that “regions where more than one such cluster exist have more easily developed the means to accelerate growth in cluster after cluster.” The work spreads as learning — what one community discovers about its conditions becomes available to the next. Success is registered as much in the spread of accumulated practical wisdom as in any statistic.

Riḍván 2021 makes the formal declaration that the threshold has been passed: “The society-building power of the Faith of Bahá’u’lláh is being manifested with ever more clarity, and this is a firm foundation upon which the coming Nine Year Plan will build.” The verb manifested is the corollary to latent. What was held in the Revelation, anticipated by the Guardian, prepared for through the cluster era, is now visible in the field. The 30 December 2021 letter then gathers the Twenty-Five-Year arc to 2046 under a single aim: “the release of the society-building power of the Faith in ever-greater measures.”

A pastoral caveat

The phrase is liable to a serious misreading, against which the Universal House of Justice has been emphatic. Society-building power is not a synonym for Bahá‘í political influence. The Faith does not contend for political power, does not align with parties, does not press for particular policy outcomes. The 30 December 2021 message describes the friends advancing the Plans as “loyal citizens who eschew partisanship and the contest for worldly power… focused on transcending differences, harmonizing perspectives, and promoting the use of consultation for making decisions.” What is being released into society is a way of life — the qualities and patterns of community generated by Bahá’u‘lláh’s teachings — by attraction, not by capture of institutions. The OSED Social Action statement (Tier D, illustrative) makes this point at length, and the practice it describes is consonant with what the Universal House of Justice itself sets out elsewhere: that social action is not political advocacy and not a substitute for the secular work of governments and agencies. The framework for action is the framework for action; it operates in a different register from the contest of politics.
