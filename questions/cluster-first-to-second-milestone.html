How does a cluster move from the first to the second milestone, and what is the qualitative shift involved?

The first milestone is reached when, in however small a number of activities, a programme of growth becomes perceptible: a few core activities sustained by local participants moving through the InstituteProcess. The second milestone is reached when that programme has matured into a self-sustaining ProgramOfGrowth — when “all the elements necessary to accelerate the expansion and consolidation of the Faith are not only in place but also functioning with an adequate degree of effectiveness.” The qualitative shift is the appearance, from inside the Cluster, of a community whose own institutions can carry the work without external life-support.

What the 28 December 2010 message names

The anchor text is the Universal House of Justice’s 28 December 2010 message to the Conference of the Continental Boards of Counsellors. The message retired the prior four-stage A/B/C/D classification in favour of a “rich and dynamic continuum” of milestones, and named the first two by their operative phenomenon, not by any administrative status.

The first milestone, the House of Justice wrote, is the moment when, “in whatever combination and however small in number, devotional gatherings, children’s classes and junior youth groups are being maintained by those progressing through the sequence of institute courses,” such that “a nascent programme for the sustained expansion and consolidation of the Faith can be perceived.” The diagnostic image is small: an “initial flow of human resources into the field of systematic action.”

The second milestone is the “launching” of an intensive programme of growth, which the same letter described as “conscious recognition that all the elements necessary to accelerate the expansion and consolidation of the Faith are not only in place but also functioning with an adequate degree of effectiveness.” It signals, in the message’s most-quoted sentence on the threshold, “the maturation of an ever-expanding, self-sustaining system for the spiritual edification of a population.”

Two points must be held together. First, the first milestone is not the launch of an IPG; it is the appearance of a programme of growth in nascent form. A common confusion collapses the two and treats every IPG launch as if it were the first milestone; the 28 December 2010 wording is precise the other way. The IPG launch is the second milestone. Second, between them is not a quantitative increment but a change in the cluster‘s capacity to sustain itself — from a small set of activities held together by visiting teachers and a few enthusiasts, to a coordinated system that no longer depends on any one person’s exertion to keep going.

What the transition asks of a cluster

The 29 December 2015 message reflected back on what the intervening years had taught about the passage. The decisive demand, the House of Justice observed, is the local rooting of the institute process: at the early stage most tutors might still be drawn from neighbouring clusters, and “efforts to usher in the first cadre of tutors from the cluster should steer a path between two undesirable outcomes” — moving people through the books too hastily, or letting study become so prolonged that it loses dynamism. Until the institute has produced its own tutors, animators, and children‘s-class teachers from within the cluster’s population, the work remains a project conducted upon the locality rather than a process arising from it.

That rooting then makes possible the operational hallmarks of an IPG. Pronounced three-month cycles of activity — expansion, consolidation, reflection, planning — settle into a rhythm. An Area Teaching Committee takes up the coordination role visiting institutions had carried. A full slate of institute coordinators — for the main sequence, children’s classes, and JuniorYouthSpiritualEmpowermentProgramme — comes into place. A steady flow of participants moves through the sequence, with Book 7 graduates routinely producing new tutors rather than tutoring being a bottleneck reserved for one or two senior friends. The 29 December 2015 message gives the scale: by the time a programme has become intensive, “perhaps tens of individuals” are “active in the work of expansion and consolidation, while those participating might well surpass a hundred.”

What does not belong to the transition is also worth naming. “It is not the provision of training by itself that brings about progress” (29 December 2015): without arrangements to accompany individuals into the arena of service, courses alone do not move a cluster. Administrative architecture without the underlying movement of human resources cannot conjure the second milestone; nor can numerical achievement without coherence among the Cluster. The milestone is — as I read these messages — recognised from the ground, not awarded from above.

A field case: Tshilenge, Democratic Republic of the Congo

R.O.G. Issue 32 (Strategies for Growth in Emerging Clusters, 2014) records the passage in motion. Tshilenge, a cluster adjacent to the long-mature Mbuji-Mayi in the DRC, sat at the first-milestone threshold: a few resident believers, sporadic core activities, no settled pipeline of tutors. Mbuji-Mayi sent an experienced teacher to accompany the cluster through one cycle. The teaching phase produced 46 declarations; what mattered was that the consolidation phase did not improvise. Tshilenge ran an institute campaign, drawing the new declarants directly into Book 1 study circles whose tutors had been prepared in Mbuji-Mayi’s institute, and then convened a reflection meeting to plan the next cycle against what the institute had produced rather than against ambition.

Within months the cluster moved from a programme of growth in nascent form to a three-month rhythm with the institute pipeline, the cycle, and the cluster agencies all working — the operational signature of the second milestone. The case is small; it is also exact. The passage is not a matter of accumulating numbers until a threshold is crossed but of installing a pattern that can carry the next cycle on its own. R.O.G. 28 makes the same point in inverse: clusters that crossed the first milestone but failed to root the institute tend to drift, declarants come and go, but the system does not consolidate.

What the transition is not

The first-to-second passage is not the achievement of a numerical target, nor the arrival of an institutional designation. It is not a one-way ratchet: clusters can recede, and R.O.G. 35 documents the typical pattern — reflection meetings dwindling into reports, statistics becoming unreliable, cluster agencies meeting separately rather than as a triad. The second-milestone state is the presence of a self-sustaining system; its absence shows up first in the learning loop, not in the activity count.

To pass the second milestone is for a cluster to have become a place from which the work can be carried by its own residents through their own institutions, in cycles disciplined by what the institute process has produced. That is the qualitative shift the 28 December 2010 message named.
