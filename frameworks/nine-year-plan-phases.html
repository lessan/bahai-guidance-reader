Phases of the Nine Year Plan (2022–2031)

Orientation

The Nine Year Plan (Riḍván 2022 – Riḍván 2031) is the second of the global Plans in the quarter-century enterprise opened by the Universal House of Justice at Riḍván 2021. Where the twenty-five-year enterprise of 1996–2021 raised up the capacity of individuals, communities, and institutions to initiate and sustain programmes of growth in clusters, the new series turns that capacity outward toward, in the words of the 30 December 2021 letter, “the release of the society-building power of the Faith in ever-greater measures.” The Nine Year Plan is the first serious test of whether that outward turn can be realised at global scale.

The Plan is unusual in two respects: it is nine years long (the longest global Plan since the 1964–1973 plan that bears the same name), and it is explicitly divided into two phases — an opening phase of four years (Riḍván 2022 – Riḍván 2026) and a second phase of five years (Riḍván 2026 – Riḍván 2031). The phasing is not merely calendrical. It was introduced to give National Spiritual Assemblies and Regional Bahá’í Councils two horizons against which to project the advancement of the cluster base — a nearer one at Riḍván 2026 and a farther one at Riḍván 2031. Phase 1 has therefore carried a distinctive character: the first full reckoning with the global cluster scheme (22,000+ clusters after boundary re-evaluation), a concerted push to establish at least one third-milestone cluster in every country and region, and a deliberate maturing of the society-building dimensions of grassroots work. Phase 2 will inherit that foundation and is expected to broaden the emphasis from cluster-by-cluster advancement toward the deeper transformation of populations in places where Bahá’í activity has become prevalent.

Narrative spine

The charter text for the entire Plan is the 30 December 2021 message of the Universal House of Justice to the Conference of the Continental Boards of Counsellors. That letter framed the quarter-century as a single long enterprise — “The series of global Plans that began at Riḍván will last a full twenty-ﬁve years. It will carry the ark of the Cause into the third century of the Bahá’í Era and conclude at Riḍván 2046” — and described the Nine Year Plan as its first major undertaking. Crucially, the 30 December 2021 letter itself does not use the word “phase.” It lays out the intensification of the movement of clusters, the expansion of programmes of growth, the deepening of the institute process, and the maturation of Local Assemblies — but it does not partition the nine years into formal stages.

The division into two phases appears for the first time in the Riḍván 2022 message. Reporting on the five months of consultation between the 30 December 2021 letter and Riḍván, the House of Justice wrote:

“We feel it would be helpful, for the purpose of gauging the progress made over time, to view the Plan as unfolding in two phases of four and five years’ duration, and National Assemblies were invited to consider the advances they expect to see in their respective communities by Riḍván 2026 and then by Riḍván 2031.”

That message also reported that the re-evaluation of cluster boundaries had raised the global total to over 22,000 clusters, and that the forecasts returned by National Assemblies projected ~14,000 clusters with a programme of growth by Riḍván 2031, ~11,000 with an intensive programme, and more than 5,000 past the third milestone. The phasing was introduced as an instrument of projection and self-assessment, not as a different kind of Plan.

The phase vocabulary then becomes progressively more explicit. The Riḍván 2025 message — one year before Phase 1 concluded — opens with it: “With but a year remaining before the first phase of the Nine Year Plan concludes, we are eager to report on its progress.” The 31 December 2025 message to the Counsellors’ Conference makes the usage canonical: “The first phase of the Nine Year Plan has cast a light on the Bahá’í community’s considerable strengths,” and, at the close, “This is the surest foundation for the progress that needs to be made in the Plan’s second phase.” The 4 January 2026 letter closing that conference confirms the transition: “At Riḍván, the first phase of the Nine Year Plan will conclude and the second phase will commence.”

The framework is therefore best read as one that was introduced at Riḍván 2022, operated silently through the Riḍván letters of 2023 and 2024, and named aloud as Phase 1 drew to a close.

What is known about Phase 1 is well-documented in the Riḍván messages of 2022–2025 and the 31 December 2025 letter. The Riḍván 2026 message has now opened Phase 2; its emphasis is on the spirit and orientation of the work — the community as protagonist, the way-of-life framing, the widening conversation — rather than on fresh numerical targets, which remain as set at Riḍván 2022.

First phase (2022–2026) — prose analysis

Phase 1 opened against the outbreak of war in Ukraine and the tail of the COVID-19 pandemic, circumstances to which the Riḍván 2022 message responded directly. Nevertheless the phase began with extraordinary energy: the 31 December 2025 letter notes that “the unprecedented energy and determination with which the Plan was launched, at over 10,000 gatherings of Bahá’ís and interested friends, quickly raised awareness of its objectives and special character.” Roughly one and a half million people attended those conferences, most of them not Bahá’ís.

Four priorities of Phase 1 can be read off the Riḍván messages and the 31 December 2025 letter:

1. Finish the global cluster map and raise forecasts against it. The re-evaluation of cluster boundaries raised the world total to over 22,000. Each National Assembly submitted two-horizon forecasts — what it expected to achieve by 2026 and by 2031 — producing an aggregate aspiration of ~14,000 programmes of growth, ~11,000 intensive, and 5,000+ past the third milestone by Plan’s end.

2. At least one third-milestone cluster in every country and region. The 30 December 2021 message made this “one of the Plan’s chief objectives.” The 31 December 2025 letter reports the outcome: “We anticipate that by Riḍván there will be a cluster where the third milestone has been passed in around half of the 160 countries and regions where, at the start of the Plan, still no such cluster existed—a dramatic accomplishment.” This was made possible partly by a coordinated ProgramOfGrowth strategy that drew “an impressive number of individuals.”

3. Intensify existing programmes of growth. The Riḍván 2025 message reports that “in clusters where a programme of growth had already begun, renewed attention has been given to applying, with creativity and ingenuity, those recognized strategies and lines of action that will enable the friends to pass the second and third milestones.” The 31 December 2025 letter describes mature third-milestone clusters as “reservoirs of knowledge and resources to the clusters that surround them” — elevating the third-milestone cluster from an endpoint to an engine for regional learning.

4. Raise the society-building dimensions of grassroots work. Phase 1 saw a multiplication of community-based initiatives of social action, most rapidly in education but also in agriculture, health, environment, the empowerment of women, and the arts. The 31 December 2025 letter observes that in the most advanced settings “the term ‘programme of growth’ no longer does full justice to such developments” — a signal that Phase 2 may need new vocabulary to match a new reality.

Alongside these priorities, Phase 1 included the culmination of the Shrine of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá project, the dedication of the national House of Worship in Port Moresby (May 2024), the announcement of additional local Houses of Worship in Kanchanpur and Mwinilunga and a national House of Worship for Canada, and the centenary of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s passing (November 2021, just before the Plan opened). The second Counsellors’ Conference of the Plan was convened 31 December 2025 – 4 January 2026 at the Bahá’í World Centre.

Phase 2 opens (Riḍván 2026)

The Riḍván 2026 message opens with the explicit pivot — “Now begins the second, greater phase of the Nine Year Plan” — and sets the phase’s character without introducing fresh numerical targets. The 30 December 2021 letter remains the “primary point of reference” (per the 31 December 2025 letter); the Phase 2 numerical horizon was set at Riḍván 2022 (~14,000 / ~11,000 / 5,000+ by Riḍván 2031), and the House does not adjust those figures at the Phase 2 opening.

What the Riḍván 2026 message does introduce is a sharpened framing of how Phase 2 will be carried out:

The community as protagonist. “A signiﬁcant development over the last four years has been the emergence of the community as a more conspicuous protagonist of the Plan, organizing itself in order to meet particular needs and advance particular ﬁelds of endeavour, to provide mutual support through collaborative arrangements, and to learn to be increasingly effective within an evolving framework for action.” The phrase “more conspicuous protagonist” is the most consequential single line of the message — formal House recognition that the community (alongside individuals and institutions, the long-standing three-protagonists frame) has matured to the point of taking initiative independently, while remaining nourished by “the loving encouragement and guidance of the institutions.” This formalises a development the 31 December 2025 letter prepared the ground for in its discussion of “groups of families and households,” of women, of youth, and of educators acting collaboratively without requiring new layers of administrative structure.

Cultivation of a way of life. “The efforts of communities and individuals not simply as the pursuit of programmes and projects, but as the cultivation of a way of life patterned on the divine teachings—a shaping of actions, interactions, and aspirations.” Phase 2 work is to be understood through this re-categorisation: the activities are not ends in themselves but means by which a population learns to live the divine teachings.

Widening conversation. “This same earnest conversation, reﬂecting a commitment to learning, is being advanced across the community from the national and regional levels all the way to the village and neighbourhood, and in a variety of settings, including meetings arranged by the institutions as well as other spaces that are emerging.” The conversation — both formal and informal — is the medium of progress; National Conventions are explicitly called out as one of its venues. The conversation is to be “extended to ever-widening circles of friends, neighbours, and other like-minded souls.”

Newly confirmed believers in the field. “We rejoice in particular to see newly conﬁrmed believers eagerly taking their place in the ﬁeld of service and action alongside those long conﬁrmed in the Faith.” The page reads this as evidence that the institute process is succeeding in bringing new declarants directly into a path of service — though the House does not itself name the institute process here.

Iran as exemplar. The “wronged yet ever-patient, ever-steadfast, ever-resilient followers of Bahá’u’lláh in the Cradle of His Faith” are held up as “a golden example of a community that has long sheltered the ﬂame of hope.” Their disciplined commitment to principle over decades of unrelenting oppression is held up by the message itself as a model for a phase in which “hope becomes a scarce and precious resource” in a darkening world.

None of these framings displaces the 30 December 2021 letter’s society-building aim; together they describe how that aim is to be advanced in the conditions Phase 2 inherits.

A continuation, not a reset. Phase 2 inherits the Phase 1 framework wholesale. The 30 December 2021 letter is reaffirmed as the charter; the framework for action is unchanged; numerical projections from Riḍván 2022 are unchanged; the cluster-by-cluster movement through the three milestones continues. What changes is emphasis: the community as protagonist; the way-of-life framing; the widening conversation; and the deliberate posture of hope in a turbulent world.

The 27 November 2025 letter concerned the institution of Ḥuqúqu’lláh trusteeship. The 31 December 2025 message to the Counsellors’ Conference and the 4 January 2026 letter at the conference’s close are best read as pre-Phase-2 calibration documents; the Riḍván 2026 message formally opens the new phase.

Relationship to earlier Plans

The phasing of the Nine Year Plan is novel in its explicitness but has antecedents. The Four Year Plan (1996–2000) was treated as unitary but inaugurated the training institute. The Five Year Plan (2001–2006) had an implicit internal inflection around the launch of ProgramOfGrowth in 2004–2005. The 2011–2016 and 2016–2021 Plans were each continuous with the one before, pieces of a longer arc that the twenty-five-year framing made visible. None of those Plans was phased in the formal, projection-bearing sense introduced at Riḍván 2022.

Relationship to other frameworks

  • three-milestones — the yardstick against which progress within each phase is gauged; Phase 1 set cluster-movement aspirations against the three milestones, and Phase 2 will inherit those targets.
  • three-dimensions-of-endeavour — expansion-and-consolidation, social action, and discourse participation are expected to rise through the Plan; Phase 1’s messages emphasise that social action and discourse must now mature in step with the process of growth.
  • three-protagonists — the Plan’s phasing presupposes that individual, community, and institutions rise together and that their relationships become more dynamic as the phases progress.
  • Cycle of activity — the three-month rhythm of expansion, consolidation, and reflection remains the unit within which each phase operates; the 31 December 2025 letter insists on “a regular pulse of study, consultation, action, and reﬂection.”
  • four-spaces-of-learning — continue to structure institutional learning within each phase.
