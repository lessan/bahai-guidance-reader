Three Dimensions of Bahá’í Endeavour

Orientation

The Bahá’í community does not, in the current period, conceive of its labour as a single activity — “teaching,” or “community life,” or “service” — but as three interlocking dimensions of one endeavour. The Universal House of Justice articulates these as (1) expansion and consolidation of the community / the programme of growth, (2) social action, and (3) participation in the prevalent discourses of society. They are not competing lanes to be balanced, nor a menu from which clusters select; they are facets of one outward-looking effort to translate Bahá’u’lláh’s Revelation into reality.

The choice of the word dimensions (rather than “activities” or “areas of work”) is deliberate. A dimension is a mode of extension of a single object, not a separable object in itself. A cluster community does not begin with the programme of growth, then graduate to social action, then finally earn entry into discourse. Rather, each dimension is latent in the others from the start and becomes more visible as capacity rises. The House of Justice has been explicit that expansion and consolidation, social action, and discourse contribution “are dimensions of a single, unified, outward-looking endeavour carried out at the grassroots of society” (30 December 2021).

The three are mutually-reinforcing: each draws on and feeds the others. Social action emerges from the human resources and consciousness generated by a mature programme of growth; discourse participation is enriched by concrete field experience from community-building and social action; and community-building itself is animated by the outward orientation, and legitimised in the eyes of surrounding society, by the two other dimensions. Remove any one and the remaining two wither.

Narrative spine

The verbal antecedent of the dimensions lexicon is found in the 2 March 2013 message to the Bahá’ís of Iran. Setting out how the worldwide Bahá’í community contributes to civilization-building, the House of Justice writes: “It sees two dimensions to its contribution. The first is related to its own growth and development, and the second to its involvement in society at large.” The second dimension is then sub-articulated into engagement with social and economic life and with the “discourses prevalent in society” — a tri-articulation in nascent form, 33 months before the 2015 letter would extend it to a coordinate framing.

The explicit articulation comes in the 29 December 2015 message to the Conference of the Continental Boards of Counsellors, launching the 2016–2021 Five Year Plan. Speaking of the gains of two decades of focused effort on entry by troops, the House of Justice observes:

“…the capacity for learning, which represented such a priceless legacy of previous Plans, is being extended beyond the realm of expansion and consolidation to encompass other areas of Bahá’í endeavour, notably social action and participation in the prevalent discourses of society.”

The same message, discussing advanced clusters, names the three explicitly as “dimensions of Bahá’í endeavour” that claim an increasing share of the friends' attention as growth matures. The Riḍván 2016 message then carried this language into the programmatic life of the Plan.

The 30 December 2021 letter launching the Nine Year Plan reiterates and amplifies the framework under the unifying arc of the society-building power of the Faith, stating that the three “are dimensions of a single, unified, outward-looking endeavour carried out at the grassroots of society” — pursued according to “a common framework for action.” Riḍván 2022 carries this frame into the opening cycles of the Nine Year Plan.

The 29 December 2015 message provides concrete content for each row. On expansion and consolidation, the goal set was “to raise the number of clusters where a programme of growth has become intensive to 5,000 by Riḍván 2021.” On social action, the message describes how “a natural outcome of the rise both in resources and in consciousness of the implications of the Revelation for the life of a population is the stirrings of social action,” with forms including “tutorial assistance to children, projects to better the physical environment, and activities to improve health and prevent disease.” On discourse, it observes that as the societal impact of the Faith becomes more evident, the Bahá’í community “is afforded higher standing as a distinctive moral voice in the life of a people and is able to contribute an informed perspective to the discourses around it.”

Why mutually-reinforcing

The House of Justice is emphatic that the three dimensions are not parallel tracks but a single integrated flow of capacity.

Community-building generates the human and moral resources for social action. The 29 December 2015 message traces the origin of social action initiatives directly to the institute process: “In various places the founding of a community school at the grassroots has arisen from a heightened concern for the proper education of children and awareness of its importance, flowing naturally from the study of institute materials.” Social action does not arrive from outside as an NGO-style intervention; it emerges from the junior youth programme, from consultations at reflection meetings, from the consciousness of local realities that expansion and consolidation work cultivates. The R.O.G. 34 special edition on Tanna, Vanuatu — documenting the Bahá’í community's response to Cyclone Pam — makes the mechanism concrete: a believer serving at the Isangel Disaster Management Office observes that “the principles of the Faith, the tools we have learned to use through the process of expansion and consolidation, and our familiarity with Tanna have been helping the relief process a lot,” naming the specific capacities transferred — reading reality, collecting statistics, planning, consulting, reflecting. Without a mature programme of growth, the relief effort would have had neither the personnel nor the habits of mind to act coherently.

Social action and community-building feed discourse. The 30 December 2021 letter makes the logical chain explicit: “closer association with a population, brought about through the work of expansion and consolidation, leads to increased consciousness of an area's prevailing social problems, as well as of the aspirations of its people to overcome them. As the number of those participating in community-building activities rises, so does the need for the Bahá’í community to offer, as a unified body, its considered perspective on obstacles to social progress.” Discourse contribution without grassroots grounding becomes abstract theorising; with grounding, it becomes the articulate voice of a community that has actually tried what it proposes.

Discourse and social action deepen community-building. The outward-facing dimensions prevent the community from collapsing inward. The 30 December 2021 letter describes how contact with wider social realities “draws” the community “to respond to such realities as its circumstances permit.” This outward orientation is not an optional embellishment — it is what keeps the community-building enterprise from becoming self-referential. Engagement with prevailing discourses likewise keeps the community's self-understanding tested against the thought of the age, rather than retreating into insularity.

One framework, three expressions. The 30 December 2021 letter emphasises that “all these efforts are pursued according to a common framework for action, and this above all else brings coherence to the overall pattern of activity.” The three dimensions are not harmonised by negotiation between separate domains; they are coherent because they express one vision of religion as a force for civilisation-building. Remove the common framework and the dimensions fragment into the sectarian categories — proselytism, charity, public affairs — that they explicitly transcend.

Evolution

  • Pre-1983 — implicit. The three dimensions live implicitly across different institutional lanes: Bahá’í Writings on service to humanity and the betterment of society; Shoghi Effendi's framing of the Faith as embryo of a new world civilisation; BIC work at the UN since 1948.
  • 20 October 1983 — establishment of OSED by the Universal House of Justice, signalling institutional recognition of social action as a distinct domain of Bahá’í endeavour. The establishment letter grounds the move in the Writings: “the concept of social and economic development is enshrined in the sacred Teachings of our Faith.” Social action is henceforth coordinated, studied, and learned from systematically.
  • October 1985 — The Promise of World Peace. A statement of the Universal House of Justice transmitted via the Bahá’í International Community to heads of state, religious leaders, and the United Nations. This is the first major Tier C document framed as a public-discourse contribution — opening the genre that the third dimension will later name.
  • March 1995 — The Prosperity of Humankind. Prepared at the Bahá’í World Centre under UHJ direction and issued through the BIC for the UN World Summit for Social Development; the foundational external statement on social and economic development. One Common Faith (2005), commissioned by the House of Justice, extends the same register into the discourse on religion. Together the three documents establish, well before 2015, a 30-year arc of Bahá’í participation in the prevalent discourses of society.
  • 1996–2010 — integration begins. Successive Five Year Plans develop the institute process; the Bahá’í community's involvement in the life of society becomes a more direct focus.
  • 28 December 2010 — bi-articulation. The message to the Counsellors' Conference names “two interconnected, mutually reinforcing areas of activity: involvement in social action and participation in the prevalent discourses of society” — the bi-articulation that the 29 December 2015 letter would extend to a tri-articulation by promoting expansion-and-consolidation alongside as the third coordinate dimension. The 2010 framing is sequenced (“once human resources are in sufficient abundance, the community's engagement with society can, and indeed must, increase”); the 2021 framing is coordinate. The connecting concept is scale-recursion — at the cluster's nascent stage, sequencing holds; at maturity, the three run as coordinate dimensions of one endeavour.
  • 26 November 2012 — OSED Social Action paper. The Department of the Secretariat transmits OSED's Social Action — the framework document for Bahá’í social action.
  • 2 March 2013 — two dimensions lexicon. The message to the Bahá’ís of Iran names “two dimensions” of the Bahá’í community's contribution to civilization-building — own growth and development, and involvement in society at large — the direct verbal antecedent to the 2015/2021 dimensions lexicon.
  • 29 December 2015 — explicit articulation. The three dimensions are named together as extensions of one capacity for learning — the programmatic frame is born.
  • 1 March 2018 — institutional consolidation. OSED is restructured as the Bahá’í International Development Organization (BIDO), reflecting the scale of the social action enterprise.
  • 30 December 2021 / Riḍván 2022 — society-building frame. The Nine Year Plan unifies all three dimensions under the arc of the release of the society-building power of the Faith, presenting them as facets of one outward-looking endeavour.

Relationship to other frameworks

  • ThreeProtagonists (individual, community, institutions) describe who acts; the three dimensions describe what is done. The three protagonists act across all three dimensions.
  • ThreeMilestones trace the development of a programme of growth within Dimension 1; social action and discourse become more prominent as clusters pass the third milestone.
  • ProgramOfGrowth is the home of Dimension 1. Dimensions 2 and 3 grow out of a mature programme of growth in most cluster settings, though they can also be entered through individual initiative.
  • FourSpacesOfLearning operate largely inside Dimension 1 (the institute process and its coordination), but increasingly shape Dimensions 2 and 3 as the learning mode extends.
  • FrameworkForAction is the over-arching term for the common framework by which all three dimensions are pursued coherently.

Growth of Dimension 3 (discourse):

  • BIC: from a single office at the UN in New York to additional offices in Geneva, Brussels, Addis Ababa, and Jakarta (Africa, Asia, Europe) over the same period.
  • National Offices of External Affairs: a “vastly augmented, greatly fortified network” (30 December 2021) for whom discourse engagement became the principal focus.
  • ISGP undergraduate and graduate seminars now serve Bahá’í youth from “well over 100 countries” (30 December 2021).

Growth of Dimension 1 (programme of growth):

  • Clusters with a programme of growth: target of 5,000 by Riḍván 2016 “looks set to be achieved” (29 December 2015).
  • Completions of at least the first book of the institute sequence worldwide by end of 2015: “half a million individuals” (29 December 2015).
  • Youth participating in the 2013 conferences and follow-on gatherings by end of 2015: “more than 80,000” plus “an additional cohort of over 100,000” (29 December 2015).
