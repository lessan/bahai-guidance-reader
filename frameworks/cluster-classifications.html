Cluster Classifications

Orientation

Since the cluster was introduced as the basic unit of planning in the 9 January 2001 message to the Conference of the Continental Boards of Counsellors, the Universal House of Justice has used successive classification schemes to describe where each cluster stands and what the work of the moment is. The schemes are diagnostic rather than taxonomic: each one answers a question of the form “where is this cluster now, and what does it need next?” As the question has changed, so has the scheme.

The first scheme — four strength-based categories (in field shorthand, A/B/C/D) — was in force from 2001 to roughly 2010. It asked a readiness question: is this cluster yet in a condition to launch an intensive programme of growth? The 28 December 2010 message retired that scheme and replaced it with the milestone continuum documented in Three Milestones of Cluster Development, which asks a developmental question: what phase of an unfolding programme of growth has this cluster reached? From the 30 December 2021 message onward, a further conceptual layer — the language of society-building — has been laid over the milestones. This is not a new classification but a qualitative frame for what happens beyond the third milestone, when the Faith’s community-building activities begin to engage a whole population.

The three schemes are layered rather than alternatives. A cluster today is still a locality in a particular country; it still belongs somewhere on the strength spectrum the 2001 letter first named; but what is actually reported is its milestone and, for the most advanced clusters, the degree to which society-building has become visible. Reading the classification history is one way of reading the maturation of the Plans themselves.

Narrative spine

Two anchor texts structure the story. The first is Turning Point, the compilation of messages from the Universal House of Justice, the International Teaching Centre and the Continental Boards of Counsellors surrounding the 9 January 2001 letter to the Counsellors’ Conference. Turning Point is where the cluster concept is introduced and where the four strength-based categories first appear (originally numbered, in the 9 January letter itself, as four “categories of development” that areas within a region will fall into — the A/B/C/D letters are field shorthand that emerged in the years following). The 17 January 2003 message, also collected in Turning Point, is the first systematic report-back on the scheme: by that date Bahá’ís worldwide had divided their territories into roughly 17,000 clusters, and the House of Justice was already clarifying that “to assign a cluster to one or another category is not to make a statement about status. Rather, it is a way of evaluating its capacity for growth.”

The second anchor text is the 28 December 2010 message to the Counsellors’ Conference — the pivot. After summarising the insights of the 2006–2011 Plan, the House of Justice wrote that “a decade ago, when we introduced the concept of a cluster — a geographic construct intended to facilitate thinking about the growth of the Faith — we provided an outline of four broad stages along the path of its development. As the Bahá’í community set about implementing the provisions of the Plan, this outline proved immensely useful in giving shape and definition to what is essentially an ongoing process. The abundant experience which has since accrued enables the believers now to conceive of the movement of a population, propelled by mounting spiritual forces, in terms of a rich and dynamic continuum.” The letter then names the first two milestones. The 29 December 2015 message adds the third.

The 30 December 2021 message, launching the Nine Year Plan, does not introduce a new classification. Instead it frames the existing milestones — especially the third and beyond — in terms of the “release of the society-building power of the Faith.” The three milestones are treated as “already well known to the friends from our previous messages and from their own experience”; what the new Plan adds is the qualitative language for clusters where a pattern of community life has begun to embrace a whole population.

The A/B/C/D scheme (detailed)

The four categories were set out in the 9 January 2001 letter as a diagnostic frame for the first Five Year Plan (2001–2006). They were presented in the letter from weakest to strongest — areas not yet open to the Faith; areas with a few isolated localities and groups; areas where established communities were gaining strength through a vigorous institute process; areas where “strong communities of deepened believers” were in a position to take on systematic and accelerated expansion and consolidation. In field usage, this ordering was commonly reversed (A for the strongest, D for the weakest); the analytical content is identical. The scheme remained in force for roughly a decade.

The Milestone scheme (summary — see `three-milestones.md` for full)

The 28 December 2010 message replaced the four categories with a continuum of milestones, reflecting a shift from tracking the strength of a community to tracking the movement of a population. The detailed development is on the sibling page Three Milestones of Cluster Development; a compact summary and rough mapping follows. The equivalences in the right-hand column are approximate and pedagogical; the 28 December 2010 message explicitly framed the move from stages to continuum as a different kind of question, not a re-labelling of the old buckets.

Why the shift from A/B/C/D to milestones

The change of scheme registers four converging shifts in what the House of Justice was tracking.

First, from pre-launch readiness to internal development. A/B/C/D was an ordinal scale of readiness — the implicit zero being a cluster without Bahá’í activity and the implicit target being a cluster ready to launch an IPG. Its categories were mostly distinctions along the path to an intensive programme of growth. By 2010, many clusters had crossed that threshold; the scheme’s own terminal state (A) had become crowded and increasingly heterogeneous. The question “is this cluster ready to launch an IPG?” was no longer the frontier. The milestone framework asks instead “what has this cluster achieved inside an unfolding programme of growth?” — a question about internal development, not pre-launch status.

Second, from readiness scoring to learning-stage articulation. The 17 January 2003 message had already gestured at the limits of the older scheme when it insisted that category assignment was “not a statement about status” but “a way of evaluating its capacity for growth.” By 2010 that gesture had become a principle. The milestone framework identifies qualitative thresholds that are read by cluster agencies, not awarded by higher institutions: a milestone is a passage visible from the ground, not a grade assigned from above. The 28 December 2010 message frames this as the Bahá’í community now being able to “conceive of the movement of a population, propelled by mounting spiritual forces, in terms of a rich and dynamic continuum” rather than a ladder of stages.

Third, from inward cluster questions to outward engagement questions. The four categories looked inward — at the internal strength of the Bahá’í community within a geographic area. As clusters matured through the 2006–2011 Plan, the defining work ceased to be about the Bahá’ís’ own consolidation and began to be about their engagement with the wider population. The second milestone already implies this (an IPG presupposes a flow of seekers into the institute); the third milestone makes it central, by naming “dynamic pattern of community life” engaging “a people” as the operative condition.

Fourth, from a snapshot scheme to a continuum. The 28 December 2010 message is explicit that the milestones describe points along a continuum, not a typology. Clusters move through them at their own pace; they may plateau between them; they may recede. The older scheme — while always intended as a planning aid, not a judgement — lent itself to being read as a set of bins. The milestone language makes the continuous, organic character of the movement audible in the vocabulary itself.

The shift also had a practical corollary: goal-setting in successive Plans could now be stated in milestone language directly. The 2010 letter’s target — 5,000 clusters with a programme of growth at any level by Riḍván 2016 — and the 2015 letter’s target — 5,000 with an intensive programme — and the 2021 letter’s targets for each milestone, all draw their precision from the new framework.

Emerging clusters — a parallel taxonomy

Running beneath the main classifications is a quieter, more field-level taxonomy that describes how clusters cross the first milestone in the first place. R.O.G. Issue 32 (Strategies for Growth in Emerging Clusters, August 2014) groups emerging-cluster stories by the pattern of their opening: clusters catalysed by a “mobile tutor” or travelling teacher dispatched from a stronger cluster (Cinkassé, Togo); clusters in which a homefront pioneer worked with local youth to move from no activity to the first milestone in a few months (Sullana, Peru); clusters rooted in an existing believer family or inherited pioneer residence (Cosenza, Italy; North East Victoria, Australia); clusters sustained by cross-cluster reciprocity and visiting teams from neighbouring clusters (Uashat, Quebec, with Montreal support; Kharagpur → Maharajganj; Mbuji-Mayi → Tshilenge). This is not a formal UHJ classification but a field vocabulary for how emerging clusters begin; it is consistent with, and sits underneath, the first milestone in the milestone scheme.

Society-building framing (2021–)

From the 30 December 2021 message onward, the House of Justice has framed the most advanced clusters in a new qualitative register. The phrase “society-building power” itself originates in Shoghi Effendi’s writings on the unfolding of the Administrative Order; the 30 December 2021 message draws it back into use as the qualitative register for the most advanced milestone clusters. This is not a fourth classification, but a lens applied on top of the milestones. Clusters to which the frame applies show characteristics such as:

  • Whole populations engaging. “In the most advanced clusters … within particular neighbourhoods and villages where intense activity is under way, a significant proportion of the population is engaged” (30 Dec 2021). The Bahá’í community “has become a highly visible protagonist in society in its own right.”
  • Spiritual and material civilisation growing together. Activities no longer fall neatly under “expansion” and “consolidation”; they include education of children and junior youth across whole generations, social and economic development efforts, and Bahá’í engagement with prevalent discourses. The 31 December 2025 message observes that in such clusters “the term ‘programme of growth’ no longer does full justice to such developments.”
  • Devotional character, education, and outward service visible at neighbourhood scale. Neighbourhoods and villages, not the cluster as a whole, become the operative units of observation.
  • Self-perpetuating community development. The 31 December 2025 message describes communities whose growth and development has become “self-perpetuating … without the need for new layers of administrative structure”, with collaborative arrangements arising among families, youth, women, educators and others.

The 30 December 2021 message sets the diagnostic frontier: “at least one third-milestone cluster in every country and every region” during the Nine Year Plan, each acting as a “reservoir” of knowledge and human resources for surrounding clusters.
