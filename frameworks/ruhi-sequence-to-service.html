Ruhi Sequence of Courses to Acts of Service

Orientation

The Ruhi sequence is the principal curriculum of the more than three hundred training institutes that operate worldwide under the guidance of the Universal House of Justice. It is a numbered set of books — the main sequence — published by the Ruhi Institute of Colombia, each designed so that the completion of a book corresponds to the acquisition of the capacity needed to perform a specific act of service in the life of the community. The sequence is not a reading course, and it is not a catechism. Each book is a unit of formation for service: study and action are interwoven so that, by the time a participant closes a book, he or she is expected to have hosted a devotional meeting, made home visits, tutored children, animated a junior-youth group, or accompanied new tutors in the study circles that are themselves the principal venue for the sequence.

The Universal House of Justice’s 12 December 2011 message to all National Spiritual Assemblies names the sequence’s organising principle directly: “developing capacity to serve the Cause and humanity in a process likened to walking a path of service. This conception shapes both content and structure.” The House draws out the figure: “a path invites participation, it beckons to new horizons, it demands effort and movement, it accommodates different paces and strides, it is structured and defined.” And, decisively, “one walks the path in the company of others.” The Ruhi Institute’s own framing in the introduction to Book 1 — “the courses of the Ruhi Institute trace a path of service to humanity, upon which we each walk at our own pace, assisting and being assisted by others” — is the curriculum’s enactment of that UHJ-tier formulation, not its source. The sequence operationalises what Shoghi Effendi had called the indivisibility of the spiritual and administrative: every book joins inner transformation to an outward practice, and every practice is lodged in a space — the devotional meeting, the children’s class, the junior-youth group, the study circle — that the same sequence also teaches participants to build. The four core activities are, from one angle, the output of the sequence; from another they are the laboratory in which the sequence is lived.

For the general logic of training institutes, see InstituteProcess (saturated, Wave 1). The present page maps which book generates which act of service, which space it animates, and which protagonist-role it produces.

Narrative spine

The sequence originates not in a curriculum committee but in the consolidation crisis of the Colombian Bahá’í community in the early 1970s. After a period of mass teaching, many newly-formed communities were dissolving because they depended on visiting travelling teachers. Under the leadership of Farzam Arbab — later a member of the Universal House of Justice — and his colleagues at what would become the Ruhi Institute (formally established 1976 in Puerto Tejada, named for the deceased scholar Ruhí Arbáb), deepening booklets were developed so that local believers could form their own capacity. The parallel experience of FUNDAEC in Norte del Cauca, whose tutorial-learning philosophy permeated the emerging materials, supplied the pedagogical grammar: accompaniment, learning-in-action, small groups of five to fifteen, and low-cost reproducibility adapted to rural, post-colonial conditions.

The Universal House of Justice, in the 26 December 1995 message launching the Four Year Plan, named the “institute process” as a strategic category and called for “permanent institutes designed to provide well-organized, formally conducted programs of training on a regular schedule.” The Riḍván 1996 message turned the Plan’s first year explicitly to the building of training institutes as the Plan’s distinctive instrument; National Spiritual Assemblies were encouraged to develop their own materials. The 17 January 2003 message to the Counsellors then refined the approach, naming the training institute as the engine of human-resource development for the cluster and calling for the systematic integration of children’s classes, junior-youth groups, and study circles into a single educational process. By the close of the Twelve Month Plan (2000–2001) it was clear the Ruhi materials were filling the role most coherently. The 9 January 2001 message made the turn explicit: “The value of a sequence of courses, each one following the other in a logical pattern and each one building on the achievements of the previous ones, has become abundantly clear.” The 28 December 2005 message completed the universalisation, committing the Bahá‘í world for the next period to the Ruhi curriculum. By 2004 most NSAs had already adopted it in practice; 2005 made it canonical. On 1 January 2022 the House announced that the Ruhi Institute aims to complete its full fourteen-book curriculum by 2031, after which no further materials are planned — framing the sequence as a bounded, finite architecture rather than an open-ended publishing programme. The release of Book 8, The Covenant of Bahá’u’lláh — in pre-publication through the early 2020s and published in a first edition (1.1.1.PE) in March 2026 — was a major milestone: every participant of the main sequence now encounters the Covenant directly, as part of the same path of service.

A caution on Book 4. It generates no distinct act of service and is sometimes read as a gap in the service-formation logic. This reading misses the structural role: Book 4 is the historical-theological anchor of the whole sequence. Without its grounding in the lives of the Báb and Bahá’u’lláh, the service-forming books that precede and follow it would float free of their doctrinal source. It is also the last book in the sequence where the twofold moral purpose of the path — “attend to one’s own spiritual and intellectual growth and … contribute to the transformation of society” (Book 1 tutor introduction) — is thematised apart from any specific service role; what Books 1–3 and 5–7 prepare in practice, Book 4 reinforces in the Word.

A caution on Books 3 and 5. Each generates not a single text but a branch curriculum.

The distinction in row three is often blurred in meeting conversation but matters structurally. Book 5 forms the animator; the JY texts are the curriculum the animator then runs with the group. Confusing the two leads to a frequent pastoral confusion — adults studying JY texts in a study circle, as if they were Ruhi books — that R.O.G. 30 and 31 identify as a sign of a malformed programme.

Design principles

Six principles are visible in every book and repeatedly affirmed by the institute’s self-documentation and by UHJ messages.

Learning-in-action. Each book interleaves study passages with practice exercises that require service between sessions, not only at them. The Book 1 tutor introduction, quoted by the House of Justice in Riḍván 2010, explains that progress along the sequence “entails the development of a number of capabilities that require understanding and knowledge, spiritual qualities and praiseworthy attitudes, as well as a host of abilities and skills.” The test of a study circle’s health, R.O.G. 27 and 35 insist, is whether participants are in service between sessions.

The tutor as collaborator, not teacher-expert. Book 1’s introduction is explicit: “The relationship between the tutor and the other participants is not one of teacher-to-student; all are consciously engaged in a process in which everyone is seeking to learn. But the tutor is not a detached and passive facilitator of discussion either.” Having completed more books and undertaken more acts of service, the tutor assists the group in achieving the purpose of each unit — but without asymmetry of spiritual station. This is the single sharpest departure from the seminary and the lecture-hall. It is also the institute’s animating end: the 28 December 2010 and 12 December 2011 messages frame the sequence as a process of raising up thoughtful, creative protagonists of the Plan, equipped with skills and qualities that make universal participation possible. Paul Lample — in Revelation and Social Reality — draws this out: the institute is “not a conventional school” but a centre whose aim is to raise up “thoughtful, creative protagonists of the progress of the Faith.” The phrase tracks the House’s language; Lample’s gloss is the Tier-D analytical elaboration of a UHJ-tier formulation.

The youth-protagonist arc. The sequence’s most concentrated mobilisation comes through young people. The 1 July 2013 message to the participants in the 114 youth conferences names this directly: “To follow a path of service, whatever form one’s activity assumes, requires faith and tenacity. In this connection, the benefit of walking that path in the company of others is immense.” The House observes that young people are “gaining the most experience at aiding junior youth, and children too, with their moral and spiritual development” — the youth carry the human-resource load of Books 3 and 5 in the field. The 1 July 2013 letter is the most-cited UHJ message in Ruhi Book 5 and is the operative UHJ-tier framing of the youth’s role in the path of service that Books 5–7 prepare for.

The small-group format. Study circles are typically five to fifteen participants, meeting in a home or simple setting. The form resists institutional capture and is reproducible without capital. “The study circle, the tutor and the curriculum of the Ruhi Institute” — Riḍván 2010’s formula — are the three irreducible elements of the system. No building, no staff, no budget are required to start one.

Rural-adapted origin, universalisable form. The sequence was formed in the villages of Norte del Cauca, Colombia, and its materials reflect that origin: concrete language, exercises that assume rural or small-town conditions, an insistence that the physical environment of study is unimportant. The same form has proved directly usable in the slums of Dhaka, the neighbourhoods of Phoenix, the post-cyclone villages of Tanna, and the urban parishes of Paris. The pattern is frontloaded in Michael Karlberg’s sense: the curriculum’s coherence is held at the global level precisely so that local adaptation — of examples, applications, language — can happen without loss of shared learning.

Translation strategy. The materials have been translated into well over one hundred languages. Translation is not treated as localisation of a foreign text but as inherent to the form: because the sequence is organised around practices rather than concepts, what must travel is the practice-grammar, which does travel. The Persian, Arabic, French, Portuguese, Hindi, Swahili, and dozens of other editions are held alongside the English as peers.

Historical evolution

  • Early 1970s. Deepening booklets developed by the Colombian Bahá’í community, in response to the consolidation crisis after mass teaching.
  • 1976. Ruhi Institute formally established in Puerto Tejada, Colombia. Cornerstone laid by Hand of the Cause Raḥmatu’lláh Muhájir.
  • 1980s. First course sequence developed; decentralisation to rural villages of Norte del Cauca; FUNDAEC-style tutorial pedagogy absorbed.
  • 1985. Nine courses published in Spanish, English, and French; UHJ identifies Ruhi materials as a major factor in Central American consolidation at the close of the Seven Year Plan.
  • 1994–1996. First published editions of Books 1–4.
  • 1996. Four Year Plan adopts the training-institute framework globally (26 December 1995 and Riḍván 1996 messages); Ruhi materials are a principal option.
  • 2000–2001. Books 6 and 7 published; sequence approaches its operational form.
  • 2005. UHJ formally designates Ruhi as the main sequence of courses for training institutes worldwide (28 December 2005 message).
  • 2006. Book 5 Releasing the Powers of Junior Youth published; the Junior Youth Spiritual Empowerment Programme is formally integrated.
  • 2010. Riḍván message names the institute “a system of distance education” whose elements are “the study circle, the tutor and the curriculum of the Ruhi Institute.”
  • 2011. 12 December message distinguishes the three stages of the educational process (children / JY / youth and adult), each tied to a branch of the sequence.
  • 2022. UHJ announces full-curriculum completion target of 2031; Book 8 The Covenant of Bahá’u’lláh in pre-publication use.
  • March 2026. Book 8 published in first edition (1.1.1.PE), over 500 pages in three units. The Ruhi Institute, in its own internal framing, describes Book 8 as “surpassing in significance every other course in the sequence” — a curriculum self-description rather than a UHJ judgement.

Global scale

The quantitative arc, drawn from UHJ messages and the InternationalTeachingCentre’s Reflections on Growth corpus:

  • 1999. 344 training institutes established worldwide, nearly 100,000 participants (26 November 1999 message).
  • 2010. Riḍván message reports achievement of the 1,500-intensive-programme-of-growth target a year early; the shift in emphasis from count to “developments at the more profound level of culture.”
  • Mid-2010s onwards. Institute seminars reported drawing from “over 500 of the most advanced clusters” (R.O.G. 27) to raise the quality, not only the quantity, of the educational process at the level of the study circle.

The programme is, by any straightforward measure, the largest Bahá’í-organised educational effort in the history of the Faith. Its quantitative scale is inseparable from its qualitative character: the scale is possible only because the form is reproducible without capital, and the form is sustained only because tutors formed by Book 7 multiply from within the communities they serve.

Connections to other frameworks

  • Four spaces of learning. Books 1, 3, 5, and 7 each generate the human resource for one of the four spaces — host, teacher, animator, tutor. The sequence is the mechanism by which the spaces are populated; the spaces are the venues in which the sequence’s fruits are realised.
  • Three protagonists. The sequence forms the individual; its fruits serve the community and are coordinated by institutions. Each book is a moment in which the three converge — an individual studying, a community accompanying, an institution (the LSA, the ATC, the training institute itself) coordinating.
  • Three milestones. Book-completion rates are a cluster-health indicator: the rate at which Book 7 completions produce actively-tutoring tutors is, per R.O.G. 8, 20, and 26, the single most important metric of institute health and a proxy for where a cluster sits on the milestone continuum.
  • Three dimensions of endeavour. Books 1–8 serve Dimension 1 (expansion and consolidation); Books 11–14 are explicitly designed for Dimensions 2 (social action) and 3 (participation in prevalent discourses). The sequence’s fourteen-book architecture, when complete, maps onto the three-dimensions framework of the 30 December 2021 message.
