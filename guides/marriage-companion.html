Your Marriage — A Daily Companion

You studied Book 12 Unit 1 in a circle. The fortress metaphor made sense then. This guide is for living the marriage you already have — not preparing to marry, not writing an article. Reach for it when you’re irritated, distant, or wondering what the Writings actually mean in the kitchen at 9pm.

Book 12 Unit 1 is the backbone. Section numbers like (Bk12 §16) point back to the unit. A few practical ideas from relationship research are included where they fit without contradicting the teachings.

This is for deepening a good marriage and navigating normal hard parts — not crisis intervention. Book 12 is honest: harmony is built, often through struggle.

When you’ve got thirty seconds

  1. Marriage is a fortress — something you build and defend, not a feeling you fall into. (Bk12 §3)
  2. Two helpmates, one soul — but God stays above both of you. Your highest attachment is to Him. (Bk12 §9)
  3. Love grows. Real love is a rock of strength, not a flame that burns out. Forget soulmate/completes-me mythology. (Bk12 §6–7)
  4. The enemies are mostly internal — possessiveness, jealousy, the “what about my needs?” reflex. (Bk12 §8)
  5. Consultation is the daily engine — kindly tongue, real listening, own your faults first, resolve before it festers. (Bk12 §16)
  6. The home radiates light — devotional life, joy, hospitality, protected time together. (Bk12 §10)
  7. The centre: “We will all, verily, abide by the Will of God.” (Bk12 §22)

Seven lenses

1. Fortress, not fairy tale

“He established the law of marriage, made it as a fortress for well-being and salvation.” — Bahá’u’lláh (Bk12 §3)

Marriage is a divine institution — highly encouraged, but not the central purpose of life (knowing and loving God is). It’s a fortress for living that purpose together: stability, mutual support, raising children who will mention God’s name.

Practice: When romance fades in a hard week, remember — you’re maintaining a fortress, not chasing a mood.

2. True love vs. the counterfeit

“Real love is impossible unless one turn his face towards God.” — ‘Abdu’l-Bahá (Bk12 §7)

The love between you is a reflection of God’s love — it deepens over time; it is “a great rock of strength.” What culture sells — falling in love, soulmates, love-is-blind, love-is-all-you-need — leads to disillusionment. True love is not a flame lighted only to burn out.

Practice: Tend the marriage like a garden, not a bonfire. Small consistent acts > grand gestures.

3. Possessiveness is not love

“Love is unlimited, boundless, infinite… The perfect love needs an unselfish instrument.” — ‘Abdu’l-Bahá (Bk12 §8)

Commitment is mutual attachment of mind and heart — not ownership. “If he really loved me, he’d do what I ask” is self-gratification dressed as love. Jealousy consumeth the body — avoid it as a lion. (Bk12 §8)

Practice: When you feel possessive, ask: am I seeking their good or my comfort? Support doesn’t mean compliance.

4. Loving consultation

“One of the keys to the strengthening of unity is loving consultation.” — UHJ (Bk12 §16)

The pattern you establish in courtship carries into marriage. Features of healthy communication:

  • Kindly tongue; refrain from finding faults
  • Frank and honest, not careless
  • Listen with the “spiritual ear” — try to understand before rebutting
  • Detachment from being right; generosity of spirit
  • Resolve differences quickly — don’t let them harden into grudges
  • Remember your own faults before speaking about theirs

Practice: Soft start-up when raising a concern: “I feel ___ about ___. I need ___.” Not “you always / you never.”

5. Two helpmates

“That two people should live their lives in love and harmony is of far greater importance than their comfort.” — ‘Abdu’l-Bahá (Bk12 §9)

You are intimate friends concerned about each other’s welfare — even as a single soul. Yet each answers to God individually. Equality of men and women means shared decision-making through consultation, not dominance by either partner. (Bk12 §13–15)

Practice: Major decisions get consultation. Daily life gets mutual noticing — who carried what load today?

6. The nest that radiates light

“Blessed is the house… wherein My remembrance is celebrated.” — Bahá’u’lláh (Bk12 §10)

A Bahá’í home is orderly, joyful, a center where divine light shines out — through prayer, hospitality, conversations that reflect truth, service undertaken together. Two birds warbling on the branch of fellowship. (Bk12 §10)

Practice: Protect one weekly couple ritual. Host others sometimes. Pray together even briefly.

7. Abide by the Will of God

The marriage vow: “We will all, verily, abide by the Will of God.” (Bk12 §22)

What unites you transcends the material world. When ego, circumstance, and pain pull you apart, this orientation holds: place God’s will above your own, merge your will in His, be content with what He destines.

Practice: When stuck, pray separately or together for alignment with God’s will — not for your preferred outcome.

Friction moments playbook

“You never / you always…”

Remember: Kindly tongue; complain about the issue, not the person’s character. (Bk12 §16)

Try: Pause. “I feel overwhelmed about ___. Can we start over?” Own your fault first if you contributed.

Feeling unappreciated

Remember: Love gives before it counts. Generosity of spirit. (Bk12 §8)

Try: Name one specific thing she did today — first. Appreciation often multiplies when one person starts.

Jealousy or suspicion

Remember: “Jealousy consumeth the body… avoid these two as you would a lion.” (Bk12 §8)

Try: Name it internally; don’t accuse. Ask yourself if you’re placing her in unnecessary situations. Seek a tranquil heart.

Chore / fairness fight

Remember: Justice isn’t scorekeeping. Consult as a team. (Bk12 §13, §16)

Try: “Let’s look at the whole week, not just tonight. What would fair look like to both of us?”

Parenting disagreement

Remember: Unity in front of children; consult privately. You are one body raising gems. (Bk12 §14; Unit 2)

Try: “Let’s align before we respond. What do we both want them to learn from this?”

Money tension

Remember: Coherent life — economic choices match spiritual ideals. (Bk12 §17)

Try: Scheduled money consultation, not ambush arguments. Huqúq, Fund, family needs, service — in that conversation.

Drifting / roommates phase

Remember: Love deepens by tending, not by waiting for feeling to return. (Bk12 §6)

Try: One protected date. One explicit appreciation. One shared act of service.

Late-night argument spiraling

Remember: Resolve before it hardens; moderation and patience in speech. (Bk12 §16)

Try: “I love you. We’re tired. Can we pause and return tomorrow?” Then actually return.

Prayers to say together

  • Short Obligatory Prayer (habit builds the nest)
  • “Blessed is the house that hath attained unto My tender mercy, wherein My remembrance is celebrated…” — Bahá’u’lláh (Bk12 §10)
  • Marriage verse: “We will all, verily, abide by the Will of God.” — repeat when making decisions (Bk12 §22)
  • ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s prayer for unity and harmony in the home

Questions for consultation night

  • What went well for us this month?
  • Where did we miss each other?
  • What load is heavier on one of us right now?
  • One decision we need to align on?
  • How are we doing on couple time, prayer, service?

Coherence — one life, not competing lives

Book 5’s wholeness of reality applies directly to marriage. (Bk12 §17)

The material and spiritual can’t be at war in your household. Guard against values that seep in from materialism: admiration for power, adoration of status, love of luxury, obsession with self-gratification. Merit lies in service and virtue, not pageantry of wealth. Work in a spirit of service is a form of worship.

Watch for:

  • Service load falling unevenly and unconsulted
  • Career/status decisions made solo
  • Luxury choices that contradict stated values
  • Letting community service displace family attention without consultation (or the reverse)

Practice: Major choices get the question: does this cohere with what we say we believe?

Weathering hard seasons

“Building a strong, united marriage requires persistence, effort, and the overcoming of many difficulties together.” — UHJ (Bk12 §11)

Harmony is built — often through struggle. A hard season doesn’t mean you chose wrong. Leave no stone unturned in seeking harmony, but know that some tests are long.

When it’s hard:

  • Increase prayer, not argument
  • Consult with a trusted couple or Assembly if needed (not public complaint)
  • Look for your own faults first — always (Bk12 §16)
  • Instant forgiveness; affirm praiseworthy qualities (Bk12 §12)
  • Don’t compare your marriage to others’ highlight reels

What you model for your kids

They learn marriage from watching yours.

  • Do you consult or dominate?
  • Do you speak kindly or criticize?
  • Do you repair or stew?
  • Do you pray together?
  • Do you serve together?
  • Do you show affection and unity?

“The family unit provides an environment within which lofty and world-embracing principles can be taught and nurtured.” — UHJ (Bk12 §4; Unit 2 §4)

A note on outside material

Not scholarship — practical fit only:

  • “Love languages” — useful vocabulary if it helps you notice each other; not a replacement for consultation or spiritual orientation.
