Cluster Development: A Synthesis for Meeting Preparation

A working synthesis of how the Universal House of Justice describes the development of a Cluster — written as a guide for cluster reflection meetings, cluster-agency consultations, and LSA planning. For the saturated treatments, see the sibling pages Three Milestones of Cluster Development and Cluster Classifications and the concept page Cluster §3.

The framework rests on three messages from the Universal House of Justice to the Conference of the Continental Boards of Counsellors: 28 December 2010 (introducing the first and second milestones and explicitly retiring the prior four-stage classification), 29 December 2015 (adding the third milestone), and 30 December 2021 (treating the three milestones as established vocabulary, setting numerical goals keyed to each, and framing the most advanced clusters in terms of “the society-building power of the Faith”). The Nine Year Plan launch message of 30 December 2021 is the operative reference for current planning; the 28 December 2010 and 29 December 2015 messages remain the doctrinal anchors of the milestone vocabulary itself.

What is a cluster?

A cluster is a bounded geographic area — a grouping of villages and towns, or a city and its suburbs — whose boundaries are drawn by “culture, language, patterns of transport, infrastructure, and the social and economic life of the inhabitants” (Universal House of Justice, 9 January 2001). It is not a civil division and not a statement about the strength of the Bahá’í community within it; it is a unit of planning and action sized so that the institute process, the programme of growth, and the core activities can be coordinated as one work.

After the re-clustering exercise undertaken at the start of the Nine Year Plan, the Riḍván 2022 message reported that “the total number of clusters in the world has risen by a quarter and now stands at over 22,000.” The Plan’s headline objective is to establish a programme of growth in every one of those clusters by Riḍván 2031.

The cluster is paired with three other concepts that one cannot understand it without: institute process (the engine of human-resource development within it), programme of growth (the pattern of life it carries), and Neighbourhood (the sub-cluster setting where, in urban or large clusters, expansion and consolidation tighten into one motion).

The three milestones

The 28 December 2010 message turned the question of cluster development from “what category is this cluster in?” to “what phase of development has the population of this cluster reached?” Reflecting on a decade of experience with the original four-stage scheme, the House of Justice wrote that “the abundant experience which has since accrued enables the believers now to conceive of the movement of a population, propelled by mounting spiritual forces, in terms of a rich and dynamic continuum” (28 December 2010). It then named the first two milestones; the 29 December 2015 message added the third.

Each milestone is a qualitative threshold read from the ground, not a status conferred by an institution. Clusters can stall between milestones; clusters can recede; the framework is descriptive of movement, not a grading scheme.

Milestone 1 — A nascent programme for sustained expansion and consolidation

The first milestone is reached when, “in whatever combination and however small in number, devotional gatherings, children’s classes and junior youth groups are being maintained by those progressing through the sequence of institute courses,” such that “a nascent programme for the sustained expansion and consolidation of the Faith can be perceived” (28 December 2010). The 28 December 2010 message names the operative phenomenon as an “initial flow of human resources into the field of systematic action.”

The first milestone is therefore not the launching of an intensive programme of growth; it is the moment at which a programme of growth has become discernible, however small. The visible signs are a handful of core activities being sustained — a study circle or two, a children’s class, a junior youth group, a devotional meeting — supported by the first local participants moving through the sequence of institute courses. Often the work is initially helped by visiting teachers or a homefront pioneer; the milestone is crossed when local protagonists begin to assume the organising role those visitors had been carrying.

Field-level indicator. The crossing is visible, per Reflections on Growth Issue 32 (Strategies for Growth in Emerging Clusters), as the moment a local nucleus of friends takes ownership of activities that outsiders had been sustaining.

Milestone 2 — The launching of an intensive programme of growth

The second milestone is the “launching” of an intensive programme of growth — not the mere initiation of activity, but “conscious recognition that all the elements necessary to accelerate the expansion and consolidation of the Faith are not only in place but also functioning with an adequate degree of effectiveness” (28 December 2010). The House of Justice describes this state as “the maturation of an ever-expanding, self-sustaining system for the spiritual edification of a population.”

What this looks like operationally: pronounced three-month cycles of activity of expansion, consolidation, reflection and planning; an Area Teaching Committee functioning; a full slate of institute coordinators for the three educational stages (children’s classes, junior youth, main sequence); a steady flow of participants through the sequence of courses; community-building activities established in at least one receptive neighbourhood or village. The 29 December 2015 message describes the typical scale: “tens of individuals active in the work of expansion and consolidation, while those participating might well surpass a hundred.”

The 28 December 2010 message reports that “some 1,600 clusters worldwide” had reached this state by 2010 — narrowly meeting the 1,500-cluster target the House had set for the 2006–2011 Plan. By the close of the One Year Plan in Riḍván 2022, “close to 5,000” clusters had passed the second milestone (30 December 2021).

Milestone 3 — A dynamic pattern of community life

The third milestone, named in the 29 December 2015 message, denotes “the appearance of a system for extending, in centre after centre, a dynamic pattern of community life that can engage a people — men and women, youth and adults — in the work of their own spiritual and social transformation.” Around two hundred clusters had reached this level by 2015.

Crossing the second milestone does not lead automatically to the third. What the 29 December 2015 message identified as the decisive shift is the systematic concentration of activity in neighbourhoods and villages where “receptivity wells up” — small centres of population where “the society-building power inherent in the Faith becomes most visible.” The third milestone is reached when this neighbourhood-and-village concentration has spread broadly enough that the cluster itself has developed a system for extending it from one centre of intense activity to another. Complexity is now the central challenge: hundreds or thousands of participants, coordinators working in teams, Auxiliary Board assistants appointed to single villages, the Local Spiritual Assembly consulting on the community’s life as a whole, stirrings of social action and engagement with prevalent discourses of society beginning to claim a share of the friends’ attention.

The phrase “society-building power” the 29 December 2015 message uses is, as the 30 December 2021 message later makes explicit, drawn from Shoghi Effendi — a Guardian formulation that the third-milestone vocabulary surfaces by name. The 30 December 2021 message frames the next twenty-five years of Plans as “the release of what the Guardian described as ‘the society-building power’ of the Faith.”

By Riḍván 2022, 1,300 clusters had passed the third milestone. The Nine Year Plan’s specific sub-goal is “that during the Nine Year Plan the process of growth reach this level of intensity in at least one cluster in every country and every region” (30 December 2021), each acting as a “reservoir” of knowledge and pioneers for surrounding clusters.

Beyond the third milestone

The 30 December 2021 message and the 31 December 2025 mid-Plan message describe a further horizon — not as a fourth milestone but as a qualitative condition that is becoming visible in the most advanced centres of intense activity within third-milestone clusters. “In the most advanced clusters … within particular neighbourhoods and villages where intense activity is under way, a significant proportion of the population is engaged in community-building activities” (30 December 2021); the Bahá’í community there “has become a highly visible protagonist in society in its own right.” The 31 December 2025 message observes that in such centres “the term ‘programme of growth’ no longer does full justice to such developments” — the vocabulary of the present is being stretched by the reality the friends are encountering.

Cycles of activity within the cluster

The unit of work within a cluster operating at or near the second milestone is the three-month cycle of activity — what the 27 December 2005 Framework for Action message codified as the basic rhythm of an intensive programme of growth. Each cycle moves through expansion (intensified teaching, opening of new core activities), consolidation (deepening with new declarants, accompaniment of new tutors and animators), reflection (the cluster reflection meeting), and planning (commitments for the next cycle).

The 29 December 2015 message warns specifically against turning this rhythm into a checklist: “It is not the provision of training by itself that brings about progress.” The cycle is the form that a learning community takes; its quality depends on whether the cluster is reading its own reality honestly.

Human resources: the institute process

The training institute is the engine of every milestone transition. The Riḍván 2010 message names “the study circle, the tutor and the curriculum of the Ruhi Institute” as the irreducible trio through which the institute does its work. Book 7 — Walking Together on a Path of Service — is the gateway book: a graduate of Book 7 is qualified to tutor others through Books 1–6, multiplying the institute’s capacity at the cluster level.

The 12 December 2011 message on the courses of the training institute is the most detailed UHJ articulation of how the institute’s three educational streams — children’s classes, junior youth empowerment, and the main sequence — must run “in parallel” under a single coordinating arrangement at cluster level. The cluster’s pedagogical coherence depends on this parallelism.

Institutions at the cluster level

The 9 January 2001 letter named the triad that does the actual coordinating work inside a cluster: the institute (through its cluster coordinators for the three educational stages), the Area Teaching Committee, and the Auxiliary Board members and their assistants. The triad’s functioning together is the precondition for sustained progress; in the cases Reflections on Growth Issue 35 documents of clusters that have stalled, the central diagnosis is repeatedly “the cluster agencies were no longer functioning.”

Around the triad sit:

  • Local Spiritual Assembly. Where the cluster contains one or more LSAs, each Assembly supports, encourages, and protects the work; coordinates community life; consults on the spiritual and material well-being of its locality. The LSA does not substitute for the triad but works alongside it.
  • Regional Bahá’í Council. Coordinates across the clusters of a region; identifies clusters where the conditions are ripe for the next milestone; deploys regional resources accordingly.
  • Continental Board of Counsellors and ITC. Accompany at scale; provide the longer perspective that the 28 December 2010 / 29 December 2015 / 30 December 2021 series of messages itself is addressed to. The International Teaching Centre’s Insights from the Frontiers of Learning (2013) is the operational elaboration of the milestone framework; the UHJ Counsellor-Conference messages remain the doctrinal source.

Questions for meeting preparation

Grounded in the framework above, the following questions can structure a cluster reflection meeting, an LSA planning consultation, or a Counsellor visit.

  1. Where on the continuum is this cluster? Not which “category” — what is the qualitative state of the population’s movement? Are core activities being maintained by local participants progressing through the institute? Is there a self-sustaining IPG with three-month cycles in pronounced rhythm? Is there a dynamic pattern of community life extending neighbourhood to neighbourhood?
  2. What is the state of the institute pipeline? How many participants are currently moving through Books 1–7? How many tutors graduated from Book 7 in the last cycle? How many junior-youth animators (Book 5) and children’s-class teachers (Book 3) are active? What is the rate of new entries to Book 1?
  3. Which cycle are we in, and what did we learn from the last one? What was planned; what actually happened; what does the difference disclose? Is the reflection meeting happening, and is it functioning as a learning gathering rather than a reporting session?
  4. Are the cluster agencies functioning together? Are the institute coordinators, the Area Teaching Committee, and the ABM/assistants meeting in coordination? Where the cluster contains an LSA, is it engaged?
  5. What is the neighbourhood (or village) strategy? Within urban or large clusters: which specific neighbourhood is the centre of intense activity? Is the work concentrating tightly enough to allow expansion and consolidation to be one motion?
  6. Who is being engaged? Is the participation diverse enough to actually represent the population the cluster sits inside, or are the activities being carried by a narrow sub-population?
  7. What stirrings of social action and engagement with discourses are visible? Even at the second milestone, these begin to appear; at the third they become substantive. What is being learned from them?

Read-flags worth attending to

  • Reflection meetings missed or reduced to reporting → the cluster’s learning loop has broken.
  • Few or no graduates of Book 7 in recent cycles → tutor capacity is not accumulating; the pipeline will choke.
  • Core activities sustained only by Bahá’ís → the work has not yet opened to the wider population.
  • Statistics unreliable or contested → the cluster cannot read its own reality (the first remedy R.O.G. 35 prescribes for stalled clusters).
  • Cluster agencies meeting separately, not together → the triad has ceased to act as a triad.
  • A cluster-internal vocabulary still using the old A/B/C/D categories → the cluster is reading the Plan through a frame the House of Justice itself retired in 2010 (see cluster-classifications for the supersession).
  • Confusion between “passing a milestone” and “being awarded a milestone” → milestones are read from the ground, not conferred.

Note on supersession of earlier classifications

A full account of the prior four-stage classification (the field-shorthand A/B/C/D) and its supersession by the milestone continuum lives at cluster-classifications. In brief: the four categories of the 9 January 2001 message were diagnostic of a cluster’s readiness to launch an intensive programme of growth; once large numbers of clusters had crossed that threshold, the question changed, and the 28 December 2010 message replaced the readiness scheme with the continuum of milestones. The two schemes do not map onto one another cleanly — the 28 December 2010 message frames the change as a different kind of question, not a re-labelling.
