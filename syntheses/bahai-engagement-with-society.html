Engagement with the Discourses of Society

The Bahá’í community’s engagement with the prevalent discourses of society is one of three coordinate dimensions of Bahá’í endeavour named by the Universal House of Justice — alongside the programme of growth (expansion and consolidation) and social action. The three are not parallel tracks; the 30 December 2021 letter launching the Nine Year Plan describes them as “dimensions of a single, unified, outward-looking endeavour carried out at the grassroots of society.” This synthesis treats the discourse dimension specifically; for the unified framework see Three Dimensions of Bahá’í Endeavour.

By discourses of society the House of Justice means the conversations already underway in schools, universities, professional bodies, civic associations, governments, and intergovernmental institutions about the great problems facing humanity — peace, justice, the equality of women and men, the relation of religion and science, the disparity between rich and poor, governance at the global scale, the climate emergency. The Bahá’í community’s contribution to those conversations is offered as insight, not as prescription, and is grounded in three decades of public statements that this page treats as a single arc.

The verbal antecedent

The lexicon of dimensions enters UHJ vocabulary in the 2 March 2013 message to the Bahá’ís of Iran, which sets out two dimensions of the worldwide community’s contribution to civilisation-building: “The first is related to its own growth and development, and the second to its involvement in society at large.” The second dimension is then sub-articulated into engagement with social and economic life and engagement with “the discourses prevalent in society” — the tri-articulation in nascent form. The 29 December 2015 message to the Continental Counsellors extends the framing to a coordinate three; the 30 December 2021 letter unifies all three under the arc of the society-building power of the Faith. Discourse contribution is thus not an optional extension of community life — it is one of the three modes through which the Faith translates Revelation into reality.

The three spine documents

Three documents anchor the contemporary Bahá’í engagement with the prevalent discourses of society. Their precise institutional framings matter, because the channel of issue carries the document’s standing.

The Promise of World Peace — UHJ, October 1985

The Promise of World Peace is the Universal House of Justice’s October 1985 statement to the peoples of the world, transmitted via the channels of the Bahá’í International Community to heads of state, religious leaders, and the United Nations during the surrounding International Year of Peace. The signatures, voice, and authority are UHJ; the BIC’s role is as channel and presenter — not as authoring institution. This is a distinction of consequence, sometimes lost in informal references that describe Promise as a “BIC statement.”

The statement places peace within an organic civilisational arc. The House of Justice writes:

“World peace is not only possible but inevitable. It is the next stage in the evolution of this planet — in the words of one great thinker, ‘the planetization of mankind’.”

Peace will come, the House of Justice continues, “only after unimaginable horrors precipitated by humanity’s stubborn clinging to old patterns of behaviour, or… now by an act of consultative will.” The choice is not between war and peace but between which path leads there. Six obstacles are then named — racism, the inordinate disparity between rich and poor, unbridled nationalism, religious strife, the inequality of women and men, and the lack of universal education and a universal auxiliary language — each receiving substantive treatment. By Riḍván 1987 the statement had been distributed in over a million copies across roughly seventy languages, the most widely circulated single Bahá’í document to that date.

What Promise opens is the genre of contemporary Bahá’í public-discourse contribution. Before 1985 the community’s external voice was largely confined to teaching and proclamation; after Promise, the register of contribution to a global conversation — speaking from within the Writings to a world audience — becomes a sustained mode of Bahá’í engagement.

The Prosperity of Humankind — BIC under UHJ direction, 3 March 1995

The Prosperity of Humankind is a statement prepared at the Bahá’í World Centre under the direction of the Universal House of Justice and issued by the Bahá’í International Community in March 1995 for the United Nations World Summit for Social Development in Copenhagen. Institutionally it sits one register down from Promise: BIC-issued, UHJ-directed — what the framework page on the three dimensions calls “BWC under UHJ direction.” Doctrinally it is formed to UHJ-tier standard.

Prosperity re-frames its subject. Prosperity is not material accumulation but the comprehensive fulfilment of human potential, material and spiritual together; development is not a sectoral activity done by experts to recipient populations but “a global enterprise” in which every member of the human family is a protagonist. The pivotal axiom is named without hedge:

“The principle of the oneness of humankind, the pivot round which all the teachings of Bahá’u’lláh revolve, is no mere outburst of ignorant emotionalism or an expression of vague and pious hope. Its implications are deeper, its claims greater than any which the Prophets of old were allowed to advance.”

The principle is offered as a sociological reality that must restructure institutions — not as moral exhortation. The empowerment of populations to participate in the generation, application, and refinement of knowledge is named as constitutive of true development; this framing precedes the 26 December 1995 Counsellors’ message on the institute process by nine months and is best read as its public-facing companion. Prosperity also occupies a unique place in the Phase B literature survey: it is L1, the highest-priority document in the elevation list, and the principal text Ruhi Book 13 is expected to draw on when published. Its absence from any synthesis of Bahá’í engagement with society’s discourses would be a structural defect.

One Common Faith — BWC publication on UHJ commission, Naw-Rúz 2005

One Common Faith was commissioned by the Universal House of Justice and released at Naw-Rúz 2005 by the Bahá’í World Centre as a companion piece to the April 2002 letter to the world’s religious leaders. It belongs to the same register as Prosperity: BWC-issued under UHJ commission. The phrasing matters because BWC publications carry the institutional weight of the World Centre but are not signed UHJ letters; the distinction preserved across the three spine documents — UHJ-via-BIC for Promise, BWC-under-UHJ-direction for Prosperity, BWC-on-UHJ-commission for One Common Faith — is the one Authority and infallibility §5 makes load-bearing.

One Common Faith reads twentieth-century history as the consolidation of materialist ideology and its inability to satisfy humanity’s spiritual needs, the consequent late-century resurgence of religion (often in fanatical forms), and the necessity of recognising religion as one continuous reality if the discourse on religious truth is to be elevated. Bahá’u’lláh’s warning is quoted directly:

“Religious fanaticism and hatred are a world-devouring fire, whose violence none can quench. The Hand of Divine power can, alone, deliver mankind from this desolating affliction.”

The document closes by naming the responsibility resting on the Bahá’í community: to engage the public in discussion of religion above sectarian and transient considerations, drawing on Bahá’u’lláh’s Writings to elevate the discourse.

Read together, the three documents form a thirty-year arc. Promise opens the genre on peace; Prosperity extends it to development and the oneness of humankind as social axiom; One Common Faith extends it again to the relation of religion and the secular age. Every subsequent Tier C–adjacent BIC paper — on values, governance, climate, equality — operates within the framework these three opened.

Institutional standing of the BIC

The Bahá’í International Community has held consultative status with the United Nations Economic and Social Council (ECOSOC) since 1970, and a working relationship with UNICEF and other UN agencies. Its offices in New York, Geneva, Brussels, Addis Ababa, and Jakarta carry the discourse work into UN processes, regional bodies, and civil-society forums; the BIC statements compilation gathers the corpus from 1928 to the present, with the principal contemporary growth in the post-1985 discourse-engagement register. National Offices of External Affairs, established by NSAs and described in the 30 December 2021 letter as a “vastly augmented, greatly fortified network,” extend the same work into national contexts.

A pastoral caveat is in order. The BIC is an institution whose authority is conferred by the UHJ; its statements are taken seriously and shape opinion both inside and outside the community, but they do not carry the UHJ’s own conferred freedom from error. When BIC framings are cited in conversation, they are best presented as the considered institutional voice of the Bahá’í community’s external-affairs arm — not as binding interpretation. The doctrinal grounding lies in the Writings and in UHJ guidance; the BIC’s contribution is a contemporary articulation of that grounding for international consultation.

What discourse contribution actually is

The 30 December 2021 letter is precise about the mechanism by which the discourse dimension is generated. Closer association with a population, brought about through the work of expansion and consolidation, leads to “increased consciousness of an area’s prevailing social problems, as well as of the aspirations of its people to overcome them. As the number of those participating in community-building activities rises, so does the need for the Bahá’í community to offer, as a unified body, its considered perspective on obstacles to social progress.” Discourse contribution is therefore not an act of opinion-broadcasting; it is the articulate voice of a community that has actually tried, at the grassroots, what it is now offering as insight.

Two implications follow for the individual believer. First, the most consequential discourse engagement happens in the conversations and contexts the friends already inhabit — the workplace, the campus, the professional society, the civic body. The 2 March 2013 message describes how “women and men, working and consulting together, contribute to the discourses prevalent in society.” Discourse work is collegial and conversational before it is platform-based. Second, the proper register is contribution, not advocacy. The community offers a perspective grounded in principle and tested by field experience; it does not offer “the Bahá’í solution,” does not align with partisan positions, does not press for particular outcomes. The aim is to elevate the conversation, drawing on the Writings to introduce concepts and questions the surrounding discourse may otherwise lack.

The community has also learned, painfully and across decades, that the avoidance of partisanship is not a constraint on engagement but its enabling condition. Where the friends speak from principle, the conversation is open to those who do not share the community’s beliefs; where the community is read as politically aligned, the conversation closes. The 30 December 2021 letter’s framing of the three dimensions as “a common framework for action” applies here: the avoidance of partisanship is not a separate rule but a structural property of the framework itself.

Specific discourses

Peace and the next stage of social evolution

Promise of World Peace remains the spine document. The argument that peace is “the next stage in the evolution of this planet” — that the choice before humanity is the path by which peace is reached, not whether — has aged into structural relevance as the international order has frayed. Subsequent UHJ messages on world conditions, and BIC statements at the UN, draw on this 1985 framing as their recurring base.

The oneness of humankind and equality

The principle of the oneness of humankind, named in Prosperity as the “pivot round which all the teachings of Bahá’u’lláh revolve,” is the operative axiom across the discourse arc. Bahá’u’lláh’s own formulation, from the Lawḥ-i-Maqṣúd in Tablets of Bahá’u’lláh Revealed after the Kitáb-i-Aqdas, is the source for the much-quoted line: “The earth is but one country, and mankind its citizens.” Cited in this register, the line is not a slogan but a sociological assertion about the unit at which human affairs must henceforth be organised.

On the equality of women and men, the authoritative formulations from ‘Abdu’l-Bahá (in Selections from the Writings of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá and the Promulgation of Universal Peace) are firmly attested: “Until the reality of equality between man and woman is fully established and attained, the highest social development of mankind is not possible.” See GenderEquality for the saturated treatment. Promise of World Peace names the inequality of women and men as one of the six obstacles to peace, and the BIC’s sustained engagement with the UN Commission on the Status of Women has been one of the most active contemporary discourse threads.

Race unity and the elimination of prejudice

The most precise authoritative statement on race in the contemporary corpus is Shoghi Effendi’s, in The Advent of Divine Justice (1938), addressed to the Bahá’ís of the United States and Canada. The Guardian writes:

“As to racial prejudice, the corrosion of which, for well-nigh a century, has bitten into the fiber, and attacked the whole social structure of American society… it should be regarded as constituting the most vital and challenging issue confronting the Bahá’í community at the present stage of its evolution.”

The framing is specific to North America at the period the letter was written, but the obligation it places on the friends — rectitude of conduct, freedom from prejudice, patient and active labour by both white and Black believers — has remained load-bearing across the seven decades since. The Bahá’í community’s own composition and its sustained practice of integrated community life are themselves part of its discourse contribution: a demonstration that the principle is operable.

Religion and the secular age

One Common Faith is the spine document for the discourse on religion. Its argument that materialist ideology and religious fanaticism share a common failure to grasp the nature of progressive revelation is the contribution most distinctively Bahá’í in this conversation, and the one most often picked up in interfaith and academic settings.

Climate, environment, and trusteeship

The Bahá’í contribution to environmental discourse is grounded in ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s tablets on the relationship between humanity and nature, articulated through the framing of trusteeship — humanity as steward of creation rather than owner. The BIC has been active at successive UN climate conferences and in the SDG framework; the trusteeship image is the distinctive lexical contribution.

Youth, service, and the empowerment of populations

The mobilisation of Bahá’í youth in community service since the 2013 conferences — the cohort the 29 December 2015 message numbered at “more than 80,000” with “an additional cohort of over 100,000” by the end of that year — is itself a contribution to societal discourse on the role of young people. Discourse engagement at this register is not statement-making; it is the visible operation of an alternative model of youth life, observed by surrounding communities and increasingly studied by scholars.

A note on the present moment

The 28 November 2023 message of the Universal House of Justice on the centenary of the ascension of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá observes that the Bahá’í community “now millions strong, has today spread to some one hundred thousand localities in 235 countries and territories.” This scale matters for the discourse dimension specifically: discourse contribution carries weight insofar as the contributing community is genuinely living the principle it articulates. A community present at this geographic and demographic scale, with a sustained pattern of community-building activity at the grassroots, is in a different position to contribute to the prevalent discourses of society than a small community speaking from outside the field of action.
