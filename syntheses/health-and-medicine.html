Health, Medicine, and the Bahá’í Approach: Principle, Profession, and the Shape of the Future

Executive summary

The Bahá’í corpus treats medicine as a permanent and dignified human undertaking, not a stopgap to be transcended by spirituality. Bahá’u’lláh commands the believer to “resort ye, in times of sickness, to competent physicians,” and — unusually for a religious founder — explicitly confirms the use of material means rather than setting them aside (Kitáb-i-Aqdas, v.113). That single verse is a long-range bet: the profession of healing will be needed, and trusted, for the duration. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá then sets the frame inside which that profession operates: healing has two arms, material and spiritual, both to be used; the outer cause of most disease is imbalance, the inner cause is intemperance; and the trajectory of the science is toward prevention and nutrition — “the people of Bahá must develop the science of medicine to such a high degree that they will heal illnesses by means of foods.”

My position on the central question: the future of medicine is not “AI replaces the physician” and it is not “drugs forever.” It is a re-weighting, and the Writings predicted the direction of that re-weighting a century before the technology arrived. The diagnostic and triage layer — reviewing symptoms, identifying the malady, prescribing the remedy — is precisely the “perfect method of the skilful physician” ‘Abdu’l-Bahá names, and it is the part of medicine most suited to knowledge-based systems and AI. The intervention layer — surgery, imaging — moves toward purpose-built robotics of superhuman precision. The centre of gravity, though, shifts upstream: toward balance, prevention, and food, and toward healing made accessible at the heart of community life, exactly as the Mashriqu’l-Adhkár’s dependencies (hospital, dispensary) envisage. For anyone deciding how to act or where to build, the highest-leverage contribution is not a better pill but a better knowledge layer — diagnosis, triage, and preventive guidance — made cheap, trustworthy, and accessible, while the advanced material means remain concentrated where only institutions can sustain them.

“Resort ye to competent physicians”: the bet on the profession

Most spiritual traditions treat physical medicine warily — as a concession, a distraction from prayer, sometimes a rival to faith healing. The Bahá’í Writings do the opposite. In the Kitáb-i-Aqdas, the Book of Laws itself, Bahá’u’lláh writes:

“Resort ye, in times of sickness, to competent physicians; We have not set aside the use of material means, rather have We confirmed it through this Pen, which God hath made to be the Dawning-place of His shining and glorious Cause.”

The phrasing is deliberate. It does not merely permit medicine; the Pen of Revelation confirms it. This is a clear, durable judgment about the world: competent physicians will exist, will be worth consulting, and consulting them is itself an act in harmony with — not opposed to — the spiritual life. The word that carries the weight is competent: the obligation is not to credentials or institutions as such but to genuine skill.

That this is a living principle, not a relic, is shown by how the Universal House of Justice applies it. In guidance issued during the coronavirus pandemic, the House of Justice grounded its counsel in this very verse and concluded that “the friends should follow the counsel of medical and other scientific experts on the advisability and efficacy of the various vaccination options” — trusting competent expertise and material means in real time, under uncertainty. The principle is not antiquarian; it is operational.

For anyone weighing where the world is going, this is the foundational stance: medicine is a permanent human good, and the practitioner of medicine — the physician — is affirmed by the highest authority in the Faith. Whatever transformation the field undergoes, the Writings give no warrant for expecting the need for skilled healing, or for those who provide it, to disappear.

The two healings: material means and spiritual means

‘Abdu’l-Bahá sets the physician’s work inside a larger account of how human beings actually heal. There are, He writes, two ways:

“There are two ways of healing sickness, material means and spiritual means. The first is by the treatment of physicians; the second consisteth in prayers offered by the spiritual ones to God and in turning to Him. Both means should be used and practiced.”

Crucially, He does not subordinate one to the other or treat the spiritual as a substitute for the material. Illnesses “which occur by reason of physical causes should be treated by doctors with medical remedies”; those due to spiritual causes — affliction, fear, “nervous impressions” — respond more effectively to spiritual means. And then the decisive sentence for anyone who imagines the Faith counsels prayer over medicine:

“Therefore thou shouldst also accept physical remedies inasmuch as these too have come from the mercy and favor of God, Who hath revealed and made manifest medical science so that His servants may profit from this kind of treatment also.”

Medical science is, in this reading, itself a divine bestowal. The two arms are “not contradictory”; both are to be followed. There is even a third note — the manner of healing. In a Tablet to a physician ‘Abdu’l-Bahá writes: “When giving medical treatment turn to the Blessed Beauty, then follow the dictates of thy heart. Remedy the sick by means of heavenly joy and spiritual exultation … When at the bedside of a patient, cheer and gladden his heart.” The healer’s own spirit is part of the treatment — a claim modern medicine is only now re-learning under the language of the therapeutic relationship and placebo response.

This two-arm structure matters for the future. As diagnosis and intervention become more automated, the question of what only a human contributes sharpens. The Writings answer it directly: the spiritual arm of healing — presence, hope, the gladdening of the heart — is irreducibly human, and it is not optional.

Balance, prevention, and the diminishing role of intervention

The most striking — and most forward-looking — body of Bahá’í teaching on health is ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s account of why people fall ill. He names two layers of cause. The inner, spiritual cause is intemperance: “man hath perversely continued to serve his lustful appetites, and he would not content himself with simple foods … he abandoned the temperance and moderation of a natural way of life. The result was the engendering of diseases both violent and diverse.” He contrasts this with animals, which “content … with simple foods” and so suffer far fewer ailments.

The outer, physical cause is imbalance:

“The outer, physical causal factor in disease … is a disturbance in the balance, the proportionate equilibrium of all those elements of which the human body is composed … So long as these constituents remain in their due proportion … there will be no physical cause for the incursion of disease.”

From this diagnosis flows a prediction that reads, today, as uncannily precise. Citing the Báb, ‘Abdu’l-Bahá writes:

“The people of Bahá must develop the science of medicine to such a high degree that they will heal illnesses by means of foods … It is the function of a skilled physician to determine which constituent of his patient’s body hath suffered diminution, which hath been augmented. Once he hath discovered this, he must prescribe a food containing the diminished element … to reestablish the body’s essential equilibrium.”

And: “It is certain that in this wonderful new age the development of medical science will lead to the doctors’ healing their patients with foods.” He even offers the mechanism by analogy — animals, untaught, are led “by a natural urge, which is the appetite” to the very plant that restores the missing component.

Three things follow for how to read the trajectory of medicine. First, the Writings locate the long-term centre of gravity upstream of intervention: in balance, temperance, and nutrition, not in an ever-growing pharmacopoeia. Second, this is explicitly framed as the direction of progress — “this wonderful new age,” “the development of medical science will lead to” — not nostalgia for a pre-modern simplicity. Third, it implies the role of acute drug intervention, while never abolished, should recede in relative weight as prevention and nutritional medicine mature. The contemporary “food as medicine” movement, the rise of metabolic and lifestyle medicine, and the data linking diet to chronic disease are the field beginning to arrive where the Writings pointed.

One guardrail: ‘Abdu’l-Bahá is describing a mature science of nutrition, not licensing anyone to abandon their physician for a diet. The same body of guidance that elevates food insists, in the same breath, on consulting competent physicians and using material remedies. Prevention is the destination; it is not a reason to refuse treatment on the way.

Diagnosis: the physician’s method, and the AI use case

Running through Bahá’u’lláh’s and ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s language is a recurring image: the skilled physician as one who first reads the condition correctly, then prescribes. Bahá’u’lláh, in the Tablet to Maneckji Sahib, writes:

“The All-Knowing Physician hath His finger on the pulse of mankind. He perceiveth the disease, and prescribeth, in His unerring wisdom, the remedy. Every age hath its own problem, and every soul its particular aspiration … Be anxiously concerned with the needs of the age ye live in.”

‘Abdu’l-Bahá, counselling wisdom in action, makes the method explicit: “First diagnose the disease and identify the malady, then prescribe the remedy, for such is the perfect method of the skilful physician.” (In context He is speaking of teaching with discernment — but the image He reaches for, as a self-evident model of skill, is precisely the physician’s diagnostic method.)

Read against the present, this is the part of medicine most native to knowledge-based systems and AI. Diagnosis and triage are pattern-recognition over a vast, structured body of knowledge — symptoms in, malady identified, remedy proposed. It is the work that, done by a human, depends on having internalised more cases than any one mind can hold; it is exactly where machine systems already match or exceed average clinical performance in narrow domains, and where, generalised, they begin to look like the standing “holo-doctor” of imagination: an always-available first reader of symptoms that triages, escalates, and never tires. The Writings’ framing fits this cleanly: the physician’s method (diagnose, then prescribe) is honoured as the model of skill; nothing in the texts ties that method to a human substrate exclusively. What the texts do reserve to the human is the second arm — the gladdening of the heart, the spiritual treatment, the judgment at the threshold of consequence.

The field today (external sources — field description, not Bahá’í authority)

The Writings supply the principles; they cannot describe the current state of the art. The following is drawn from current industry and research sources as of 2026 and is offered as description of the field, never as authority for a Bahá’í teaching.

  • Diagnosis and triage are crossing from research into the clinic. As of early 2026 the U.S. FDA’s list of authorised AI/ML-enabled medical devices exceeds 880, with radiology accounting for roughly three-quarters of clearances. In January 2026 the FDA cleared the first multi-condition AI triage system for body CT (Aidoc’s CARE foundation model), reporting mean sensitivity ~97% and specificity ~98% across its indications — a shift from narrow single-disease detectors toward general triage. Adoption still lags capability (only ~30% of radiologists report routine clinical use), which is the gap, not the ceiling.
  • Intervention is being rebuilt around purpose-built robotics. Intuitive Surgical’s da Vinci platform has surpassed ~8,000 installed systems and ~12 million procedures; the firm projects ~13–16% procedure growth for 2026, with the da Vinci 5 generation adding force feedback and deeper AI integration. Competition is opening (Medtronic’s Hugo cleared for urologic procedures). These are specialised robots, not humanoids — the precision frontier favours machines built for one anatomy, one motion.
  • Consumer-accessible medtech is the disruptive edge. The AI-wearables market is forecast past ~$39B in 2026; devices now do continuous biomarker sensing via microneedle interstitial-fluid sampling, multi-lead ECG, smartphone-attached point-of-care ultrasound, and pre-symptomatic infection detection (patterns flagged up to ~48 hours before symptoms). The boundary between consumer electronics and regulated diagnostics is dissolving — the cheap device in the pocket becomes the first triage node.
  • “Food as medicine” is moving from slogan to evidence. Produce-prescription modelling (Tufts) estimates a national programme could avert ~292,000 cardiovascular events and save ~$39.6B; metabolic programmes report substantial type-2 diabetes reversal. This is the contemporary field walking toward ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s prediction that medicine would learn to heal “by means of foods.”

The pattern across all four is the same shape the Writings imply: a cheap, distributed knowledge-and-sensing layer (diagnosis, triage, monitoring, prevention) over a concentrated, capital-intensive intervention layer (advanced imaging, robotic surgery) — with the upstream weight shifting toward prevention and nutrition.

Medicine at the heart of community: the Mashriqu’l-Adhkár

The Bahá’í vision does not leave healing to the market alone. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá places it at the literal centre of community life — among the dependencies of the House of Worship:

“The Mashriqu’l-Adhkár is one of the most vital institutions in the world, and it hath many subsidiary branches. Although it is a House of Worship, it is also connected with a hospital, a drug dispensary, a traveler’s hospice, a school for orphans, and a university for advanced studies. Every Mashriqu’l-Adhkár is connected with these five things.”

Healing is thus designed to radiate from the devotional centre of the community, not to sit apart in a commercial or purely state sphere. The hospital and the dispensary are not charitable add-ons; they are constitutive of the institution — worship and the care of bodies administered as one fabric (see MashriqulAdhkar). And the model is fractal: ‘Abdu’l-Bahá envisions not a handful of grand temples but, ultimately, “a hundred thousand Mashriqu’l-Adhkárs,” descending to the scale of villages and neighbourhoods.

This carries a concrete implication for the future of medical access. The basic medicine provided at the community pivot need not stay basic. As diagnosis, triage, monitoring, and even pharmacy logistics become cheaper and more capable — driven by the very AI and consumer-medtech trends above — the “drug dispensary” and “hospital” of the local Mashriqu’l-Adhkár could deliver, at neighbourhood scale, what once required a regional centre. The architecture anticipated decentralised, community-anchored healing a century before the technology made it affordable. What remains centralised is what must be: the heavy, capital-intensive material means — advanced imaging, surgical robotics, specialised hardware — which (absent the replicators and transporters of a more distant future) will continue to live in concentrated institutions for a long time.

“Be anxiously concerned with the needs of the age”: humility about means

A final teaching guards against over-reading any single text — including the Bahá’í texts themselves on medical specifics. Bahá’u’lláh: “Every age hath its own problem … The remedy the world needeth in its present-day afflictions can never be the same as that which a subsequent age may require.” Remedies are time-bound; the principle (consult skill, use material means, seek balance) endures while the means change.

The Faith models this humility about its own healing texts. Bahá’u’lláh’s Lawḥ-i-Ṭibb (Tablet to a Physician) contains medical counsel, but the Universal House of Justice, citing guidance from Shoghi Effendi, notes that the Tablet “was addressed to a man who was a student of the old type of healing prevalent in the East and familiar with the terminology used in those days” — i.e., not every phrase is a literal prescription for all time. The enduring core of that Tablet is not a remedy but a posture: the famous healing prayer, “Thy Name is my healing, O my God, and remembrance of Thee is my remedy.” The principle is permanent; the pharmacology of 1870 is not. The same discipline should apply to our age’s confident medical claims — including the AI ones: hold the principle, expect the means to be superseded.

My position: what follows for someone deciding how to act

Pulling the threads together, the Bahá’í frame and the current field point the same way, and they let me state a defended position rather than a survey.

  1. Medicine is a permanent, growing, dignified field — a durable long-term bet. The Writings affirm the profession at the highest level; demographics and the chronic-disease burden guarantee demand. The question was never whether to be exposed to health, but which layer.
  2. The knowledge layer (diagnosis, triage, prevention) is where AI compounds fastest and where the Writings’ “skilful physician” maps most directly. This is the holo-doctor thesis, and it is the highest-leverage place to build or back: cheap, trustworthy, accessible symptom-review and triage; preventive and nutritional guidance; monitoring. It is also the most humane layer to democratise — it brings competent first-reading to people who today have none.
  3. The intervention layer rewards purpose-built robotics, not humanoids. Surgery and imaging favour machines engineered for one anatomy and one motion, powered by software increasingly written or assisted by AI. This layer stays capital-intensive and institution-bound — and that concentration is a feature, not a bug, of the foreseeable future.
  4. Consumer-accessible medtech is the disruptive frontier and the bridge to community-scale healing — the cheap pocket device as first triage node, feeding a system whose advanced means remain centralised. This is the technological enabler of the Mashriqu’l-Adhkár’s “basic medicine that need not stay basic.”
  5. The upstream weight shifts to balance, prevention, and food. Over decades the relative role of acute drug intervention should diminish as nutritional and lifestyle medicine matures — the explicit direction of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s teaching, and increasingly the direction of the evidence. Healthier, more local, less chemically-burdened food is part of the medical story, not separate from it (see agriculture).
  6. The irreducibly human core is the second arm of healing — presence, hope, the gladdening of the heart, judgment at the threshold of consequence. Whatever automates, this does not, and it is what the profession should be freed to do more of.

The synthesis: back and build the cheap knowledge-and-prevention layer; respect the concentrated intervention layer; expect the centre of gravity to move upstream toward balance and food; and never confuse the material means with the whole of healing.

Open questions / honest limits

  • The “healing by foods” prediction is directional, not a timetable. ‘Abdu’l-Bahá states that medicine will mature toward nutrition; the texts give no date, no mechanism beyond the balance-of-constituents framing, and no warrant for rejecting current treatment in the meantime. How fast, and how far, the relative role of drugs actually recedes is genuinely open.
  • “Competent” is doing heavy lifting. The Writings command consulting competent physicians but do not adjudicate what counts as competent when the competent agent is partly or wholly a machine. The mapping from “skilful physician” to AI diagnosis is mine, by analogy from the texts’ own imagery — defensible, but an interpretation, not a ruling.
  • The texts do not address AI, robotics, or biotech directly. They supply principles (material means affirmed, balance, prevention, the two healings, community-anchored care); they do not bless or condemn any specific technology. Treat the field claims as field claims.
  • The external figures will age fast. FDA device counts, surgical-robot procedure growth, wearable market sizes, and food-as-medicine cost estimates are 2026 snapshots from industry and research sources; they will be superseded. They describe the field’s shape, not its fixed magnitude.
  • The Lawḥ-i-Ṭibb caution cuts both ways. Just as 19th-century medical terminology should not be read literally, today’s confident medical and AI claims should be held loosely — principle permanent, means provisional.
