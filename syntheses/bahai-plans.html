The Bahá’í Plans: A Chronological Arc

A Bahá’í Plan is a global teaching and consolidation campaign, unfolded in successive stages under the direction of the Universal House of Justice, that translates ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s Tablets of the Divine Plan into concrete goals for the worldwide community. Shoghi Effendi systematised the Divine Plan’s execution and launched its first global stage — the Ten Year Crusade (1953–1963). Since Riḍván 1964 the House of Justice has initiated a continuing series of Plans.

This page is the chronological arc — dates, headline goals, what each Plan achieved, and what shifted into the next. It does not duplicate the year-by-year index; that spine lives in the Bahá’í Chronological Index. For the cluster-era mechanism — programmes of growth, three-month cycles, reflection meetings — see ProgramOfGrowth. For the current Plan’s phase structure, see nine-year-plan-phases and nine-year-plan-2022.

The Tablets of the Divine Plan are Tier A: ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s mandate for the systematic diffusion of the Faith across the globe. Shoghi Effendi’s letters (Tier B) elaborated the stages of that diffusion; UHJ Plans (Tier C) assign measurable goals to each national community for a defined period. Each Plan is inaugurated by a Riḍván message, with progress reviewed in subsequent messages until the next Plan opens.

Ten Year Crusade (1953–1963)

Dates: Riḍván 1953 – April 1963 (First Bahá’í World Congress, London).

Headline goals: The first world-encompassing teaching plan — launched by Shoghi Effendi and prosecuted by all twelve then-existing National Spiritual Assemblies in one simultaneous global enterprise: opening 131 virgin territories, raising National Spiritual Assemblies to fifty-seven, translating literature into a hundred new languages, completing the superstructure of the Mother Temple of Europe in Frankfurt, initiating the Mother Temple of Africa in Kampala, and developing World Centre properties.

What was achieved: After Shoghi Effendi’s passing in 1957, the Hands of the Cause followed undeviatingly the path he had outlined and brought the Crusade to triumphant conclusion. The Universal House of Justice records the scale:

“The Faith spread to 131 new countries and territories, and the number of localities where Bahá’ís resided surpassed eleven thousand, with a total of fifty-six National Spiritual Assemblies and more than 3,500 Local Assemblies. The enterprise culminated in the election of the Universal House of Justice by the members of those National Spiritual Assemblies, according to the provisions set down by ‘Abdu’l-Bahá.” — Universal House of Justice, A Century of Bahá’í Social and Economic Development (reflection on the first century of the Formative Age)

“In 1953, the Ten Year Crusade was initiated, uniting the efforts of all twelve existing National Assemblies in one common global Plan—the first of its kind.” — Universal House of Justice, Reflections on the First Century of the Formative Age

What shifted: The Crusade completed the worldwide framework of the Administrative Order and created the conditions — a community present in virtually every nation, with functioning national and local institutions — from which the House of Justice could launch its own series of global Plans. The Guardian’s dialectic of victory and crisis — unprecedented triumphs provoking opposition that would test and increase the community’s strength — would recur in every subsequent Plan.

At Riḍván 1964 the House situated the Crusade within the Guardianship’s long-range vision:

“The Ten Year Crusade, so recently consummated in a blaze of victory and rejoicing, constituted the entire ninth part of this process. It saw the Cause of God leap forward in one mighty decade-long effort to the point at which the foundations of its Administrative Order were laid throughout the world, thus preparing the way for that awakening of the masses which must characterize the future progress of the Faith.” — Universal House of Justice, Riḍván 1964

Nine Year Plan (1964–1973)

Dates: Riḍván 1964 – Riḍván 1973 (centenary of the Revelation of the Kitáb-i-Aqdas).

Disambiguation: this historical Plan is distinct from the current Nine Year Plan (2022–2031).

Headline goals: The first global Plan under the Universal House of Justice — the second world-encircling campaign of the Formative Age. Twin objectives named at launch:

“Opening at Riḍván 1964… it must, during its nine-year course, witness a huge expansion of the Cause of God and universal participation by all believers in the life of that Cause.” — Universal House of Justice, Riḍván 1964

“Expansion and universal participation are the twin objectives of this initial phase of the second epoch of the Divine Plan.” — Universal House of Justice, Riḍván 1964

Goals for the sixty-nine national communities included opening seventy virgin territories; raising National Spiritual Assemblies from sixty-nine to 108; increasing Local Spiritual Assemblies to over 13,700; building two more Mashriqu’l-Adhkárs; translating literature into 133 more languages; and coordinating worldwide commemoration of the 1967/68 centenary of Bahá’u’lláh’s Proclamation to the kings and rulers.

What was achieved: At Riḍván 1973 the House reported the Plan “completed in overwhelming victory”:

“We announce with joyful and thankful hearts the completion in overwhelming victory of the world-encircling Nine Year Plan. The Army of Light has won its second global campaign; it has surpassed the goals set for expansion and has achieved a truly impressive degree of universal participation, the twin objectives of the Plan.” — Universal House of Justice, Riḍván 1973

Recorded achievements included 95 new territories opened; 113 National Spiritual Assemblies (five more than called for); more than 17,000 Local Spiritual Assemblies; Bahá’ís in 69,500 localities; literature in 571 languages; dedication of the Mother Temple of Latin America; and initiation of work on the Seat of the Universal House of Justice on Mount Carmel. During the Plan the eleven Continental Boards of Counsellors were established (June 1968); the InternationalTeachingCentre was founded (June 1973).

What shifted: The community had crossed from laying the Administrative Order’s foundations into a phase of mass teaching and institutional maturation. The 13 July 1964 Teaching the Masses letter set the expansion-consolidation balance that would govern subsequent Plans. The Seat of the Universal House of Justice was commissioned — inseparable from the Arc Project.

Five Year Plan (1974–1979)

Dates: Naw-Rúz / Riḍván 1974 – Riḍván 1979.

Headline goals: The third global Plan under the House of Justice — the opening campaign of a critical eighteen-year period leading to the centenary of Bahá’u’lláh’s Ascension. Three governing aims at Naw-Rúz 1974:

“This Five Year Plan has three major objectives: preservation and consolidation of the victories won; a vast and widespread expansion of the Bahá’í community; development of the distinctive character of Bahá’í life particularly in the local communities.” — Universal House of Justice, Naw-Rúz 1974

“The Five Year Plan to which this community is now summoned is the opening campaign of these critical years. It is the third global plan embarked upon by the Army of Light in its implementation of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’s Divine Plan.” — Universal House of Justice, Naw-Rúz 1974

World Centre goals included acceptance of Ḥusayn Amánat’s design for the Seat of the Universal House of Justice (February 1974) and the initiation of construction on Mount Carmel.

What was achieved: Consolidation of earlier victories occupied many communities for months before expansion accelerated. Eight International Teaching Conferences at the Plan’s midpoint (1976–1977) diffused inspiration worldwide. By Riḍván 1978 the House reported more than 19,000 Local Spiritual Assemblies and over 83,000 localities where Bahá’ís resided, with advances toward distinctive Bahá’í community life and strengthened Counsellor–Assembly collaboration.

What shifted: The Plan drew to its close shortly after the outbreak of severe persecution of the Bahá’ís in Iran following the 1979 Islamic Revolution — a catastrophe that would define the opening of the Seven Year Plan. The victories nonetheless provided, in the House’s assessment, the “firm base” from which the community could face the challenges ahead.

Seven Year Plan (1979–1986)

Dates: Riḍván 1979 – Riḍván 1986.

Headline goals: The final Plan of the third epoch of the Formative Age (1963–1986). The Plan opened as the Iranian Revolution unleashed a new wave of cruelty against the friends in the Cradle of the Faith:

“Bahá’u’lláh’s heroic compatriots, the custodians of the Holy Places of our Faith in the land of its birth, were yet again called upon to endure the passions of brutal mobs… They, like their immortal forebears, the Dawn-Breakers, are standing steadfast in face of this new persecution.” — Universal House of Justice, Naw-Rúz 1979

Worldwide defense of the persecuted Iranian community and the emergence of the Faith from obscurity became load-bearing dimensions of the Plan — not secondary concerns.

What was achieved: Substantial expansion despite the persecutions; further National Spiritual Assemblies established; occupation of the Seat of the Universal House of Justice on Mount Carmel (January 1983); dedication of the Mother Temple of the Pacific (1984). At the Plan’s midpoint (1982), International Conferences in Lagos, Montreal, Dublin, Quito, and Canberra galvanised the friends. In October 1985 the House issued The Promise of World Peace, addressed “To the Peoples of the World” — a principal instrument of proclamation during the Plan’s final phase.

“Galvanized by the fires of fierce opposition and nurtured by the blood of the martyrs, the forces of the Cause of Bahá’u’lláh are, at long last, emerging from obscurity.” — Universal House of Justice, to the International Conference in Canberra, 1982

What shifted: The Plan closed the third epoch. At Riḍván 1986 the House launched the Six Year Plan and recognised the inception of the fourth epoch — a new stage in which national goals would be formulated collaboratively by National Spiritual Assemblies and Boards of Counsellors rather than assigned centrally from the World Centre.

Six Year Plan (1986–1992)

Dates: Riḍván 1986 – Riḍván 1992.

Headline goals: The opening Plan of the fourth epoch. Principal expectations named 2 January 1986:

“The organic growth of the Cause of God… becomes markedly apparent in the light of the main objectives and expectations of the Six Year Plan: a vast expansion of the numerical and financial resources of the Cause; enlargement of its status in the world… further acceleration in the process of the maturation of local and national Bahá’í communities… an extension of the involvement of the Bahá’í world community in the needs of the world around it; and the pursuit of social and economic development in well-established Bahá’í communities.” — Universal House of Justice, 2 January 1986

“This new process, whereby the national goals of the next Plan are to be largely formulated by National Spiritual Assemblies and Boards of Counselors, signalizes the inauguration of a new stage in the unfoldment of the Administrative Order… the inception of the fourth epoch of that Age.” — Universal House of Justice, 2 January 1986

What was achieved: Riḍván 1992 inaugurated a Holy Year commemorating the centenary of the Ascension of Bahá’u’lláh and celebrating the inauguration of His Covenant at the Second Bahá’í World Congress in New York. ~1.5 million enrolled during the Plan; 12 new National/Regional Spiritual Assemblies formed (total reaches 165); ~1,000 social and economic development projects active. The systematic pursuit of social and economic development received Plan-level emphasis for the first time. The Plan unfolded during historic political change, including the opening of Eastern Europe.

What shifted: The community had grown in size, institutional capacity, and engagement with the wider society. Collaborative national goal-setting became operational. The Holy Year and World Congress consolidated identity and proclamation capacity for the Plans that followed.

1993–2000: Three Year and Four Year Plans

Dates: Riḍván 1993 – Riḍván 2000.

Headline goals: The Three Year Plan (1993–1996) carried a triple theme: vitality of individual faith, development of human resources, and proper functioning of institutions. The 26 December 1995 message to the Conference of Continental Boards of Counsellors — the hinge of the modern growth framework — launched the Four Year Plan (1996–2000) with a single aim: “a significant advance in the process of entry by troops”; its principal instrument, a network of training institutes “on a scale never before attempted.”

What was achieved: Training institutes proliferated worldwide; tens of thousands completed institute courses; Regional Bahá’í Councils were formally established (30 May 1997). Entry by troops — large-scale enrollment sustained by consolidation — became the organising phenomenon successive Plans would address. See chronology rows for 1993–2000 and turning-point for the narrative.

What shifted: Consolidation ceased to mean ad-hoc post-enrolment care and became the patterned output of an institute process. The 26 November 1999 Unfoldment of the Divine Plan message announced a Twelve Month Plan (2000–2001) and the Five Year Plan (2001–2006), introducing Area Growth Programmes — precursors to clusters.

2001–2021: Cluster era and successive Five Year Plans

Dates: Twelve Month Plan (Riḍván 2000 – Riḍván 2001); Five Year Plans 2001–2006, 2006–2011, 2011–2016, 2016–2021.

Headline goals: The 9 January 2001 message to the Counsellors’ Conference is the founding text of cluster-based planning — introducing clusters, intensive programmes of growth, the sequence of courses, area teaching committees, and homefront pioneers. The community entered the Fifth Epoch of the Formative Age. Successive Five Year Plans pursued entry by troops through the framework for action: 1,500 intensive programmes of growth (IPGs) by 2010; 5,000 IPGs by 2016; greater intensity and third-milestone vision through 2021.

Intensive programmes of growth — era vs mechanism: From 2001 onward, Plans set numerical IPG goals as the measure of cluster-era maturity — the era in which growth is organised cluster-by-cluster through three-month cycles. The mechanism of how a programme of growth works — cycles, reflection meetings, institute process, milestones — is treated in ProgramOfGrowth; this arc page holds only the Plan-level goals and transitions. See chronology for IPG goal announcements (e.g. 27 December 2005: 1,500 IPGs; Riḍván 2010: achieved one year early; 29 December 2015: 5,000 achieved).

What was achieved: By Riḍván 2021 the House reported 300,000 core activities sustained involving 2 million participants — closing a twenty-five-year series of Plans (1996–2021) and announcing the start of the third epoch of the Divine Plan. The three milestones, three protagonists, and junior youth programme as a fourth core activity crystallised across this period. The Arc Project reached completion (May 2001); Reflections on Growth documented field learning (2004–2016).

What shifted: The question was no longer primarily Can we generate growth? but What does that growth produce in the world around it? The 30 December 2021 message set a twenty-five-year arc (through Riḍván 2046) aimed at “the release of the society-building power of the Faith.”

Nine Year Plan (2022–2031) — current Plan

Dates: Riḍván 2022 – Riḍván 2031 (second Plan bearing this name). One Year Plan (2021–2022) bridged from the 2016–2021 Five Year Plan.

Headline goals: Establish an intensive programme of growth in every cluster in the world — pushing clusters past the first milestone and, increasingly, past the second and third. Aspirations at launch (after 2022 re-clustering): ~14,000 clusters with programmes of growth; ~11,000 IPGs; 5,000+ past the third milestone by 2031; at least one third-milestone cluster in every country and region. The Plan unfolds in two phases: Phase 1 (2022–2026); Phase 2 (2026–2031). See nine-year-plan-phases for phase detail and chronology from 2022 onward.

What is under way: ~10,000 launch conferences (2022); formal launch of Phase 2 at Riḍván 2026. Expansion, consolidation, social action, and contributing to prevalent discourses are framed as dimensions of a single unified endeavour — “the release of the society-building power of the Faith.”

What this Plan hands forward: The 2022–2046 arc positions the Nine Year Plan as the opening movement of society-building at scale — the generative pattern the Covenant now traces through neighbourhoods and villages, not only through institutional succession.
